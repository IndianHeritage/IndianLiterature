एकाचेतत सरस्वती नदीनां शुचिर यती गिरिभ्य आ समुद्रात् | रायश चेतन्ती भुवनस्य भूरेर घृतं पयो दुदुहे नाहुषाय ||
ekācetat sarasvatī nadīnāṁ śucir yatī giribhya ā samudrāt | rāyaś cetantī bhuvanasya bhūrer ghṛtam payo duduhe nāhuṣāya ||
  • ekācetat: This term can be broken down into "eka" meaning "one" and "cetat" meaning "mind" or "thought". It suggests a singular focus or unity of thought.
  • sarasvatī: Refers to the goddess of knowledge, music, art, wisdom, and learning. In a broader sense, it symbolizes the flow of knowledge and creativity.
  • nadīnāṁ: This means "of the rivers". Rivers are often seen as sources of life and nourishment, representing the flow of wisdom and knowledge.
  • śucir: Translates to "pure" or "clean". This purity can refer to the clarity of thought or the untainted nature of knowledge.
  • yatī: This word means "ascetic" or "one who practices self-discipline". It signifies the importance of discipline in the pursuit of knowledge.
  • giribhya: Meaning "from the mountains". Mountains often symbolize stability and strength, suggesting that knowledge is grounded in solid foundations.
  • ā samudrāt: Translates to "from the ocean". The ocean represents vastness and depth, indicating that knowledge is boundless and profound.
  • rāyaś: This means "wealth" or "prosperity". It signifies the benefits that come from acquiring knowledge.
  • cetantī: Refers to "thinking" or "contemplating". It emphasizes the active engagement of the mind in the pursuit of understanding.
  • bhuvanasya: Meaning "of the world" or "of the universe". This indicates that the knowledge gained is universal and applicable to all aspects of life.
  • bhūrer: This translates to "of the earth". It signifies the grounding nature of knowledge in reality.
  • ghṛtam: Refers to "clarified butter" or "ghee\ which is often used in rituals and symbolizes nourishment and purity.
  • payo: Meaning "milk\ which is another symbol of nourishment and sustenance.
  • duduhe: This means "to extract" or "to draw out". It suggests the process of deriving knowledge from various sources.
  • nāhuṣāya: Refers to "to Nahusha\ who is a figure in Hindu mythology, often associated with power and authority. This indicates that the knowledge gained can lead to strength and leadership.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the importance of focused thought and the pursuit of knowledge, drawing from both the purity of rivers and the vastness of oceans. It highlights that true wealth comes from understanding and wisdom, which is essential for personal and societal growth. In real life, this can be applied in educational settings, professional development, and personal growth, where discipline and a quest for knowledge lead to prosperity and a deeper understanding of the world. The mantra serves as a reminder that knowledge is not just an accumulation of facts but a profound journey that enriches our lives and the lives of those around us.
