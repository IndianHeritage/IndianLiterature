आत्मन्वन नभो दूह्यते घृतं पयः ऋतस्य नाभिर अमृतं वि जायते |समीचीनाः सुदानवः प्रीणन्ति तं नरो हितम अव मेहन्ति पेर्वः ||
ātmanvan nabho duhyate ghṛtam paya ṛtasya nābhir amṛtaṁ vi jāyate | samīcīnāḥ sudānavaḥ prīṇanti taṁ naro hitam ava mehanti peravaḥ ||
  • Translation: The mantra can be translated as: "The one who possesses the self (ātman) does not churn the sky (nabhah) for ghee (ghṛtam) and the essence of truth (ṛtasya) is born from the navel (nābhir) of immortality (amṛtam). The well-behaved (samīcīnāḥ) and the good (sudānavaḥ) delight in him, and the people (naro) do not reject (mehanti) what is beneficial (hitam) for them."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • ātmanvan: One who possesses the self or soul.
    • nabhah: The sky or the heavens.
    • duhyate: To churn or to extract.
    • ghṛtam: Ghee, a symbol of nourishment and purity.
    • payah: Milk, representing sustenance and life.
    • ṛtasya: Of truth or righteousness.
    • nābhir: Navel, often symbolizing the source or origin.
    • amṛtam: Immortality or eternal essence.
    • vi jāyate: Is born or arises.
    • samīcīnāḥ: Well-behaved or righteous individuals.
    • sudānavaḥ: Good or virtuous people.
    • prīṇanti: Delight or take pleasure in.
    • taṁ: Him or that person.
    • naro: People or humans.
    • hitam: Beneficial or good.
    • ava mehanti: Do not reject or dismiss.
    • peravaḥ: What is beneficial or advantageous.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • The concept of self-awareness (ātmanvan) is crucial in personal development. For instance, individuals who engage in mindfulness practices often find that they are more in tune with their thoughts and emotions, leading to better decision-making.
    • The metaphor of churning the sky (nabhah) for ghee (ghṛtam) can be likened to the futile efforts some people make in seeking external validation or material success, rather than focusing on inner growth and self-sufficiency.
    • The idea that truth (ṛtasya) arises from the essence of immortality (amṛtam) can be seen in the pursuit of knowledge and wisdom, where individuals seek deeper understanding and enlightenment, leading to a more meaningful life.
    • Well-behaved individuals (samīcīnāḥ) and virtuous people (sudānavaḥ) contribute positively to society, as seen in community leaders who inspire others through their actions and integrity.
    • The notion that people (naro) do not reject what is beneficial (hitam) can be observed in how communities embrace initiatives that promote health and well-being, such as wellness programs or environmental sustainability efforts.
  • Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its emphasis on self-awareness, the pursuit of truth, and the importance of virtuous behavior. In real life, it encourages individuals to focus on their inner selves and the impact of their actions on the community. By embodying these principles, one can lead a fulfilling life that not only benefits oneself but also contributes positively to society. The mantra serves as a reminder that true nourishment comes from within and that our actions should align with the greater good.
