स्वयं साऽ ऋषयध्यै या न उपेषे अत्रैः |हतेम आसन न वक्षति क्षिप्ता जूर्णिर न वक्षति ||
svayaṁ sā riṣayadhyai yā na upeṣe atraiḥ | hatem asan na vakṣati kṣiptā jūrṇir na vakṣati ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • svayaṁ: oneself
    • sā: that
    • riṣayadhyai: to the sages or seers
    • yā: who
    • na: not
    • upeṣe: to approach or come near
    • atraiḥ: with these
    • hatem: having been struck down
    • asan: not existing
    • na: not
    • vakṣati: will speak or declare
    • kṣiptā: thrown or cast away
    • jūrṇir: the old or decayed
    • na: not
    • vakṣati: will speak or declare
  • Interpretation:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of self-awareness and the connection to wisdom.
    • It suggests that true wisdom does not come from external sources but from within oneself.
    • The phrase "that who does not approach these" implies that one should not rely solely on others for guidance.
    • The latter part of the mantra indicates that those who are struck down or thrown away (perhaps referring to outdated beliefs or practices) do not have the power to declare truth.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In personal development, individuals often seek external validation or advice. This mantra encourages self-reflection and trusting one's own intuition.
    • In the context of leadership, effective leaders rely on their inner wisdom and values rather than solely on popular opinion or trends.
    • In education, students are encouraged to think critically and form their own opinions rather than just accepting information from teachers or textbooks.
  • Conclusion:
    • The significance of this mantra lies in its call for self-reliance and inner wisdom.
    • It teaches that true understanding and knowledge come from within, and one should not be swayed by external influences that may be outdated or irrelevant.
    • This principle is applicable in various aspects of life, including personal growth, leadership, and education, promoting a culture of critical thinking and self-awareness.
