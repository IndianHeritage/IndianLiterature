शतपवित्राः स्वधया मदन्तीर देवीर देवानाम् अपि यान्ति पाथः |ताः इन्द्रस्य न मिनन्ति व्रतानि सिन्धुभ्यः हव्यम् घृतवज् जुहोत ||
śatapavitrāḥ svadhayā madantīr devīr devānām api yanti pāthaḥ | tā indrasya na minanti vratāni sindhubhyo havyaṁ ghṛtavaj juhota ||
  • śatapavitrāḥ: This term can be broken down into "śata" meaning "hundred" and "pavitrāḥ" meaning "pure" or "sacred." It signifies the idea of being surrounded by purity and abundance.
  • svadhayā: This word translates to "by one's own will" or "self-directed." It emphasizes the importance of individual agency and the power of self-determination.
  • madantīr: This term refers to "those who are intoxicated" or "filled with joy." It suggests a state of bliss or ecstasy, often associated with divine experiences.
  • devīr: This means "goddesses" or "divine beings." It indicates the presence of higher powers or spiritual entities that guide and protect.
  • devānām: This translates to "of the gods." It signifies the connection to divine forces and the acknowledgment of their influence in our lives.
  • api: This word means "even" or "also," suggesting inclusivity and the idea that all beings are part of a larger cosmic order.
  • yanti: This means "go" or "move towards." It indicates a journey or progression, often towards a higher state of being or understanding.
  • pāthaḥ: This translates to "path" or "way." It signifies the journey one takes in life, guided by principles and values.
  • tā: This means "those" or "they," referring back to the divine beings mentioned earlier.
  • indrasya: This term refers to "of Indra," the king of the gods in Vedic tradition, symbolizing strength, power, and leadership.
  • na minanti: This phrase means "do not diminish" or "do not weaken." It emphasizes resilience and the strength of commitment.
  • vratāni: This translates to "vows" or "sacred commitments." It signifies the importance of promises made to oneself or to the divine.
  • sindhubhyo: This means "from the rivers," symbolizing the flow of life, abundance, and nourishment.
  • havyaṁ: This translates to "offering" or "sacred oblation," indicating the act of giving and devotion.
  • ghṛtavaj: This term means "like ghee" or "clarified butter," symbolizing purity, richness, and the essence of offerings.
  • juhota: This means "may they offer" or "may they invoke," indicating the act of prayer or ritualistic offering to the divine.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the significance of purity, self-determination, and divine guidance in our lives. It encourages individuals to remain committed to their vows and to seek the blessings of the divine in their journey. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied to personal growth, spiritual practices, and the pursuit of one's goals. By recognizing the divine presence in our lives and making conscious choices, we can navigate our paths with strength and resilience, ultimately leading to a more fulfilling existence.
