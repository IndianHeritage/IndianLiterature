मन्त्र: हिरण्यहस्तम अश्विनā रराणā पुत्रं नरā वध्रिमत्यā अदत्तम् | त्रिधा ह श्यावम् अश्विनā विकस्तम् उज जीवस एयरयतं सुदानु ||
hiraṇyahastam aśvinā rarāṇā putraṁ narā vadhrimatyā adattam | tridhā ha śyāvam aśvinā vikastam uj jīvasa airayataṁ sudānū ||
  • hiraṇyahastam: This term translates to "golden-handed" or "one who possesses wealth." In a real-life context, this can refer to individuals or entities that are prosperous and generous, often sharing their wealth for the betterment of society.
  • aśvinā: The Aśvins are twin deities in Vedic mythology, symbolizing health, medicine, and the dawn. They represent the duality of life and the balance between opposites, such as light and darkness, health and illness.
  • rarāṇā: This word means "to create" or "to bring forth." It signifies the act of manifestation, whether it be in terms of ideas, relationships, or material wealth.
  • putraṁ: This translates to "son" or "child." In a broader sense, it can represent the fruits of one's labor, including projects, ideas, or even the next generation.
  • narā: This means "man" or "human." It emphasizes the importance of humanity and the collective experience of people in the pursuit of prosperity and well-being.
  • vadhrimatyā: This term can be interpreted as "with strength" or "with vigor." It highlights the necessity of resilience and determination in achieving one's goals.
  • adattam: This means "to give" or "to bestow." It emphasizes the importance of generosity and sharing one's resources with others.
  • tridhā: This translates to "threefold" or "in three ways." It suggests a holistic approach to life, encompassing various aspects such as physical, mental, and spiritual well-being.
  • ha: This is a particle often used for emphasis, reinforcing the preceding statements.
  • śyāvam: This term means "dark" or "black," which can symbolize challenges or obstacles in life that need to be overcome.
  • vikastam: This translates to "expanded" or "spread out." It signifies growth and the unfolding of potential, whether in personal development or in broader societal contexts.
  • uj jīvasa: This phrase means "to live well" or "to thrive." It emphasizes the importance of a fulfilling and prosperous life.
  • airayataṁ: This term can be interpreted as "to be nourished" or "to be sustained." It highlights the need for support and resources to achieve one's goals.
  • sudānū: This translates to "good nourishment" or "well-being." It signifies the importance of health and sustenance in achieving a balanced life.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of prosperity, resilience, and the importance of community. It emphasizes that true wealth is not just material but also includes health, relationships, and the ability to give back to society. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as in business practices that prioritize ethical giving, in personal development where one seeks to grow holistically, and in community initiatives that aim to uplift others. By embodying the principles of generosity, strength, and balance, individuals can create a more prosperous and harmonious world.
