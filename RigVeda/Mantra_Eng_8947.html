तवष्टा दुतित्रे वहतुं कृतोतितीदं विश्वं भुवनं सम एति |यमस्य माता पर्युह्यमाना महो जाया विवस्वतो ननाश ||
tvaṣṭā duhitre vahatuṁ kṛṇotītīdaṁ viśvam bhuvanaṁ sam eti | yamasya mātā paryuhyamānā maho jāyā vivasvato nanāśa ||
  • tvaṣṭā: This term refers to the divine craftsman or the creator, often associated with the act of shaping and forming the universe.
  • duhitre: Meaning "daughter," this word signifies the feminine aspect of creation, representing nurturing and growth.
  • vahatuṁ: This translates to "may it bring forth" or "may it carry," indicating a desire for the creation to manifest and flourish.
  • kṛṇotītīdaṁ: This phrase means "may it create" or "may it accomplish," emphasizing the active role of creation in the universe.
  • viśvam: Meaning "the whole" or "the universe," it signifies the entirety of existence.
  • bhuvanaṁ: This translates to "world" or "realm," referring to the various planes of existence within the universe.
  • sam eti: This means "comes together" or "unites," indicating the interconnectedness of all things in creation.
  • yamasya: Referring to "Yama," the god of death and the moral order, this term highlights the balance between life and death.
  • mātā: Meaning "mother," it signifies the nurturing aspect of the universe, often associated with the Earth or nature.
  • paryuhyamānā: This translates to "being surrounded" or "enveloped," indicating the protective and encompassing nature of the mother figure.
  • maho: Meaning "great" or "mighty," it emphasizes the power and significance of the forces at play in the universe.
  • jāyā: This term means "wife" or "consort," representing partnership and collaboration in creation.
  • vivastvato: This translates to "of the sun," indicating the life-giving and illuminating qualities associated with solar energy.
  • nanāśa: Meaning "to perish" or "to die," it reflects the cyclical nature of life, death, and rebirth.

The mantra speaks to the intricate relationship between creation, nurturing, and the cyclical nature of existence. It emphasizes the role of both the divine craftsman and the nurturing mother in shaping the universe. The invocation of Yama suggests a balance between life and death, highlighting the importance of moral order in the cosmos.


In real life, this mantra can be applied to various aspects of existence. For instance, in the context of environmental conservation, the nurturing aspect of the Earth (mātā) can be seen in efforts to protect natural resources and promote sustainability. The idea of creation (kṛṇotītīdaṁ) resonates with innovation and creativity in technology and art, where individuals strive to bring forth new ideas and solutions for the betterment of society.


In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of all beings and the importance of nurturing and creating in harmony with the universe. It encourages us to recognize the cycles of life and death, and to embrace our roles as both creators and caretakers of the world around us.
