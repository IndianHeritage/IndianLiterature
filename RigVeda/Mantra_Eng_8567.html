मन्त्र: इन्दुर वाजी पवते गोन्योगाः इन्द्रे सोमः सह इन्बन् मदाय | हन्ति रक्षो बाधते पर्य अरातीर वरिवः कृत्वान वृजनस्य राजा ||
indur vājī pavate gonyoghā indre somaḥ saha invan madāya | hanti rakṣo bādhate pary arātīr varivaḥ kṛṇvan vṛjanasya rājā ||
  • Indur: This word refers to the moon, often symbolizing nourishment, tranquility, and the mind's reflective qualities.
  • Vājī: This term means "the one who brings strength" or "the powerful one," often associated with the divine or celestial forces that empower individuals.
  • Pavate: This means "to blow" or "to move," indicating the action of the wind or the divine breath that brings life and energy.
  • Gonyoghā: This can be interpreted as "the one who moves in the direction of the goals," suggesting a purposeful journey or pursuit.
  • Indre: Referring to Indra, the king of gods in Vedic literature, symbolizing power, leadership, and protection.
  • Somaḥ: Soma is a sacred drink in Vedic tradition, representing vitality, immortality, and divine inspiration.
  • Saha: This means "together" or "with," indicating unity and collaboration.
  • Invan: This term suggests "to invoke" or "to call upon," emphasizing the act of seeking divine assistance.
  • Madāya: This refers to "for intoxication" or "for joy," indicating the blissful state that comes from divine blessings.
  • Hanti: This means "to destroy" or "to overcome," often in the context of overcoming obstacles or negativity.
  • Rakṣo: Referring to "demons" or "evil forces," symbolizing challenges or adversities in life.
  • Bādhate: This means "to hinder" or "to obstruct," indicating the presence of obstacles that need to be addressed.
  • Pary: This means "around" or "in all directions," suggesting a comprehensive approach to challenges.
  • Arātīr: This term refers to "enemies" or "opponents," representing the various challenges one faces in life.
  • Varivaḥ: This means "to create" or "to bring forth," indicating the act of manifesting positive outcomes.
  • Kṛṇvan: This means "to make" or "to do," emphasizing action and effort in achieving goals.
  • Vṛjanasya: This refers to "the one who is free from sin" or "the righteous," symbolizing purity and virtue.
  • Rājā: This means "king" or "ruler," representing authority, leadership, and governance.

The mantra speaks to the invocation of divine forces for strength, protection, and overcoming obstacles. It emphasizes the importance of unity and collaboration in the pursuit of goals, while also acknowledging the presence of challenges and adversities. The imagery of the moon and the sacred drink Soma highlights the significance of nourishment and joy derived from divine blessings.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • In personal development, individuals can invoke their inner strength and seek divine guidance to overcome challenges.
  • In leadership roles, the mantra serves as a reminder to lead with strength and protect those under one's care.
  • In community efforts, it emphasizes the importance of collaboration and unity in achieving common goals.

In conclusion, this mantra signifies the power of divine assistance in overcoming life's challenges, the importance of unity, and the pursuit of joy and strength. It serves as a reminder to seek higher guidance and to act with purpose and righteousness in all endeavors.
