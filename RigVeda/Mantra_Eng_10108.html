suparṇaṁ viprāḥ kavayo vacobhir ekaṁ santam bahudhā kalpayanti | chandāṁsi ca dadhato adhvareṣu grahān somasya mimate dvādaśa ||
  • suparṇaṁ: This term refers to the "beautifully winged" or "well-winged" one, often symbolizing the divine or the higher consciousness. In real life, this can be likened to the pursuit of higher ideals and aspirations, much like how a bird soars high in the sky, representing freedom and enlightenment.
  • viprāḥ: This word means "the wise" or "the learned." It signifies those who possess knowledge and wisdom. In contemporary society, this can be related to scholars, teachers, or anyone who seeks to impart knowledge and understanding to others.
  • kavayo: This translates to "the poets" or "the seers." Poets and seers are individuals who have the ability to perceive deeper truths and express them creatively. In modern contexts, this can be seen in artists, writers, and thinkers who inspire others through their insights.
  • vacobhir: Meaning "with words," this emphasizes the power of language and communication. In everyday life, effective communication is crucial in various fields, such as business, education, and personal relationships, where conveying ideas clearly can lead to success.
  • ekaṁ: This means "one" or "unity." It highlights the concept of oneness, which is essential in understanding the interconnectedness of all beings. In real life, this can be observed in community efforts where individuals come together for a common cause, promoting harmony and collaboration.
  • santam: This term refers to "the existent" or "the eternal." It signifies the ultimate reality or truth that underlies all existence. In practical terms, this can relate to the search for meaning in life, where individuals seek to understand their purpose and place in the universe.
  • bahudhā: This means "in many ways" or "diversely." It suggests that the truth can be perceived from various perspectives. In real life, this is evident in the diversity of cultures, beliefs, and opinions that enrich human experience and understanding.
  • kalpayanti: This translates to "they create" or "they conceive." It indicates the creative power of thought and imagination. In modern contexts, this can be seen in innovation and creativity in fields like technology, art, and science, where new ideas shape the future.
  • chandāṁsi: This refers to "the meters" or "the rhythms" of poetry. It symbolizes the structure and form that give beauty to expression. In everyday life, this can be related to the importance of structure in communication, whether in writing, music, or any form of artistic expression.
  • dadhato: Meaning "they hold" or "they maintain," this emphasizes the importance of preserving knowledge and tradition. In real life, this can be seen in educational institutions and cultural practices that strive to keep wisdom alive for future generations.
  • adhvareṣu: This term refers to "in the rituals" or "in the sacrifices." It highlights the significance of rituals in connecting with the divine. In contemporary life, rituals can be observed in various forms, such as weddings, festivals, and personal practices that foster a sense of community and spirituality.
  • grahān: This means "the celestial bodies" or "the planets." It signifies the influence of cosmic forces on human life. In real life, this can be related to astrology and the belief that celestial movements can impact personal and collective experiences.
  • somasya: Referring to "of the soma," which is often associated with a divine drink or elixir that grants immortality and enlightenment. In modern terms, this can symbolize the pursuit of knowledge and experiences that elevate the human spirit.
  • mimate: This translates to "they contemplate" or "they meditate." It emphasizes the importance of reflection and introspection in understanding oneself and the universe. In real life, practices like meditation and mindfulness are increasingly recognized for their benefits in mental health and well-being.
  • dvādaśa: Meaning "twelve," this number often symbolizes completeness and wholeness. In various cultures, the number twelve is significant, representing cycles, such as the twelve months of the year or the twelve zodiac signs, which can be seen as a reflection of the cyclical nature of life.
