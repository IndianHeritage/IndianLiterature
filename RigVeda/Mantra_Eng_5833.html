उवाच मे वरुणो मेधिराया त्रिः सप्त नामाघ्न्या बिभर्ति |विद्वान् पदस्य गुह्या न वोकद युगाय विप्र उपराय शिक्षन् ||
uvāca me varuṇo medhirāya triḥ sapta nāmāghnyā bibharti | vidvān padasya guhyā na vocad yugāya vipra uparāya śikṣan ||
  • Translation: The mantra can be translated as: "Varuna, the wise one, speaks to me, holding the three and seven names, and he who knows the secret of the path does not speak of it to the unworthy, O learned one, for the sake of instruction."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • uvāca: spoke or said
    • me: to me
    • varuṇo: Varuna, the deity associated with cosmic order and water
    • medhirāya: wise or intelligent
    • triḥ: three
    • sapta: seven
    • nāmāghnyā: names or titles
    • bibharti: holds or carries
    • vidvān: the learned or knowledgeable
    • padasya: of the path
    • guhyā: secret or hidden
    • na vocad: does not speak
    • yugāya: for the sake of
    • vipra: the learned or wise person
    • uparāya: for instruction or teaching
    • śikṣan: to teach or instruct
  • Meaning and Explanation:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of wisdom and knowledge in communication. Varuna, as a deity, represents the cosmic order and the moral authority of the universe.
    • The mention of "three and seven names" can symbolize various aspects of existence, such as the three realms (heaven, earth, and the underworld) and the seven elements or principles that govern life.
    • The phrase "the learned one does not speak of it to the unworthy" highlights the idea that knowledge should be shared wisely and only with those who are ready to understand and appreciate it.
    • In real life, this can be seen in educational settings where teachers impart knowledge to students who show interest and dedication, rather than to those who are not ready to learn.
  • Conclusion:
    • The significance of this mantra lies in its call for discernment in sharing knowledge. It teaches us that wisdom is a precious resource that should be respected and shared judiciously.
    • In practical terms, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as mentoring relationships, where a mentor chooses to guide a mentee who shows potential and eagerness to learn.
    • Ultimately, it reminds us that true understanding comes from a place of readiness and respect for the knowledge being shared.
