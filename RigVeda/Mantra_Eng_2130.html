यः सुन्वन्तम अवति यः पचन्तं यः शंसन्तं यः शशमानम् ऊती |यस्य ब्रह्म वर्धनं यस्य सोमो यस्येदं राधः स जनासा इन्द्रः ||
yaḥ sunvantam avati yaḥ pacantaṁ yaḥ śaṁsantaṁ yaḥ śaśamānam ūtī | yasya brahma vardhanaṁ yasya somo yasyedaṁ rādhaḥ sa janāsa indraḥ ||
  • yaḥ sunvantam avati: This phrase can be translated as "He who protects the one who is nourishing." In real life, this can be related to the role of caregivers or parents who provide sustenance and protection to their children. For example, a mother who ensures her child is well-fed and safe embodies this principle.
  • yaḥ pacantaṁ: This means "He who cooks or prepares." This can be seen in the context of chefs or home cooks who prepare meals for their families or communities. The act of cooking is not just about food; it is an expression of love and care, as seen in family gatherings where meals are shared.
  • yaḥ śaṁsantaṁ: This translates to "He who praises." This can be related to the importance of encouragement and recognition in our lives. For instance, teachers who praise their students for their efforts foster a positive learning environment, motivating students to strive for excellence.
  • yaḥ śaśamānam ūtī: This means "He who brings forth abundance." This can be connected to the idea of prosperity and growth in various aspects of life, such as in agriculture or business. For example, a farmer who cultivates his land diligently and reaps a bountiful harvest exemplifies this abundance.
  • yasya brahma vardhanaṁ: This translates to "Whose growth is in the divine." This can be interpreted as the spiritual growth that individuals experience through their actions and intentions. For instance, someone who engages in selfless service or charity contributes to their spiritual development and the well-being of others.
  • yasya somo: This means "Whose essence is like the moon." The moon is often associated with calmness and serenity. In real life, this can be seen in individuals who bring peace and tranquility to their surroundings, such as a mediator in a conflict resolution scenario.
  • yasyedaṁ rādhaḥ: This translates to "Whose support is this world." This can refer to the interconnectedness of all beings and the support systems we create. For example, community organizations that provide assistance to those in need illustrate this support.
  • sa janāsa indraḥ: This means "He is the Indra of the people." Indra is often seen as a king or leader. In a modern context, this can relate to leaders who inspire and uplift their communities, such as a mayor or community leader who works for the welfare of the people.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of nurturing, protection, praise, abundance, spiritual growth, tranquility, support, and leadership. In real life, these principles can be applied in various contexts, such as family dynamics, community service, and personal development. By embodying these qualities, individuals can contribute to a harmonious and prosperous society, reflecting the essence of the mantra in their daily lives.
