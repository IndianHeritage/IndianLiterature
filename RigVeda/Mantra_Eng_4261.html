उग्रो वां ककुहो ययिः श्रिण्वे यामेषु संतानिः |यद वां दंsobhir अश्विनात्रिर नराववर्तति ||
ugro vāṁ kakuho yayiḥ śṛṇve yāmeṣu saṁtaniḥ | yad vāṁ daṁsobhir aśvinātrir narāvavartati ||
  • ugro vāṁ: The term "ugro" translates to "mighty" or "powerful." It signifies strength and intensity. In real life, this can be related to individuals or forces that exhibit great power, such as natural disasters or influential leaders.
  • kakuho: This word can be interpreted as "the one who is capable of hearing" or "the one who listens." It emphasizes the importance of being attentive and receptive to the world around us. For example, a good leader listens to the concerns of their team to make informed decisions.
  • yayiḥ: This term means "to whom" or "for whom." It indicates a sense of purpose or direction. In a practical context, it can refer to actions taken for the benefit of others, such as community service or charitable work.
  • śṛṇve: This translates to "I hear" or "I listen." It reinforces the idea of active listening and understanding. In everyday life, this can be seen in effective communication, where individuals truly listen to each other rather than just waiting for their turn to speak.
  • yāmeṣu: This word means "in the nights" or "during the night." It symbolizes introspection and reflection, often associated with the quiet moments of the night when one can contemplate their thoughts and actions.
  • saṁtaniḥ: This term can be understood as "the one who is steadfast" or "the one who remains firm." It represents resilience and determination. For instance, a person facing challenges in their career but continuing to strive for success embodies this quality.
  • yad vāṁ: This phrase translates to "what you" or "that which you." It indicates a connection to the listener or the subject being addressed, emphasizing the importance of personal involvement and responsibility.
  • daṁsobhir: This word refers to "with the help of" or "through the means of." It suggests collaboration and support from others. In real life, teamwork in a project or community efforts to achieve a common goal exemplifies this concept.
  • aśvinātrir: This term can be interpreted as "the two Ashvins," who are divine twin horsemen in Vedic mythology, symbolizing health, medicine, and the duality of life. They represent the balance between different aspects of existence, such as work and rest.
  • narāvavartati: This phrase means "the one who moves among men" or "the one who interacts with people." It highlights the importance of social connections and relationships in human life. For example, a community leader who engages with citizens to understand their needs embodies this principle.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the significance of strength, listening, purpose, reflection, resilience, personal responsibility, collaboration, balance, and social interaction. In real life, these qualities are essential for personal growth, effective leadership, and community building. By embodying these principles, individuals can navigate challenges, foster meaningful relationships, and contribute positively to society. The mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of these values and their applicability in various aspects of life.
