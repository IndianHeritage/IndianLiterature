एव नो अग्नि समिधा वृधानो रेवत पावक श्रवसे वि भाहि |तन नो मित्रो वरुणो मामहन्ताम् अदितिः सिंधुः पृथिवी उता द्यौः ||
evā no agne samidhā vṛdhāno revat pāvaka śravase vi bhāhi | tan no mitro varuṇo māmahantām aditiḥ sindhuḥ pṛthivī uta dyauḥ ||
  • evā no agne: This phrase translates to "O Agni, may you be with us." Agni is the fire god in Vedic tradition, symbolizing energy, transformation, and purification. In real life, this can be likened to the role of fire in cooking, warmth, and even in industrial processes where transformation occurs.
  • samidhā vṛdhāno: This means "kindled and growing." It signifies the importance of nurturing and fostering growth, whether in personal development, relationships, or community. For example, a mentor nurturing a young professional can be seen as a modern-day interpretation of this concept.
  • revat pāvaka: Translated as "the radiant and pure fire," this emphasizes the qualities of brightness and purity. In our lives, this can represent the pursuit of knowledge and truth, akin to how a scientist seeks to illuminate the unknown through research and discovery.
  • śravase vi bhāhi: This means "may you shine forth in glory." It suggests the aspiration for recognition and success. In a real-world context, this could relate to an artist striving for their work to be acknowledged and celebrated, much like how a musician hopes their songs resonate with audiences.
  • tan no mitro varuṇo: This translates to "may the friends Mitra and Varuna be with us." Mitra and Varuna are deities representing friendship and cosmic order, respectively. In everyday life, this can be seen in the importance of maintaining harmonious relationships and ethical conduct in society, similar to how communities thrive on mutual respect and cooperation.
  • māmahantām aditiḥ: This means "the great Aditi." Aditi is the mother of the gods and symbolizes infinity and the universe. In practical terms, this can be related to the concept of nurturing and protecting the environment, as Aditi embodies the nurturing aspect of nature.
  • sindhuḥ pṛthivī uta dyauḥ: This translates to "the ocean, the earth, and the sky." These elements represent the vastness of existence and the interconnectedness of all life. In real life, this can be reflected in environmental conservation efforts, where the health of the ocean, earth, and sky is crucial for sustaining life on our planet.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a deep reverence for the forces of nature and the divine, emphasizing growth, purity, friendship, and the interconnectedness of all elements of existence. Its significance lies in the reminder to cultivate positive relationships, pursue knowledge, and respect the natural world. In contemporary life, these principles can guide individuals and communities in fostering harmony, ethical living, and environmental stewardship, ultimately leading to a more balanced and fulfilling existence.
