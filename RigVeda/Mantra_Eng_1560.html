शुचिर देवेष्व अर्पिता होत्रा मरुत्सु भारतī |इला सरस्वती मही बर्हिः सिदन्तु यज्ञियाः ||
śucir deveṣv arpitā hotrā marutsu bhāratī | iḻā sarasvatī mahī barhiḥ sīdantu yajñiyāḥ ||
  • śucir: This word translates to "pure" or "clean." In a real-life context, purity can refer to both physical cleanliness and moral integrity. For example, maintaining a clean environment is essential for health and well-being.
  • deveṣv: This means "among the gods." It signifies the divine or spiritual aspect of existence. In our lives, we often seek guidance or inspiration from higher powers or ideals, such as love, compassion, and wisdom.
  • arpitā: This translates to "offered" or "dedicated." In practical terms, this can relate to the act of giving, whether it be time, resources, or love to others. For instance, volunteering for a cause is a way of offering oneself to the community.
  • hotrā: This refers to the "priest" or "one who invokes." In modern life, this can be seen in leaders or mentors who inspire and guide others, much like a teacher or a community leader.
  • marutsu: This word means "in the winds" or "in the atmosphere." It can symbolize the unseen forces that influence our lives, such as thoughts, emotions, and energies that we cannot physically see but can feel.
  • bhāratī: This refers to "speech" or "knowledge." In today's world, effective communication and the sharing of knowledge are crucial for personal and professional growth. For example, public speaking or writing can be powerful tools for spreading ideas.
  • iḻā: This word signifies "prosperity" or "wealth." In a real-life context, it can refer to both material wealth and the richness of experiences and relationships that contribute to a fulfilling life.
  • sarasvatī: This is the name of the goddess of knowledge, music, and arts. In our lives, we can seek inspiration from creativity and learning, whether through education, art, or music, to enrich our existence.
  • mahī: This translates to "earth" or "the great." It symbolizes stability and grounding. In practical terms, it can refer to our connection to nature and the importance of environmental stewardship.
  • barhiḥ: This means "sacred grass" or "the offerings." It represents the rituals and traditions that connect us to our heritage and culture. In real life, participating in cultural practices can foster a sense of belonging and identity.
  • sīdantu: This translates to "may they sit" or "may they dwell." It implies a sense of permanence and stability. In our lives, creating a safe and nurturing environment for ourselves and others is essential for growth and well-being.
  • yajñiyāḥ: This refers to "sacrificial" or "related to offerings." It signifies the importance of selflessness and the act of giving for the greater good. In real life, acts of kindness and charity can create a positive impact on society.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the significance of purity, dedication, and the divine in our lives. It encourages us to offer ourselves to the greater good, seek knowledge, and maintain a connection with nature and our cultural roots. In real-life scenarios, these principles can guide us in our personal and communal endeavors, fostering a sense of purpose and harmony in our interactions with others and the world around us.
