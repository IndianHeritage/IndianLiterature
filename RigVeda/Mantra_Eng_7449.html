उत त्यं वीरं धनसाम ऋजीषिणं दूरे चित संतम अवसे हवामहे |यस्य स्वादिष्टा सुमतिः पितुर यथा मा नो वियौष्टं सख्या मुमोचतम् ||
uta tyaṁ vīraṁ dhanasām ṛjīṣiṇaṁ dūre cit santam avase havāmahe | yasya svādiṣṭhā sumatiḥ pitur yathā mā no vi yauṣṭaṁ sakhyā mumocatam ||
  • Translation: "We invoke that hero, the one who is rich in wealth, who is righteous and far away; may he come to us. Whose good understanding is like that of a father, may he free us from all obstacles and grant us friendship."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • uta: a particle used to indicate a connection or addition, often translated as "and" or "also."
    • tyaṁ: refers to "that" or "that one," indicating a specific entity.
    • vīraṁ: means "hero" or "brave one," symbolizing strength and valor.
    • dhanasām: translates to "rich in wealth," indicating abundance and prosperity.
    • ṛjīṣiṇaṁ: means "righteous" or "virtuous," emphasizing moral integrity.
    • dūre: means "far away," suggesting distance or separation.
    • cit: translates to "thought" or "intellect," indicating wisdom.
    • santam: means "peaceful" or "calm," referring to a state of tranquility.
    • avase: means "to come" or "to arrive," indicating a call for presence.
    • havāmahe: translates to "we invoke" or "we call upon," a plea for divine assistance.
    • yasya: means "whose," indicating possession or relation.
    • svādiṣṭhā: translates to "good understanding" or "wisdom," referring to sound judgment.
    • sumatiḥ: means "good thought" or "intellect," emphasizing positive mental faculties.
    • pitur: means "of the father," indicating a paternal figure or authority.
    • yathā: translates to "as" or "like," indicating a comparison.
    • mā: means "may not," a negation or plea for avoidance.
    • no: means "us," indicating the speaker's group.
    • vi yauṣṭaṁ: translates to "obstacles" or "hindrances," referring to challenges faced.
    • sakhyā: means "friendship," indicating a bond of camaraderie.
    • mumocatam: translates to "may he free" or "may he liberate," a plea for release from difficulties.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In times of personal struggle, individuals often seek guidance from mentors or wise figures who embody the qualities of the "hero" mentioned in the mantra. For instance, a student may look up to a teacher who provides not only academic support but also moral guidance.
    • In a corporate setting, a leader who is both wealthy in resources and righteous in decision-making can inspire their team to overcome challenges, fostering a sense of unity and purpose.
    • During community gatherings, invoking the spirit of friendship and understanding can help resolve conflicts and build stronger relationships among members.
  • Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of invoking wisdom, strength, and friendship in overcoming life's challenges. It serves as a reminder that seeking guidance from virtuous and understanding figures can lead to personal and communal growth. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as education, leadership, and community building, where the qualities of righteousness and friendship are essential for success and harmony.
