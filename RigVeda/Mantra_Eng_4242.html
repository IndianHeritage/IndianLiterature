इरावतीर वरुण धेनवो वाम मधुमद वां सिन्धवो मित्र दुहरे |त्रयस् तस्थुर वृषभासस तिसृणां धिषणानां रेतोधा विं द्युमन्तः ||
irāvatīr varuṇa dhenavo vām madhumad vāṁ sindhavo mitra duhre | trayas tasthur vṛṣabhāsas tisṛṇāṁ dhiṣaṇānāṁ retodhā vi dyumantaḥ ||
  • irāvatīr: This term refers to the "flowing" or "moving" aspect, often associated with rivers or water bodies. In real life, rivers are vital for agriculture, transportation, and sustaining ecosystems.
  • varuṇa: Varuṇa is the deity of water and the celestial ocean, representing the moral order and cosmic law. In contemporary terms, this can be likened to the importance of maintaining ecological balance and respecting natural laws.
  • dhenavo: This word means "cows" or "nourishing," symbolizing wealth and sustenance. In modern agriculture, cows are essential for dairy production, which is a significant part of many economies.
  • vām: This translates to "your" or "of you," indicating a personal connection or possession. It emphasizes the relationship between humans and nature, reminding us of our responsibility towards the environment.
  • madhumad: Meaning "sweet" or "honey-like," this term can represent the sweetness of life and the rewards of hard work. In everyday life, this can be seen in the joy derived from achieving personal goals.
  • vāṁ: Similar to "vām," it signifies possession or connection, reinforcing the theme of interconnectedness.
  • sindhavo: This refers to "rivers" or "ocean," highlighting the importance of water bodies in sustaining life. In real life, rivers are crucial for irrigation, drinking water, and biodiversity.
  • mitra: Mitra is the deity of friendship and alliances, symbolizing the importance of relationships. In modern society, building strong relationships is essential for personal and professional success.
  • duhre: This term means "to flow" or "to move," again emphasizing the dynamic nature of life and the importance of adaptability.
  • trayas: This means "three," which can symbolize balance and harmony in various aspects of life, such as mind, body, and spirit.
  • tasthur: This term refers to "those who are strong" or "powerful," indicating the importance of strength in overcoming challenges.
  • vṛṣabhāsas: This translates to "bulls" or "strength," symbolizing power and fertility. In agriculture, bulls are often used for plowing fields, representing hard work and productivity.
  • tisṛṇāṁ: Meaning "threefold" or "triple," this can represent the triad of creation, preservation, and destruction, which is a fundamental concept in many philosophies.
  • dhiṣaṇānāṁ: This refers to "the wise" or "those who know," emphasizing the importance of knowledge and wisdom in making informed decisions.
  • retodhā: This term means "the source of energy" or "vitality," highlighting the importance of energy in sustaining life and progress.
  • vi: This is a preposition meaning "in" or "within," indicating the presence of something.
  • dyumantaḥ: This translates to "shining" or "radiant," symbolizing enlightenment and the pursuit of knowledge.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the interconnectedness of nature, the importance of water, and the relationships we build in life. It serves as a reminder of our responsibilities towards the environment and the significance of strength, wisdom, and adaptability in overcoming life's challenges. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire individuals to cultivate sustainable practices, foster meaningful relationships, and seek knowledge and enlightenment in their personal and professional lives.
