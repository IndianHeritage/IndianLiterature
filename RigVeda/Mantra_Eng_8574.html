वृष्टिं नो अर्ष दिव्यां जिगत्नुम इळावतीं शङ्गयीं जीरदात्नुम् |स्तुकेव वीत धन्वा विचिन्वान बन्धूम् इमां अवराम् इन्दो वायून् ||
vṛṣṭiṁ no arṣa divyāṁ jigatnum iḻāvatīṁ śaṁgayīṁ jīradānum | stukeva vītā dhanvā vicinvan bandhūm̐r imām̐ avarām̐ indo vāyūn ||
  • vṛṣṭiṁ: This word translates to "rain" or "precipitation." In a real-life context, rain is essential for agriculture, replenishing water sources, and sustaining life.
  • no: This means "for us" or "to us," indicating a request or a plea directed towards a higher power.
  • arṣa: This can be interpreted as "divine" or "sacred," suggesting that the rain being requested is not just any rain, but one that is blessed or auspicious.
  • divyāṁ: Meaning "heavenly" or "celestial," this word emphasizes the divine nature of the rain being sought.
  • jigatnum: This term can be understood as "to be strong" or "to be powerful," indicating the strength that the rain brings to the earth.
  • iḻāvatīṁ: This word refers to "prosperity" or "wealth," suggesting that the rain will lead to abundance and fertility.
  • śaṁgayīṁ: This can be interpreted as "harmonious" or "peaceful," indicating that the rain will bring about a sense of balance and tranquility.
  • jīradānum: This term means "nourishing" or "life-giving," highlighting the essential role of rain in sustaining life.
  • stukeva: This translates to "like a drop," which can symbolize the small yet significant contributions that lead to greater outcomes.
  • vītā: Meaning "freed" or "liberated," this word suggests a release from constraints, possibly referring to the liberation of the earth through rain.
  • dhanvā: This can be interpreted as "bow" or "archer," symbolizing strength and precision in the delivery of rain.
  • vicinvan: This means "searching" or "seeking," indicating an active pursuit of the desired rain.
  • bandhūm̐r: This term refers to "friends" or "allies," suggesting a communal aspect to the request for rain.
  • imām̐: Meaning "this," it points to the specific context or situation being addressed.
  • avarām̐: This can be interpreted as "lower" or "inferior," possibly referring to the earth or the state of being before the rain.
  • indo: This word means "Indra," the king of gods in Vedic tradition, who is often associated with rain and thunderstorms.
  • vāyūn: This translates to "winds," indicating the natural elements that accompany rain and play a role in its distribution.

  • Significance: This mantra is a prayer for divine rain, emphasizing the importance of rain in agriculture, ecology, and sustenance. It reflects a deep understanding of the interconnectedness of nature and the need for harmony between human beings and the environment.
  • Real-life Application: In agricultural communities, such prayers are often recited during droughts or before the planting season to invoke favorable weather conditions. The mantra serves as a reminder of the reliance on natural elements for survival and prosperity.

  • Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of seeking divine intervention for rain, highlighting its critical role in nurturing life and promoting prosperity. It serves as a spiritual and practical guide for communities to connect with nature and seek balance in their lives.
