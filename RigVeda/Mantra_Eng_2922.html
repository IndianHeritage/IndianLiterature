तिष्ठा सु कम मघवन मा परा गाह सोमस्य नु त्वा सुषुतस्य यक्षि |पितुर न पुत्रः सिचम् आ रभे त इन्द्र स्वादिष्टया गिरा शचीवः ||
tiṣṭhā su kam maghavan mā parā gāḥ somasya nu tvā suṣutasya yakṣi | pitur na putraḥ sicam ā rabhe ta indra svādiṣṭhayā girā śacīvaḥ ||
  • tiṣṭhā: This word means "stand" or "be firm." It signifies stability and strength in one's actions and thoughts.
  • su: This translates to "good" or "well," indicating positivity and auspiciousness.
  • kam: This means "who" or "what," often used to inquire or express curiosity.
  • maghavan: Referring to "the bountiful one," it is often associated with the deity Indra, who represents strength and abundance.
  • mā: This means "not," used to negate or express caution.
  • parā: This translates to "beyond" or "further," suggesting a sense of transcendence or reaching beyond limits.
  • gāḥ: This means "songs" or "praises," often referring to hymns or expressions of devotion.
  • somasya: This refers to "of Soma," a sacred drink in Vedic rituals, symbolizing immortality and divine inspiration.
  • nu: This is an expression of emphasis, often translated as "indeed" or "certainly."
  • tvā: This means "you," addressing the divine or a higher power directly.
  • suṣutasya: This translates to "of the well-spoken" or "of good speech," indicating the importance of communication and expression.
  • yakṣi: This means "to bestow" or "to grant," often referring to the act of giving or sharing blessings.
  • pitur: This means "of the father," indicating lineage, heritage, and the importance of familial bonds.
  • na: This means "not," used for negation.
  • putraḥ: This translates to "son," representing the continuation of lineage and the responsibilities that come with it.
  • sicam: This means "to sprinkle" or "to pour," often used in the context of blessings or offerings.
  • ā: This means "from" or "out of," indicating a source or origin.
  • rabhe: This means "I invoke" or "I call upon," expressing a desire to connect with the divine.
  • ta: This means "that," often used to refer to something specific.
  • indra: Referring to the king of gods, symbolizing power, strength, and protection.
  • svādiṣṭhayā: This translates to "by the best of offerings," indicating the importance of sincerity and quality in one's offerings.
  • girā: This means "by speech" or "through words," emphasizing the power of communication.
  • śacīvaḥ: This translates to "of the powerful" or "of the mighty," often referring to the strength and capability of the divine.

In this mantra, the speaker calls upon the divine, particularly Indra, to stand firm and bestow blessings. The invocation emphasizes the importance of good speech, familial bonds, and the act of giving. It reflects a desire for strength, abundance, and the continuation of lineage, while also highlighting the significance of communication in achieving one's goals.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • In personal development, one can invoke the strength to stand firm in their beliefs and actions.
  • In family dynamics, it emphasizes the importance of nurturing relationships and honoring one's heritage.
  • In professional settings, it highlights the value of effective communication and the power of well-spoken words in influencing others.
  • In spiritual practices, it serves as a reminder to seek blessings and abundance through sincere offerings and intentions.

In conclusion, this mantra signifies the importance of strength, communication, and the blessings of the divine in our lives. It encourages individuals to stand firm in their pursuits, honor their familial ties, and communicate effectively to achieve their goals. By invoking these principles, one can navigate life's challenges with resilience and grace.
