न यस्य ते शवसान सख्यम आनाṁश मार्त्यः |नकिः शवांसी ते नशत ||
na yasya te śavasāna sakhyam ānaṁśa martyaḥ | nakiḥ śavāṁsi te naśat ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • na - not
    • yasya - whose
    • te - your
    • śavasāna - of the dead
    • sakhyam - friendship
    • ānaṁśa - part or portion
    • martyaḥ - mortal beings
    • nakiḥ - who
    • śavāṁsi - dead bodies
    • te - your
    • naśat - perish or decay
  • Interpretation:
    • This mantra speaks about the relationship between the living and the dead.
    • It suggests that there is no friendship or connection with the dead, emphasizing the transient nature of life.
    • The phrase "whose your dead" indicates a reflection on mortality and the inevitability of death.
    • It highlights that the dead do not have a part in the lives of the living, as they have perished.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In many cultures, there is a ritual of remembering the dead, such as the Day of the Dead in Mexico, which acknowledges the connection but also recognizes that the deceased cannot partake in the living world.
    • In modern life, we often see memorials and tributes to those who have passed, which serve as a reminder of their absence and the importance of cherishing life.
    • In personal experiences, people may feel a sense of loss and reflect on their relationships with those who have died, realizing that while memories remain, the physical connection is lost.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra serves as a reminder of the impermanence of life and the inevitability of death.
    • It encourages individuals to value their relationships and the time they have with loved ones, as life is fleeting.
    • In a broader context, it can be applied to the understanding of life cycles, the importance of legacy, and the need to live meaningfully.
    • Ultimately, it teaches us to embrace life while acknowledging the reality of death, fostering a deeper appreciation for our existence and connections.
