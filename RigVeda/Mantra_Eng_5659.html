प्र बाहवाऽसिसृतं जीवसे न आ नो गव्यूतिमुक्षतं घृतेन |आ नो जनेश्रवयतं युवाना श्रुतं मे मित्रावरुणा हवेमाऽ ||
pra bāhavā sisṛtaṁ jīvase na ā no gavyūtim ukṣataṁ ghṛtena | ā no jane śravayataṁ yuvānā śrutam me mitrāvaruṇā havemā ||
  • pra bāhavā sisṛtaṁ: This phrase can be translated as "may the powerful ones be born." Here, "bāhavā" refers to strength or power, and "sisṛtaṁ" means to be born or created. In real life, this can be interpreted as a call for the emergence of strong and capable individuals in society, such as leaders, innovators, and protectors.
  • jīvase na ā no gavyūtim: This translates to "for the sake of life, may we not be deprived of wealth." The term "jīvase" refers to life, while "gavyūtim" signifies wealth or prosperity. This line emphasizes the importance of sustaining life through the acquisition of resources. In practical terms, it highlights the need for economic stability and access to resources for a fulfilling life.
  • ukṣataṁ ghṛtena: This means "may we be nourished with ghee." "Ukṣataṁ" refers to nourishment or sustenance, and "ghṛtena" is ghee, a symbol of purity and richness in Vedic culture. This can be related to the idea of nurturing oneself with quality resources, whether they be food, knowledge, or relationships, to foster growth and well-being.
  • ā no jane śravayataṁ: This translates to "may we hear the voices of the people." "Jane" refers to people or society, and "śravayataṁ" means to hear or listen. This line underscores the importance of community and the need to be attuned to the voices and needs of others. In a real-world context, it can be seen in the importance of civic engagement and listening to diverse perspectives in decision-making processes.
  • yuvānā śrutam me: This means "may the young ones hear my call." "Yuvānā" refers to the youth, and "śrutam" means to hear or listen. This line emphasizes the role of the younger generation in carrying forward the wisdom and values of society. In contemporary terms, it can be related to mentorship and the importance of guiding youth towards positive contributions to society.
  • mitrāvaruṇā havemā: This translates to "we invoke the friendship of Mitra and Varuna." "Mitra" and "Varuna" are deities representing friendship, harmony, and cosmic order. This invocation signifies the importance of maintaining harmonious relationships and ethical conduct in society. In real life, it can be seen in the significance of diplomacy, cooperation, and mutual respect among individuals and nations.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a holistic vision for life, emphasizing the emergence of strong individuals, the importance of resources for sustenance, the need for community engagement, the role of youth in society, and the significance of harmonious relationships. Its application in real life can be seen in various contexts, such as leadership, economic stability, social responsibility, mentorship, and diplomacy. By embodying these principles, individuals and communities can work towards a more prosperous and harmonious existence.
