बरहिषदः पितर ऊत्य आर्वाग इमा वो हव्याः चक्रिमा जुषध्वम् | ता आगतावसाः शान्तमेनाथा नः शं योऽरपो दधात ||
barhiṣadaḥ pitara ūty a1rvāg imā vo havyā cakṛmā juṣadhvam | ta ā gatāvasā śaṁtamenāthā naḥ śaṁ yor arapo dadhāta ||
  • barhiṣadaḥ: This term refers to the sacred grass used in Vedic rituals, symbolizing purity and the connection between the earthly and the divine.
  • pitara: This word means "fathers" or "ancestors," highlighting the importance of lineage and the respect for those who came before us.
  • ūty: This translates to "offering" or "sacrifice," indicating the act of giving back to the divine and honoring the ancestors.
  • a1rvāg: This term can be interpreted as "with a good mind" or "with a pure heart," emphasizing the intention behind the offerings.
  • imā: Meaning "these," it refers to the offerings being made in the present moment.
  • vo: This means "to you," addressing the ancestors and deities receiving the offerings.
  • havyā: This word signifies "that which is offered," particularly in the context of sacred offerings in rituals.
  • cakṛmā: This translates to "we have made" or "we have prepared," indicating the action of creating the offerings.
  • juṣadhvam: This means "may you enjoy," expressing a wish for the ancestors and deities to accept and appreciate the offerings.
  • ta: This word means "they," referring to the ancestors and deities once again.
  • ā: This can be interpreted as "come" or "arrive," inviting the divine presence to partake in the offerings.
  • gatāvasā: This term means "having come" or "arrived," indicating the presence of the divine or ancestors in the ritual space.
  • śaṁtamenā: This translates to "with peace" or "with tranquility," suggesting a peaceful state of being during the ritual.
  • naḥ: Meaning "our," it signifies a collective identity and shared experience among the participants of the ritual.
  • śaṁ: This word means "peace" or "well-being," emphasizing the desire for harmony and prosperity.
  • yor: This translates to "of you," referring to the ancestors and deities once again.
  • arapo: This term can be interpreted as "may you grant" or "may you bestow," indicating a request for blessings.
  • dadhāta: This means "may you give" or "may you provide," reinforcing the request for divine support and blessings.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the importance of honoring our ancestors and the divine through offerings made with pure intentions. It serves as a reminder of our connection to those who came before us and the blessings we seek in our lives. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied during family gatherings, rituals, or ceremonies where we pay homage to our heritage and seek peace and prosperity. By acknowledging our roots and expressing gratitude, we foster a sense of community and continuity, ensuring that the wisdom and blessings of our ancestors guide us in our journey.
