स गोमघा जरीत्रे अश्वचन्द्रा वाजश्रवसो अधि धेहि पृक्षः |पीपिहीषः सुदुग्धाम इन्द्र धेनुम भरद्वाजेषु सुरुचो रुच्याः ||
sa gomaghā jaritre aśvaścandrā vājaśravaso adhi dhehi pṛkṣaḥ | pīpihīṣaḥ sudughām indra dhenum bharadvājeṣu suruco rurucyāḥ ||
  • sa gomaghā: This phrase can be translated as "he who has abundant cattle." In ancient Vedic society, cattle were a symbol of wealth and prosperity. For example, a farmer with many cows could provide for his family and contribute to the community.
  • jaritre: This word means "in the womb" or "in the mother." It signifies the nurturing aspect of life, emphasizing the importance of care and protection during the early stages of existence. In real life, this can relate to the care given to children and the environment, ensuring they grow up healthy and strong.
  • aśvaścandrā: This term can be interpreted as "the moon of the horses." Horses were vital for transportation and warfare in Vedic times. The moon symbolizes calmness and guidance, suggesting that one should seek guidance in their endeavors, much like how horses guided ancient travelers.
  • vājaśravaso: This means "of the one who brings victory." It refers to the idea of success and triumph in various aspects of life, whether in personal goals or community achievements. For instance, a student who works hard and achieves good grades can be seen as embodying this quality.
  • adhi dhehi pṛkṣaḥ: This phrase translates to "bestow upon us the strength." It is a plea for divine support and empowerment. In modern life, this can be related to seeking motivation and strength from mentors or spiritual practices to overcome challenges.
  • pīpihīṣaḥ: This word means "the one who desires." It reflects the human condition of longing for success, happiness, and fulfillment. For example, an entrepreneur who dreams of building a successful business embodies this desire.
  • sudughām: This translates to "the one who is difficult to attain." It signifies the challenges one faces in life. For instance, achieving a high level of education or mastering a skill often requires significant effort and perseverance.
  • indra: Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, representing power and authority. In real life, this can symbolize leadership qualities that inspire others, such as a community leader who rallies people for a common cause.
  • dhenum: This means "the cow" and symbolizes nourishment and sustenance. In contemporary terms, it can represent the importance of food security and sustainable farming practices to feed the growing population.
  • bharadvājeṣu: This refers to the Bharadvaja clan, known for their wisdom and knowledge. It emphasizes the value of education and learning in society. For example, teachers and scholars play a crucial role in shaping future generations.
  • surucho: This means "beautiful" or "pleasant." It signifies the importance of aesthetics and harmony in life. In real life, this can relate to creating a pleasant living environment or fostering positive relationships.
  • rurucyāḥ: This term can be interpreted as "the one who shines." It represents excellence and brilliance in one's endeavors. For instance, an artist whose work captivates audiences embodies this shining quality.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates various aspects of life, including prosperity, nurturing, guidance, strength, desire, challenges, leadership, nourishment, wisdom, beauty, and excellence. Its significance lies in the holistic approach it advocates for living a fulfilling life. In real-life scenarios, individuals can apply these principles by striving for personal and communal growth, seeking guidance, nurturing relationships, and pursuing excellence in their endeavors. This mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of these values and their importance in achieving a balanced and prosperous life.
