इरावतī धेनुमती हि भूतं सूयवसिनी मनुषे दशस्याऽ। व्य अस्तभ्ना रोडसी विष्णव एते दाधर्थ पृथिवीं अभितो मयूखैः ॥
irāvatī dhenumatī hi bhūtaṁ sūyavasinī manuṣe daśasyā | vy astabhnā rodasī viṣṇav ete dādhartha pṛthivīm abhito mayūkhaiḥ ||
  • Mantra: "irāvatī dhenumatī hi bhūtaṁ sūyavasinī manuṣe daśasyā | vy astabhnā rodasī viṣṇav ete dādhartha pṛthivīm abhito mayūkhaiḥ ||"
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • irāvatī: Refers to the river or flowing water, symbolizing nourishment and life.
    • dhenumatī: Means 'rich in cows' or 'nourishing', often associated with abundance and prosperity.
    • hi: A particle that emphasizes the statement, akin to 'indeed' or 'certainly'.
    • bhūtaṁ: Refers to beings or existence, indicating the essence of life.
    • sūyavasinī: Means 'one who is capable of producing', often linked to creativity and productivity.
    • manuṣe: Refers to humans or mankind, emphasizing the human aspect of the mantra.
    • daśasyā: Means 'of ten', possibly referring to completeness or wholeness.
    • vy: A prefix that can indicate 'to' or 'towards'.
    • astabhnā: Means 'to support' or 'to uphold', indicating stability.
    • rodasī: Refers to the heavens or the sky, symbolizing the divine or spiritual realm.
    • viṣṇav: Pertaining to Vishnu, the preserver in Hindu mythology, representing protection and sustenance.
    • ete: Means 'these', referring to the elements or forces being discussed.
    • dādhartha: Means 'to uphold' or 'to maintain', indicating a sense of responsibility.
    • pṛthivīm: Refers to the earth, symbolizing the physical realm and material existence.
    • abhito: Means 'around' or 'surrounding', indicating inclusiveness.
    • mayūkhaiḥ: Refers to 'rays' or 'light', symbolizing knowledge and enlightenment.
  • Explanation:
    • This mantra speaks of the interconnectedness of life, nature, and the divine.
    • The river (irāvatī) symbolizes the flow of life and nourishment, much like how rivers provide water for agriculture, which is essential for human survival.
    • The mention of cows (dhenumatī) signifies wealth and sustenance, as cows are often seen as a source of milk and agricultural support in many cultures.
    • By referring to humans (manuṣe), the mantra emphasizes the role of humanity in nurturing and sustaining life.
    • The concept of completeness (daśasyā) suggests that all aspects of life are interconnected and must be balanced.
    • The support (astabhnā) provided by the heavens (rodasī) and the divine (viṣṇav) indicates that there is a higher power that sustains and protects life.
    • The earth (pṛthivīm) is portrayed as a nurturing ground, surrounded by light (mayūkhaiḥ), which can be interpreted as the knowledge and wisdom that guide human actions.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra highlights the significance of harmony between nature, humanity, and the divine.
    • In real life, it can be applied to environmental conservation, emphasizing the need to protect our natural resources for future generations.
    • It encourages individuals to recognize their role in the ecosystem and to act responsibly towards the earth and its inhabitants.
    • Ultimately, the mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of all life and the importance of nurturing both the physical and spiritual aspects of existence.
