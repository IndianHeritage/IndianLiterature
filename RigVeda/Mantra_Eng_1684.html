यद धविष्यं ऋतुशो देवयानं त्रिर मानवाः पर्य अश्वं नयन्ति |अत्र पूषणः प्रथमो भाग एति यज्ञं देवभ्यः प्रतिवेदयन्न अजः ||
yad dhaviṣyam ṛtuśo devayānaṁ trir mānuṣāḥ pary aśvaṁ nayanti | atrā pūṣṇaḥ prathamo bhāga eti yajñaṁ devebhyaḥ prativedayann ajaḥ ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • yad: whatever
    • dhaviṣyam: is to be achieved
    • ṛtuśo: of the seasons
    • devayānaṁ: of the divine beings
    • trir: three times
    • mānuṣāḥ: human beings
    • pary: around
    • aśvaṁ: the horse
    • nayanti: lead
    • atrā: here
    • pūṣṇaḥ: the nourisher
    • prathamo: first
    • bhāga: portion
    • eti: goes
    • yajñaṁ: sacrifice
    • devebhyaḥ: to the gods
    • prativedayann: announcing
    • ajaḥ: the unborn
  • Interpretation:
    • This mantra speaks about the connection between human actions and divine forces.
    • The phrase "yad dhaviṣyam" suggests that whatever is to be achieved is influenced by the divine.
    • The reference to "ṛtuśo devayānaṁ" indicates that the seasons and their cycles are governed by divine beings.
    • The mention of "trir mānuṣāḥ pary aśvaṁ nayanti" implies that humans, through their actions, lead the divine forces, symbolized by the horse, which is often associated with power and energy.
    • "Atrā pūṣṇaḥ prathamo bhāga eti" signifies that the nourisher (Pūṣṇa) is the first to partake in the offerings made to the gods, emphasizing the importance of nourishment in both physical and spiritual realms.
    • The term "yajñaṁ devebhyaḥ prativedayann ajaḥ" highlights the act of sacrifice and the communication of offerings to the divine, suggesting that the unborn (ajaḥ) represents the eternal and unchanging aspect of divinity.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In agriculture, the changing seasons dictate planting and harvesting times. Farmers often perform rituals to honor the divine for a bountiful harvest, reflecting the mantra's theme of human actions leading to divine favor.
    • In modern life, individuals may engage in meditation or mindfulness practices to connect with their inner selves and the universe, paralleling the idea of leading divine energies through conscious actions.
    • Community rituals, such as festivals or ceremonies, serve as a way for people to come together, offer gratitude, and seek blessings, embodying the essence of yajña (sacrifice) mentioned in the mantra.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra emphasizes the interconnectedness of human actions and divine forces, illustrating how our intentions and sacrifices can influence the world around us.
    • It serves as a reminder of the importance of aligning our actions with the rhythms of nature and the divine, fostering a sense of responsibility and reverence.
    • In real life, this mantra encourages individuals to engage in practices that honor the divine, whether through rituals, mindfulness, or community involvement, ultimately leading to a more harmonious existence.
