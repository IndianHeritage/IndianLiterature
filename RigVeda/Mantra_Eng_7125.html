यत इन्द्र भयामहे ततो नो अभयं कृधि |मघवञ्च चग्धि तव तन् न ऊतिभिर विद्विषो विद्मृधो जहि ||
yata indra bhayāmahe tato no abhayaṁ kṛdhi | maghavañ chagdhi tava tan na ūtibhir vi dviṣo vi mṛdho jahi ||
  • Translation: "As we invoke Indra, may He grant us fearlessness; O Maghavan, protect us from those who wish us harm."

  • Word Breakdown:
    • Yata: As we invoke or call upon.
    • Indra: The king of gods in Vedic tradition, symbolizing strength and power.
    • Bhaya: Fear or danger.
    • Mahé: We invoke or call upon.
    • Tato: Therefore or hence.
    • No: Us.
    • Abhayaṁ: Fearlessness or safety.
    • Kṛdhi: Grant or bestow.
    • Maghavañ: O Maghavan, another name for Indra, meaning the generous one.
    • Chagdhi: Protect or shield.
    • Tava: Your.
    • Tan: That or this.
    • Na: Not.
    • Ūtibhir: By those who wish harm.
    • Vi: From or away from.
    • Dviṣo: Enemies or those who harbor ill will.
    • Vi: Again, from or away from.
    • Mṛdho: Harm or injury.
    • Jahi: Destroy or overcome.

  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In times of personal crisis, such as facing a difficult situation at work or in relationships, individuals often seek strength and protection from higher powers or their own inner resilience.
    • Communities facing external threats, such as natural disasters or social unrest, may come together in prayer or collective action, invoking a sense of unity and fearlessness.
    • In modern contexts, this mantra can be seen as a call for courage in the face of adversity, whether it be in personal challenges, societal issues, or global crises.

  • Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of invoking divine strength and protection against fear and harm. It serves as a reminder that in times of distress, seeking help from a higher power or collective strength can lead to fearlessness and resilience. This is applicable in various real-life scenarios, from personal struggles to communal challenges, highlighting the universal need for safety and courage in the face of adversity.
