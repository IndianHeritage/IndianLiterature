शयुः परस्ताद अध नु द्विमाताबन्धनश्चरति वत्स एकः |मित्रस्य ता वरुणस्य व्रतानि महद देवानाम असुरत्वम् एकम् ||
śayuḥ parastād adha nu dvimātābandhanaś carati vatsa ekaḥ | mitrasya tā varuṇasya vratāni mahad devānām asuratvam ekam ||
  • śayuḥ parastād adha nu dvimātābandhanaś carati vatsa ekaḥ
    • śayuḥ - This word refers to "life" or "existence". It signifies the essence of being.
    • parastād - Meaning "beyond" or "above\ it indicates a higher state or realm.
    • adha - This translates to "below" or "beneath\ suggesting a grounding or foundational aspect.
    • nu - A particle often used for emphasis, akin to "indeed" or "truly".
    • dvimātābandhanaś - This compound word can be broken down into "dvi" (two), "mātā" (mothers), and "bandhanaś" (bonds or ties). It suggests the duality of relationships or connections.
    • carati - Meaning "moves" or "travels\ it indicates the dynamic nature of life.
    • vatsa - Translated as "calf" or "young one\ it symbolizes innocence and the nurturing aspect of life.
    • ekaḥ - Meaning "one\ it emphasizes unity amidst diversity.
  • mitrasya tā varuṇasya vratāni mahad devānām asuratvam ekam
    • mitrasya - Referring to "Mitra\ the deity of friendship and harmony, it signifies the importance of relationships.
    • tā - This word means "those" or "that\ pointing to the qualities or attributes being discussed.
    • varuṇasya - Referring to "Varuna\ the god of cosmic order and water, it represents the moral and ethical dimensions of life.
    • vratāni - Meaning "vows" or "commitments\ it highlights the significance of promises and duties.
    • mahad - Translated as "great" or "mighty\ it indicates the vastness of divine qualities.
    • devānām - Meaning "of the gods\ it signifies the divine nature of the principles being discussed.
    • asuratvam - This term can be understood as "the quality of being non-divine" or "the nature of the non-gods\ suggesting a contrast between divine and earthly realms.
    • ekam - Again meaning "one\ it emphasizes unity in diversity, suggesting that despite differences, there is a singular essence that connects all.

Conclusion:

This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the duality of existence, the importance of relationships, and the commitment to ethical living. It highlights how life is a journey that traverses both the higher and lower realms, emphasizing the bonds we share with others, akin to the nurturing relationship between a mother and her child. In real life, this can be seen in the way we navigate our relationships, balancing personal aspirations with our duties towards family and society.

The significance of this mantra lies in its call for unity and harmony amidst diversity. It encourages individuals to recognize the interconnectedness of all beings and to uphold their commitments to one another, fostering a sense of community and shared responsibility. In today's world, where divisions often arise, this mantra serves as a reminder of the importance of empathy, cooperation, and the pursuit of a greater good.
