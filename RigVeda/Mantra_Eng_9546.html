शश्वत्तमम् ईळते दूत्याय हविष्मन्तो मनुष्यासो अग्निम् | वहिष्टैः अश्वैः सुवृतā रथेनā देवान् वक्षि न षदेह होतā ||
śaśvattamam īḻate dūtyāya haviṣmanto manuṣyāso agnim | vahiṣṭhair aśvaiḥ suvṛtā rathenā devān vakṣi ni ṣadeha hotā ||
  • śaśvattamam: This term can be translated as "the most eternal" or "the most enduring." It signifies something that is everlasting and unchanging.
  • īḻate: This word means "shines" or "radiates." It indicates a sense of brightness or illumination.
  • dūtyāya: This refers to "for the messenger" or "for the envoy." It suggests a connection to communication or the transmission of messages.
  • haviṣmanto: This term relates to "those who offer oblations" or "the sacrificial offerings." It emphasizes the importance of rituals and offerings in Vedic culture.
  • manuṣyāso: This means "of humans" or "of mankind." It highlights the human aspect of the mantra.
  • agnim: This word translates to "fire," which is a central element in Vedic rituals, symbolizing purity, transformation, and divine presence.
  • vahiṣṭhair: This means "with the best" or "with the most excellent." It denotes a sense of superiority or excellence.
  • aśvaiḥ: This term refers to "horses," which are often associated with strength, speed, and nobility in Vedic literature.
  • suvṛtā: This means "well-formed" or "well-structured." It suggests a sense of order and harmony.
  • rathenā: This translates to "by the chariot." The chariot symbolizes movement, progress, and the journey of life.
  • devān: This means "the gods." It signifies the divine beings that are revered in Vedic traditions.
  • vakṣi: This term means "you will speak" or "you will declare." It indicates communication and the expression of intentions.
  • ni: This is a preposition that can mean "in" or "within," indicating a sense of presence or location.
  • ṣadeha: This means "in this body" or "in this form." It emphasizes the physical existence of beings.
  • hotā: This term refers to "the priest" or "the one who offers sacrifices." It highlights the role of the priest in Vedic rituals.

The mantra can be interpreted as a call to the divine, emphasizing the eternal nature of the divine fire (Agni) and the importance of offerings made by humans. It suggests that through the best of intentions and actions, one can communicate with the divine and seek blessings for progress and harmony in life.


In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as:

  • Rituals and Offerings: It can be recited during rituals to invoke blessings and express gratitude for the eternal presence of the divine.
  • Personal Growth: The emphasis on excellence and well-structured actions can inspire individuals to strive for personal development and improvement.
  • Community and Communication: The idea of being a messenger can encourage individuals to communicate effectively and foster connections within their communities.

In conclusion, this mantra signifies the importance of recognizing the eternal nature of the divine, the role of human actions in connecting with the divine, and the pursuit of excellence in all endeavors. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life, the divine, and the importance of rituals in maintaining that connection.
