अस्येदु मातुः सवनेषु सद्यो महः पितुम पपिवाञ्चार्व अन्ना |मुषायद विष्णुः पचतं सहीयान विध्यद वराहं तिरो अद्रिम अस्ताः ||
asyed u mātuḥ savaneṣu sadyo mahaḥ pitum papivāñ cārv annā | muṣāyad viṣṇuḥ pacataṁ sahīyān vidhyad varāhaṁ tiro adrim astā ||
  • Mantra: "asyed u mātuḥ savaneṣu sadyo mahaḥ pitum papivāñ cārv annā | muṣāyad viṣṇuḥ pacataṁ sahīyān vidhyad varāhaṁ tiro adrim astā ||"
  • Translation: This mantra can be interpreted as a prayer for nourishment and sustenance, invoking the divine presence of Vishnu and the Earth (symbolized by the boar, Varaha) to provide food and abundance.
  • Word Breakdown:
    • asyed: This refers to 'this' or 'here', indicating a presence or a plea for immediate attention.
    • u mātuḥ: 'u' means 'and', while 'mātuḥ' refers to 'mother', symbolizing the nurturing aspect of nature.
    • savaneṣu: This word relates to 'in the offerings' or 'in the sacrifices', indicating a connection to rituals.
    • sadyo: Meaning 'immediately' or 'quickly', it emphasizes the urgency of the request.
    • mahaḥ: This translates to 'greatness' or 'magnitude', often referring to the divine or powerful forces.
    • pitum: Meaning 'father', it symbolizes the protective and providing aspect of nature.
    • papivāñ: This means 'drinking' or 'consuming', indicating the act of receiving nourishment.
    • cārv annā: 'cārv' means 'delicious' or 'tasty', while 'annā' refers to 'food', highlighting the quality of sustenance.
    • muṣāyad: This can be interpreted as 'to bring forth' or 'to produce', indicating the act of creation.
    • viṣṇuḥ: Referring to Lord Vishnu, the preserver and protector in Hindu mythology.
    • pacataṁ: Meaning 'cooking' or 'preparing', it relates to the process of making food ready for consumption.
    • sahīyān: This word means 'together' or 'in harmony', indicating a collective effort.
    • vidhyad: Meaning 'to know' or 'to understand', it emphasizes wisdom in the process of creation.
    • varāhaṁ: Referring to the boar incarnation of Vishnu, symbolizing the Earth and its resources.
    • tiro: Meaning 'to remove' or 'to take away', indicating a desire to eliminate obstacles.
    • adrim: Referring to 'the mountain' or 'the earth', symbolizing stability and support.
    • astā: Meaning 'to be' or 'to exist', indicating a state of being or presence.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In agricultural practices, farmers often pray for a bountiful harvest, invoking the divine for assistance in growing crops. This mantra reflects that sentiment, emphasizing the importance of nature's nurturing aspects.
    • During family gatherings or festivals, food plays a central role. The mantra can be recited to bless the meal, ensuring that it is nourishing and enjoyable for all, symbolizing the unity of family and the abundance provided by nature.
    • In modern contexts, this mantra can be applied to environmental conservation efforts, where individuals and communities work together to protect the Earth and its resources, recognizing the interconnectedness of all life.
  • Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its invocation of divine forces for nourishment and abundance. It highlights the relationship between humans and nature, emphasizing the need for respect and gratitude towards the Earth and its resources. In real-life scenarios, it serves as a reminder of the importance of community, sustainability, and the sacredness of food, encouraging individuals to seek harmony with the environment and each other.
