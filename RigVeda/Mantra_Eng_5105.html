इन्द्रासोमाव अहिम अपः परिष्टां हतो वृत्रम अनु वां द्यौर अमन्यत |प्रार्णांस्य ऐरयतं नदीनाम आ समुद्राणि पप्रथुः पुरूणि ||
indrāsomāv ahim apaḥ pariṣṭhāṁ hatho vṛtram anu vāṁ dyaur amanyata | prārṇāṁsy airayataṁ nadīnām ā samudrāṇi paprathuḥ purūṇi ||
  • Indrāsomāv: This term refers to the divine forces of Indra and Soma. Indra is the king of the gods and represents strength, power, and the forces of nature, while Soma symbolizes the sacred drink that grants immortality and is associated with inspiration and ecstasy.
  • Ahim: This word means "non-violence" or "harmlessness." It emphasizes the importance of peace and the avoidance of harm to others.
  • Apaḥ: This translates to "waters." In the Vedic context, waters are often seen as purifying and life-giving, representing abundance and nourishment.
  • Pariṣṭhāṁ: This means "support" or "foundation." It signifies stability and the underlying principles that hold everything together.
  • Hatho: This means "defeated" or "overcome." It indicates the triumph over obstacles or adversities.
  • Vṛtram: Vṛtra is a dragon or serpent that represents chaos and obstruction. Overcoming Vṛtra symbolizes conquering challenges and achieving victory.
  • Anu vāṁ: This phrase means "following you" or "in accordance with you," indicating a sense of alignment or harmony with divine forces.
  • Dyaur: This translates to "sky" or "heaven." It represents the vastness and the divine realm, often associated with higher consciousness.
  • Amanyata: This means "thought" or "considered." It reflects the process of contemplation and understanding.
  • Prārṇāṁsy: This refers to "breath" or "life force." It signifies vitality and the essence of life.
  • Airayataṁ: This means "to flow" or "to move." It indicates the dynamic nature of life and the importance of adaptability.
  • Nadīnām: This translates to "rivers." Rivers are often seen as symbols of life, movement, and the flow of energy.
  • Ā samudrāṇi: This means "the oceans." Oceans represent vastness, depth, and the collective unconscious, encompassing all of existence.
  • Paprathuḥ: This means "to spread" or "to expand." It signifies growth and the dissemination of knowledge or resources.
  • Purūṇi: This translates to "many" or "abundant." It reflects the richness of life and the diversity of experiences.

Conclusion: This mantra encapsulates the essence of overcoming challenges through divine support and the importance of harmony with nature and the cosmos. It emphasizes the significance of non-violence, the flow of life, and the interconnectedness of all beings. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as conflict resolution, environmental conservation, and personal growth. By embodying the principles of strength, peace, and adaptability, individuals can navigate life's challenges and contribute positively to the world around them.
