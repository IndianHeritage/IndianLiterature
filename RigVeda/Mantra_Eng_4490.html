अग्नि विश्वेभिः स्वनीक देवैर urnāvantम् प्रथमः सीद योनिम् |कुलायिनं घृतवंतं सवित्रे यज्ञं नय यजमानाय साधु ||
agne viśvebhiḥ svanīka devair ūrṇāvantam prathamaḥ sīda yonim | kulāyinaṁ ghṛtavantaṁ savitre yajñaṁ naya yajamānāya sādhu ||
  • Agne: This word refers to Agni, the fire god, who is a central figure in Vedic rituals and symbolizes energy, transformation, and purification.
  • Viśvebhiḥ: This term means "with all beings" or "universal," indicating the inclusive nature of the invocation.
  • Svanīka: This can be interpreted as "with a pleasant sound" or "melodious," suggesting harmony and auspiciousness in the offerings.
  • Devair: This means "with the gods," emphasizing the divine presence and the importance of spiritual connection in rituals.
  • Ūrṇāvantam: This word signifies "abundant" or "full of wealth," indicating the richness of the offerings and the blessings sought.
  • Prathamaḥ: This means "first," suggesting the priority of the fire in rituals and the importance of beginning with Agni.
  • Sīda: This term means "to sit" or "to reside," indicating the establishment of a sacred space for the divine presence.
  • Yonim: This refers to the "womb" or "source," symbolizing creation and the nurturing aspect of the divine.
  • Kulāyinaṁ: This word means "belonging to the family" or "of the lineage," indicating the importance of tradition and heritage in spiritual practices.
  • Ghṛtavantaṁ: This means "full of ghee," which is a sacred offering in Vedic rituals, symbolizing purity and nourishment.
  • Savitre: This refers to the sun god, Savitr, representing illumination, life, and the source of energy.
  • Yajñaṁ: This term means "sacrifice" or "offering," highlighting the act of giving and the importance of rituals in connecting with the divine.
  • Naya: This means "to lead" or "to guide," indicating the desire for divine guidance in one's actions.
  • Yajamānāya: This refers to the "sacrificer" or "the one who performs the ritual," emphasizing the role of the individual in the spiritual process.
  • Sādhu: This means "good" or "auspicious," indicating the intention behind the offerings and the desire for positive outcomes.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the significance of Agni as a central figure in Vedic rituals, representing transformation, purification, and the connection between the material and spiritual worlds. It highlights the importance of community, tradition, and the divine presence in rituals. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied to various aspects of life, such as family gatherings, community events, and personal rituals where one seeks blessings, guidance, and harmony. By invoking the divine and offering gratitude, individuals can foster a sense of connection with the universe and enhance their spiritual journey.
