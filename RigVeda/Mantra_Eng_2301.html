ऋतं देवाय कृत्न्वते सवित्र इन्द्रायाहिग्ने न रमन्त आपः |अहर-अहर यात्य अक्तुर अपां कियात्य आ प्रथमः सर्ग आसाम ||
ṛtaṁ devāya kṛṇvate savitra indrāyāhighne na ramanta āpaḥ | ahar-ahar yāty aktur apāṁ kiyāty ā prathamaḥ sarga āsām ||
  • ṛtaṁ: This term refers to the cosmic order or truth. In real life, it can be understood as the principles of justice and righteousness that govern our actions and decisions.
  • devāya: This means "to the gods" or "for the divine." It signifies the importance of aligning our actions with higher spiritual principles.
  • kṛṇvate: This translates to "they create" or "they establish." It emphasizes the active role of divine forces in shaping the universe and our lives.
  • savitra: Referring to the Sun, Savitra symbolizes illumination, knowledge, and life. In our daily lives, it represents the pursuit of enlightenment and understanding.
  • indrāyāhighne: This term can be broken down into "Indra," the king of gods, and "āhighne," which means "to the one who is powerful." It highlights the strength and authority of divine forces in the world.
  • na ramanta: This means "they do not delight." It suggests that the divine forces are not swayed by trivial pleasures, reminding us to focus on what is truly important.
  • āpaḥ: This refers to the waters or the life-giving elements. In a practical sense, it can symbolize the essential resources we need for survival and growth.
  • ahar-ahar: This means "day by day" or "every day." It emphasizes the continuous nature of existence and the importance of daily actions in shaping our lives.
  • yāty: This translates to "goes" or "moves." It signifies the flow of time and the inevitability of change in our lives.
  • aktur: This term means "the power" or "the energy." It represents the dynamic forces that drive creation and transformation in the universe.
  • apāṁ: This refers to "of the waters." It connects back to the life-giving elements and their significance in sustaining life.
  • kiyāty: This means "how much" or "to what extent." It raises questions about the limits and boundaries of our understanding and existence.
  • ā prathamaḥ: This translates to "the first" or "the foremost." It signifies the importance of beginnings and the foundational aspects of creation.
  • sarga: This means "creation" or "generation." It refers to the process of bringing forth life and the universe.
  • āsām: This term means "of these" or "of this." It emphasizes the interconnectedness of all elements in the cosmos.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of cosmic order, divine creation, and the continuous flow of life. It reminds us of the importance of aligning our actions with higher principles and recognizing the divine forces at play in our lives. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire us to seek knowledge, act with righteousness, and appreciate the interconnectedness of all existence. By understanding and embodying these principles, we can navigate our lives with purpose and clarity, contributing to the greater harmony of the universe.
