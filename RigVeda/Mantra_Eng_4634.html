शचिवतस् ते पुरुषाक शाकाः गवाम् इव श्रुतयः संचरणीः |वत्सानां न तन्तयस् त इन्द्र दामन्वन्तो अदामानः सुदामन् ||
śacīvatas te puruśāka śākā gavām iva srutayaḥ saṁcaraṇīḥ | vatsānāṁ na tantayas ta indra dāmanvanto adāmānaḥ sudāman ||
  • śacīvatas: This term can be translated as "of the powerful" or "of the strong." It signifies strength and capability, often associated with divine or heroic qualities.
  • te: This means "your," indicating possession or connection to the subject being addressed, often referring to a deity or a powerful figure.
  • puruśāka: This word refers to "the one who is capable" or "the one who has the ability." It emphasizes the qualities of a person or deity who can achieve great things.
  • śākā: This translates to "branches" or "offshoots," symbolizing growth, expansion, and the various paths one can take in life.
  • gavām: Meaning "of the cows," this term is often associated with wealth, nourishment, and prosperity in Vedic literature, as cows were considered sacred and a source of sustenance.
  • iva: This means "like" or "as," used for comparison, indicating similarity or analogy.
  • srutayaḥ: This translates to "the heard" or "the knowledge," referring to wisdom or teachings that have been passed down through generations.
  • saṁcaraṇīḥ: This means "those who move" or "those who travel," indicating a sense of journey or progression in life.
  • vatsānāṁ: This translates to "of the calves," symbolizing youth, potential, and the future, as calves represent the next generation.
  • na: This means "not," indicating negation or absence.
  • tantayas: This term refers to "the threads" or "the connections," which can symbolize relationships or the bonds that tie individuals together.
  • ta: This means "that," referring to something specific that has been mentioned or is understood in context.
  • indra: Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, representing power, strength, and authority.
  • dāmanvanto: This translates to "those who possess control" or "those who have mastery," indicating a sense of governance or leadership.
  • adāmānaḥ: This means "those who are not restrained" or "those who are free," symbolizing liberation and the ability to act without limitations.
  • sudāman: This translates to "the one who is well endowed" or "the one who is prosperous," indicating abundance and success.

  • Significance: This mantra emphasizes the qualities of strength, capability, and prosperity. It highlights the importance of wisdom and knowledge that guide individuals on their journey through life. The imagery of cows and calves symbolizes nourishment and the potential of future generations, while the references to Indra and mastery suggest the importance of leadership and control in achieving success.
  • Real-life Application: In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as leadership roles in organizations, where strong and capable leaders guide their teams towards success. It can also be relevant in personal development, where individuals strive to grow and expand their capabilities, drawing on wisdom and knowledge to navigate challenges. Additionally, the emphasis on nurturing future generations can inspire individuals to invest in education and mentorship, ensuring a prosperous future for society.
