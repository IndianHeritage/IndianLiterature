चनीयष्टं देवाः ओषधिषु अप्सु यद् योग्याः अशनवैत ऋषीणाम् |पुरूणि रत्ना दधतुः न्य अस्मे अनु पूर्वाणि चक्ष्यथुर युगानि ||
caniṣṭaṁ devā oṣadhīṣv apsu yad yogyā aśnavaithe ṛṣīṇām | purūṇi ratnā dadhatau ny a1sme anu pūrvāṇi cakhyathur yugāni ||
  • caniṣṭaṁ: This word translates to "the best" or "the most excellent." It signifies the highest quality or the most desirable aspect of something.
  • devā: Refers to "gods" or "divine beings." In the context of the mantra, it indicates the celestial powers or deities that govern various aspects of life.
  • oṣadhīṣv: This term means "in the herbs" or "in the plants." It highlights the importance of nature and the healing properties found in plants.
  • apsu: Translates to "in the waters." Water is often seen as a source of life and purity, essential for sustenance and growth.
  • yad: Means "that" or "which." It serves as a connector in the mantra, linking the divine to the earthly elements.
  • yogyā: This word means "fit" or "suitable." It suggests that certain qualities or elements are appropriate for specific purposes.
  • aśnavaithe: Translates to "may consume" or "may partake." It indicates the act of receiving or utilizing the blessings of the divine.
  • ṛṣīṇām: Refers to "of the sages" or "of the seers." This emphasizes the wisdom and knowledge of ancient sages who understood the connection between the divine and the natural world.
  • purūṇi: Means "many" or "numerous." It signifies abundance and the richness of life.
  • ratnā: Translates to "gems" or "jewels." In a metaphorical sense, it represents valuable insights or blessings.
  • dadhatau: Means "they bestow" or "they give." This indicates the act of sharing or providing blessings.
  • ny a1sme: This phrase can be interpreted as "to us" or "for us." It signifies the personal aspect of receiving blessings.
  • anu: Means "following" or "after." It indicates a sequence or a continuation of blessings.
  • pūrvāṇi: Refers to "the ancient" or "the previous." It connects the present with the wisdom of the past.
  • cakhyathur: Translates to "they have revealed" or "they have shown." This indicates the sharing of knowledge or insights.
  • yugāni: Means "ages" or "epochs." It signifies the passage of time and the continuity of wisdom across generations.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the interconnectedness of the divine, nature, and human existence. It highlights the importance of seeking the best qualities in life, drawing from the wisdom of the sages, and recognizing the blessings that come from nature. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied in various ways, such as:

  • Emphasizing the importance of herbal medicine and natural remedies in health and wellness.
  • Encouraging sustainable practices that respect and preserve the environment.
  • Recognizing the value of ancient wisdom in modern decision-making and personal growth.

Ultimately, the mantra serves as a reminder to appreciate the abundance of life and the blessings that come from both the divine and the natural world, urging us to live in harmony with our surroundings and to seek wisdom from the past.
