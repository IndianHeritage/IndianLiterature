उभा हि दश्रां भिषजां मयोभुवोभा दक्षस्य वचसो बभूवथुः |तां वां विश्वको हवते तनूकृतhe मा नो वियौष्टं सख्या मुमोचतम् ||
ubhā hi dasrā bhiṣajā mayobhuvobhā dakṣasya vacaso babhūvathuḥ | tā vāṁ viśvako havate tanūkṛthe mā no vi yauṣṭaṁ sakhyā mumocatam ||
  • Ubhā hi dasrā bhiṣajā mayobhuvobhā dakṣasya vacaso babhūvathuḥ
    • Ubhā: This word translates to "both" or "together," indicating a sense of unity or duality.
    • hi: A particle that emphasizes the preceding word, often translated as "indeed" or "certainly."
    • dasrā: Refers to "the two," which can symbolize pairs or dual aspects of existence.
    • bhiṣajā: This means "healers" or "physicians," suggesting a focus on health and well-being.
    • mayobhuvobhā: This can be interpreted as "may we be endowed with," indicating a desire for blessings or gifts.
    • dakṣasya: Refers to "of the skilled" or "of the capable," highlighting the importance of competence and ability.
    • vacaso: This means "of speech" or "of words," emphasizing the power of communication.
    • babhūvathuḥ: This translates to "may it come to be" or "may it manifest," indicating a wish for realization or fulfillment.
  • Tā vāṁ viśvako havate tanūkṛthe
    • Tā: This means "those" or "that," referring to the subjects of the mantra.
    • vāṁ: This translates to "you," indicating a direct address to the divine or the audience.
    • viśvako: This means "universal" or "all-encompassing," suggesting a connection to the greater cosmos.
    • havate: This translates to "may offer" or "may invoke," indicating a call to action or a request for blessings.
    • tanūkṛthe: This means "for the sake of the body" or "for the physical form," emphasizing the importance of the material aspect of existence.
  • Mā no vi yauṣṭaṁ sakhyā mumocatam
    • Mā: This means "not," indicating a negation or a desire to avoid something.
    • no: This translates to "us," referring to the collective or the community.
    • vi: This means "in" or "within," suggesting an internal aspect.
    • yauṣṭaṁ: This can be interpreted as "suffering" or "pain," indicating a desire to be free from hardship.
    • sakhyā: This means "friendship" or "companionship," emphasizing the importance of relationships.
    • mumocatam: This translates to "may it be released" or "may it be liberated," indicating a wish for freedom from suffering.

Conclusion:

The mantra emphasizes the importance of unity, healing, and the power of words in our lives. It calls for blessings from the skilled and capable, highlighting the significance of communication and relationships. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as:

  • In healthcare, where the role of healers is crucial for well-being.
  • In personal relationships, where effective communication fosters understanding and companionship.
  • In community settings, where collective efforts can lead to shared blessings and support.

Ultimately, the mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of all beings and the importance of seeking harmony and healing in our lives.
