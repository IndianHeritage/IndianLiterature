यजस्व होतर इषितो यजीयान अग्ने बादो मरुतां न प्रयुक्ति |आ नो मित्रावरुणा नासत्यā dyāvā होत्राय पृथिवी ववृत्याः ||
yajasva hotar iṣito yajīyān agne bādho marutāṁ na prayukti | ā no mitrāvaruṇā nāsatyā dyāvā hotrāya pṛthivī vavṛtyāḥ ||
  • yajasva: This term translates to "perform" or "offer." It signifies the act of performing rituals or sacrifices, which is a central theme in Vedic traditions.
  • hotar: Refers to the "priest" or "one who invokes." This indicates the role of the priest in conducting rituals and invoking deities.
  • iṣito: Means "desired" or "sought after." It emphasizes the importance of the offerings being made with intention and purpose.
  • yajīyān: This word means "worthy of worship" or "sacred." It highlights the divine nature of the offerings and the reverence associated with them.
  • agne: Refers to "Agni," the fire god, who is a significant deity in Vedic rituals, symbolizing purity and the divine connection between the earthly and the celestial.
  • bādho: Translates to "obstruct" or "hinder." This suggests a plea for protection against negative influences or obstacles.
  • marutāṁ: Refers to the "Maruts," the storm deities associated with strength and power. They are often invoked for their protective qualities.
  • na prayukti: Means "not to be hindered" or "not to be obstructed." This phrase reinforces the desire for a smooth and unobstructed path in life.
  • ā no: Translates to "may we be" or "let us be." It is an invocation for blessings and positive outcomes.
  • mitrāvaruṇā: Refers to the duality of the deities Mitra and Varuna, representing friendship and cosmic order, respectively. This highlights the importance of harmony and balance in life.
  • nāsatyā: Refers to the "Asvins," the twin deities associated with health and well-being. They symbolize the pursuit of truth and the importance of good health.
  • dyāvā: Means "heaven" or "sky," representing the divine realm and the connection between the earthly and the celestial.
  • hotrāya: Refers to the "invocation" or "sacrifice." It emphasizes the act of calling upon the divine for assistance and blessings.
  • pṛthivī: Means "earth," symbolizing the physical realm and the nurturing aspect of nature.
  • vavṛtyāḥ: Translates to "may it be fulfilled" or "may it manifest." This indicates a desire for the blessings and intentions to come to fruition.

Conclusion: This mantra encapsulates the essence of Vedic rituals, emphasizing the importance of intention, divine connection, and the pursuit of harmony in life. It serves as a reminder to seek blessings from the divine while acknowledging the interconnectedness of all elements in the universe. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during personal rituals, community gatherings, or even in moments of reflection and meditation. By invoking these principles, individuals can strive for a balanced and fulfilling life, fostering a sense of peace and purpose in their daily endeavors.
