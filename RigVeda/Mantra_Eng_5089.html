इन्द्राविष्णू पिबतं मध्वो अस्य सोमस्य दश्रां जठरं पृणेताम् |आ वाम अन्धांसी मदिराण्य अग्मन् उप ब्रह्माणि श्रुणुतं हवम् मे ||
indrāviṣṇū pibatam madhvo asya somasya dasrā jaṭharam pṛṇethām | ā vām andhāṁsi madirāṇy agmann upa brahmāṇi śṛṇutaṁ havam me ||
  • Indrāviṣṇū: This term refers to the deities Indra and Vishnu, who are significant figures in Vedic literature. Indra is often associated with rain and thunderstorms, while Vishnu represents preservation and protection.
  • Pibatam: This word means "to drink" or "to consume." It signifies the act of receiving or partaking in something beneficial.
  • Madhvo: This translates to "sweetness" or "delight." It often refers to the essence of life and joy.
  • Asya: Meaning "of this," it indicates possession or belonging.
  • Somasya: This refers to "soma," a sacred drink in Vedic rituals believed to confer immortality and divine inspiration.
  • Dasrā: This term means "to the belly" or "to the stomach," symbolizing nourishment and sustenance.
  • Jaṭharam: This word also relates to the stomach or the core of being, emphasizing the importance of inner strength and vitality.
  • Pṛṇethām: Meaning "may you fill," it expresses a desire for abundance and fulfillment.
  • Ā vām: This translates to "to you both," referring to the deities Indra and Vishnu, indicating a plea for their attention and blessings.
  • Andhāṁsi: This means "blindness" or "ignorance," representing obstacles or challenges in life.
  • Madirāṇy: This term refers to "intoxicating substances," often symbolizing the allure of worldly pleasures that can lead one astray.
  • Agmann: Meaning "to be removed" or "to be dispelled," it signifies the desire to overcome ignorance and obstacles.
  • Upa: This means "near" or "towards," indicating a movement towards something desirable.
  • Brahmāṇi: This term refers to the ultimate reality or the cosmic spirit, representing the highest truth.
  • Śṛṇutaṁ: Meaning "listen," it is a call for attention and receptiveness to divine guidance.
  • Havam me: This translates to "my offering," indicating a personal connection and devotion to the divine.

Conclusion: This mantra is a profound invocation that seeks the blessings of Indra and Vishnu for nourishment, wisdom, and the dispelling of ignorance. In real life, it can be applied in various scenarios, such as during rituals, personal prayers, or moments of reflection when one seeks clarity and abundance. The significance lies in recognizing the need for divine support in overcoming life's challenges and striving for a deeper understanding of existence. By invoking these deities, individuals can cultivate a sense of connection to the divine and seek guidance in their personal journeys.
