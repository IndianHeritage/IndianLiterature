इन्द्र ओषधीर असनोद अहानि वनस्पतींर असनोद अन्तरिक्षम् | बिभेद वलं नुनुदे विवाचोऽथाभवद दामिताभिकृतूनाम् ||
indra oṣadhīr asanod ahāni vanaspatīm̐r asanod antarikṣam | bibheda valaṁ nunude vivāco 'thābhavad damitābhikratūnām ||
  • Indra: Refers to the king of gods in Vedic mythology, symbolizing strength, power, and leadership.
  • Oṣadhīr: Means 'herbs' or 'plants', representing nature and its healing properties.
  • Asanod: Translates to 'he has made' or 'he has created', indicating the act of creation or manifestation.
  • Ahāni: Means 'in the days' or 'during the times', suggesting a temporal aspect.
  • Vanaspatīm̐r: Refers to 'trees' or 'forest', symbolizing growth, stability, and the interconnectedness of life.
  • Antarikṣam: Means 'the space' or 'the atmosphere', representing the vastness of the universe.
  • Bibheda: Translates to 'he split' or 'he divided', indicating a transformative action.
  • Valam: Means 'the strength' or 'the power', emphasizing the importance of strength in overcoming challenges.
  • Nunude: Refers to 'he has made known' or 'he has revealed', suggesting the act of enlightenment or awareness.
  • Vivāco: Means 'of the wise' or 'of the learned', indicating the value of knowledge and wisdom.
  • 'Thābhavad: Translates to 'that which has become', indicating transformation or evolution.
  • Damita: Means 'controlled' or 'restrained', emphasizing the importance of self-control.
  • Abhikratūnām: Refers to 'those who are active' or 'those who strive', highlighting the significance of effort and action.

This mantra speaks to the interconnectedness of nature, the divine, and human effort. It emphasizes the role of Indra as a powerful force that creates and transforms the natural world, including plants and trees, which are essential for life. The mention of space suggests a broader cosmic perspective, indicating that our actions have implications beyond our immediate surroundings.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts:

  • Environmental Awareness: Just as Indra is associated with nature, we can draw inspiration to protect and nurture our environment. For example, community gardening initiatives can reflect the principles of growth and interconnectedness.
  • Personal Growth: The idea of transformation and self-control can be applied to personal development. Setting goals and striving for self-improvement mirrors the efforts of 'those who strive' in the mantra.
  • Leadership: Indra symbolizes leadership and strength. In a workplace setting, effective leaders can inspire their teams to work together harmoniously, much like the elements of nature working in unison.

In conclusion, this mantra encapsulates the essence of creation, transformation, and the importance of wisdom and strength in our lives. It serves as a reminder of our responsibility towards nature and the significance of our actions in the broader context of existence.
