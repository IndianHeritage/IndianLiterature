सं नः शिशीहि भुरिजोर इव क्षुरं रास्व रायो विमोचन |त्वे तं नः सुवेदं उष्रियं वसु यं त्वं हिनोषि मार्त्यम् ||
saṁ naḥ śiśīhi bhurijor iva kṣuraṁ rāsva rāyo vimocana | tve tan naḥ suvedam usriyaṁ vasu yaṁ tvaṁ hinoṣi martyam ||
  • saṁ naḥ śiśīhi: This phrase can be translated as "May you protect us." The word "saṁ" implies togetherness or unity, while "śiśīhi" means to protect or guard. In real life, this can be seen in the context of community support, where individuals come together to safeguard each other's well-being.
  • bhurijor iva kṣuraṁ: This part translates to "like the sharpness of a razor." Here, "bhurijor" refers to something that is abundant or rich, and "kṣuraṁ" means razor or sharpness. This can symbolize the need for clarity and precision in our thoughts and actions, akin to how a razor cuts through confusion. For example, in decision-making, having a clear and sharp mind can lead to better outcomes.
  • rāyo vimocana: This translates to "the release of wealth." "Rāyo" refers to wealth or prosperity, and "vimocana" means liberation or release. In practical terms, this can relate to the idea of freeing oneself from financial burdens or obstacles that prevent one from achieving prosperity. For instance, a person may seek financial education to liberate themselves from debt.
  • tve tan naḥ suvedam: This means "may your knowledge be good for us." "Tve" refers to "your," "tan" means "knowledge," and "suvedam" implies something that is beneficial or good. This highlights the importance of seeking wisdom and knowledge that can positively impact our lives. In real life, this can be seen in the pursuit of education and learning from mentors.
  • usriyaṁ vasu: This translates to "the wealth of the dawn." "Usriyaṁ" refers to dawn or new beginnings, and "vasu" means wealth or abundance. This can symbolize the opportunities that come with new beginnings, such as starting a new job or embarking on a new project. For example, the dawn of a new day often brings fresh opportunities and hope.
  • yaṁ tvaṁ hinoṣi martyam: This means "which you remove from mortality." "Hinoṣi" refers to the act of removing or taking away, and "martyam" means mortality or death. This can be interpreted as a plea for divine intervention to help transcend the limitations of human existence. In real life, this can relate to the pursuit of health and longevity, as well as spiritual practices aimed at achieving a higher state of being.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a deep desire for protection, clarity, prosperity, knowledge, new beginnings, and transcendence from mortality. Its significance lies in the holistic approach it advocates for a fulfilling life. In real-life scenarios, individuals can apply the teachings of this mantra by fostering community support, seeking clarity in decision-making, pursuing financial literacy, valuing education, embracing new opportunities, and striving for spiritual growth. By embodying these principles, one can navigate life's challenges more effectively and work towards a prosperous and meaningful existence.
