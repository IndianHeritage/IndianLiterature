स्वाहाकृतस्य तृम्पतं सुतस्य देवावन्धसः |आ यातम अश्विनाऽगतं अवस्युर वाम अहं हुवे धत्तं रत्नानि दाशुषे ||
svāhākṛtasya tṛmpataṁ sutasya devāv andhasaḥ | ā yātam aśvinā gatam avasyur vām ahaṁ huve dhattaṁ ratnāni dāśuṣe ||
  • svāhākṛtasya: This term can be broken down into "svāha," which is an invocation used in Vedic rituals, and "kṛtasya," meaning "of what has been done." Together, it signifies the offerings made to the divine.
  • tṛmpataṁ: This word refers to "the one who is satisfied" or "the one who is content." It suggests a state of fulfillment and happiness.
  • sutasya: This translates to "of the son" or "of the offspring," indicating a connection to lineage and the continuation of family or community.
  • devāv: This means "gods" or "divine beings," referring to the higher powers that govern the universe.
  • andhasaḥ: This term means "of the blind" or "those who are in darkness," symbolizing ignorance or lack of awareness.
  • ā yātam: This phrase translates to "may we go" or "let us proceed," indicating a desire for movement or progress.
  • aśvinā: Referring to the Aśvins, the twin gods of medicine and health in Vedic mythology, symbolizing healing and well-being.
  • gatam: This means "gone" or "departed," suggesting a transition or change.
  • avasyur: This term means "certainly" or "undoubtedly," indicating a strong affirmation.
  • vām: This translates to "your" or "of you," indicating a connection to the divine or the audience of the mantra.
  • ahaṁ: Meaning "I" or "myself," it signifies the speaker's personal involvement or intention.
  • huve: This means "I invoke" or "I call upon," indicating a plea or request to the divine.
  • dhattaṁ: This translates to "may you bestow" or "may you grant," suggesting a desire for blessings or gifts.
  • ratnāni: This means "jewels" or "precious things," symbolizing wealth, prosperity, and valuable gifts.
  • dāśuṣe: This term means "to the one who serves" or "to the devotee," indicating a connection to service and devotion.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a plea for divine blessings and fulfillment. It emphasizes the importance of offerings to the gods, the pursuit of satisfaction, and the desire for healing and prosperity. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during rituals for health, family well-being, or when seeking guidance in times of uncertainty. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of the divine, the individual, and the community, encouraging us to seek blessings not just for ourselves but also for those we serve and care for.
