त्री यच छतां महिषाणाम् अघो मास त्रि सरांसी मगवाः सोम्यापाः |कारं न विश्वे अह्वन्त देवाः भरम् इन्द्राय यद अहिं जग्हाना ||
trī yac chatā mahiṣāṇām agho mās trī sarāṁsi maghavā somyāpāḥ | kāraṁ na viśve ahvanta devā bharam indrāya yad ahiṁ jaghāna ||
  • Translation: The mantra can be translated as: "Three are the great powers of the mighty ones, three are the streams of the Maghavan (Indra), the divine ones call upon the strength of Indra, who has slain the serpent."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • trī: Three - signifies completeness or a triad of powers.
    • yac: That which - refers to the qualities or attributes being discussed.
    • chatā: Great powers - indicates strength or might.
    • mahiṣāṇām: Of the mighty ones - refers to powerful beings or deities.
    • agho: Strength or might - often associated with the ability to overcome obstacles.
    • mās: Month - can symbolize cycles or periods of time.
    • sarāṁsi: Streams - represents flow, abundance, or resources.
    • maghavā: Maghavan (Indra) - the chief deity associated with strength and power.
    • somyāpāḥ: Those who drink the soma - refers to the divine beings who partake in the sacred drink, symbolizing enlightenment and divine connection.
    • kāraṁ: Action or cause - indicates the reason behind a phenomenon.
    • na: Not - negation, indicating what is not to be done.
    • viśve: All - signifies universality.
    • ahvanta: Call upon - refers to invoking or summoning.
    • devā: Deities - divine beings or gods.
    • bharam: Strength or burden - indicates the weight of responsibility or power.
    • indrāya: To Indra - directed towards the deity Indra.
    • yad: That which - refers to the action or event being discussed.
    • ahiṁ: Serpent - often symbolizes obstacles or challenges.
    • jaghāna: Slain - indicates overcoming or defeating.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • The concept of "three great powers" can be seen in various cultures where triads are revered, such as the Holy Trinity in Christianity or the Trimurti in Hinduism (Brahma, Vishnu, Shiva).
    • The idea of invoking strength can be applied in personal development, where individuals call upon their inner strength to overcome challenges, much like athletes preparing for a competition.
    • The reference to "slaying the serpent" can be likened to overcoming fears or obstacles in life, such as a student facing the fear of public speaking or an entrepreneur overcoming the challenges of starting a new business.
  • Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its emphasis on the invocation of divine strength and the overcoming of obstacles. It teaches us that by recognizing and calling upon our inner powers, we can face challenges head-on. In real life, this mantra can inspire individuals to seek strength in unity (the triad), to draw upon their resources (streams), and to confront their fears (the serpent) with the courage of Indra. It serves as a reminder that divine support is available to those who seek it, and that overcoming challenges is a universal experience that can lead to personal growth and empowerment.
