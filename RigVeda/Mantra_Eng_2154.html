अध्वर्यवः पयसोद्धर यथा गोः सोमैभिर इमं पृणतां भोजम् इन्द्रम् |वेदाहम अस्य निभृतं मा एतद दित्सन्तं भूयो यजतश्चिकेत ||
adhvaryavaḥ payasodhar yathā goḥ somebhir īm pṛṇatā bhojam indram | vedāham asya nibhṛtam ma etad ditsantam bhūyo yajataś ciketa ||
  • adhvaryavaḥ: This term refers to the priest who performs the rituals, particularly the one who oversees the offerings. In real life, this can be likened to a project manager who ensures that all aspects of a project are executed properly.
  • payasodhar: This word means "one who holds the milk" or "the one who provides nourishment." In a modern context, this can represent caregivers or parents who provide sustenance and support to their families.
  • yathā goḥ: This phrase translates to "like the cow." Cows are often seen as symbols of wealth and nourishment in many cultures. In real life, this can be related to the importance of agriculture and livestock in sustaining communities.
  • somebhir: This term refers to the offerings made with soma, a sacred drink in Vedic rituals. It symbolizes the essence of life and vitality. In contemporary terms, it can be compared to the importance of health and wellness in our lives.
  • īm pṛṇatā: This means "may it be filled" or "may it be satisfied." It reflects the desire for abundance and fulfillment. In everyday life, this can relate to our aspirations for success and happiness.
  • bhojam indram: This phrase translates to "the feast of Indra," where Indra is the king of the gods and represents strength and power. In a modern context, this can symbolize the pursuit of excellence and the rewards that come from hard work.
  • vedāham: This means "I know" or "I have knowledge." It emphasizes the importance of wisdom and understanding in our actions. In real life, this can be related to the value of education and continuous learning.
  • asya nibhṛtam: This translates to "of this, firmly established." It signifies stability and security. In contemporary life, this can be associated with building a strong foundation in our personal and professional lives.
  • ma etad ditsantam: This means "may I not desire this." It reflects a sense of humility and the understanding that not all desires are necessary. In real life, this can be related to the practice of mindfulness and contentment.
  • bhūyo yajataś ciketa: This translates to "may he who worships again and again understand." It emphasizes the importance of dedication and perseverance in our endeavors. In modern life, this can be likened to the idea of continuous improvement and striving for excellence.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of ritual, nourishment, wisdom, and the pursuit of fulfillment. It highlights the significance of dedication and the importance of understanding our desires. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as in personal development, professional growth, and community building. By embodying the principles of nourishment, stability, and continuous learning, individuals can strive for a balanced and fulfilling life, much like the rituals that seek to connect the material and spiritual realms.
