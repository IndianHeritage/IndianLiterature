परायतीं मातरं अन्व आचष्ट न नानु गान्य अनु नू गमानी |त्वष्टुर गृहे अपिबत सोमं इन्द्रः शतधन्यं चम्वोः सुतस्य ||
parāyatīm mātaram anv acaṣṭa na nānu gāny anu nū gamāni | tvaṣṭur gṛhe apibat somam indraḥ śatadhanyaṁ camvoḥ sutasya ||
  • parāyatīm mātaram: This phrase translates to "the mother who is all-encompassing." In a real-life context, this can refer to the Earth or nature, which nurtures and sustains all life forms. Just as a mother provides for her children, the Earth provides resources such as food, water, and shelter.
  • anv acaṣṭa: This means "she has been revealed" or "she has been made known." This can be interpreted as the understanding or realization of the importance of nature and the environment in our lives. For example, as we learn more about climate change, we become more aware of our responsibility to protect the Earth.
  • na nānu gāny anu nū gamāni: This translates to "not to be neglected, nor to be forsaken." It emphasizes the importance of caring for our environment and not taking it for granted. In real life, this can be seen in movements advocating for environmental conservation, where individuals and communities work to protect natural resources.
  • tvaṣṭur gṛhe: This means "in the house of the creator." It signifies the divine aspect of creation and the interconnectedness of all beings. In practical terms, this can relate to how we view our homes and communities as part of a larger ecosystem that requires harmony and balance.
  • apibat somam: This translates to "Indra drank the soma." Soma is often associated with immortality and divine inspiration. In a modern context, this can symbolize the pursuit of knowledge and enlightenment, akin to how people seek education and personal growth to improve their lives and communities.
  • indraḥ śatadhanyaṁ: This means "Indra, the one who grants a hundredfold blessings." Indra is a deity associated with rain and thunderstorms, representing abundance and prosperity. In real life, this can be seen in agricultural practices where proper rainfall leads to bountiful harvests, thus ensuring food security for communities.
  • camvoḥ sutasya: This translates to "of the son of the cow." Cows are revered in many cultures, particularly in India, symbolizing nourishment and wealth. This can relate to the importance of livestock in agriculture and the economy, highlighting the need for sustainable farming practices.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the interconnectedness of nature, the divine, and human responsibility. It calls for a recognition of the nurturing aspects of the Earth and the importance of caring for our environment. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied through environmental conservation efforts, sustainable agriculture, and the pursuit of knowledge. By understanding and respecting these principles, we can work towards a more harmonious existence with nature, ensuring prosperity for future generations.
