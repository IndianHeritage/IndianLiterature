इदं सु मे जरीतर आ चिकीद्धि प्रतिपं शापं नद्यो वहन्ति |लोपाशः सिंहं प्रत्यञ्चम अट्साः क्रोष्टा वराहं निर अक्त कक्षात ||
idaṁ su me jaritar ā cikiddhi pratīpaṁ śāpaṁ nadyo vahanti | lopāśaḥ siṁham pratyañcam atsāḥ kroṣṭā varāhaṁ nir atakta kakṣāt ||
  • idaṁ su me jaritar ā cikiddhi: This phrase can be translated to "This is good for me, O friend." Here, "idaṁ" means "this," "su" means "good," "me" means "for me," "jaritar" refers to "friend" or "companion," and "cikiddhi" means "to understand" or "to know." In real life, this can be seen in friendships where individuals seek the well-being of each other, emphasizing the importance of supportive relationships.
  • pratīpaṁ śāpaṁ nadyo vahanti: This translates to "the rivers carry the curse." "Pratīpaṁ" means "to carry" or "to bear," "śāpaṁ" means "curse," and "nadyo" refers to "rivers." This can be related to how negative experiences or burdens can be passed along, much like how rivers carry water. In life, we often see how grievances or burdens can affect communities, similar to how pollution in a river affects all who depend on it.
  • lopāśaḥ siṁham pratyañcam: This means "the lion is fierce and powerful." "Lopāśaḥ" refers to "fierce" or "strong," "siṁham" means "lion," and "pratyañcam" means "powerful." The lion is often a symbol of strength and courage. In real life, this can be seen in leadership roles where individuals must embody strength and decisiveness, much like a lion leading its pride.
  • atsāḥ kroṣṭā varāhaṁ: This translates to "the boar is a protector." "Atsāḥ" means "protector," "kroṣṭā" refers to "boar," and "varāhaṁ" means "to protect." In many cultures, the boar is seen as a guardian figure. This can be related to how certain individuals or groups take on protective roles in society, such as community leaders or activists who stand up for the rights of others.
  • nir atakta kakṣāt: This means "from the untainted womb." "Nir" means "without," "atakta" refers to "taint" or "blemish," and "kakṣāt" means "womb" or "origin." This can symbolize purity and the importance of a clean start or origin. In real life, this can be seen in the context of new beginnings, such as starting a new project or initiative with a clear and honest intention.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the significance of friendship, the burdens we carry, the strength of leadership, the role of protectors, and the importance of purity in origins. In real life, these themes are applicable in various scenarios, such as personal relationships, community dynamics, leadership roles, and the pursuit of integrity in new endeavors. By reflecting on these concepts, individuals can strive for better relationships, responsible leadership, and a commitment to purity in their actions and intentions.
