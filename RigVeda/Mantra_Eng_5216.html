प्र-प्रायम् अग्निर भरतस्य श्रिण्वे वि यत सूर्यो न रोचते बृहद्भा: | अभि यः पूरुम पृथनासु तस्थौ द्युतानो दैव्यो अतिथि: शुशोच ||
pra-prāyam agnir bharatasya śṛṇve vi yat sūryo na rocate bṛhad bhāḥ | abhi yaḥ pūrum pṛtanāsu tasthau dyutāno daivyo atithiḥ śuśoca ||
  • pra-prāyam: This term can be understood as "the beginning" or "the initiation." It signifies the start of a journey or a new phase in life.
  • agnir: Referring to "fire," which symbolizes energy, transformation, and purification. In many cultures, fire is seen as a sacred element that brings warmth and light.
  • bharatasya: This word relates to "Bharata," which can refer to the land of India or the people of India. It signifies a connection to heritage and culture.
  • śṛṇve: Meaning "to hear" or "to listen," this term emphasizes the importance of being receptive to knowledge and wisdom.
  • vi yat: Translated as "wherever" or "in whatever," it indicates a sense of universality and inclusiveness.
  • sūryo: Referring to the "sun," which is a symbol of life, vitality, and enlightenment. The sun is often associated with growth and prosperity.
  • na rocate: Meaning "does not shine" or "does not illuminate," this phrase can imply a lack of clarity or understanding in certain situations.
  • bṛhad bhāḥ: Translated as "great light" or "vast brightness," it signifies the pursuit of knowledge and truth that transcends ordinary understanding.
  • abhi: This term means "towards" or "in the direction of," indicating a movement or progression towards a goal.
  • yaḥ: Meaning "who" or "that which," it introduces a subject that is significant in the context of the mantra.
  • pūrum: This word can be understood as "full" or "complete," suggesting a sense of fulfillment or wholeness.
  • pṛtanāsu: Referring to "in the struggles" or "in the challenges," it acknowledges the difficulties faced in life.
  • tasthau: Meaning "stood" or "remained," this term indicates resilience and steadfastness in the face of adversity.
  • dyutāno: Translated as "radiant" or "brilliant," it signifies the qualities of light and positivity.
  • daivyo: Meaning "divine" or "heavenly," this term connects the mantra to higher spiritual realms and divine guidance.
  • atithiḥ: Referring to "the guest" or "the visitor," it emphasizes the importance of hospitality and welcoming others.
  • śuśoca: Meaning "lamented" or "grieved," this term can indicate a sense of loss or the need for compassion in difficult times.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the essence of seeking knowledge, embracing challenges, and recognizing the divine presence in our lives. It teaches us the importance of listening, being receptive to wisdom, and standing firm in our struggles. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied to personal growth, where individuals face challenges and seek enlightenment. It encourages us to be resilient, to welcome others with kindness, and to strive for a greater understanding of ourselves and the world around us. The significance of this mantra lies in its timeless message of hope, resilience, and the pursuit of truth, which remains relevant in our daily lives.
