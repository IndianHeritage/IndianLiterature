मन्त्र: द्वादशारं न हि तज् जरा य वर्वर्ति चक्रं परि द्यां ऋतस्य | आ पुत्रा अग्ने मिथुनासो अत्र सप्त शतानि विंशतिः च तस्थुः ||
dvādaśāraṁ nahi taj jarāya varvarti cakram pari dyām ṛtasya | ā putrā agne mithunāso atra sapta śatāni viṁśatiś ca tasthuḥ ||
  • dvādaśāraṁ: This term refers to the number twelve, which can symbolize completeness or a cycle, as there are twelve months in a year.
  • nahi: This means "not" or "no," indicating a negation or absence of something.
  • taj: This translates to "that," referring to something previously mentioned or understood.
  • jarāya: This word means "to old age" or "to decay," often associated with the inevitable aging process.
  • varvarti: This means "to revolve" or "to turn," suggesting a cyclical nature.
  • cakram: This translates to "wheel," which can symbolize the cycle of life, time, or the universe.
  • pari: This means "around" or "surrounding," indicating something that encompasses or encircles.
  • dyām: This refers to "the sky" or "heaven," often representing the divine or the higher realms.
  • ṛtasya: This means "of truth" or "of order," referring to the cosmic order or the principle of righteousness.
  • ā: This can mean "from" or "towards," indicating direction or origin.
  • putrā: This translates to "sons" or "children," symbolizing lineage and continuity.
  • agne: This refers to "Agni," the fire god, representing energy, transformation, and purification.
  • mithunāso: This means "in pairs" or "couples," indicating unity and partnership.
  • atra: This translates to "here" or "in this place," indicating presence or immediacy.
  • sapta: This means "seven," often symbolizing completeness or perfection.
  • śatāni: This translates to "hundreds," indicating abundance or a large quantity.
  • viṁśatiś: This means "twenty," which can also symbolize completeness in a different context.
  • ca: This means "and," indicating addition or connection.
  • tasthuḥ: This translates to "may they stand" or "may they exist," indicating a wish or blessing for stability and presence.

The mantra speaks to the cyclical nature of life and the importance of continuity through generations. It emphasizes the idea that while life may revolve and change, there is a fundamental truth and order that persists. The reference to Agni suggests that through transformation and purification, one can achieve a state of harmony and balance.


In real life, this mantra can be applied to the understanding of family and community dynamics. For instance, in many cultures, the passing of knowledge and values from one generation to the next is crucial for maintaining a sense of identity and purpose. The mention of "sons" and "couples" highlights the importance of relationships and partnerships in fostering growth and stability.


In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life, the importance of maintaining order and truth, and the role of relationships in achieving a harmonious existence. It encourages individuals to embrace the cycles of life while remaining grounded in their values and connections with others.
