दिदृक्षन्त उषसो याम्न्नक्तोर विवस्वत्यां महि चित्रं अनीकम् |विश्वे जानन्ति महिना यद आगाद इन्द्रस्य कर्म सुक्रतā पुरूणि ||
didṛkṣanta uṣaso yāmann aktor vivasvatyā mahi citram anīkam | viśve jānanti mahinā yad āgād indrasya karma sukṛtā purūṇi ||
  • Didṛkṣanta: This word translates to "seeking" or "desiring to see." It reflects a yearning for knowledge and understanding.
  • Uṣaso: This refers to "the dawn" or "morning." In many cultures, dawn symbolizes new beginnings and enlightenment.
  • Yāmann: This means "who" or "that which." It introduces a subject of inquiry or contemplation.
  • Aktor: This word can be interpreted as "the sun" or "light." It signifies illumination, both literally and metaphorically.
  • Vivasvatyā: This relates to "Vivasvat," the sun god, representing the source of life and energy.
  • Mahi: This means "great" or "vast," often referring to the universe or the expanse of existence.
  • Citram: This translates to "wonderful" or "extraordinary," indicating the beauty and complexity of creation.
  • Anīkam: This means "the multitude" or "the collective," suggesting a gathering of diverse elements or beings.
  • Viśve: This word means "all" or "universal," indicating inclusivity and the interconnectedness of all things.
  • Jānanti: This translates to "know" or "understand," emphasizing the importance of knowledge and awareness.
  • Mahinā: This means "by greatness" or "through power," suggesting that understanding comes from recognizing the vastness of existence.
  • Yad: This means "that" or "which," referring to the knowledge or understanding being sought.
  • Āgād: This translates to "has come" or "arrived," indicating the attainment of knowledge or realization.
  • Indrasya: This refers to "Indra," the king of gods, symbolizing strength, power, and authority.
  • Karma: This means "action" or "deed," highlighting the significance of one's actions in shaping reality.
  • Sukṛtā: This translates to "good deeds" or "virtuous actions," emphasizing the importance of righteousness.
  • Purūṇi: This means "ancient" or "of old," suggesting that these truths are timeless and have been recognized throughout history.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the quest for knowledge and understanding of the universe, symbolized by the dawn and the sun. It emphasizes the importance of seeking wisdom through virtuous actions and recognizing the interconnectedness of all beings. In real life, this can be applied in various scenarios, such as in education, where the pursuit of knowledge is akin to the dawn of understanding. It also resonates in ethical decision-making, where one's actions contribute to the greater good. Ultimately, this mantra serves as a reminder of the significance of knowledge, virtue, and the collective journey towards enlightenment.
