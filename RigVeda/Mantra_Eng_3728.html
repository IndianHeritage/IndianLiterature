अग्निः होतā दास्वतः क्षयस्य वृक्तबरिषः |सं यज्ञासश्चरन्ति यं सं वाजासः श्रवस्यवः ||
agnir hotā dāsvataḥ kṣayasya vṛktabarhiṣaḥ | saṁ yajñāsaś caranti yaṁ saṁ vājāsaḥ śravasyavaḥ ||
  • Agnir: This word refers to "Agni," the fire god in Vedic literature. Fire is often seen as a purifying and transformative force.
  • hotā: This term means "the one who offers" or "the priest." It signifies the role of a mediator between the divine and the human.
  • dāsvataḥ: This word can be interpreted as "of the Dasa," referring to the enemies or those who oppose the divine order.
  • kṣayasya: This means "of destruction" or "of decay," indicating the transient nature of life and the inevitability of change.
  • vṛktabarhiṣaḥ: This term refers to "the one who is adorned with the sacred grass," symbolizing purity and the offerings made to the deities.
  • saṁ: This prefix means "together" or "with," indicating unity and collective action.
  • yajñāsaś: This refers to "sacrifices" or "rituals," which are essential in Vedic culture for maintaining harmony with the cosmos.
  • caranti: This means "they move" or "they act," suggesting the dynamic nature of existence and the importance of action.
  • yaṁ: This word means "who" or "which," referring to the subject of the mantra.
  • vājāsaḥ: This term means "the strong" or "the powerful," often associated with strength and vitality.
  • śravasyavaḥ: This means "those who are renowned" or "those who have fame," indicating the importance of reputation and legacy.

The mantra can be interpreted as a call to Agni, the fire god, to act as a mediator in the sacrifices performed by the community. It emphasizes the importance of collective action in rituals to overcome adversities and maintain harmony. The mention of destruction and decay serves as a reminder of the impermanence of life, urging individuals to act with purpose and intention.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • Community Gatherings: Just as the mantra calls for collective action, community events like festivals or social service initiatives can bring people together to achieve common goals.
  • Personal Growth: The acknowledgment of impermanence encourages individuals to embrace change and strive for personal development, much like how fire transforms materials.
  • Conflict Resolution: The role of the hotā (priest) can be likened to mediators in conflicts, emphasizing the need for dialogue and understanding to resolve disputes.

In conclusion, this mantra highlights the significance of unity, the transformative power of rituals, and the importance of acknowledging the transient nature of life. It serves as a reminder to engage in collective actions that foster harmony and growth, both individually and within the community.
