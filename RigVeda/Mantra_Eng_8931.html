ये चेह पितरो ये च नेह याम्श च विद्म यामु च न प्रविद्म |त्वं वेत्त यति ते जातवेदः स्वधाभिर यज्ञं सुखृतं जुषस्व ||
ye ceha pitaro ye ca neha yām̐ś ca vidma yām̐ u ca na pravidma | tvaṁ vettha yati te jātavedaḥ svadhābhir yajñaṁ sukṛtaṁ juṣasva ||
  • Translation: "Those who are here, the ancestors, and those who are not here, we know them; we do not know those who are not here. You, O Jātaveda, know them; may you enjoy the offerings made with our own actions."
  • Word-by-Word Explanation:
    • ye ceha pitaro: "Those who are here, the ancestors" - This refers to the ancestors who have passed on but are still honored and remembered in rituals.
    • ye ca neha: "and those who are not here" - This acknowledges the ancestors who are not physically present but are still part of the spiritual lineage.
    • yām̐ś ca vidma: "we know them" - This signifies the awareness and recognition of the ancestors' presence in our lives.
    • yām̐ u ca na pravidma: "we do not know those who are not here" - This reflects the mystery of those ancestors who may not be known to us personally.
    • tvaṁ vettha: "You, O Jātaveda, know them" - Jātaveda is a name for Agni, the fire god, who is believed to have the knowledge of all beings, including the ancestors.
    • yati te jātavedaḥ: "may you enjoy" - A request for the divine to accept and partake in the offerings made.
    • svadhābhir yajñaṁ: "the offerings made with our own actions" - This emphasizes the importance of personal effort and intention in rituals and offerings.
    • sukṛtaṁ juṣasva: "may you enjoy the good deeds" - A prayer for the divine to appreciate and bless the good actions performed by the devotees.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In many cultures, rituals are performed to honor ancestors, such as the Day of the Dead in Mexico, where families create altars to remember and celebrate their deceased loved ones.
    • In Hinduism, during the festival of Pitru Paksha, families perform shraddha rituals to pay homage to their ancestors, reflecting the same reverence expressed in this mantra.
    • In modern life, remembering and honoring ancestors can be seen in family gatherings during significant festivals, where stories of ancestors are shared, keeping their memory alive.
  • Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the connection between the living and the ancestors, highlighting the importance of remembrance and honoring those who came before us. It signifies that our actions and intentions in rituals are vital for spiritual growth and connection. In real life, this can be applied through family traditions, remembrance ceremonies, and personal reflections on the impact of our ancestors in shaping our identities. By acknowledging our roots, we cultivate a sense of belonging and continuity, enriching our lives and those of future generations.
