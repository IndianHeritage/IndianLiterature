यन् नासत्याऽ भुरण्यथो यद् वा देव भीषज्यथः |अयं वां वत्सो मतीभिर न विन्दते हविष्मन्तं हि गच्छथः ||
yan nāsatyā bhuraṇyatho yad vā deva bhiṣajyathaḥ | ayaṁ vāṁ vatso matibhir na vindhate haviṣmantaṁ hi gacchathaḥ ||
  • yan nāsatyā bhuraṇyatho: This phrase can be translated as "what is not attained by the Asvins." The Asvins are twin deities in Vedic mythology, often associated with health, medicine, and the dawn. This line suggests that there are certain things that even these divine beings cannot achieve.
  • yad vā deva bhiṣajyathaḥ: This translates to "or what the gods themselves cannot heal." It emphasizes the limitations even the gods face in terms of healing and assistance, indicating that some ailments or challenges may be beyond divine intervention.
  • ayaṁ vāṁ vatso matibhir: This means "this is your calf, with thoughts." The term "vatso" refers to a calf, which symbolizes nourishment and care. It suggests that the thoughts and intentions we nurture are crucial for our growth and well-being.
  • na vindhate haviṣmantaṁ hi gacchathaḥ: This translates to "do not attain the offerings that go away." It implies that if one does not focus on the right intentions and thoughts, they may miss out on the blessings and offerings that life has to offer.

Conclusion: This mantra reflects the idea that there are limitations to what even divine beings can achieve, highlighting the importance of human agency and intention. In real life, this can be applied to various situations where individuals face challenges that may seem insurmountable, reminding us that while we may seek help from higher powers, our own thoughts and actions play a crucial role in overcoming obstacles. It encourages us to cultivate positive intentions and focus on what we can control, rather than solely relying on external forces. This mantra serves as a reminder to nurture our inner thoughts and to be proactive in our pursuits, ensuring that we do not miss out on the opportunities and blessings that life presents to us.
