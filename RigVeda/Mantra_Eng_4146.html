वाशिमन्त ऋष्टिमन्तो मनिषिणः सुदन्वाना इषुमन्तो निषङ्गिणः |स्वश्वाः स्थ सुरथाः पृष्णिमातरः स्वायुधा मरुतो याथना शुभम् ||
vāśīmanta ṛṣṭimanto manīṣiṇaḥ sudhanvāna iṣumanto niṣaṅgiṇaḥ | svaśvāḥ stha surathāḥ pṛśnimātaraḥ svāyudhā maruto yāthanā śubham ||
  • vāśīmanta: This term can be interpreted as "those who possess control" or "those who are powerful." In real life, this can refer to individuals or leaders who have the ability to influence and guide others effectively.
  • ṛṣṭimanto: This word signifies "those who are righteous" or "those who follow the path of truth." In contemporary society, this can be seen in individuals who uphold ethical standards and integrity in their actions, such as activists fighting for justice.
  • manīṣiṇaḥ: This translates to "the wise" or "the intelligent." In everyday life, this can refer to people who make informed decisions based on knowledge and experience, such as educators or mentors who guide others.
  • sudhanvāna: Meaning "those with good bows," this can symbolize individuals who are well-prepared and equipped to face challenges. In a modern context, this could relate to professionals who are skilled and ready to tackle obstacles in their careers.
  • iṣumanto: This term means "those who possess arrows," which can represent individuals who have the tools or resources necessary to achieve their goals. For example, entrepreneurs who have the right skills and connections to launch successful businesses.
  • niṣaṅgiṇaḥ: This translates to "those who are fearless" or "those who are resolute." In real life, this can be seen in individuals who stand up for their beliefs despite facing opposition, such as whistleblowers or social reformers.
  • svaśvāḥ: This term can be interpreted as "those who are self-reliant" or "those who are independent." In today's world, this can refer to individuals who take charge of their own lives and decisions, such as freelancers or self-employed individuals.
  • stha: This means "to stand firm" or "to be stable." In a practical sense, this can relate to people who maintain their principles and values in the face of adversity, such as community leaders who advocate for their neighborhoods.
  • surathāḥ: This translates to "those who are well-guided" or "those who are fortunate." In real life, this can refer to individuals who have mentors or support systems that help them navigate challenges successfully.
  • pṛśnimātaraḥ: This term can mean "those who are swift" or "those who are agile." In a modern context, this can relate to individuals or organizations that adapt quickly to changes in their environment, such as tech companies that innovate rapidly.
  • svāyudhā: This translates to "those who are armed with their own weapons," symbolizing self-sufficiency and preparedness. In real life, this can refer to individuals who equip themselves with knowledge and skills to face life's challenges.
  • maruto: This term refers to "the storm gods" or "the forces of nature," symbolizing power and energy. In a contemporary context, this can relate to individuals or movements that harness collective energy for social change, such as environmental activists.
  • yāthanā: This means "to strive" or "to endeavor." In real life, this can be seen in individuals who work hard to achieve their dreams, such as students pursuing higher education or athletes training for competitions.
  • śubham: This translates to "auspicious" or "good." In a practical sense, this can refer to positive outcomes resulting from righteous actions, such as community service leading to improved social conditions.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of qualities such as wisdom, righteousness, preparedness, and fearlessness. It encourages individuals to be self-reliant and to strive for positive outcomes in their lives and communities. In real-life scenarios, these attributes are essential for personal growth, leadership, and social change. By embodying these qualities, individuals can contribute to a more just and harmonious society.
