अध श्वेतं कलशं गोभिरक्तं आपिप्याणं मगवाऽशुक्रं अन्धः |अध्वर्युभिः प्रयतम् मध्वो अग्रं इन्द्रो मदाय प्रतिधात् पिबध्यै शूरो मदाय प्रतिधात् पिबध्यै ||
adha śvetaṁ kalaśaṁ gobhir aktam āpipyānam maghavā śukram andhaḥ | adhvaryubhiḥ prayatam madhvo agram indro madāya prati dhat pibadhyai śūro madāya prati dhat pibadhyai ||
  • Adha: This word translates to "down" or "below," indicating a direction or a state of being.
  • Śvetaṁ: Meaning "white," it symbolizes purity, clarity, and auspiciousness.
  • Kalaśaṁ: Refers to a "pot" or "vessel," often used in rituals to hold sacred substances.
  • Gobhir: This means "by the cows," which can symbolize nourishment, wealth, and prosperity in Vedic culture.
  • Aktam: Translates to "filled" or "saturated," suggesting abundance.
  • Āpipyānam: Means "to be filled" or "to be satisfied," indicating a state of fulfillment.
  • Maghavā: Refers to "the mighty one," often associated with the deity Indra, representing strength and power.
  • Śukram: Meaning "bright" or "pure," it signifies clarity and brilliance.
  • Andhaḥ: Translates to "blind" or "dark," which can symbolize ignorance or lack of awareness.
  • Adhvaryubhiḥ: Refers to "the priests" or "those who perform rituals," indicating the importance of ritualistic practices.
  • Prayatam: Means "with effort" or "with intention," emphasizing the need for dedication in actions.
  • Madhvo: Refers to "sweetness" or "honey," symbolizing the essence of life and joy.
  • Agram: Meaning "the best" or "the foremost," indicating the highest quality or priority.
  • Indro: Refers to "Indra," the king of gods, representing authority and leadership.
  • Madāya: Means "for intoxication" or "for joy," suggesting a state of bliss or ecstasy.
  • Prati dhat: Translates to "to give" or "to bestow," indicating generosity and sharing.
  • Pibadhyai: Means "to drink," symbolizing the act of receiving or taking in blessings.
  • Śūro: Refers to "the brave" or "the strong," emphasizing courage and valor.

This mantra can be interpreted as a call for abundance, purity, and strength. It invokes the blessings of Indra and emphasizes the importance of rituals and efforts in achieving fulfillment and joy. The imagery of a white pot filled with nourishing substances, surrounded by cows, symbolizes prosperity and the richness of life.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • In personal development, it encourages individuals to seek clarity and purity in their intentions and actions.
  • In community rituals, it highlights the importance of collective effort and the sharing of resources for mutual benefit.
  • In leadership, it serves as a reminder for leaders to be generous and to provide for their followers, fostering a sense of unity and strength.

Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its call for a harmonious balance between effort, generosity, and the pursuit of purity and strength. It serves as a reminder that through dedicated actions and a clear intention, one can achieve abundance and joy in life. This mantra can be recited during rituals, personal reflections, or community gatherings to invoke blessings and foster a spirit of togetherness and prosperity.
