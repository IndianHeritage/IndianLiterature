स्तुषे नराः दिवो अस्य प्रसन्ताश्विना हुवे जरामाणो अर्कैः |या सद्य उष्रा व्युषि ज्मो अन्तान युयूषतः पर्य उरू वरांसी ||
stuṣe narā divo asya prasantāśvinā huve jaramāṇo arkaiḥ | yā sadya usrā vyuṣi jmo antān yuyūṣataḥ pary urū varāṁsi ||
  • stuṣe: This word can be translated as "to praise" or "to extol." It signifies the act of offering respect and admiration.
  • narā: This means "men" or "humans," indicating the focus on human beings and their actions.
  • divo: Translated as "of the heavens" or "of the sky," it connects the earthly realm with the divine.
  • asya: This means "of this," referring to the context or subject being discussed.
  • prasantāśvinā: This term refers to the "two Ashvins," who are divine twin horsemen in Vedic mythology, symbolizing health, medicine, and the dawn.
  • huve: This means "I invoke" or "I call upon," indicating a prayer or supplication.
  • jaramāṇo: This translates to "aging" or "growing old," reflecting the human condition and the inevitability of aging.
  • arkaiḥ: This means "with hymns" or "with praises," suggesting the use of words and songs to honor the divine.
  • yā: This means "who" or "which," introducing a clause that describes a particular aspect.
  • sadya: Translated as "immediately" or "suddenly," indicating a sense of urgency or immediacy.
  • usrā: This means "the dawn" or "the light," symbolizing new beginnings and hope.
  • vyuṣi: This translates to "in the morning," reinforcing the theme of renewal and the start of a new day.
  • jmo: This can be interpreted as "to come" or "to arrive," suggesting the arrival of something significant.
  • antān: This means "the end" or "the limits," indicating boundaries or conclusions.
  • yuyūṣataḥ: This translates to "desiring" or "longing for," reflecting human aspirations and desires.
  • pary: This means "around" or "about," indicating a sense of encompassing or surrounding.
  • urū: This translates to "wide" or "broad," suggesting expansiveness and inclusivity.
  • varāṁsi: This means "blessings" or "boons," indicating the gifts or favors bestowed by the divine.

The mantra essentially invokes the divine Ashvins, praising them for their blessings and assistance in overcoming the challenges of aging and the human condition. It reflects a deep appreciation for the divine forces that guide and support humanity, especially during times of transition, such as the dawn of a new day.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • Health and Well-being: Just as the Ashvins are associated with health and medicine, individuals can invoke this mantra during times of illness or when seeking wellness, emphasizing the importance of divine support in healing.
  • New Beginnings: The reference to dawn symbolizes new opportunities. People can recite this mantra when starting a new venture, job, or phase in life, seeking blessings for success and guidance.
  • Reflection on Aging: The acknowledgment of aging reminds individuals to embrace the process of growing older with grace and wisdom, encouraging a positive outlook on life’s transitions.

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of humanity and the divine. It emphasizes the importance of seeking blessings and guidance in our lives, particularly during moments of change and uncertainty. By invoking the Ashvins, individuals can cultivate a sense of hope, health, and renewal, making it a significant part of spiritual practice and daily life.
