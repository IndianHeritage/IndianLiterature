जुषेथां यज्ञं बोधतं हवस्य मे विश्वेह देवौ सवनावा गच्छताम् |सजोषसां उषसा सूर्येण चेषं नो वोळ्हम् अश्विनाः ||
juṣethāṁ yajñam bodhataṁ havasya me viśveha devau savanāva gacchatam | sajoṣasā uṣasā sūryeṇa ceṣaṁ no voḻham aśvinā ||
  • juṣethāṁ: This word translates to "may we enjoy" or "may we be pleased." It signifies a desire for joy and fulfillment in life.
  • yajñam: Refers to "sacrifice" or "offering." In the Vedic context, yajña is a ritual of offerings to the deities, symbolizing the importance of selflessness and community.
  • bodhataṁ: This means "may we be enlightened" or "may we gain knowledge." It emphasizes the pursuit of wisdom and understanding.
  • havasya: Translates to "of the offerings" or "of the oblations." It highlights the significance of offerings in rituals and their connection to divine blessings.
  • me: Means "my" or "to me," indicating a personal connection to the offerings and the divine.
  • viśveha: This means "all" or "universal," suggesting inclusivity and the idea that the offerings are for all beings.
  • devau: Refers to "gods" or "deities," indicating the divine entities to whom the offerings are made.
  • savanāva: This translates to "may they be invoked" or "may they come," expressing a wish for the presence of the divine in the rituals.
  • gacchatam: Means "may they go" or "may they proceed," indicating a desire for the offerings to reach the deities.
  • sajoṣasā: This word means "with joy" or "with happiness," emphasizing the positive emotions associated with the rituals.
  • uṣasā: Refers to "dawn" or "morning," symbolizing new beginnings and the arrival of light and knowledge.
  • sūryeṇa: This means "with the sun," indicating the importance of the sun as a source of life and energy.
  • ceṣaṁ: Translates to "action" or "effort," highlighting the need for active participation in rituals and life.
  • no: Means "our" or "to us," indicating a collective aspect of the mantra.
  • voḻham: This word means "to you" or "to them," suggesting a connection between the worshippers and the divine.
  • aśvinā: Refers to the "Ashvins," the twin deities associated with health, medicine, and well-being, symbolizing the importance of divine blessings in our lives.

Conclusion: This mantra encapsulates the essence of Vedic rituals, emphasizing the importance of joy, knowledge, and community in our offerings to the divine. It serves as a reminder that our actions, whether in rituals or daily life, should be filled with positivity and a sense of purpose. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire individuals to engage in selfless acts, seek knowledge, and foster a sense of unity with others. By invoking the blessings of the divine, we can enhance our well-being and contribute to the greater good of society.
