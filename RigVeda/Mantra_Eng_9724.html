इन्द्राणīm आसु नारीषु सुभगाम अहम् अश्रवम् |नहि अस्याऽ अपरं चन जरा सा मरते पतिर्विश्वस्माद इन्द्र उत्तरः ||
indrāṇīm āsu nāriṣu subhagām aham aśravam | nahy asyā aparaṁ cana jarasā marate patir viśvasmād indra uttaraḥ ||
  • Indrāṇīm: This term refers to the divine or celestial qualities associated with Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic literature. Indra is often seen as a symbol of strength, power, and leadership.
  • Āsu: This word means 'quickly' or 'swiftly,' indicating a sense of urgency or immediacy in the context of the mantra.
  • Nāriṣu: This term translates to 'among women,' suggesting a focus on feminine qualities or the role of women in society.
  • Subhagām: Meaning 'auspicious' or 'fortunate,' this word highlights the positive attributes and blessings associated with the subject of the mantra.
  • Aham: This means 'I' or 'myself,' indicating a personal connection or declaration from the speaker.
  • Aśravam: This term can be interpreted as 'I have spoken' or 'I have declared,' suggesting an assertion of intent or purpose.
  • Nahy: This means 'not' or 'no,' indicating a negation or denial of something.
  • Aparāṁ: This translates to 'another' or 'further,' suggesting a limitation or boundary in the context of the mantra.
  • Cana: This word means 'at all' or 'in any way,' reinforcing the negation expressed by 'nahy.'
  • Jarasā: This term refers to 'old age' or 'decay,' symbolizing the inevitable decline that comes with time.
  • Marate: This means 'to die' or 'to perish,' indicating the transient nature of life.
  • Patir: This translates to 'lord' or 'master,' often referring to a higher power or authority.
  • Viśvasmād: This means 'from all' or 'from the universe,' suggesting a universal perspective.
  • Indra: Again referring to the deity Indra, emphasizing his significance in the context of the mantra.
  • Uttaraḥ: This term means 'higher' or 'superior,' indicating a sense of elevation or transcendence.

The mantra expresses a deep connection to the divine qualities of Indra, particularly in relation to women and the auspiciousness they bring. It emphasizes the speaker's declaration of intent and the recognition of the limitations imposed by time and mortality. The mantra suggests that despite the inevitability of aging and death, there is a higher power (Indra) that transcends these limitations.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as:

  • Empowerment of Women: Recognizing the strength and auspiciousness of women in society, encouraging their leadership and contributions.
  • Acceptance of Mortality: Understanding and accepting the transient nature of life, which can lead to a more meaningful existence.
  • Spiritual Connection: Fostering a connection with higher powers or divine qualities, which can provide guidance and strength in challenging times.

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the divine qualities present in all beings, the importance of recognizing the strengths of women, and the acceptance of life's impermanence. It encourages individuals to seek higher understanding and empowerment, both personally and collectively.
