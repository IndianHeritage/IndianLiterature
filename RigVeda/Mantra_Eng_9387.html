मो षु णः सोम मृत्यवे परा दाः पश्येम नु सूर्यं उच्छरन्तम् |द्युर्भिर हितो जरीमा सू नो अस्तु परातरं सु निरृतिर जिहीताम् ||
mo ṣu ṇaḥ soma mṛtyave parā dāḥ paśyema nu sūryam uccarantam | dyubhir hito jarimā sū no astu parātaraṁ su nirṛtir jihītām ||
  • mo ṣu ṇaḥ: This phrase can be interpreted as "may we be free from" or "may we not be affected by." It expresses a desire for liberation from negative influences or experiences.
  • soma: Soma is often associated with a sacred drink in Vedic tradition, symbolizing immortality and divine inspiration. It represents the essence of life and vitality.
  • mṛtyave: This word means "death" or "mortality." It acknowledges the inevitability of death and the transient nature of life.
  • parā dāḥ: This can be translated as "the highest gifts" or "supreme offerings." It signifies the pursuit of the highest spiritual and material blessings.
  • paśyema: This means "may we see" or "may we perceive." It is a call for awareness and understanding of the world around us.
  • nu sūryam: "nu" can be interpreted as "now" or "indeed," while "sūryam" refers to the sun. Together, they express a desire to witness the sun, which symbolizes enlightenment and knowledge.
  • uccarantam: This word means "rising" or "ascending." It signifies the upward movement towards higher consciousness and spiritual awakening.
  • dyubhir hito: This phrase can be understood as "by the heavens" or "through the skies." It suggests a connection to the divine and the cosmic order.
  • jarimā: This word refers to "old age" or "decay." It acknowledges the natural process of aging and the challenges that come with it.
  • sū no astu: This translates to "may there be for us" or "let it be for us." It expresses a wish for well-being and prosperity.
  • parātaraṁ: This means "the highest" or "the supreme." It emphasizes the aspiration for the ultimate truth and realization.
  • su nirṛtir: "su" means "good" or "auspicious," while "nirṛtir" refers to "disorder" or "chaos." This phrase expresses a desire for harmony and order in life.
  • jihītām: This word means "may it be removed" or "may it be dispelled." It is a plea for the removal of negativity and obstacles.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates a profound aspiration for liberation from the cycles of death and decay, while seeking enlightenment and the highest blessings in life. It emphasizes the importance of awareness, spiritual growth, and the pursuit of harmony. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in moments of reflection, meditation, or during challenges, reminding individuals to seek clarity, positivity, and a deeper understanding of their existence. It serves as a guiding principle for those striving for personal and spiritual development, encouraging them to rise above difficulties and embrace the light of knowledge and wisdom.
