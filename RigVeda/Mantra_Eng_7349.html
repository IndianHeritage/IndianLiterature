इमं नु मायिनं हुव इन्द्रं ईशानम् ओजसा |मरुत्वन्तं न वृञ्जसे ||
imaṁ nu māyinaṁ huva indram īśānam ojasā | marutvantaṁ na vṛñjase ||
  • Translation: "I invoke the mighty Indra, the lord of strength, who is the master of the illusion. You, O Maruts, do not forsake us."

  • Word Breakdown:
    • imaṁ: This means "this" or "here," indicating a direct invocation.
    • nu: A particle used for emphasis, often translated as "indeed" or "now."
    • māyinaṁ: Refers to "the one who creates illusion" or "the powerful," often associated with divine forces.
    • huva: This is a verb meaning "I invoke" or "I call upon."
    • indram: Refers to "Indra," the king of gods in Vedic tradition, symbolizing strength and power.
    • īśānam: Meaning "lord" or "master," indicating authority and control.
    • ojasā: Translates to "with strength" or "with vigor," emphasizing the power of the invocation.
    • marutvantaṁ: Refers to "the Maruts," the storm deities associated with wind and strength.
    • na: Means "not," indicating a plea or request.
    • vṛñjase: Means "to forsake" or "to abandon," expressing a desire for support and protection.

  • Contextual Significance:
    • This mantra is a call for divine assistance, particularly from Indra and the Maruts, who are seen as powerful forces in nature.
    • In real life, this invocation can be likened to seeking help during challenging times, such as during natural disasters or personal crises, where one feels overwhelmed.
    • For example, during a storm, people may pray for safety and strength, similar to invoking the Maruts for protection.

  • Conclusion:
    • The mantra emphasizes the importance of seeking divine support and strength in times of need.
    • It serves as a reminder that, like the forces of nature, we can call upon higher powers for guidance and protection.
    • In modern life, this can be applicable in various scenarios, such as during personal challenges, community struggles, or even global issues, where collective strength and support are essential.
