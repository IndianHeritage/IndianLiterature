शुष्णं पिप्रुं कुवयं वृत्रम इन्द्र यदावधीर वि पुरः शम्बरस्य |तन नो मित्रो वरुणो मामहन्ताम् अदितिः सिन्धुः पृथिवी उता द्यौः ||
śuṣṇam pipruṁ kuyavaṁ vṛtram indra yadāvadhīr vi puraḥ śambarasya | tan no mitro varuṇo māmahantām aditiḥ sindhuḥ pṛthivī uta dyauḥ ||
  • śuṣṇam pipruṁ kuyavaṁ vṛtram indra yadāvadhīr vi puraḥ śambarasya
    • śuṣṇam: This word can be interpreted as "the one who is dry" or "the one who is parched." It symbolizes a state of lack or need.
    • pipruṁ: This refers to "the one who is red" or "the one who is vibrant." It can signify vitality or life force.
    • kuyavaṁ: This term can mean "the one who is deceitful" or "the one who is harmful." It represents obstacles or negative forces.
    • vṛtram: This word means "the obstacle" or "the demon." In the context of the mantra, it refers to challenges that hinder progress.
    • indra: Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic literature, representing strength, power, and the ability to overcome challenges.
    • yadāvadhīr: This means "who has vanquished" or "who has defeated." It signifies triumph over adversity.
    • vi puraḥ: This translates to "in front" or "before." It indicates the presence of challenges that need to be faced.
    • śambarasya: Referring to "the demon Shambara," it symbolizes formidable challenges or negative forces that need to be overcome.
  • tan no mitro varuṇo māmahantām aditiḥ sindhuḥ pṛthivī uta dyauḥ
    • tan: This means "that" or "this," referring to the divine qualities being invoked.
    • no: This translates to "for us," indicating a plea or request for assistance.
    • mitro: This refers to "Mitra," the god of friendship and harmony, symbolizing support and positive relationships.
    • varuṇo: This is "Varuna," the god of cosmic order and water, representing the natural laws and balance in life.
    • māmahantām: This means "the great one" or "the powerful one," indicating the strength of the divine forces being called upon.
    • aditiḥ: Aditi is the mother of the gods, symbolizing boundless space and the nurturing aspect of the universe.
    • sindhuḥ: This means "ocean" or "river," representing the flow of life and abundance.
    • pṛthivī: This refers to "the earth," symbolizing stability and grounding.
    • uta dyauḥ: This translates to "and the sky," representing the vastness and the connection between the earth and the heavens.

  • Conclusion:
    • The mantra speaks to the struggle against obstacles and the invocation of divine support to overcome them.
    • In real life, this can be applied to various challenges we face, such as personal struggles, professional hurdles, or societal issues.
    • By calling upon the qualities of strength, harmony, and nurturing, individuals can find the resilience needed to navigate difficulties.
    • This mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life, the importance of seeking support, and the power of overcoming adversity with divine assistance.
