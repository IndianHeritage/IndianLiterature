गोमां अग्ने विमां अश्वी यज्ञो नृवत्सखाः सदमिद अप्रमृष्यः |इळावां एषो असुर प्रजावान दीर्घो रयः पृथुबुध्नः सभावान् ||
gomām̐ agne 'vimām̐ aśvī yajño nṛvatsakhā sadam id apramṛṣyaḥ | iḻāvām̐ eṣo asura prajāvān dīrgho rayiḥ pṛthubudhnaḥ sabhāvān ||
  • gomām̐: This word refers to "cows," which are often seen as symbols of wealth and nourishment in Vedic literature. In real life, cows are essential for agriculture and dairy production, providing milk and other resources.
  • agne: This means "O Agni," referring to the fire god, who is a significant deity in Vedic rituals. Fire symbolizes purity and is a medium for offerings to the gods. In modern life, fire is essential for cooking, heating, and energy production.
  • 'vimām̐: This term can be interpreted as "we invoke" or "we call upon." It signifies the act of seeking divine assistance or blessings, which is a common practice in various cultures today, such as prayer or meditation.
  • aśvī: This translates to "horses," which symbolize strength, speed, and vitality. In contemporary society, horses are still valued in sports, agriculture, and as companions, representing freedom and grace.
  • yajño: This word means "sacrifice" or "offering," which is a central theme in Vedic rituals. Sacrifices can be seen in modern contexts as acts of charity or service to others, reflecting selflessness and community support.
  • nṛvatsakhā: This term refers to "the one who is friendly to humans." It emphasizes the importance of harmony and cooperation among people, which is vital in today's interconnected world.
  • sadam: This means "a place of dwelling" or "home." It highlights the significance of having a safe and nurturing environment, which is essential for personal and communal well-being.
  • id: This word can be interpreted as "this" or "here," indicating presence and immediacy. It reminds us to be mindful of our current circumstances and surroundings.
  • apramṛṣyaḥ: This term means "unobstructed" or "unhindered," suggesting the idea of freedom and the ability to pursue one's goals without barriers. In real life, this can relate to the pursuit of education, career, and personal aspirations.
  • iḻāvām̐: This can be understood as "we are endowed" or "we possess." It reflects the idea of abundance and the blessings one receives, which can be seen in the appreciation of resources and opportunities in life.
  • eṣo: This means "this" or "he," referring to a specific entity or presence, emphasizing the importance of recognizing and acknowledging the divine or significant aspects in our lives.
  • asura: This term often refers to "powerful beings" or "deities." In modern contexts, it can symbolize the pursuit of strength and wisdom in overcoming challenges.
  • prajāvān: This means "one who is wise" or "knowledgeable." It highlights the value of wisdom and knowledge in making informed decisions in life.
  • dīrgho: This translates to "long" or "lasting," suggesting the importance of longevity and sustainability in our actions and relationships.
  • rayiḥ: This word means "wealth" or "prosperity," emphasizing the significance of material and spiritual abundance in life.
  • pṛthubudhnaḥ: This term refers to "broad-minded" or "open-minded," highlighting the importance of inclusivity and understanding in a diverse world.
  • sabhāvān: This means "one who has a good nature" or "virtuous." It underscores the importance of character and ethics in personal and professional relationships.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of invoking divine blessings for prosperity, wisdom, and harmonious living. It emphasizes the interconnectedness of nature, community, and spirituality. In real life, this mantra serves as a reminder to appreciate the resources we have, to act selflessly, and to seek knowledge and understanding. It encourages us to create a nurturing environment for ourselves and others, fostering a sense of unity and purpose in our daily lives.
