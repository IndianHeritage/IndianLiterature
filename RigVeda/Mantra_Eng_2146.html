अध्वर्यवो यो अपो ववृवांसं वृतं जगहानाशन्येव वृक्षम् |तस्मा एतम् भारत तद्वशायं एष इन्द्रो अर्हति पीतिम अस्य ||
adhvaryavo yo apo vavrivāṁsaṁ vṛtraṁ jaghānāśanyeva vṛkṣam | tasmā etam bharata tadvaśāyam̐ eṣa indro arhati pītim asya ||
  • adhvaryavo: This term refers to the priests who perform the rituals, particularly the ones who are responsible for the offerings to the deities. In real life, this can be likened to individuals who take on leadership roles in various ceremonies or community events, ensuring that traditions are upheld.
  • yo: This means "who" or "that," indicating a subject or a doer. It emphasizes the active role of the priests in the rituals.
  • apo: This word translates to "waters," which in Vedic literature often symbolizes purity, life, and sustenance. In modern contexts, water is essential for survival and is a symbol of nourishment and cleansing.
  • vavrivāṁsaṁ: This term can be interpreted as "having taken" or "having seized." It suggests an action of claiming or obtaining something significant.
  • vṛtraṁ: Vṛtra is a demon or a force that represents obstacles or challenges. In everyday life, this can be seen as the struggles we face, whether they are personal, social, or professional.
  • jaghānāśanyeva: This phrase means "having slain" or "having overcome." It signifies victory over challenges, akin to overcoming fears or obstacles in life.
  • vṛkṣam: This translates to "tree," which symbolizes growth, stability, and life. Trees are essential for the environment, providing oxygen and shelter, and can also represent personal growth and resilience.
  • tasmā: This means "therefore," indicating a conclusion or a result of the previous actions.
  • etam: This translates to "this," referring to the subject of the mantra, which is the actions and their significance.
  • bharata: This term can refer to the people of Bharat (India) or the concept of unity and community. It emphasizes the collective strength and identity of a group.
  • tadvaśāyam̐: This means "under its influence" or "by its power," suggesting that the actions taken have a profound impact on the community.
  • eṣa: This translates to "this" or "he," referring to Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic mythology.
  • indro: Indra is often associated with strength, power, and the ability to overcome challenges. In real life, this can be likened to leaders or figures who inspire and empower others.
  • arhati: This means "deserves" or "is worthy of," indicating that the actions taken are commendable and deserving of recognition.
  • pītim: This translates to "nourishment" or "satisfaction," suggesting that the outcome of the actions leads to fulfillment and well-being.
  • asya: This means "of this," referring to the collective actions and their results.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of overcoming challenges through collective effort and ritualistic practices. It highlights the importance of community, leadership, and the pursuit of nourishment and fulfillment. In real life, this can be applied in various scenarios such as community service, teamwork in professional settings, or even personal growth journeys where individuals face and overcome their own Vṛtras. The significance lies in recognizing that through unity and purposeful action, individuals and communities can achieve greatness and satisfaction, much like the priests who perform rituals to invoke divine blessings.
