सम् माऽ तपन्त्य अभितः sapatnīr इव पर्शवः |निबाधते अमतिर नग्नता जसुर वेर न वेवीयते मतिः ||
sam mā tapanty abhitaḥ sapatnīr iva parśavaḥ | ni bādhate amatir nagnatā jasur ver na vevīyate matiḥ ||
  • Translation: "As the co-wives do not harm each other, so the thoughts of the wise do not harm one another. The unwise are not hindered by nakedness; the wise are not disturbed by anger."
  • Word-by-Word Explanation:
    • sam: together, in harmony
    • mā: not
    • tapanty: harm, trouble
    • abhitaḥ: around, in the vicinity
    • sapatnīr: co-wives, rivals
    • iva: like, as
    • parśavaḥ: thoughts, intentions
    • ni: not
    • bādhate: hinder, obstruct
    • amatir: unwise, foolish
    • nagnatā: nakedness, vulnerability
    • jasur: wise, knowledgeable
    • ver: anger, hostility
    • na: not
    • vevīyate: disturbed, shaken
    • matiḥ: mind, intellect
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In a workplace, colleagues can have differing opinions and approaches to tasks. If they respect each other's perspectives and work collaboratively, they can achieve better results without causing harm to one another.
    • In family dynamics, siblings may have conflicts, but if they learn to communicate openly and support each other, they can maintain harmony and avoid unnecessary disputes.
    • In community settings, diverse groups may have conflicting interests. However, through dialogue and understanding, they can coexist peacefully and work towards common goals.
  • Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of harmony and understanding among individuals, particularly in situations of rivalry or conflict. It teaches that wise individuals can coexist peacefully, regardless of their differences, and that unwise actions, such as anger and hostility, can lead to unnecessary strife. In real life, this mantra is applicable in various scenarios, including personal relationships, professional environments, and community interactions. By fostering a mindset of respect and collaboration, we can create a more harmonious society.
