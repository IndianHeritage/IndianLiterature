याभिः सोमो मोदते हर्षते च कल्याणीभिर युवतिभिर न मर्यः |ताऽध्वर्यो अपो अच्छा परेहि यद आसिञ्चा ओषधिभिः पुनीतात ||
yābhiḥ somo modate harṣate ca kalyāṇībhir yuvatibhir na maryaḥ | tā adhvaryo apo acchā parehi yad āsiñcā oṣadhībhiḥ punītāt ||
  • yābhiḥ - "by whom" or "with whom": This word indicates a connection or relationship, suggesting that the following actions or states are influenced by certain entities or forces.
  • somo - "soma": In Vedic literature, Soma is both a deity and a sacred drink that is believed to confer immortality and joy. It symbolizes the essence of life and vitality.
  • modate - "rejoices": This term conveys a sense of joy and celebration, indicating a state of happiness or bliss.
  • harṣate - "delights": Similar to "modate," this word emphasizes the feeling of delight and pleasure, reinforcing the theme of joy.
  • ca - "and": A conjunction that connects the ideas presented in the mantra.
  • kalyāṇībhir - "with auspicious ones": This term refers to those who are virtuous or bring good fortune, highlighting the importance of positive influences in life.
  • yuvatibhir - "by young women": This phrase suggests the involvement of youthful energy and vitality, often associated with creativity and new beginnings.
  • na - "not": A negation that indicates the absence of something.
  • maryaḥ - "mortals": This term refers to human beings, emphasizing the transient nature of life.
  • tā - "those": A demonstrative pronoun that points to the subjects being discussed.
  • adhvaryo - "the priests": This term refers to the officiating priests in Vedic rituals, who play a crucial role in connecting the divine with the earthly realm.
  • apo - "waters": Symbolizing purity and life, waters are often associated with cleansing and rejuvenation.
  • acchā - "good" or "auspicious": This word denotes positivity and beneficial qualities.
  • parehi - "go away" or "depart": This term suggests a movement away from something, often indicating a transition or change.
  • yad - "that which": A relative pronoun that introduces a clause, referring to something specific.
  • āsiñcā - "having poured": This term indicates an act of offering or bestowing, often in a ritualistic context.
  • oṣadhībhiḥ - "by herbs" or "plants": This refers to the healing and nourishing qualities of plants, which are essential for life and well-being.
  • punītāt - "purification": This word emphasizes the importance of cleansing and sanctification, both physically and spiritually.

Conclusion: The mantra speaks to the interconnectedness of joy, vitality, and auspiciousness in life. It highlights the role of divine influences, such as Soma, and the importance of positive relationships, particularly with virtuous individuals and the natural world. In real life, this can be applied to the significance of community, the joy found in relationships, and the healing power of nature. By fostering positive connections and engaging with the world around us, we can experience a sense of joy and fulfillment, much like the celebration of life depicted in this mantra. The emphasis on purification also reminds us of the need for spiritual and emotional cleansing, which can be achieved through practices such as meditation, mindfulness, and connecting with nature.
