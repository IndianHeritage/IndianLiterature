सुपर्ण इथ्था नखम् आ सिषायावरुद्धः परिपदं न सिंहः |निरुद्धश चिन् महिषस तर्ष्यावान गोधा तस्मा अयथं कर्षद एतत् ||
suparṇa itthā nakham ā siṣāyāvaruddhaḥ paripadaṁ na siṁhaḥ | niruddhaś cin mahiṣas tarṣyāvān godhā tasmā ayathaṁ karṣad etat ||
  • suparṇa: This term refers to a "beautifully winged" or "well-favored" entity, often associated with birds, particularly the mythical Garuda in Hindu mythology. In real life, this can symbolize freedom and the pursuit of higher knowledge.
  • itthā: This word means "thus" or "in this manner," indicating a way of being or a state of existence. It suggests that the following actions or states are to be understood in a specific context.
  • nakham: Translated as "nail" or "claw," this can represent strength and the ability to grasp or hold onto something. In a real-life context, it can symbolize the tools or skills we use to achieve our goals.
  • ā siṣāyāvaruddhaḥ: This phrase can be interpreted as "having been restrained or held back." It suggests a state of limitation or control, which can be seen in situations where individuals or groups face obstacles in their pursuits.
  • paripadaṁ: Meaning "to surround" or "to encompass," this term indicates a protective or encompassing force. In life, this can relate to the support systems we have, such as family and friends, that help us navigate challenges.
  • na siṁhaḥ: Translated as "not a lion," this phrase can imply a lack of ferocity or dominance. In a real-life scenario, it may refer to situations where one must adopt a more subtle or strategic approach rather than relying on brute strength.
  • niruddhaś: This means "restrained" or "held back," indicating a state of being confined or limited. This can be applicable in contexts where individuals feel trapped by circumstances or societal expectations.
  • cin mahiṣas: This phrase translates to "the great buffalo," which can symbolize strength and resilience. In real life, it can represent the challenges we face that require us to tap into our inner strength to overcome.
  • tarṣyāvān: Meaning "thirsty" or "desiring," this term reflects a yearning for something greater, whether it be knowledge, success, or fulfillment. It highlights the human condition of always seeking more.
  • godhā: This word refers to "the cow," which is often seen as a symbol of nourishment and sustenance in many cultures. In a practical sense, it can represent the resources we rely on for our well-being.
  • tasmā: Meaning "therefore," this word indicates a conclusion or result stemming from the previous statements. It emphasizes the importance of understanding the context before making decisions.
  • ayathaṁ: This translates to "as it is" or "in this way," suggesting acceptance of the current state of affairs. It encourages individuals to acknowledge their reality before taking action.
  • karṣad: Meaning "to cultivate" or "to work," this term emphasizes the importance of effort and labor in achieving one's goals. It reflects the idea that success comes from hard work and dedication.
  • etat: This means "this," referring to the subject matter at hand, which ties back to the overall theme of the mantra.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the journey of life, highlighting the interplay between freedom, strength, limitations, and the pursuit of desires. It serves as a reminder that while we may face obstacles and constraints, it is through our efforts and the support of those around us that we can cultivate our paths. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied to personal growth, professional endeavors, and the pursuit of knowledge, encouraging individuals to embrace their circumstances and work diligently towards their aspirations.
