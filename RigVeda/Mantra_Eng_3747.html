प्राग्नये बृहत् यज्ञियाय ऋतस्य वृष्णे असुराय मनमा |घृतं न यज्ञ आस्ये सुपूतं गिरं भरे वृषभाय प्रतिचीम् ||
prāgnaye bṛhate yajñiyāya ṛtasya vṛṣṇe asurāya manma | ghṛtaṁ na yajña āsye3 supūtaṁ giram bhare vṛṣabhāya pratīcīm ||
  • prāgnaye: This term refers to wisdom or knowledge. It emphasizes the importance of understanding and insight in our actions and decisions.
  • bṛhate: Meaning "great" or "vast," this word signifies the expansive nature of the universe and the divine.
  • yajñiyāya: This relates to sacrifice or offerings, particularly in the context of rituals that honor the divine and seek blessings.
  • ṛtasya: Referring to "truth" or "cosmic order," this word highlights the importance of living in harmony with the natural laws of the universe.
  • vṛṣṇe: This term is associated with the divine, often linked to Lord Vishnu, representing preservation and protection.
  • asurāya: This word can mean "to the non-divine" or "to the demons," indicating a contrast between divine and non-divine forces.
  • manma: This translates to "my mind" or "my thoughts," suggesting a personal connection to the divine and the importance of intention.
  • ghṛtaṁ: Meaning "ghee" or "clarified butter," this is a sacred offering in Vedic rituals, symbolizing purity and nourishment.
  • na yajña āsye: This phrase can be interpreted as "in the mouth of the sacrifice," indicating the central role of offerings in rituals.
  • supūtaṁ: This means "well-prepared" or "well-cooked," emphasizing the quality of offerings made to the divine.
  • giram: Referring to "speech" or "words," this highlights the power of communication and the importance of chanting mantras.
  • bhare: This means "to fill" or "to bring forth," suggesting the act of invoking blessings or abundance.
  • vṛṣabhāya: This term can be interpreted as "to the bull," which is often a symbol of strength and fertility in Vedic literature.
  • pratīcīm: Meaning "towards the west," this direction is often associated with the setting sun and the end of cycles, symbolizing completion and reflection.

Conclusion: This mantra encapsulates the essence of Vedic rituals, emphasizing the importance of knowledge, truth, and divine connection in our lives. It serves as a reminder to approach our actions with wisdom and intention, ensuring that our offerings—whether they be in the form of prayers, good deeds, or personal sacrifices—are made with purity and sincerity. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire individuals to engage in self-reflection, cultivate positive intentions, and contribute to the greater good, aligning their actions with the cosmic order. By doing so, one can foster a harmonious relationship with the universe and experience the blessings of abundance and protection in their lives.
