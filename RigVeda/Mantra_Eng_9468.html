त्रिः सप्त सस्रां नद्यो महीर अपो वनस्पतīn पर्वतां अग्निम ऊतये |कृषाणुम अस्त्रं तिष्यं सदस्थ आ रुद्रं रुद्रेषु रुद्रियं हवामहे ||
triḥ sapta sasrā nadyo mahīr apo vanaspatīn parvatām̐ agnim ūtaye | kṛśānum astṝn tiṣyaṁ sadhastha ā rudraṁ rudreṣu rudriyaṁ havāmahe ||
  • triḥ sapta sasrā nadyo: This phrase translates to "three times seven hundred rivers." It signifies the abundance and diversity of water sources in nature, which are essential for life. In real life, rivers are crucial for agriculture, drinking water, and transportation. For example, the Ganges River in India is not only a source of water but also holds spiritual significance for millions.
  • mahīr apo: This means "the great waters." It refers to the vastness and depth of water bodies, symbolizing the life-giving properties of water. In practical terms, this can be seen in how oceans and lakes support ecosystems and human activities, such as fishing and tourism.
  • vanaspatīn: This word means "plants" or "vegetation." It highlights the importance of flora in sustaining life. For instance, forests provide oxygen, habitat for wildlife, and resources for medicine and food. The Amazon rainforest is a prime example of how vital vegetation is for the planet's health.
  • parvatām̐: This translates to "mountains." Mountains are often seen as sacred in many cultures and are vital for biodiversity. They influence climate and weather patterns and are sources of rivers. The Himalayas, for example, are not only a natural barrier but also a source of major rivers in Asia.
  • agnim ūtaye: This means "we invoke the fire." Fire symbolizes energy, transformation, and purification. In daily life, fire is essential for cooking, heating, and even in industrial processes. The use of fire in rituals also signifies its spiritual importance, as seen in various cultural ceremonies.
  • kṛśānum astṝn: This phrase can be interpreted as "the one who is small or humble." It suggests the importance of humility and respect for all beings. In real life, this can be reflected in how we treat others, regardless of their status or background, promoting kindness and empathy in society.
  • tiṣyaṁ sadhastha: This means "to be steadfast in the assembly." It emphasizes the value of community and collaboration. In modern life, teamwork in workplaces or community service projects illustrates how collective efforts can lead to greater achievements.
  • ā rudraṁ rudreṣu rudriyaṁ: This refers to invoking Rudra, a deity associated with storms and the hunt. It symbolizes the duality of destruction and creation. In real life, this can be seen in how natural disasters can lead to rebuilding efforts and innovations in infrastructure, showcasing resilience.
  • havāmahe: This means "we offer." It signifies the act of giving and sharing, which is fundamental in human relationships. In everyday life, acts of charity and community support reflect this principle, fostering a sense of belonging and mutual aid.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the interconnectedness of nature, the importance of humility, community, and the duality of existence. Its significance lies in reminding us to respect and nurture our environment, work together for common goals, and recognize the value of every being. In real life, these principles can guide our actions towards sustainability, compassion, and resilience in the face of challenges.
