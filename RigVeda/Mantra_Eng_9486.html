पर्जन्यावताऽवृषभा पुरिषिणेन्द्रवायू वरुणो मित्रो आर्यमाः |देवां आदित्यां अदितिं हवामहे ये पार्थिवासो दिव्यासो अप्सु ये ||
parjanyāvātā vṛṣabhā purīṣiṇendravāyū varuṇo mitro aryamā | devām̐ ādityām̐ aditiṁ havāmahe ye pārthivāso divyāso apsu ye ||
  • Parjanyāvātā: This term refers to the winds that bring rain. In a real-life context, this can be likened to the importance of monsoon winds in agriculture, which are crucial for crop growth.
  • Vṛṣabhā: This word means "the bull," symbolizing strength and fertility. In farming communities, bulls are often seen as vital for plowing fields, representing hard work and productivity.
  • Purīṣiṇendravāyū: This phrase can be interpreted as the powerful wind or air that is akin to the might of Indra, the king of gods. In modern terms, this can relate to the energy and force of nature, such as storms that can both create and destroy.
  • Varuṇo: Varuna is the god of water and cosmic order. In everyday life, this can be seen in the importance of water conservation and management, especially in areas facing drought.
  • Mitro: This term means "friend," indicating the importance of friendship and community. In real life, this can be reflected in the support systems we build with friends and family.
  • Aryamā: Aryama is associated with the ancestors and the concept of righteousness. This can be related to the values we inherit from our forebears and how they guide our moral compass.
  • Devām̐: This means "gods," representing the divine forces in our lives. In a contemporary context, this can be seen in the way people seek guidance and inspiration from spiritual beliefs.
  • Ādityām̐: Referring to the sun, this highlights the significance of sunlight for life on Earth. In practical terms, it can be related to solar energy and its role in sustainable living.
  • Aditiṁ: Aditi represents the infinite and the mother of gods. This can symbolize the nurturing aspect of nature and the universe, akin to how we care for our environment.
  • Havāmahe: This term means "we invoke" or "we call upon." It signifies the act of seeking blessings or assistance from the divine, which can be seen in various rituals and prayers in daily life.
  • Ye pārthivāso: This phrase refers to those who dwell on Earth, emphasizing our connection to the planet. In real life, this can relate to environmental stewardship and our responsibility to protect the Earth.
  • Divyāso: Meaning "divine beings," this can be interpreted as the higher ideals we strive for in our lives, such as compassion and justice.
  • Apsu ye: This refers to the waters, highlighting the essential role of water in sustaining life. In practical terms, it can relate to the importance of clean water access for communities.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the interconnectedness of nature, divinity, and humanity. It calls upon various forces—natural and divine—to support and sustain life on Earth. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied to our understanding of environmental conservation, the importance of community, and the need for spiritual guidance. By recognizing these connections, we can foster a more harmonious existence with the world around us, ensuring that we respect and nurture the resources that sustain us. This mantra serves as a reminder of our responsibilities towards nature and each other, encouraging us to live in balance and harmony.
