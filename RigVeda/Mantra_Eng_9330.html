अयं यो होतā कि रु स यमस्य कम अप्य ūहे यत समञ्जन्ति देवाः |अहर-अहर जायते मासि-मास्य अथā देवā दधिरे हव्यवाहम् ||
ayaṁ yo hotā kir u sa yamasya kam apy ūhe yat samañjanti devāḥ | ahar-ahar jāyate māsi-māsy athā devā dadhire havyavāham ||
  • ayaṁ yo hotā: This phrase refers to the one who performs the ritual or the priest. In real life, this can be likened to a leader or a guide who facilitates important events or ceremonies, such as a wedding officiant or a community leader.
  • kir u sa yamasya: This part can be interpreted as the one who is connected to the divine or the ultimate truth. In modern terms, this could represent individuals who seek to understand deeper truths in life, such as philosophers or spiritual leaders.
  • kam apy ūhe: This translates to "whatever is offered." It signifies the importance of offerings in rituals, which can be seen in various cultures today where people offer food, flowers, or prayers in temples or during festivals.
  • yat samañjanti devāḥ: This means "what the gods agree upon." It highlights the idea of harmony and consensus among divine forces. In contemporary life, this can be related to the importance of community consensus in decision-making processes, such as in local governance or group projects.
  • ahar-ahar: This phrase means "day by day." It emphasizes the continuity and regularity of rituals or practices. In real life, this can be compared to daily routines or habits that contribute to personal growth, such as meditation or exercise.
  • jāyate: This translates to "is born" or "comes into being." It signifies the emergence of new ideas or practices. In a modern context, this can relate to innovation and creativity in various fields, such as technology or art.
  • māsi-māsy: This means "month by month." It indicates the cyclical nature of time and the importance of regular observances. In real life, this can be seen in the observance of monthly rituals or celebrations, such as full moon festivals or monthly community gatherings.
  • athā devā dadhire: This translates to "the gods have established." It signifies the divine approval or establishment of certain practices. In contemporary life, this can be related to the establishment of ethical standards or laws that guide societal behavior.
  • havyavāham: This means "the one who carries offerings." It symbolizes the act of giving and sharing. In real life, this can be seen in charitable acts, where individuals or organizations provide help to those in need, embodying the spirit of generosity.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the significance of rituals, offerings, and the divine connection in our lives. It teaches us the importance of community, regular practices, and the harmony between human actions and divine will. In today's world, these principles can be applied in various scenarios, such as community service, ethical decision-making, and the establishment of meaningful traditions that foster unity and growth. By understanding and embodying these teachings, individuals can contribute to a more harmonious and compassionate society.
