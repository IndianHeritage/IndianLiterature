नकीष ṭा एता व्रता मिनन्ति नृभ्यः यद एभ्यः श्रुष्टिं चकार्थ ||
nakiṣ ṭa etā vratā minanti nṛbhyo yad ebhyaḥ śruṣṭiṁ cakartha ||
  • Word Breakdown:
    • nakiṣ: This term can be interpreted as "not" or "no." It negates the following action or state.
    • ṭa: This is a suffix that can imply a state or condition.
    • etā: Meaning "these" or "this," referring to the actions or states being discussed.
    • vratā: This word translates to "vows" or "resolutions," indicating a commitment or promise.
    • minanti: This means "to harm" or "to injure," suggesting an action that is detrimental.
    • nṛbhyo: This term refers to "humans" or "mankind," indicating the subject of the action.
    • yad: Meaning "that" or "which," it introduces a clause that provides further information.
    • ebhyaḥ: This means "from these," referring to the previous subjects or actions.
    • śruṣṭiṁ: This translates to "creation" or "manifestation," indicating the act of bringing something into existence.
    • cakartha: This means "to create" or "to make," indicating the action taken.
  • Meaning:
    • The mantra suggests that the resolutions or vows made by individuals do not harm humanity.
    • It emphasizes that the actions taken by these individuals lead to creation and positive outcomes.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • Consider a community that comes together to plant trees. Their vow to protect the environment does not harm others; instead, it contributes to the creation of a healthier ecosystem.
    • In a workplace, a team may commit to ethical practices. Their resolution to uphold integrity does not harm their colleagues but fosters a culture of trust and collaboration.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra highlights the significance of positive intentions and actions in human society.
    • It serves as a reminder that our commitments should lead to constructive outcomes, benefiting not just ourselves but also the larger community.
    • In real life, this can be applied in various scenarios, such as community service, ethical business practices, and personal relationships, where the focus is on creating a positive impact rather than causing harm.
