इदं वचः परजण्याय स्वराजे हृदो अस्त्व अन्तरं तज जुजोषत् |मयोभुवो वृष्टयः सन्त्व अस्मे सुपिप्पला ओषधीर देवगोपाः ||
idaṁ vacaḥ parjanyāya svarāje hṛdo astv antaraṁ taj jujoṣat | mayobhuvo vṛṣṭayaḥ santv asme supippalā oṣadhīr devagopāḥ ||
  • idaṁ vacaḥ: This phrase translates to "these words" or "this speech." It signifies the importance of communication and the power of words in shaping our reality.
  • parjanyāya: Referring to "the rain" or "the rain god," Parjanya is often associated with abundance and nourishment. In real life, rain is essential for agriculture and sustenance.
  • svarāje: This means "in the realm of sound" or "in the kingdom of sound." It emphasizes the significance of sound and vibration in the universe, akin to how music can influence emotions and environments.
  • hṛdo astv antaraṁ: This translates to "may there be a space in the heart." It suggests the importance of emotional openness and connection, which is vital in personal relationships and community bonding.
  • taj jujoṣat: Meaning "may that be nourished," this phrase calls for the nurturing of positive energies and relationships, similar to how we cultivate friendships and support systems in our lives.
  • mayobhuvo: This can be interpreted as "of our own making" or "created by us." It highlights the idea that we are responsible for our own realities and experiences, much like how our choices shape our futures.
  • vṛṣṭayaḥ: This means "rains" or "showers," reinforcing the theme of abundance and the cyclical nature of life, akin to how seasons affect our lives and livelihoods.
  • santv asme: This translates to "may there be peace for us." It emphasizes the universal desire for peace and harmony, which is essential for personal and societal well-being.
  • supippalā: Referring to "the best of herbs" or "nourishing plants," this symbolizes health and well-being, akin to how we seek nutritious food for our physical health.
  • oṣadhīr: This means "herbs" or "medicinal plants," highlighting the importance of nature in healing and sustenance, similar to how traditional medicine relies on natural remedies.
  • devagopāḥ: This translates to "the protectors of the divine" or "the guardians of the gods," suggesting a connection to spirituality and the divine forces that guide us in life.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the interconnectedness of words, nature, and the divine. It highlights the importance of communication, emotional openness, and the nurturing of positive energies in our lives. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied to personal relationships, community building, and our relationship with nature. By recognizing the power of our words and actions, we can create a more harmonious and abundant life, fostering peace and well-being for ourselves and those around us.
