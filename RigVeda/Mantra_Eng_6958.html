य ऋष्वः श्रावयत्सखā विश्वेत स वेद जनिमā पुरुष्टुतः |तं विश्वे मानवश युगेंद्रं हवन्ते तविषं यतस्रुचः ||
ya ṛṣvaḥ śrāvayatsakhā viśvet sa veda janimā puruṣṭutaḥ | taṁ viśve mānuṣā yugendraṁ havante taviṣaṁ yatasrucaḥ ||
  • ya ṛṣvaḥ: This phrase can be translated as "who is the swift one" or "the one who is quick." In real life, this can refer to individuals or entities that are agile and responsive, such as a quick-thinking leader or a fast-moving business that adapts to market changes.
  • śrāvayatsakhā: This means "who brings forth the sound" or "the one who communicates." In a modern context, this could relate to effective communicators, such as teachers or public speakers, who convey knowledge and ideas clearly and persuasively.
  • viśvet: This translates to "all" or "universal." It signifies inclusivity and the idea that the subject encompasses everything. In real life, this can be seen in community initiatives that aim to include diverse groups of people.
  • sa veda: This means "he knows" or "he understands." It emphasizes wisdom and knowledge. For example, a scientist who understands complex theories or a wise elder in a community who provides guidance can embody this concept.
  • janima: This translates to "birth" or "creation." It signifies the beginning of life or new ideas. In real life, this can be related to the birth of a child, the launch of a new project, or the inception of innovative ideas in technology.
  • puruṣṭutaḥ: This means "the one who is praised" or "the one who is honored." This can refer to individuals who are recognized for their contributions, such as leaders, artists, or activists who have made a significant impact on society.
  • taṁ viśve mānuṣā: This translates to "to him, all humans" or "to that one, all people." It emphasizes the collective acknowledgment of a figure or principle by humanity. In real life, this can be seen in the way communities come together to honor a leader or a cause.
  • yugendraṁ: This means "the lord of the ages" or "the ruler of the epochs." It signifies a timeless authority or wisdom that transcends generations. In modern contexts, this could refer to influential thinkers or movements that shape the course of history.
  • havante: This translates to "they invoke" or "they offer." It signifies the act of calling upon or seeking blessings. In real life, this can be seen in rituals, prayers, or community gatherings where people come together to seek guidance or support.
  • taviṣaṁ: This means "of strength" or "of power." It emphasizes the importance of strength in various forms, whether physical, mental, or spiritual. In real life, this can relate to athletes, leaders, or individuals who demonstrate resilience in the face of challenges.
  • yatasrucaḥ: This translates to "from whom the offerings come" or "from whom the blessings flow." It signifies the source of abundance and generosity. In real life, this can be seen in charitable organizations or individuals who give back to their communities.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of agility, communication, inclusivity, wisdom, creation, honor, collective acknowledgment, timeless authority, invocation, strength, and generosity. Its significance lies in the recognition of these qualities as essential for personal and communal growth. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as leadership, community building, education, and social responsibility, reminding us of the interconnectedness of all individuals and the importance of fostering these virtues in our daily lives.
