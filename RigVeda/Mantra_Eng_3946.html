ऋजीषी वज्री वृषभस तुराशाट चुष्मी राजा वृत्रहा सोमपावा |युक्त्वा हरिभ्यां उप यासद अर्वाङ् माध्यंदिनि सवने मत्सद इन्द्रः ||
ṛjīṣī vajrī vṛṣabhas turāṣāṭ chuṣmī rājā vṛtrahā somapāvā | yuktvā haribhyām upa yāsad arvāṅ mādhyaṁdine savane matsad indraḥ ||
  • ṛjīṣī: This term can be interpreted as "the one who is righteous" or "the one who follows the path of truth." In real life, this can relate to individuals who uphold ethical standards in their personal and professional lives, such as a judge who ensures justice is served.
  • vajrī: This word means "the one who wields the thunderbolt" or "powerful." It symbolizes strength and resilience. For example, a leader who stands firm in the face of adversity can be seen as vajrī, inspiring others to remain strong.
  • vṛṣabhas: This term translates to "the bull," often representing strength and fertility. In agriculture, a strong bull is essential for plowing fields, symbolizing hard work and productivity in society.
  • turāṣāṭ: This can be interpreted as "swift" or "quick." In modern contexts, this could refer to the rapid advancements in technology, such as the speed of information transfer through the internet, which has transformed communication.
  • chuṣmī: This word means "the one who is wise" or "intelligent." In real life, this could refer to educators or mentors who guide others with their knowledge and wisdom, shaping future generations.
  • rājā: This translates to "king" or "ruler." It signifies leadership and authority. A contemporary example could be a CEO of a company who leads their organization with vision and strategy.
  • vṛtrahā: This term means "the slayer of Vritra," a demon representing obstacles or challenges. In life, overcoming personal challenges, such as addiction or financial difficulties, can be seen as a form of vṛtrahā.
  • somapāvā: This translates to "the one who drinks soma," a drink associated with immortality and divine inspiration. In modern terms, this could symbolize the pursuit of knowledge and enlightenment through education and experience.
  • yuktvā: This means "having united" or "joined together." It emphasizes the importance of collaboration and teamwork in achieving common goals, such as community projects or corporate teamwork.
  • haribhyām: This term can be interpreted as "with the help of the divine" or "with the support of the gods." In real life, this could refer to the belief in seeking guidance from a higher power during difficult times, such as prayer or meditation.
  • upa yāsad: This means "approached" or "came near." It signifies the act of seeking knowledge or guidance, akin to students approaching teachers for wisdom.
  • arvāṅ: This term can be interpreted as "the one who is strong" or "powerful." In a real-life context, this could refer to athletes who train rigorously to achieve peak performance.
  • mādhyaṁdine: This translates to "in the midday" or "at the peak." It symbolizes the importance of timing and seizing opportunities when they arise, such as launching a business at the right moment.
  • savane: This term means "in the offering" or "sacrifice." It highlights the significance of giving back to the community, such as volunteering or charitable donations.
  • matsad: This translates to "my mind" or "my thoughts." It emphasizes the importance of self-reflection and mindfulness in personal growth and decision-making.
  • indraḥ: This refers to Indra, the king of gods in Vedic mythology, symbolizing power and authority. In modern contexts, this could represent influential leaders or figures who inspire change.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of righteousness, strength, wisdom, and leadership. It emphasizes the importance of collaboration, divine guidance, and the pursuit of knowledge. In real life, these principles can be applied in various scenarios, such as personal development, community service, and professional leadership. By embodying the qualities represented in this mantra, individuals can strive for excellence and contribute positively to society.
