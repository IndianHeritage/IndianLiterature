अयं त इन्द्र सोमो निपूतो अधि बर्हिषि |एहीम अस्य द्रवाऽ पिब ||
ayaṁ ta indra somo nipūto adhi barhiṣi | ehīm asya dravā piba ||
  • ayaṁ - This word translates to "this" or "here." It signifies the presence of something significant in the context of the mantra.
  • ta - Meaning "that," it indicates a connection or relationship between the subject and the object.
  • indra - Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic literature, often associated with rain, storms, and strength. He represents power and authority.
  • somo - This refers to "soma," a sacred drink in Vedic rituals believed to confer immortality and divine inspiration.
  • nipūto - This word means "poured" or "offered," indicating an act of giving or sharing something valuable.
  • adhi - Translated as "upon" or "over," it suggests a sense of elevation or importance.
  • barhiṣi - This term refers to the sacred grass used in rituals, symbolizing purity and the connection between the earthly and the divine.
  • ehīm - Meaning "come," it is an invitation or call to partake in something significant.
  • asya - This translates to "of this," indicating possession or belonging.
  • dravā - Referring to "liquid" or "flowing substance," it emphasizes the essence of the offering.
  • piba - This means "drink," suggesting the act of consuming or receiving the offering.

The mantra can be interpreted as a call to Indra to partake in the sacred soma that has been poured out, inviting him to bless the offerings made with purity and devotion. It reflects the importance of rituals in Vedic culture, where offerings to the gods were believed to bring prosperity, strength, and divine favor.


In real life, this mantra can be seen as a metaphor for seeking strength and support in challenging times. Just as the Vedic practitioners called upon Indra for blessings, individuals today can invoke their inner strength and seek support from their community or higher powers when facing difficulties. The act of sharing resources, whether material or emotional, is crucial in fostering connections and building resilience.


Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its emphasis on the importance of offerings, community, and divine connection. It teaches us that in times of need, we can seek strength from both the divine and our surroundings. By sharing our resources and inviting support, we can navigate life's challenges more effectively, much like the ancient practitioners who sought the blessings of Indra through their rituals.
