त्वं अपो यदवे तुर्वशायारमयः सुदुघाः पार इन्द्र | उग्रं अयातम अवहो ह कुत्सं सं ह यद वाम उशनारन्त देवाः ||
tvam apo yadave turvaśāyāramayaḥ sudughāḥ pāra indra | ugram ayātam avaho ha kutsaṁ saṁ ha yad vām uśanāranta devāḥ ||
  • tvam apo yadave turvaśāyāramayaḥ sudughāḥ pāra indra
    • tvam - "You" or "Thou": This refers to a divine entity, often interpreted as Indra, the king of gods in Vedic literature.
    • apo - "water": Water is a vital element, symbolizing life, purity, and sustenance.
    • yadave - "who is the protector": This indicates a guardian or protector role, emphasizing the divine's role in safeguarding the universe.
    • turvaśāyāramayaḥ - "one who is powerful and strong": This describes the might and strength of the divine, often associated with Indra's valor in battles.
    • sudughāḥ - "very difficult to attain": This suggests that the divine qualities or the divine itself are not easily accessible, requiring devotion and effort.
    • pāra - "beyond": This indicates transcendence, suggesting that the divine exists beyond the ordinary realm.
    • indra - "Indra": The chief deity in Vedic mythology, representing strength, rain, and thunder.
  • ugram ayātam avaho ha kutsaṁ saṁ ha yad vām uśanāranta devāḥ
    • ugram - "fierce" or "terrible": This refers to the formidable nature of the divine, highlighting the power that can be both protective and destructive.
    • ayātam - "not to be approached": This suggests that the divine is beyond human reach, emphasizing the need for reverence.
    • avaho - "bring forth": This indicates a call to action, urging the divine to manifest its power.
    • ha kutsaṁ - "O Kutsā": Kutsā is a sage or a divine figure, possibly invoking a specific aspect of divinity.
    • saṁ ha - "together": This signifies unity and collective invocation of divine powers.
    • yad vām - "that which is yours": This phrase acknowledges the divine's ownership and control over the universe.
    • uśanāranta - "the one who brings light": This refers to the enlightening aspect of the divine, often associated with knowledge and wisdom.
    • devāḥ - "gods": This term encompasses all divine beings, indicating a collective presence of divinity.

Conclusion:

This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the multifaceted nature of divinity, portraying it as a powerful protector, a source of life, and a fierce force that is both revered and feared. In real life, this can be applied in various contexts:

  • Spiritual Practice: Individuals can use this mantra in meditation or prayer to connect with the divine, seeking strength and guidance in their lives.
  • Environmental Awareness: The reference to water highlights the importance of preserving natural resources, reminding us of our responsibility towards the environment.
  • Community Unity: The call for collective invocation encourages communities to come together in times of need, fostering a sense of unity and shared purpose.

Overall, this mantra serves as a reminder of the divine's omnipresence and the need for respect, devotion, and collective action in our lives.
