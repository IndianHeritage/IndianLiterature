इमं बिभर्मि सुखृतं ते अङ्कुशं येनारुजासि मगवञ्च छफारुजः |अस्मिन सु ते सवने अस्त्वोक्त्यं सुत इष्टौ मगवन् बोध्याभगः ||
imam bibharmi sukṛtaṁ te aṅkuśaṁ yenārujāsi maghavañ chaphārujaḥ | asmin su te savane astv okyaṁ suta iṣṭau maghavan bodhy ābhagaḥ ||
  • Translation: This mantra can be translated as: "I hold this good deed of yours, O Maghavan, with the goad by which you rise up. May this be well for you in the sacrifice, and may you be aware of the offerings made to you, O Maghavan, who is rich in blessings."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • imam: This means "this" or "here." It indicates the speaker's focus on a specific action or deed.
    • bibharmi: This translates to "I hold" or "I carry," suggesting a sense of responsibility or stewardship.
    • sukṛtaṁ: Meaning "good deed" or "virtuous action," it emphasizes the importance of righteousness.
    • te: This means "your," indicating possession or connection to the subject.
    • aṅkuśaṁ: Refers to a "goad" or "stick," symbolizing control or guidance.
    • yenārujāsi: Translates to "by which you rise up," suggesting elevation or ascension through virtuous actions.
    • maghavañ: This is a name for the deity Indra, often associated with strength and abundance.
    • chaphārujaḥ: This term can be interpreted as "rich in blessings" or "one who is generous."
    • asmin: Meaning "in this," it refers to the context of the sacrifice or offering.
    • su: This means "well" or "good," indicating positivity.
    • te: Again, "your," reinforcing the connection to the deity.
    • savane: Refers to "sacrifice" or "offering," a central theme in Vedic rituals.
    • astv: Meaning "may it be," a wish or prayer for a favorable outcome.
    • okyaṁ: This can be interpreted as "well-being" or "prosperity."
    • suta: Meaning "offspring" or "progeny," often used in the context of offerings.
    • iṣṭau: Refers to "offerings" or "sacrifices," highlighting the importance of rituals.
    • maghavan: Again referring to Indra, emphasizing his role in blessings and abundance.
    • bodhy: Meaning "to be aware" or "to understand," indicating consciousness and mindfulness.
    • ābhagaḥ: This term can be interpreted as "blessed" or "fortunate," suggesting a state of grace.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In daily life, the concept of holding onto good deeds can be seen in acts of kindness, where individuals help others without expecting anything in return.
    • The idea of using a "goad" can be likened to the guidance we receive from mentors or leaders who help us rise to our potential.
    • In a corporate setting, recognizing and rewarding good work can be seen as a way to encourage employees to continue their virtuous actions.
    • During festivals or rituals, offerings made to deities can be a way for communities to come together, reinforcing social bonds and shared values.
  • Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its emphasis on the importance of good deeds, guidance, and the act of offering. It teaches us that by holding onto virtuous actions and recognizing the blessings in our lives, we can elevate ourselves and those around us. In real life, this mantra encourages us to be mindful of our actions, to support one another, and to engage in rituals that foster community and gratitude. Whether in personal relationships, professional environments, or spiritual practices, the principles encapsulated in this mantra are universally applicable, promoting a life of purpose and connection.
