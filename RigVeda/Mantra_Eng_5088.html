इन्द्राविष्णु हविषा वावृधानाग्राद्वान नमसाऽ रातहव्याः |घृतासुती द्रविणं धत्तम अस्मे समुद्रः स्थः कलशः सोमधानः ||
indrāviṣṇū haviṣā vāvṛdhānāgrādvānā namasā rātahavyā | ghṛtāsutī draviṇaṁ dhattam asme samudraḥ sthaḥ kalaśaḥ somadhānaḥ ||
  • Indrāviṣṇū: This term refers to the combined powers of Indra and Vishnu, two of the most significant deities in Vedic literature. Indra is often associated with rain, thunder, and war, while Vishnu represents preservation and protection. Together, they symbolize the balance of power and sustenance in the universe.
  • Haviṣā: This word means offerings or sacrifices. In the context of the mantra, it signifies the act of giving and the importance of offerings in Vedic rituals, which are believed to please the gods and ensure their blessings.
  • Vāvṛdhānā: This term can be interpreted as "those who increase" or "those who grow." It emphasizes the idea of growth and prosperity, suggesting that the offerings made to the deities lead to abundance and well-being.
  • Namasa: This word means "with reverence" or "with respect." It highlights the importance of approaching the divine with humility and devotion, acknowledging the higher powers at play in our lives.
  • Rātahavyā: This term refers to the offerings made during rituals, particularly those that are pleasing to the gods. It signifies the connection between the material and spiritual worlds through the act of giving.
  • Ghṛtāsutī: This word translates to "the essence of ghee" or "clarified butter." Ghee is considered sacred in Vedic traditions and is often used in rituals as a symbol of purity and nourishment.
  • Draviṇaṁ: This term means "wealth" or "resources." It signifies the material abundance that is sought through spiritual practices and offerings.
  • Dhattam: This word means "to bestow" or "to grant." It emphasizes the act of giving and the blessings that come from the divine in response to sincere offerings.
  • Asme: This term means "to us" or "for us." It indicates a personal connection to the divine, suggesting that the blessings sought are intended for the individual or community making the offerings.
  • Samudraḥ: This word means "ocean." In a metaphorical sense, it represents vastness and abundance, suggesting that the blessings from the divine are as plentiful as the ocean.
  • Sthaḥ: This term means "standing" or "established." It signifies stability and permanence, indicating that the blessings received will be enduring.
  • Kalaśaḥ: This word refers to a pot or vessel, often used in rituals to hold offerings. It symbolizes the container of blessings and the importance of receptivity in spiritual practices.
  • Somadhānaḥ: This term refers to the offering of soma, a sacred drink in Vedic rituals believed to confer immortality and divine insight. It symbolizes the pursuit of higher knowledge and spiritual awakening.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of devotion, offering, and the pursuit of abundance in both material and spiritual realms. It emphasizes the importance of approaching the divine with respect and humility, recognizing the interconnectedness of all life. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during rituals, personal prayers, or community gatherings, where individuals seek blessings for prosperity, health, and spiritual growth. By understanding and embodying the principles expressed in this mantra, one can cultivate a deeper connection with the divine and foster a sense of gratitude and abundance in their life.
