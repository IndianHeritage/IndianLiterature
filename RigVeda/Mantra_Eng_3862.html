युजं हि माम अकृतः आद इन्द्र शिरो दासस्य नमुचेर मथायन |अश्मानं चित स्वार्यम् वर्तमानं प्र चक्रियेव रोधसी मरुद्भ्यः ||
yujaṁ hi mām akṛthā ād id indra śiro dāsasya namucer mathāyan | aśmānaṁ cit svarya1ṁ vartamānam pra cakriyeva rodasī marudbhyaḥ ||
  • Mantra: "yujaṁ hi mām akṛthā ād id indra śiro dāsasya namucer mathāyan | aśmānaṁ cit svaryaṁ vartamānam pra cakriyeva rodasī marudbhyaḥ ||"
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • yujaṁ: to unite or join
    • hi: indeed
    • mām: me
    • akṛthā: not done or not created
    • ād: from the beginning
    • id: this
    • indra: Indra, the king of gods
    • śiro: head
    • dāsasya: of the servant
    • namucer: of Namuchi, a demon
    • mathāyan: the one who churns
    • aśmānaṁ: of stones
    • cit: gathered or collected
    • svaryaṁ: heavenly or celestial
    • vartamānam: existing or present
    • pra: forth or forward
    • cakriyeva: like a wheel
    • rodasī: the two worlds or the sky
    • marudbhyaḥ: from the Maruts, the storm deities
  • Explanation:
    • This mantra speaks of a plea for unity and strength, invoking the divine powers of Indra, who is often associated with rain, storms, and the heavens.
    • The phrase "yujaṁ hi mām" suggests a desire for connection and support, emphasizing the importance of community and collaboration in overcoming challenges.
    • By referencing "dāsasya namucer," it highlights the struggle against negativity or demonic forces, symbolized by Namuchi, which can represent obstacles in life.
    • The imagery of "aśmānaṁ cit svaryaṁ" evokes the idea of gathering strength from the earth (stones) to achieve a higher purpose, akin to how individuals draw upon their experiences and resources to reach their goals.
    • The metaphor of "cakriyeva rodasī" likens the cyclical nature of life and the universe to a wheel, suggesting that just as the wheel turns, so too do our fortunes and challenges evolve.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In a team setting, this mantra can be applied to the importance of collaboration. Just as Indra is called upon for strength, team members can support each other to overcome obstacles.
    • In personal development, individuals may face their own "Namuchis" in the form of fears or doubts. This mantra serves as a reminder to gather inner strength and seek support from others.
    • In environmental contexts, the reference to stones and the earth can inspire efforts to protect nature, emphasizing the need to unite for a common cause, much like the Maruts who represent the forces of nature.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra encapsulates the essence of unity, strength, and the cyclical nature of life. It serves as a reminder that challenges can be overcome through collaboration and inner strength.
    • Its significance lies in its applicability to various aspects of life, from personal struggles to collective efforts in communities, encouraging individuals to seek support and work together for a greater purpose.
    • Ultimately, it teaches that by drawing upon our collective resources and strengths, we can navigate the complexities of life and emerge victorious against our challenges.
