त्रातā नो बोधि ददृशाना आपिर अभिख्यातā मार्डितā सोम्यानाम् |सखा पिता पितृतमः पितृणां कर्तेमु लोकम् उषते वयोद्धाः ||
trātā no bodhi dadṛśāna āpir abhikhyātā marḍitā somyānām | sakhā pitā pitṛtamaḥ pitṝṇāṁ kartem u lokam uśate vayodhāḥ ||
  • trātā no bodhi: This phrase can be translated as "may the protector grant us wisdom." Here, "trātā" refers to a protector or savior, while "bodhi" signifies enlightenment or wisdom. In real life, this can be seen in the context of seeking guidance from mentors or spiritual leaders who help individuals navigate through life's challenges.
  • dadṛśāna āpir: This means "may we see the divine." "Dadṛśāna" refers to the act of seeing or perceiving, and "āpir" can be interpreted as the divine or the higher power. This can relate to moments in life when individuals experience a sense of connection to something greater, such as during meditation or in nature, where one feels a profound sense of peace and understanding.
  • abhikhyātā marḍitā somyānām: This translates to "may we be known and recognized among the gentle." "Abhikhyātā" means to be known or recognized, "marḍitā" refers to being gentle or kind, and "somyānām" relates to the peaceful or serene. This can be applied in social contexts where kindness and compassion are valued, such as in community service or acts of charity, where individuals strive to be recognized for their good deeds.
  • sakhā pitā pitṛtamaḥ: This phrase means "the friend, the father, the best of fathers." "Sakhā" refers to a friend, "pitā" means father, and "pitṛtamaḥ" signifies the best or supreme father. This highlights the importance of relationships in our lives, emphasizing the roles of friends and family as sources of support and guidance. In real life, this can be seen in the way families come together during times of need, providing emotional and practical support.
  • pitṝṇāṁ kartem u lokam: This translates to "may we create a world for our ancestors." "Pitṝṇāṁ" refers to ancestors, "kartem" means to create or make, and "u lokam" signifies the world. This reflects the idea of honoring one's heritage and working towards a better future for the coming generations. In practice, this can be observed in cultural traditions where families pass down values, stories, and practices that shape the identity of future generations.
  • uśate vayodhāḥ: This means "may the strong dwell here." "Uśate" refers to dwelling or residing, and "vayodhāḥ" signifies the strong or powerful. This can be interpreted as a call for strength and resilience in the face of adversity. In real life, this can be seen in communities that come together to support each other during crises, showcasing the strength that arises from unity and collective effort.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a profound message about seeking wisdom, recognizing the divine, valuing relationships, honoring ancestors, and fostering strength within communities. Its significance lies in its applicability to everyday life, encouraging individuals to cultivate kindness, support one another, and create a legacy for future generations. By embodying these principles, we can contribute to a more compassionate and resilient society.
