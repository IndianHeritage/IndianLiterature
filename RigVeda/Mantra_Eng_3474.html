पīvोअश्वाः शुचद्रथा हि भूतायःशिप्रā वाजिनः सुनीष्काः |इन्द्रस्य सूनो शवसो नपातो 'नु वश चेत्य अग्रियम मदाय ||
pīvoaśvāḥ śucadrathā hi bhūtāyaḥśiprā vājinaḥ suniṣkāḥ | indrasya sūno śavaso napāto 'nu vaś cety agriyam madāya ||
  • pīvoaśvāḥ: This term can be broken down into "pīva," meaning "rich" or "abundant," and "aśvāḥ," which refers to "horses." In the context of the mantra, it signifies the abundance of strength and vitality, akin to the vigor of well-fed horses.
  • śucadrathā: This word combines "śucā," meaning "pure" or "clean," and "rathā," which means "chariot." It symbolizes a pure and noble vehicle, representing the journey of life that is guided by righteousness and clarity.
  • hi bhūtāyaḥ: "hi" translates to "indeed," while "bhūtāyaḥ" refers to "for the beings" or "for the existence." This phrase emphasizes the importance of actions and offerings that benefit all living beings, highlighting a sense of community and interconnectedness.
  • śiprā vājinaḥ: "śiprā" means "swift" or "quick," and "vājinaḥ" refers to "those who possess strength" or "warriors." This part of the mantra invokes the idea of swift and powerful individuals who are ready to act for the greater good.
  • suniṣkāḥ: This term means "well-prepared" or "well-equipped." It suggests that one should be ready and equipped to face challenges, embodying the spirit of preparedness in life.
  • indrasya sūno: "Indrasya" refers to "of Indra," the king of gods, and "sūno" means "son." This phrase connects the mantra to divine blessings and the strength derived from higher powers, indicating that one should seek guidance from the divine.
  • śavaso napāto: "śavaso" means "of the dead" or "of the ancestors," and "napāto" refers to "descendants." This part emphasizes the importance of honoring one's ancestors and recognizing the legacy they leave behind.
  • 'anu vaś: "anu" means "following" or "after," and "vaś" refers to "control" or "influence." This phrase suggests that one should follow the path of righteousness and be influenced by the teachings of the wise.
  • cety agriyam madāya: "cety" means "that which is known," "agriyam" refers to "the foremost" or "the best," and "madāya" means "for the sake of joy" or "for the sake of delight." This part of the mantra encourages the pursuit of knowledge and excellence for the joy it brings.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the essence of strength, purity, and the importance of community and divine guidance. It serves as a reminder to be well-prepared, swift in action, and to honor our ancestors while seeking knowledge and joy in our pursuits. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as in leadership roles where one must embody strength and clarity, in community service where actions benefit all beings, and in personal development where the pursuit of knowledge and preparedness leads to success. Ultimately, it emphasizes living a life that is rich in values, guided by higher principles, and connected to the legacy of those who came before us.
