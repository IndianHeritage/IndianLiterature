प्र व इन्द्राय मादनं हर्यश्वाय गायत |सखायः सोमपावने ||
pra va indrāya mādanaṁ haryaśvāya gāyata | sakhāyaḥ somapāvne ||
  • pra va indrāya mādanaṁ: This phrase can be translated as "to Indra, the one who brings joy." Indra is a significant deity in the Rigveda, often associated with rain, thunder, and the welfare of the community. In real life, this can be likened to leaders or figures who bring happiness and prosperity to their people, much like a good leader who inspires and uplifts their community.
  • haryaśvāya gāyata: This translates to "sing to the one who has beautiful horses." The term "haryaśvā" refers to a divine being or a powerful entity that is often depicted with magnificent horses, symbolizing strength and speed. In contemporary terms, this could represent the pursuit of excellence and the celebration of achievements, akin to honoring those who excel in their fields, such as athletes or innovators.
  • sakhāyaḥ somapāvne: This means "friends of the one who purifies with soma." Soma is a sacred drink in Vedic rituals, symbolizing nourishment and spiritual enlightenment. This phrase emphasizes the importance of friendship and community in achieving spiritual and material well-being. In modern life, this can be seen in the value of teamwork and collaboration, where collective efforts lead to greater success.

  • Significance of the Mantra: This mantra encapsulates the essence of community, leadership, and the pursuit of joy and excellence. It highlights the importance of divine blessings in achieving prosperity and well-being.
  • Real-Life Application: The teachings of this mantra can be applied in various aspects of life, such as in leadership roles where one aims to uplift and inspire others, in teamwork where collaboration leads to success, and in celebrating achievements that bring joy to the community.
  • Conclusion: The mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of joy, strength, and community. By invoking these principles in our daily lives, we can foster a more harmonious and prosperous existence, much like the ideals celebrated in the Rigveda.
