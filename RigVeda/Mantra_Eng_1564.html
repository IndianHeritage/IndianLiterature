स्वाहाकृतान्यागह्युप हव्यानि वीतये। इन्द्रा गहि श्रुधी हवं त्वां हवन्ते अध्वरे॥
svāhākṛtāny ā gahy upa havyāni vītaye | indrā gahi śrudhī havaṁ tvāṁ havante adhvare ||
  • Mantra: "svāhākṛtāny ā gahy upa havyāni vītaye | indrā gahi śrudhī havaṁ tvāṁ havante adhvare ||"
  • Translation: This mantra can be translated as: "O Indra, accept these offerings that have been prepared; may they reach you, as we invoke you in our rituals."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • svāhākṛtāny: Refers to offerings that have been prepared with the invocation of 'Svāha', a term used to signify the completion of a ritual offering.
    • ā gahy: Means "may you accept" or "take in," indicating a request for acceptance of the offerings.
    • upa havyāni: Refers to the offerings that are to be presented or brought forth.
    • vītaye: Means "for the purpose of" or "for the benefit of," indicating the intention behind the offerings.
    • indrā: Directly addresses Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic tradition, symbolizing strength and power.
    • gahi: Again means "accept" or "take," reinforcing the request for acceptance.
    • śrudhī: Refers to the "intellect" or "understanding," suggesting that the offerings are made with wisdom.
    • havaṁ: Means "the offering" or "the invocation," referring to the act of calling upon the divine.
    • tvāṁ: Means "you," referring to Indra directly.
    • havante: Means "those who offer," indicating the community or individuals making the offering.
    • adhvare: Refers to the "ritual" or "sacrifice," emphasizing the ceremonial aspect of the offering.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In modern Hindu rituals, offerings such as fruits, flowers, and incense are made to deities during puja (worship) ceremonies, similar to the offerings mentioned in the mantra.
    • In a corporate setting, when a team presents a project to higher management, they may prepare a detailed presentation (the offerings) and seek approval (acceptance) for their ideas, akin to invoking a deity for blessings.
    • During community festivals, people come together to offer prayers and food to the gods, reflecting the collective spirit of devotion and the desire for divine acceptance.
  • Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its emphasis on the act of offering and the intention behind it. It teaches us the importance of preparation, sincerity, and community in our endeavors. In real life, whether in personal, professional, or communal contexts, the principles of respect, gratitude, and seeking acceptance from a higher authority or the community can lead to positive outcomes. This mantra serves as a reminder that our actions, when performed with intention and devotion, can resonate beyond the immediate and connect us to something greater.
