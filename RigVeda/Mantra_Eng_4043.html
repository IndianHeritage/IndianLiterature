उक्षां समुद्रो अरुषः सुपर्णः पूर्वस्य योनिम पितुर आविवेश |मध्येदिवो निहितः पृष्णिर अश्मा विचक्रमे रजसः पात्य अन्तौ ||
ukṣā samudro aruṣaḥ suparṇaḥ pūrvasya yonim pitur ā viveśa | madhye divo nihitaḥ pṛśnir aśmā vi cakrame rajasas pāty antau ||
  • ukṣā: This word can be interpreted as "awakening" or "arising." It signifies the beginning of something new, much like the dawn of a new day.
  • samudro: Translated as "ocean," it represents vastness and depth, symbolizing the collective consciousness or the universe.
  • aruṣaḥ: Meaning "red" or "brilliant," it often refers to the sun or light, symbolizing knowledge and enlightenment.
  • suparṇaḥ: This term means "well-winged" or "having good wings," often associated with the divine or spiritual beings, indicating freedom and transcendence.
  • pūrvasyā: This word means "of the east," which is significant as the east is traditionally associated with new beginnings and the rising sun.
  • yonim: Translated as "womb" or "source," it signifies the origin or the place from which something emerges.
  • pitur: Meaning "father," it represents authority, guidance, and protection.
  • ā viveśa: This phrase means "entered" or "came into," indicating a transition or arrival into a new state or realm.
  • madhye: Meaning "in the middle," it signifies balance and harmony, often representing the center of existence.
  • divo: Translated as "of the sky" or "heaven," it symbolizes the divine realm and higher consciousness.
  • nihitaḥ: This word means "hidden" or "placed," indicating something that is not immediately visible but is significant.
  • pṛśnir: This term can refer to "the one who is firm" or "the strong one," symbolizing stability and strength.
  • aśmā: Meaning "stone," it represents permanence and the unchanging aspects of life.
  • vi cakrame: This phrase means "moved" or "revolved," indicating action and dynamism.
  • rajasas: Translated as "of the dust" or "of the earth," it signifies the material world and its transient nature.
  • pāty: This word means "to protect" or "to guard," indicating a sense of safety and security.
  • antau: Meaning "at the end," it signifies conclusion or culmination.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the essence of creation, transformation, and the interplay between the material and spiritual realms. It emphasizes the importance of awakening to new possibilities, the guidance of paternal figures, and the balance between the earthly and the divine. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios such as personal growth, seeking enlightenment, and understanding the cyclical nature of existence. It encourages individuals to embrace change, seek knowledge, and find harmony within themselves and their surroundings.
