प्रैष स्तोमः पृथिवीं अन्तरिक्षं वनस्पतीं ओषधि राये अश्याः |देवो-देवः सुहवो भूत्व मह्यम् मा नो माता पृथिवी दुर्मतौ धात ||
praiṣa stomaḥ pṛthivīm antarikṣaṁ vanaspatīm̐r oṣadhī rāye aśyāḥ | devo-devaḥ suhavo bhūtu mahyam mā no mātā pṛthivī durmatau dhāt ||
  • praiṣa stomaḥ: This phrase can be interpreted as a call or invocation. "Praiṣa" suggests a request or a plea, while "stomaḥ" refers to praise or hymn. In real life, this can be likened to how we often seek blessings or express gratitude in various cultures, such as during a wedding ceremony where families invoke blessings for the couple.
  • pṛthivīm antarikṣaṁ: "Pṛthivīm" means the Earth, and "antarikṣaṁ" refers to the space or the atmosphere. This highlights the connection between the Earth and the cosmos. In our daily lives, this can be seen in environmental movements that emphasize the importance of caring for our planet and recognizing our place in the universe.
  • vanaspatīm̐r oṣadhī: "Vanaspati" refers to trees or plants, and "oṣadhī" means herbs or medicinal plants. This part of the mantra emphasizes the significance of nature and its healing properties. In modern life, this is reflected in the growing trend of herbal medicine and the use of natural remedies for health and wellness.
  • rāye aśyāḥ: "Rāye" means wealth or prosperity, and "aśyāḥ" refers to nourishment or sustenance. This suggests a prayer for abundance and well-being. In practical terms, this can be seen in the way communities come together to support local businesses and promote sustainable practices that ensure economic stability.
  • devo-devaḥ suhavo: "Devo-devaḥ" translates to the God of gods, indicating a supreme divine presence, while "suhavo" means benevolent or kind. This reflects the idea of seeking divine guidance and support in our lives. For instance, many people turn to prayer or meditation in times of uncertainty, seeking comfort and direction.
  • bhūtu mahyam: "Bhūtu" means existence or being, and "mahyam" translates to "to me." This phrase emphasizes the importance of being grounded in reality and acknowledging one's existence. In everyday life, this can be related to mindfulness practices that encourage individuals to stay present and aware of their surroundings.
  • mā no mātā pṛthivī: "Mā no" means "may not," and "mātā pṛthivī" refers to Mother Earth. This part of the mantra expresses a desire for Earth not to be harsh or unforgiving. In real life, this can be seen in the context of climate change discussions, where there is a collective hope for a more harmonious relationship with nature.
  • durmatau dhāt: "Durmatau" means difficult or challenging, and "dhāt" refers to support or sustenance. This suggests a plea for strength and resilience in the face of adversity. In practical terms, this can be related to personal struggles, where individuals seek support from their communities or spiritual practices to overcome challenges.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates a deep reverence for nature, a plea for divine support, and a recognition of the interconnectedness of life. Its significance lies in its timeless message of gratitude, respect for the Earth, and the pursuit of prosperity and well-being. In today's world, it is applicable in various scenarios, such as environmental conservation efforts, community support systems, and personal growth practices. By embodying the principles expressed in this mantra, individuals and communities can foster a more harmonious existence with nature and each other.
