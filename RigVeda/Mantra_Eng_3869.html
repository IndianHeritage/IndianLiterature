चतुःसहस्रं गव्यस्य पश्वः प्रत्य अग्रभीष्म रुशमेष्व अग्ने |घर्मश्चित्तप्तः प्रवृजे य आसीद आयसमयस्तं वादाम विप्राः ||
catuḥsahasraṁ gavyasya paśvaḥ praty agrabhīṣma ruśameṣv agne | gharmaś cit taptaḥ pravṛje ya āsīd ayasmayas tam v ādāma viprāḥ ||
  • catuḥsahasraṁ: This term translates to "four thousand." In a broader context, it can signify abundance or a large quantity.
  • gavyasya: This refers to "of cattle" or "of the herd." Cattle are often seen as a symbol of wealth and prosperity in many cultures, including ancient Indian society.
  • paśvaḥ: This means "animals" or "beasts." It emphasizes the importance of livestock in agrarian societies.
  • praty agrabhīṣma: This phrase can be interpreted as "to the one who is the foremost" or "to the one who is the best." It suggests a focus on excellence or superiority.
  • ruśameṣv: This term can be understood as "in the midst of the sacrifices" or "in the rituals." It highlights the significance of rituals in connecting with the divine.
  • agne: This is a direct address to Agni, the fire god, who represents energy, transformation, and the divine messenger.
  • gharmaś: This means "the warmth" or "the heat." It symbolizes the nurturing aspect of fire, which is essential for life.
  • cit taptaḥ: This translates to "that which is heated" or "that which is kindled." It refers to the transformative power of fire.
  • pravṛje: This means "to bring forth" or "to produce." It indicates the generative aspect of fire in rituals and life.
  • ya āsīd: This translates to "that which existed" or "that which was." It connects the past with the present, emphasizing continuity.
  • ayasmayas: This term can be interpreted as "the one who is not to be feared" or "the fearless." It suggests a sense of security and protection.
  • tam v ādāma: This means "we invoke that" or "we call upon that." It indicates a plea for divine assistance or blessings.
  • viprāḥ: This refers to "the wise" or "the learned." It emphasizes the role of knowledge and wisdom in spiritual practices.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the importance of abundance, particularly in terms of livestock, and the role of fire (Agni) in rituals and life. It highlights the connection between the divine and the material world, showcasing how rituals can invoke blessings and protection. In real life, this mantra can be applicable in agricultural practices, where the prosperity of livestock is crucial for sustenance. It also serves as a reminder of the significance of knowledge and wisdom in navigating life's challenges, encouraging individuals to seek divine guidance and support in their endeavors.
