अनु यद ईम मारुतो मंदसानम् आर्चन्न इन्द्रं पपिवांसं सुतस्य |आदत्त वज्रम अभि यद अहिं हन्न अपो यह्वीः असृजत सार्तवाऽउ ||
anu yad īm maruto mandasānam ārcann indram papivāṁsaṁ sutasya | ādatta vajram abhi yad ahiṁ hann apo yahvīr asṛjat sartavā u ||
  • anu yad īm maruto mandasānam ārcann indram papivāṁsaṁ sutasya
    • anu - This word means "following" or "after." It indicates a sequence or a cause-and-effect relationship.
    • yad - Translates to "that" or "which," referring to something specific.
    • īm - This can be interpreted as "to us" or "for us," indicating a personal connection.
    • maruto - Refers to the "Maruts," the storm deities in Vedic literature, symbolizing power and energy.
    • mandasānam - This means "with gentle or soft seats," suggesting comfort or a nurturing environment.
    • ārcann - Means "worshipping" or "adoring," indicating reverence towards the divine.
    • indram - Refers to "Indra," the king of gods, associated with rain, thunder, and strength.
    • papivāṁsaṁ - This translates to "drinking" or "consuming," often in the context of receiving blessings or nourishment.
    • sutasya - Means "of the son" or "of the offspring," which can symbolize lineage or continuity.
  • ādatta vajram abhi yad ahiṁ hann apo yahvīr asṛjat sartavā u
    • ādatta - Means "he took" or "he seized," indicating an action of acquisition or acceptance.
    • vajram - Refers to "the thunderbolt" or "the weapon of Indra," symbolizing strength and power.
    • abhi - Means "towards" or "against," indicating directionality in action.
    • yad - Again, translates to "that" or "which," referring to a specific action or event.
    • ahiṁ - Means "the serpent" or "the adversary," often representing challenges or obstacles.
    • hann - Translates to "he struck" or "he defeated," indicating victory over challenges.
    • apo - Means "water," symbolizing life, nourishment, and purification.
    • yahvīr - Refers to "the strong" or "the powerful," indicating resilience and strength.
    • asṛjat - Means "he created" or "he produced," indicating the act of bringing forth or manifesting.
    • sartavā - Refers to "the path" or "the way," indicating direction or guidance.
    • u - A particle often used for emphasis or to indicate a conclusion.

Conclusion:

This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the importance of divine blessings and the strength derived from them. It illustrates the relationship between worship, the reception of divine energy, and the ability to overcome challenges. In real life, this can be seen in various contexts such as:

  • In personal struggles, individuals often seek spiritual guidance or support, akin to worshipping deities for strength and resilience.
  • In community settings, collective worship can foster a sense of unity and shared strength, helping groups to overcome adversities.
  • In leadership, the metaphor of Indra and the thunderbolt can represent the qualities of decisiveness and strength needed to lead effectively.

Thus, the mantra serves as a reminder of the power of faith, the importance of community, and the strength that comes from divine support in overcoming life's challenges.
