असावी सोमः पुरुहूत तुभ्यं हरिभ्यां यज्ञं उप याहि तुयम् |तुभ्यं गिरो विप्रवीराः इयानā दधन्विर इन्द्र पिबा सुतस्य ||
asāvi somaḥ puruhūta tubhyaṁ haribhyāṁ yajñam upa yāhi tūyam | tubhyaṁ giro vipravīrā iyānā dadhanvira indra pibā sutasya ||
  • Mantra: "asāvi somaḥ puruhūta tubhyaṁ haribhyāṁ yajñam upa yāhi tūyam | tubhyaṁ giro vipravīrā iyānā dadhanvira indra pibā sutasya ||"
  • Translation: This mantra is a call to the deity Soma, inviting him to partake in the offerings made during a yajna (sacred ritual). It expresses a desire for the blessings of Indra, the king of gods, and emphasizes the importance of the offerings made by the priests (vipravīrā).
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • asāvi: This refers to the essence or the divine nature of Soma.
    • somaḥ: Soma is both a deity and a sacred drink, symbolizing immortality and divine inspiration.
    • puruhūta: This term means "one who is invoked frequently," referring to the deities who are called upon in rituals.
    • tubhyaṁ: This means "to you," indicating the direction of the invocation.
    • haribhyāṁ: This refers to the offerings made to the gods, particularly in the context of the yajna.
    • yajñam: A sacred ritual or sacrifice performed to please the deities.
    • upa: This means "towards" or "near," indicating the approach to the divine.
    • yāhi: This is a call to come or approach.
    • tūyam: This means "your," referring to the offerings made to the deity.
    • giro: This refers to the sacred chants or hymns sung during the rituals.
    • vipravīrā: This means "the wise priests," who are knowledgeable in performing the rituals.
    • iyānā: This means "coming" or "arriving," indicating the arrival of blessings.
    • dadhanvira: This means "bestowing strength," referring to the blessings of the deity.
    • indra: Indra is the king of gods, associated with rain, thunder, and strength.
    • pibā: This means "drink," referring to the act of consuming the offerings.
    • sutasya: This refers to the offerings or the sacred drink prepared for the deities.

  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In modern times, the essence of this mantra can be seen in various religious ceremonies where offerings are made to deities, such as during Diwali or Navratri in Hindu culture.
    • In a corporate setting, the concept of 'yajna' can be likened to team-building exercises where individuals come together to achieve a common goal, invoking the spirit of collaboration and unity.
    • In personal life, the act of sharing food and resources with others can be seen as a modern-day yajna, where the intention is to foster community and goodwill.

  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra signifies the importance of divine blessings in our endeavors and the role of rituals in connecting with the divine.
    • It teaches us that through sincere offerings and intentions, we can invite positive energies into our lives.
    • In real-life scenarios, whether in personal, social, or professional contexts, the principles of collaboration, gratitude, and seeking blessings can lead to success and fulfillment.
