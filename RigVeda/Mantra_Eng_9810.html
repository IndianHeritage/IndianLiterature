ब्राह्मणोऽस्य मुखं आसीद बाहू राजन्यः कृतः |ऊरु तदस्य यद वैश्यः पद्भ्यां शूद्रो अजायत ||
brāhmaṇo 'sya mukham āsīd bāhū rājanyaḥ kṛtaḥ | ūrū tad asya yad vaiśyaḥ padbhyāṁ śūdro ajāyata ||
  • Translation: "From his mouth came the Brahman (the priest), from his arms came the Kshatriya (the warrior), from his thighs came the Vaishya (the merchant), and from his feet came the Shudra (the laborer)."
  • Meaning: This mantra from the Rigveda describes the origin of the four varnas (social classes) in ancient Indian society, illustrating a cosmic order where each class has a specific role and function.
  • Brahman (Priest): The mouth symbolizes the role of the Brahman, who is responsible for spiritual knowledge, rituals, and guidance. For example, in modern society, this can be likened to educators or spiritual leaders who impart wisdom and ethical values.
  • Kshatriya (Warrior): The arms represent the Kshatriya, tasked with protection and governance. In contemporary terms, this can be compared to military personnel, law enforcement, or leaders who ensure safety and uphold justice in society.
  • Vaishya (Merchant): The thighs signify the Vaishya, who engages in commerce and agriculture. Today, this can be seen in entrepreneurs, business owners, and farmers who contribute to the economy and provide goods and services.
  • Shudra (Laborer): The feet denote the Shudra, who performs the essential tasks that support society. In real life, this includes workers in various sectors, such as construction, sanitation, and service industries, who are vital for the functioning of daily life.

  • Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the importance of each social class and their interdependence in maintaining societal harmony. It teaches us that every role, whether high or low, is essential for the overall functioning of society. In modern contexts, it encourages respect for all professions and highlights the value of collaboration among diverse groups to achieve common goals.
