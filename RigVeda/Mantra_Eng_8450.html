पवस्व सोम दिव्येषु धामसु सृजाना इन्दो कलशे पवित्रा आ |सीदन्न इन्द्रस्य जठरे कनिक्रदन् नृभिर यतः सूर्यं आरोहयो दिवि ||
pavasva soma divyeṣu dhāmasu sṛjāna indo kalaśe pavitra ā | sīdann indrasya jaṭhare kanikradan nṛbhir yataḥ sūryam ārohayo divi ||
  • pavasva: This word can be translated as "purify" or "make pure." In a real-life context, this can refer to the act of cleansing oneself, both physically and spiritually, to prepare for a higher purpose or connection.
  • soma: Soma is often associated with a sacred drink in Vedic tradition, symbolizing vitality and divine inspiration. In modern life, this can be likened to the pursuit of knowledge and experiences that nourish the soul.
  • divyeṣu: Meaning "divine" or "heavenly," this word emphasizes the connection to higher realms or spiritual experiences. It suggests that we should seek out and embrace the divine aspects of life.
  • dhāmasu: This translates to "in the realms" or "in the abodes." It indicates the various planes of existence, both physical and spiritual, where one can seek enlightenment or understanding.
  • sṛjāna: Meaning "creation" or "bringing forth," this word highlights the importance of creativity and manifestation in our lives. It encourages individuals to create and contribute positively to the world.
  • indo: Referring to "Indra," the king of gods in Vedic mythology, this word symbolizes strength, power, and leadership. In a contemporary context, it can represent the qualities of a good leader or protector in society.
  • kalaśe: This means "in the pot" or "vessel," often symbolizing abundance and nourishment. It can be related to the idea of gathering resources or knowledge for personal growth.
  • pavitra: Translated as "pure" or "sacred," this word emphasizes the importance of purity in thoughts, actions, and intentions. In daily life, this can be reflected in ethical living and mindfulness.
  • sīdann: This word means "sitting" or "residing," suggesting a state of being or presence. It encourages individuals to be grounded and aware of their surroundings.
  • indrasya: Meaning "of Indra," this phrase connects the individual to divine protection and guidance. It can be seen as a reminder to seek support from higher powers in times of need.
  • jaṭhare: This translates to "in the belly" or "within," symbolizing the inner self or core. It encourages introspection and understanding of one's true nature.
  • kanikradan: This word can be interpreted as "the one who brings forth" or "the creator." It emphasizes the role of individuals in shaping their destinies and the world around them.
  • nṛbhir: Meaning "by men" or "by humans," this word highlights the collective effort of humanity in achieving goals and aspirations.
  • yataḥ: This translates to "from where" or "from which," indicating a source or origin. It encourages individuals to seek the roots of their actions and beliefs.
  • sūryam: Referring to the "sun," this word symbolizes light, knowledge, and enlightenment. It serves as a reminder to seek wisdom and clarity in life.
  • ārohayo: This means "to ascend" or "to rise," suggesting the pursuit of higher goals and aspirations. It encourages individuals to strive for personal and spiritual growth.
  • divi: Meaning "in the heavens" or "in the sky," this word emphasizes the connection to the divine and the importance of looking beyond the material world.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of purification, divine connection, and the importance of creativity and leadership in life. It serves as a reminder to seek purity in thoughts and actions, to embrace the divine aspects of existence, and to strive for personal and spiritual growth. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in practices such as meditation, ethical living, and community leadership, encouraging individuals to contribute positively to society while remaining connected to their inner selves and the divine. By embodying the principles expressed in this mantra, one can cultivate a life of purpose, clarity, and fulfillment.
