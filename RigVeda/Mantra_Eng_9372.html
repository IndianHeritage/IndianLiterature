यत् ते यमं वैवस्वतं मनो जगाम दूरकम् |तत् त आ वर्तयामसिह क्षयाय जीवसे ||
yat te yamaṁ vaivasvatam mano jagāma dūrakam | tat ta ā vartayāmasīha kṣayāya jīvase ||
  • yat te - "What is yours" or "That which belongs to you"
  • yamaṁ vaivasvatam - "Yama, the son of Vivasvat" (Yama is the god of death and the underworld in Hindu mythology)
  • mano jagāma dūrakam - "The mind has gone far away" or "The mind has wandered far"
  • tat ta - "That which" or "That which is"
  • ā vartayāmasīha - "We shall bring back" or "We shall return"
  • kṣayāya - "For the purpose of destruction" or "For the purpose of annihilation"
  • jīvase - "For the sake of life" or "For the sake of living"

This mantra can be interpreted as a plea or a reflection on the nature of life and death. It acknowledges the inevitability of death (represented by Yama) while also expressing a desire to reclaim or return to life, despite the mind's tendency to wander away from the present moment.


In real life, this mantra can be seen as a reminder of the importance of mindfulness and being present. For example, in today's fast-paced world, individuals often find their minds drifting towards worries about the future or regrets about the past. This mantra encourages us to focus on the present and to recognize the transient nature of life.


Furthermore, it highlights the significance of life and the need to cherish it, even in the face of mortality. In practical terms, this could manifest in various ways, such as:

  • Practicing mindfulness meditation to bring awareness to the present moment.
  • Engaging in activities that promote well-being and joy, such as spending time with loved ones or pursuing hobbies.
  • Reflecting on one's life goals and values, ensuring that actions align with what is truly important.

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a profound reminder of the balance between life and death, urging us to live fully and mindfully. It is applicable in various real-life scenarios, encouraging individuals to embrace the present, appreciate life, and confront the inevitability of death with grace and understanding.
