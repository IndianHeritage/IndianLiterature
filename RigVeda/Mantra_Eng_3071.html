अच्छा वोचेय शुशुचानम् अग्निं होतारं विश्वभरसं यजिष्टम् |शुच्य ऊधो अतृणन् न गवाम् अन्धो न पूतम् परिषिक्तम् अंशोः ||
acchā voceya śuśucānam agniṁ hotāraṁ viśvabharasaṁ yajiṣṭham | śucy ūdho atṛṇan na gavām andho na pūtam pariṣiktam aṁśoḥ ||
  • acchā voceya: This phrase can be interpreted as "may we speak well" or "let us express ourselves clearly." It emphasizes the importance of clear communication and the power of words in shaping our reality.
  • śuśucānam: This term refers to "the one who listens well" or "the attentive listener." It highlights the value of being receptive and open to understanding others, which is crucial in building relationships and fostering harmony.
  • agniṁ: This word means "fire," which in Vedic literature symbolizes purity, transformation, and the divine presence. Fire is often associated with rituals and offerings, representing the connection between the material and spiritual worlds.
  • hotāraṁ: This translates to "the one who offers" or "the priest." It signifies the role of the mediator between humans and the divine, emphasizing the importance of rituals and offerings in spiritual practices.
  • viśvabharasaṁ: This phrase means "the one who nourishes all" or "the universal sustainer." It reflects the idea of interconnectedness and the responsibility to care for all beings and the environment.
  • yajiṣṭham: This term refers to "the most worthy of worship" or "the one who is to be revered." It underscores the significance of recognizing and honoring the divine in all aspects of life.
  • śucy: This word means "pure" or "clean," indicating the importance of purity in thoughts, words, and actions. It suggests that purity leads to spiritual growth and clarity of purpose.
  • ūdho: This translates to "the one who rises" or "the elevated." It symbolizes aspiration and the pursuit of higher knowledge and understanding.
  • atṛṇan: This term means "not to be bound" or "free from bondage." It signifies liberation from ignorance and the limitations of the material world.
  • na gavām: This phrase translates to "not of the cows," indicating a distinction from material possessions or wealth. It suggests that true value lies beyond material accumulation.
  • andho: This word means "blind" or "ignorant." It serves as a reminder to seek knowledge and awareness, avoiding the pitfalls of ignorance.
  • na pūtam: This translates to "not impure" or "not contaminated." It emphasizes the importance of maintaining purity in our actions and intentions.
  • pariṣiktam: This term means "anointed" or "consecrated," indicating the significance of rituals in sanctifying our actions and intentions.
  • aṁśoḥ: This word refers to "part" or "aspect," suggesting that every action and intention is a part of a larger whole, contributing to the universal tapestry of existence.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates profound wisdom about communication, spirituality, and the interconnectedness of all beings. It teaches us the importance of clear expression, attentive listening, and the role of rituals in connecting with the divine. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as in personal relationships where effective communication fosters understanding, in community gatherings where rituals bring people together, and in our daily actions where purity of intention leads to a more harmonious existence. By embodying the principles of this mantra, we can contribute to a more compassionate and enlightened world.
