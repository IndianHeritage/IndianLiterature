योध्दासि क्रत्वा शवसोता दंशना विश्वा जाताभि मञ्जना |आ त्वायम अर्क ऊतये ववर्तति यं गोतमाऽजीजनन ||
yoddhāsi kratvā śavasota daṁsanā viśvā jātābhi majmanā | ā tvāyam arka ūtaye vavartati yaṁ gotamā ajījanan ||
  • yoddhāsi: This word translates to "you are a warrior" or "you are a fighter." It signifies strength, courage, and the ability to confront challenges. In real life, this can be seen in individuals who stand up for justice, such as activists or soldiers who fight for their country.
  • kratvā: This term means "by your power" or "through your actions." It emphasizes the importance of taking action and using one's abilities to achieve goals. For example, a person who works hard to achieve their dreams exemplifies this concept.
  • śavasota: This can be interpreted as "the one who overcomes obstacles." It refers to the ability to rise above difficulties. In everyday life, this could relate to someone who faces personal struggles, such as illness or financial hardship, yet perseveres to find success.
  • daṁsanā: This word means "with a bite" or "with intensity." It suggests a fierce approach to challenges. For instance, a competitive athlete who trains rigorously to win a championship embodies this intensity.
  • viśvā: This translates to "all" or "universal." It indicates inclusiveness and the idea that the warrior's strength is not just for personal gain but for the benefit of all. A community leader who works for the welfare of their entire community exemplifies this universal approach.
  • jātābhi: This means "born from" or "arising from." It signifies the source of strength and capability. For example, a person who draws strength from their heritage or culture can be seen as embodying this concept.
  • majmanā: This term refers to "the one who is engaged in action" or "the doer." It highlights the importance of being proactive. An entrepreneur who takes risks to start a new business is a real-life example of this principle.
  • ā tvāyam: This phrase means "to you" or "for you." It indicates a focus on the individual and their journey. It can be related to personal development and self-improvement efforts.
  • arka: This translates to "light" or "radiance." It symbolizes knowledge, enlightenment, and positivity. For instance, a teacher who inspires students to learn and grow represents this light.
  • ūtaye: This means "for the purpose" or "for the aim." It emphasizes the importance of having a goal or objective. In real life, setting personal or professional goals is crucial for success.
  • vavartati: This term means "it revolves" or "it turns." It signifies the cyclical nature of life and the ongoing journey of growth and learning. For example, the seasons changing can be seen as a metaphor for life's cycles.
  • yaṁ: This word means "whom" or "that which." It refers to the subject of the mantra, emphasizing the connection between the warrior and their purpose.
  • gotamā: This term refers to "the one who is wise" or "the enlightened one." It signifies the importance of wisdom in guiding actions. A mentor or guide who provides valuable insights to others exemplifies this wisdom.
  • ajījanan: This means "who has given birth" or "who has created." It highlights the role of creation and nurturing in the journey of life. For instance, a parent who nurtures their child to become a responsible adult embodies this aspect.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the qualities of a warrior—strength, action, overcoming obstacles, and wisdom. It serves as a reminder that each individual has the potential to be a fighter in their own life, using their abilities for the greater good. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire people to take action in their communities, pursue their goals with intensity, and draw strength from their heritage and experiences. It encourages a proactive approach to life, where one not only seeks personal success but also contributes positively to the world around them.
