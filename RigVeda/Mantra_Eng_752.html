जामिः सिन्धूनाम् भ्रातेव स्वस्राम् इभ्यां न राजा वनान्यत्ति ||
jāmiḥ sindhūnām bhrāteva svasrām ibhyān na rājā vanāny atti ||
  • Mantra: "jāmiḥ sindhūnām bhrāteva svasrām ibhyān na rājā vanāny atti"
  • Translation: "I am the river of rivers, like a sister to my brothers; the king does not eat from the forests."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • jāmiḥ: I am
    • sindhūnām: of rivers
    • bhrāteva: like a sister
    • svasrām: to my brothers
    • ibhyān: from them
    • na: not
    • rājā: the king
    • vanāny: from the forests
    • atti: eats
  • Explanation:
    • This mantra metaphorically represents the interconnectedness of nature and the relationships within it. The speaker identifies as a river, emphasizing the importance of rivers in sustaining life and connecting different elements of the ecosystem.
    • The phrase "like a sister to my brothers" suggests a sense of unity and kinship among natural elements, highlighting how rivers (water) support and nurture the land (brothers) around them.
    • The latter part, "the king does not eat from the forests," can be interpreted as a reminder that even those in power (the king) must respect the natural world and cannot take from it without consequence. It emphasizes the idea of sustainability and the need for balance in nature.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In many cultures, rivers are revered as sacred entities that provide water for drinking, agriculture, and transportation. For instance, the Ganges River in India is considered holy and is treated with great respect.
    • Environmental movements often advocate for the protection of rivers and forests, recognizing their vital role in maintaining ecological balance. The mantra serves as a reminder of our responsibility to protect these natural resources.
    • In urban planning, the importance of green spaces and water bodies is increasingly recognized, as they contribute to the well-being of communities and the environment.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra encapsulates the essence of harmony between humanity and nature. It teaches us to recognize our place within the ecosystem and the importance of nurturing our environment.
    • In real life, it is applicable in discussions about environmental conservation, sustainable living, and the respect for natural resources. It encourages individuals and societies to live in balance with nature, ensuring that future generations can also benefit from the rivers and forests.
    • Ultimately, the mantra serves as a philosophical guide, urging us to foster relationships that honor the interconnectedness of all life forms.
