तद दधाना अवस्यवो युष्माभिर दक्षितरः |स्याम मारुत्वतो वृधे ||
tad dadhānā avasyavo yuṣmābhir dakṣapitaraḥ | syāma marutvato vṛdhe ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • tad: that
    • dadhānā: having established
    • avasyavo: surely, certainly
    • yuṣmābhir: by you (plural)
    • dakṣapitaraḥ: skilled fathers or creators
    • syāma: may we be
    • marutvato: of the Maruts (storm deities)
    • vṛdhe: in growth or increase
  • Interpretation:
    • This mantra speaks to the establishment of a connection with the divine forces, particularly the Maruts, who are often associated with storms, winds, and the vitality of nature.
    • The phrase "dakṣapitaraḥ" refers to the skilled creators or fathers, suggesting a reverence for those who have the ability to create and nurture life.
    • The invocation of the Maruts indicates a desire for their blessings in terms of strength, vitality, and growth, which can be interpreted in both a physical and spiritual sense.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In agriculture, farmers often pray for favorable weather conditions, which can be likened to invoking the Maruts for their blessings to ensure a good harvest.
    • In personal development, individuals may seek guidance from mentors (the "skilled fathers") who can help them grow and succeed in their endeavors.
    • Communities may come together to celebrate festivals that honor nature and its forces, reflecting the essence of this mantra in collective growth and harmony.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of connection with divine forces and the acknowledgment of skilled creators in our lives.
    • Its significance lies in the understanding that growth—whether personal, communal, or agricultural—requires both divine blessings and the wisdom of those who guide us.
    • In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire individuals and communities to seek harmony with nature and to value the contributions of mentors and leaders in their journey towards growth and success.
