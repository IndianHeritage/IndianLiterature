अभूर् व औक्षीर् व्य उ1 आयुर् आनड् दर्शनु पूर्वो अपरो नु दर्शन्त् |द्वे पवस्ते परि तं न भूतो यो अस्य पारि रजसो विवेष ||
abhūr v aukṣīr vy u1 āyur ānaḍ darṣan nu pūrvo aparo nu darṣat | dve pavaste pari taṁ na bhūto yo asya pāre rajaso viveṣa ||
  • abhūr: This word can be interpreted as "not existing" or "non-existent." It suggests a state of absence or void.
  • v: A conjunction meaning "and," linking the ideas that follow.
  • aukṣīr: This term can be understood as "to be strong" or "to be powerful." It denotes strength or vigor.
  • vy: This is a prefix that can imply "to be" or "to exist," indicating a state of being.
  • u1: This may refer to a form of emphasis or a specific state of existence.
  • āyur: Meaning "life" or "longevity," it emphasizes the importance of life and vitality.
  • ānaḍ: This word translates to "bliss" or "joy," indicating a state of happiness or fulfillment.
  • darṣan: Meaning "vision" or "sight," it refers to perception or understanding.
  • nu: A particle that can imply "indeed" or "certainly," adding emphasis to the statement.
  • pūrvo: This means "the past" or "previous," indicating something that has already occurred.
  • aparo: Meaning "the future" or "later," it refers to what is yet to come.
  • dve: This translates to "two," indicating a duality or a pair.
  • pavaste: This can be interpreted as "of the winds" or "of the air," suggesting movement or change.
  • pari: Meaning "around" or "about," it indicates a surrounding or encompassing nature.
  • taṁ: This word means "that," referring to something specific that has been mentioned.
  • na: A negation meaning "not," indicating the absence of something.
  • bhūto: This can mean "become" or "to exist," referring to the state of being.
  • yo: Meaning "who," it introduces a relative clause.
  • asya: This means "of this," indicating possession or relation to something specific.
  • pāre: Meaning "beyond" or "across," it suggests a crossing over or transcending.
  • rajaso: This term can be interpreted as "of passion" or "of activity," indicating a dynamic state.
  • viveṣa: Meaning "to enter" or "to penetrate," it suggests an engagement or involvement.

The mantra speaks to the duality of existence, contrasting the past and the future, the non-existent and the powerful, and the dynamic nature of life. It emphasizes the importance of understanding both the transient and the eternal aspects of life. The mention of "two" suggests a balance between opposing forces, such as joy and sorrow, existence and non-existence.


In real life, this mantra can be applied to various situations. For instance, in moments of personal struggle, one might reflect on the transient nature of difficulties, recognizing that they are part of a larger cycle of life. Similarly, in the pursuit of goals, understanding the balance between past experiences and future aspirations can lead to a more grounded approach to personal growth.


Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its exploration of the dualities of existence and the importance of balance in life. It encourages individuals to reflect on their experiences, both past and future, and to find strength in the present moment. By recognizing the interplay of different forces, one can navigate life's challenges with greater wisdom and resilience.
