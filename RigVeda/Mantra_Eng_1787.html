यत् त्वेषयामā नदयन्त पर्वतान् दिवो वा पृष्ठं नार्याः अचुच्यवुः |विश्वो वो अज्मन भयते वनस्पती रथीयन्तीव प्र जिहीत ओषधिः ||
yat tveṣayāmā nadayanta parvatān divo vā pṛṣṭhaṁ naryā acucyavuḥ | viśvo vo ajman bhayate vanaspatī rathīyantīva pra jihīta oṣadhiḥ ||
  • yat tveṣayāmā nadayanta parvatān divo vā pṛṣṭhaṁ naryā acucyavuḥ
    • yat - "that which" or "what". This word indicates a reference to something specific.
    • tveṣayāmā - "you have made manifest" or "you have brought forth". This suggests the act of creation or emergence.
    • nadayanta - "flowing" or "moving". This can refer to rivers or streams, symbolizing life and continuity.
    • parvatān - "mountains". Mountains often symbolize stability, strength, and the challenges one faces in life.
    • divo vā - "of the heavens" or "from the sky". This phrase connects the earthly with the divine.
    • pṛṣṭhaṁ - "the back" or "the support". This can imply a foundation or something that supports life.
    • naryā - "by men" or "by humans". This indicates human involvement or action.
    • acucyavuḥ - "they have spoken" or "they have declared". This suggests communication or expression of thoughts.
  • viśvo vo ajman bhayate vanaspatī rathīyantīva pra jihīta oṣadhiḥ
    • viśvo - "all" or "universal". This word encompasses everything and everyone.
    • vo - "to you". This indicates a direction towards a group or collective.
    • ajman - "the mind" or "thought". This refers to the cognitive aspect of beings.
    • bhayate - "is afraid" or "is in fear". This suggests vulnerability or concern.
    • vanaspatī - "the lord of the forest" or "trees". This symbolizes nature and its protective qualities.
    • rathīyantīva - "like a charioteer". This implies guidance and direction in life.
    • pra jihīta - "may it be directed" or "may it lead". This indicates a desire for guidance or purpose.
    • oṣadhiḥ - "herbs" or "plants". This symbolizes healing and nourishment from nature.

Conclusion:

This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the interconnectedness of nature, humanity, and the divine. It reflects on how the elements of the earth, such as rivers and mountains, are manifestations of a greater cosmic order. The mention of fear and the need for guidance suggests that humans often seek support from nature and the divine in times of uncertainty.

In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • Environmental conservation efforts, where the importance of preserving nature is highlighted.
  • Personal growth and self-reflection, where individuals seek guidance from their surroundings and inner thoughts.
  • Community building, where collective efforts are made to support each other, akin to how nature supports life.

Ultimately, this mantra serves as a reminder of our reliance on the natural world and the importance of harmony between humanity and the environment.
