तत त इन्द्रियम् परमम् पराचैर अधारयन्त कवयः पूरेदम् |क्षमेदम् अन्यद दिव्य अन्यद अस्य समी पृच्यते समानेव केतुः ||
tat ta indriyam paramam parācair adhārayanta kavayaḥ puredam | kṣamedam anyad divy a1nyad asya sam ī pṛcyate samaneva ketuḥ ||
  • tat: This word translates to "that" and signifies a reference to something beyond the immediate perception, often pointing towards the ultimate reality or truth.
  • ta: This can be interpreted as "that" as well, reinforcing the idea of a higher state of being or consciousness.
  • indriyam: This refers to the senses or faculties of perception. In a real-life context, it can be related to how we perceive the world around us through our senses.
  • paramam: Meaning "supreme" or "highest," this word indicates the ultimate state of existence or consciousness that transcends ordinary experience.
  • parācair: This term means "by the wise" or "by those who have knowledge." It emphasizes the role of wisdom in understanding deeper truths.
  • adhārayanta: This translates to "supporting" or "upholding," suggesting that the supreme reality is the foundation upon which everything else rests.
  • kavayaḥ: This word means "the seers" or "the poets," referring to those who have the insight to perceive and articulate profound truths.
  • puredam: This can be interpreted as "this city" or "this place," symbolizing the physical world or the human experience.
  • kṣamedam: Meaning "this is to be understood," it indicates the importance of comprehension and insight in our journey through life.
  • anyad: This translates to "other" or "different," suggesting the existence of multiple layers of reality or understanding.
  • divy: Meaning "divine" or "heavenly," this word points to the higher, spiritual aspects of existence.
  • anyad: Reiterating the idea of "other," it emphasizes the multiplicity of experiences and perceptions.
  • asya: This means "of this," referring to the context or the subject being discussed.
  • sam: Meaning "together" or "equally," it suggests harmony and balance in understanding different aspects of reality.
  • ī: This can be interpreted as "to be" or "exist," reinforcing the idea of existence as a fundamental concept.
  • pṛcyate: This translates to "is asked" or "is inquired," indicating the importance of questioning and seeking knowledge.
  • samaneva: Meaning "as if equal," it suggests that all aspects of reality are interconnected and should be viewed with equal importance.
  • ketuḥ: This word refers to a "banner" or "flag," symbolizing guidance and direction in the pursuit of knowledge and truth.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the importance of perceiving the supreme reality through our senses and intellect. It highlights the role of wisdom and inquiry in understanding the deeper truths of existence. In real life, this can be applied in various scenarios, such as in education, where seeking knowledge and understanding is paramount, or in personal growth, where one strives to perceive the world beyond superficial experiences. The mantra encourages individuals to seek harmony and balance in their understanding of life, recognizing that all experiences are interconnected and contribute to a greater understanding of the divine. Ultimately, it serves as a reminder to pursue wisdom and insight in our daily lives, guiding us towards a more profound comprehension of our existence and the universe around us.
