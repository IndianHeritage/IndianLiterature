सोमः पुनानो अव्यये वारे शिशुर न क्रीळन् पवमानो अक्षाः | सहस्रधारः शतवाज इन्दुः ||
somaḥ punāno avyaye vāre śiśur na krīḻan pavamāno akṣāḥ | sahasradhāraḥ śatavāja induḥ ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • somaḥ: Refers to the sacred drink, Soma, which is associated with immortality and divine inspiration.
    • punāno: Means 'purifying' or 'refreshing'.
    • avyaye: Translates to 'imperishable' or 'eternal'.
    • vāre: Means 'in the waters' or 'in the flow'.
    • śiśur: Refers to 'child' or 'young one'.
    • na: Means 'not'.
    • krīḻan: Means 'playing' or 'engaging'.
    • pavamāno: Refers to 'the one who is purifying' or 'the purifier'.
    • akṣāḥ: Means 'the eyes' or 'the vision'.
    • sahasradhāraḥ: Translates to 'having a thousand streams' or 'multifaceted'.
    • śatavāja: Means 'having a hundred powers' or 'strong'.
    • induḥ: Refers to 'moon' or 'nectar', often symbolizing nourishment and fertility.
  • Interpretation:
    • This mantra invokes the essence of Soma, symbolizing the divine and the life-giving properties of nature.
    • The reference to 'purifying' and 'imperishable' suggests a quest for spiritual clarity and eternal truth.
    • The imagery of 'a child not playing' may represent innocence or the purity of a new beginning, emphasizing the importance of maintaining purity in our actions.
    • 'The one who is purifying' indicates a force or energy that cleanses and rejuvenates, akin to the role of water in nature.
    • 'Having a thousand streams' and 'a hundred powers' signifies abundance and the multifaceted nature of existence, suggesting that life is rich with opportunities and resources.
    • The mention of 'moon' or 'nectar' symbolizes nourishment, both physical and spiritual, highlighting the importance of sustenance in our lives.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In daily life, the concept of 'purification' can be seen in practices such as meditation, yoga, or even cleansing rituals that aim to refresh the mind and spirit.
    • The idea of 'innocence' and 'new beginnings' can be related to the birth of a child, where the purity of the newborn symbolizes hope and potential.
    • In nature, rivers and streams serve as purifiers, providing clean water that sustains life, reflecting the mantra's emphasis on the importance of purity and nourishment.
    • In a broader context, the abundance of resources in our environment can be likened to the 'thousand streams', reminding us to appreciate and utilize what we have wisely.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra encapsulates the essence of life, emphasizing the importance of purity, nourishment, and the multifaceted nature of existence.
    • Its significance lies in reminding us to seek spiritual clarity, appreciate the resources around us, and maintain a sense of innocence and purity in our actions.
    • In real-life scenarios, it encourages us to engage in practices that promote well-being, both for ourselves and the environment, fostering a deeper connection with the world around us.
