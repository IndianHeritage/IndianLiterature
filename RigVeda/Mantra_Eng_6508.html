दशस्यन्ता मनवे पूर्व्यं दिवि यवं वृकेण कर्षथः |तां वाम अद्य सुमतिभिः शुभस पतिः अश्विनां प्र स्तुवीमहि ||
daśasyantā manave pūrvyaṁ divi yavaṁ vṛkeṇa karṣathaḥ | tā vām adya sumatibhiḥ śubhas patī aśvinā pra stuvīmahi ||
  • daśasyantā: This term can be interpreted as "those who are tenfold" or "the tenfold ones." In a broader sense, it may refer to completeness or a multitude of aspects in life.
  • manave: This word translates to "to Manu," who is often considered the progenitor of humanity in Vedic texts. It signifies the importance of human values and ethics.
  • pūrvyaṁ: Meaning "from the past" or "ancient," this word emphasizes the wisdom and knowledge passed down through generations.
  • divi: This translates to "in the heavens" or "in the sky," indicating a connection to the divine or higher realms.
  • yavaṁ: This means "barley" or "grain," symbolizing sustenance and nourishment, both physical and spiritual.
  • vṛkeṇa: This term means "with the help of the wolf" or "by the strength of the wolf," which can symbolize strength, protection, and the wild aspects of nature.
  • karṣathaḥ: This translates to "you cultivate" or "you plow," indicating the act of working hard and putting effort into one's endeavors.
  • tā: This means "those" or "that," referring to the blessings or qualities being invoked.
  • vām: This translates to "your" or "of you," indicating a personal connection to the divine or the qualities being sought.
  • adya: Meaning "today," this word emphasizes the immediacy and relevance of the invocation in the present moment.
  • sumatibhiḥ: This translates to "with good thoughts" or "with wisdom," highlighting the importance of positive intentions and clarity of mind.
  • śubhas: Meaning "auspicious" or "good," this word signifies the positive outcomes and blessings one seeks.
  • patī: This translates to "lord" or "master," often referring to divine beings or higher powers that guide and protect.
  • aśvinā: Referring to the Aśvins, the twin deities of health and medicine in Vedic mythology, symbolizing healing and well-being.
  • pra stuvīmahi: This means "we praise" or "we invoke," indicating the act of offering reverence and seeking blessings.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a deep reverence for the divine, the importance of human effort, and the cultivation of good thoughts and auspicious outcomes. It serves as a reminder that while we draw from ancient wisdom and the strength of nature, our actions today, guided by positive intentions, can lead to a fruitful and harmonious life. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during agricultural practices, personal development, or community gatherings, where collective efforts and positive thoughts are essential for success and well-being.
