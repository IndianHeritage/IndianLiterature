तन् नो वातो मयोभुव वातु भेषजं तन् मातृ पृथिवी तत् पिता द्यौः |तद् ग्रावाणः सोमसुतो मयोभुवस तद् अश्विनाः श्रिणुतं धिष्ण्याः युवम् ||
tan no vāto mayobhu vātu bheṣajaṁ tan mātā pṛthivī tat pitā dyauḥ | tad grāvāṇaḥ somasuto mayobhuvas tad aśvinā śṛṇutaṁ dhiṣṇyā yuvam ||
  • tan no vāto mayobhu vātu bheṣajaṁ: This phrase can be translated as "May the wind (vāta) that is beneficial (mayobhu) be our healer (bheṣaja)." Here, the wind symbolizes the vital life force or energy that sustains life. In real life, we can relate this to the importance of clean air and the role of nature in our health. For example, spending time in nature, such as forests or mountains, can rejuvenate our spirits and improve our well-being.
  • tan mātā pṛthivī tat pitā dyauḥ: This translates to "The Earth (pṛthivī) is our mother (mātā) and the sky (dyauḥ) is our father (pitā)." This line emphasizes the nurturing aspects of nature. In our daily lives, we can see this in the way we depend on the Earth for resources like food and water, and how the sky provides sunlight and weather patterns essential for life. For instance, sustainable farming practices reflect this relationship, as they honor the Earth as a provider.
  • tad grāvāṇaḥ somasuto mayobhuvas: This can be interpreted as "May the son of the moon (somasuto) bring us prosperity (mayobhuvas)." The moon is often associated with calmness and fertility. In real life, this can be seen in agricultural practices that align with lunar cycles, which many cultures believe enhance crop yields. For example, some farmers plant certain crops during specific lunar phases to maximize growth.
  • tad aśvinā śṛṇutaṁ dhiṣṇyā yuvam: This translates to "Listen (śṛṇutaṁ) to us, O Aśvins (aśvinā), in our dwelling (dhiṣṇyā)." The Aśvins are twin deities associated with health and medicine. This line calls for divine attention and assistance in our lives. In modern contexts, this can relate to seeking help from healthcare professionals or the importance of community support in times of illness or distress. For instance, community health initiatives often embody this call for collective well-being.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a deep reverence for nature and the interconnectedness of life. It highlights the significance of the elements—wind, earth, and sky—as essential to our existence and well-being. In real life, this mantra encourages us to respect and nurture our environment, seek harmony with nature, and recognize the importance of community and health. By embodying these principles, we can foster a more sustainable and healthy world for ourselves and future generations.
