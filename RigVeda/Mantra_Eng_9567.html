ब्रह्मणस पतिर एता सं कर्मार इवाधमत् |देवानाम पूर्व्ये युगे 'सतः सद अजायत ||
brahmaṇas patir etā saṁ karmāra ivādhamat | devānām pūrvye yuge 'sataḥ sad ajāyata ||
  • Translation: The mantra translates to "O Lord of the Brahmanas, you who are the master of actions, you were born in the ancient times of the gods, in the age of the hundred." This invocation addresses a divine figure associated with knowledge and action.
  • Word Breakdown:
    • Brahmaṇas: Refers to the learned or wise individuals, often associated with the priestly class in Vedic society.
    • Patir: Means 'lord' or 'master', indicating authority and leadership.
    • Etā: Translates to 'this' or 'here', pointing to the present context.
    • Saṁ: Means 'together' or 'with', suggesting unity in action.
    • Karmāra: Refers to 'the doer of actions', emphasizing the importance of action in life.
    • Ivādhamat: Means 'like the ancient', indicating a connection to timeless wisdom.
    • Devānām: Refers to 'of the gods', highlighting the divine aspect.
    • Pūrvye: Means 'in the ancient times', suggesting a historical or mythological context.
    • Yuge: Refers to 'age' or 'era', indicating a specific period in time.
    • 'Sataḥ: Means 'of a hundred', which can symbolize completeness or fullness.
    • Sad: Means 'truth' or 'reality', emphasizing the importance of authenticity.
    • Ajāyata: Means 'was born', indicating the emergence of something significant.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In a corporate setting, a leader who embodies the qualities of a 'Brahmana' can inspire their team to work together harmoniously, emphasizing the importance of collective action.
    • In education, teachers who guide students with wisdom and knowledge can be seen as modern-day 'Brahmanas', fostering a culture of learning and inquiry.
    • In community service, individuals who take initiative to help others reflect the essence of 'Karmāra', demonstrating that actions rooted in compassion can lead to positive change.
  • Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its call for unity, wisdom, and action. It reminds us that true leadership is not just about authority but also about guiding others towards a common goal. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as leadership in organizations, teaching in educational institutions, and community service initiatives. By embodying the principles of knowledge, action, and unity, we can create a more harmonious and productive society.
