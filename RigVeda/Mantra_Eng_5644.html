अव वेदिं होत्राभिर्यजेत रिपः काश्चिद्वरुणधृतः सः |परि द्वेषोभिरार्यमा वृणक्तूरुं सुदासे वृषणा उ लोकम् ||
ava vediṁ hotrābhir yajeta ripaḥ kāś cid varuṇadhrutaḥ saḥ | pari dveṣobhir aryamā vṛṇaktūruṁ sudāse vṛṣaṇā u lokam ||
  • ava vediṁ: This phrase can be interpreted as "to approach the altar" or "to come near the sacred space." In real life, this can symbolize the act of preparing oneself for a spiritual or significant endeavor, much like how individuals prepare for important events or rituals in their lives.
  • hotrābhir: This term refers to "the priests" or "the ones who perform the rituals." In contemporary terms, this can relate to leaders or guides in various fields, such as mentors in personal development or spiritual leaders in communities.
  • yajeta: This means "to perform a sacrifice" or "to offer." In modern contexts, this can be seen as the act of giving or contributing to a cause, such as volunteering time or resources for community service or charitable activities.
  • ripaḥ: This word translates to "the enemy" or "opponent." In everyday life, this can represent challenges or adversities that one faces, whether in personal relationships, professional settings, or internal struggles.
  • kāś cid: This phrase means "somehow" or "in some way." It reflects the idea that there are various paths and methods to achieve goals, emphasizing flexibility and adaptability in life.
  • varuṇadhrutaḥ: This term refers to "one who holds the cosmic order" or "the upholder of truth." In real life, this can symbolize the importance of integrity and ethical behavior in our actions and decisions, akin to leaders who uphold justice and fairness.
  • saḥ: This simply means "he" or "that one," referring to the subject of the mantra, which can be interpreted as the individual or the community engaged in the ritual.
  • pari dveṣobhir: This translates to "with enmity all around." It signifies the presence of conflict or negativity in one's environment. In real life, this can relate to situations where one feels surrounded by competition or hostility, such as in a challenging workplace or during personal conflicts.
  • aryamā: This term refers to "the noble one" or "the one who is worthy." It can represent individuals who inspire others through their actions and character, such as role models or community leaders.
  • vṛṇaktūruṁ: This means "may he choose" or "may he select." It emphasizes the power of choice and decision-making in life, highlighting the importance of making conscious and thoughtful choices.
  • sudāse: This translates to "with good deeds" or "with virtuous actions." It underscores the significance of acting with kindness and integrity, which can lead to positive outcomes in personal and communal relationships.
  • vṛṣaṇā: This term refers to "the strong" or "the powerful." It can symbolize resilience and strength in facing life's challenges, akin to individuals who overcome adversity through determination and courage.
  • u lokam: This means "to the world" or "to the realm." It signifies the broader impact of one's actions and choices on society and the environment, reminding us of our interconnectedness with the world around us.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of approaching challenges with integrity, making conscious choices, and acting with virtue in the face of adversity. Its significance lies in the reminder that, despite the presence of conflict and negativity, one can strive to uphold truth and goodness. In real-life scenarios, this mantra encourages individuals to engage in self-reflection, act ethically, and contribute positively to their communities, ultimately fostering a harmonious and just society.
