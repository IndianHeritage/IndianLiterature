देवाः कपोत इषितो यदिच्छन् दूतो निरृत्या इदम आजगाम |तस्मा अर्चाम कृतवाम निष्कृतिं शं नो अस्तु द्विपदे शं चतुष्पदे ||
devāḥ kapota iṣito yad icchan dūto nirṛtyā idam ājagāma | tasmā arcāma kṛṇavāma niṣkṛtiṁ śaṁ no astu dvipade śaṁ catuṣpade ||
  • devāḥ kapota iṣito: This phrase can be translated as "the gods, like the messenger pigeon." Here, the messenger pigeon symbolizes communication and the divine connection between the earthly realm and the celestial beings.
  • yad icchan dūto nirṛtyā: This means "whatever the messenger desires, he comes with the power of the goddess of death." It suggests that the messenger brings important messages, sometimes related to life and death, emphasizing the weight of the communication.
  • idam ājagāma: Translated as "this has come," indicating the arrival of the message or the divine presence in the material world.
  • tasmā arcāma: This means "therefore, we worship." It signifies the act of reverence and acknowledgment of the divine forces at play.
  • kṛṇavāma niṣkṛtiṁ: This translates to "let us create a release from bondage." It implies a desire for liberation from the troubles and constraints of life.
  • śaṁ no astu dvipade: Meaning "may peace be upon us, both two-legged," referring to humans and their need for peace and harmony.
  • śaṁ catuṣpade: This translates to "and peace upon the four-legged," which includes animals and nature, highlighting the interconnectedness of all living beings.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the importance of communication with the divine and the need for peace and liberation in our lives. It serves as a reminder that our actions and intentions should be aligned with the greater good, not just for ourselves but for all living beings. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as in conflict resolution, fostering harmony in communities, and promoting environmental consciousness. By acknowledging the divine and seeking peace, we can create a more harmonious existence for ourselves and the world around us.
