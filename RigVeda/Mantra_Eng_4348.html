नीचीनबाणं वरुणः कवन्धम् प्र ससर्ज रोदसी अन्तरिक्षम् |तेन विश्वस्य भुवनस्य राजा यवं न वृष्टिर व्युनत्ति भूम ||
nīcīnabāraṁ varuṇaḥ kavandham pra sasarja rodasī antarikṣam | tena viśvasya bhuvanasya rājā yavaṁ na vṛṣṭir vy unatti bhūma ||
  • nīcīnabāraṁ: This term can be broken down into "nīcī" meaning low or humble, and "nābaraṁ" which refers to the sky or the heavens. Together, it suggests a connection between the humble and the divine.
  • varuṇaḥ: Varuna is a significant deity in Vedic literature, often associated with the cosmic order (Rita) and the waters. He represents the moral and ethical aspects of the universe.
  • kavandham: This word can be interpreted as the one who is all-encompassing or the one who holds everything together. It signifies the unity of existence.
  • pra sasarja: "Pra" means forth or out, and "sasarja" means to create or to bring into being. This indicates the act of creation or manifestation.
  • rodasī: Refers to the two realms of existence, often interpreted as the earth and the sky, or the physical and the spiritual.
  • antarikṣam: This term refers to the space or the atmosphere, the intermediary realm between the earth and the heavens.
  • tena: Meaning "by that" or "through that," indicating a causal relationship.
  • viśvasya: This means "of the universe" or "of all," suggesting a universal application.
  • bhuvanasya: Refers to "of the worlds" or "of the realms," indicating the various planes of existence.
  • rājā: Meaning "king" or "ruler," this signifies authority and governance.
  • yavaṁ: This term can be interpreted as "grain" or "food," symbolizing sustenance and nourishment.
  • na vṛṣṭir: "Na" means "not," and "vṛṣṭir" refers to "rain" or "precipitation," indicating a lack or absence.
  • vy unatti: This can be understood as "to rise" or "to elevate," suggesting growth or improvement.
  • bhūma: Refers to the earth or the ground, symbolizing stability and foundation.

Conclusion: This mantra reflects the interconnectedness of the humble and the divine, emphasizing the role of Varuna as the cosmic ruler who creates and sustains the universe. It highlights the importance of balance between the physical and spiritual realms, and the necessity of nourishment for growth. In real life, this can be applied to our understanding of environmental stewardship, where we recognize the need for sustainable practices to ensure the health of our planet (bhūma) and the well-being of all living beings. It serves as a reminder that our actions have consequences on the broader universe, urging us to act with responsibility and respect towards nature and each other.
