श्रुधी न इन्द्र हवयामसि त्वा महो वाजस्य सातौ वावृषाणः |सं यद विश्वो 'यन्त शूरसाता उग्रं नो 'वः पार्ये अहन दाः ||
śrudhī na indra hvayāmasi tvā maho vājasya sātau vāvṛṣāṇāḥ | saṁ yad viśo 'yanta śūrasātā ugraṁ no 'vaḥ pārye ahan dāḥ ||
  • śrudhī: This word can be translated as "listen" or "hear." It emphasizes the importance of being attentive and receptive to divine guidance.
  • na: This is a negation particle, meaning "not." It sets a tone of urgency or importance in the context of the mantra.
  • indra: Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic tradition, often associated with strength, rain, and thunder. He represents power and authority.
  • hvayāmasi: This means "we invoke" or "we call upon." It signifies a plea or request directed towards Indra.
  • tvā: This translates to "you," referring directly to Indra, indicating a personal connection in the invocation.
  • maho: Meaning "great" or "mighty," it highlights the immense power of Indra.
  • vājasya: This word means "of strength" or "of vigor," suggesting the qualities that are being sought from Indra.
  • sātau: This can be interpreted as "in the battle" or "in the contest," indicating a context of struggle or challenge.
  • vāvṛṣāṇāḥ: This term refers to "the strong ones" or "the powerful," possibly indicating warriors or those who are courageous.
  • saṁ: This means "together" or "with," suggesting unity in the invocation.
  • yad: Translated as "when" or "that which," it introduces a condition or a scenario.
  • viśo: This refers to "the people" or "the community," emphasizing the collective aspect of the invocation.
  • 'yanta: This means "they go" or "they move," indicating action or progress.
  • śūrasātā: This translates to "the brave" or "the valiant," highlighting the qualities of those being invoked.
  • ugraṁ: Meaning "fierce" or "powerful," it describes the intensity of the qualities being sought.
  • no: This means "our," indicating possession or a personal connection to the qualities being invoked.
  • 'vaḥ: This translates to "to you," reinforcing the direct address to Indra.
  • pārye: This means "to protect" or "to guard," indicating a desire for safety and security.
  • ahan: This translates to "day" or "light," symbolizing clarity and enlightenment.
  • dāḥ: This means "gifts" or "boons," indicating the blessings being sought from Indra.

Conclusion: This mantra is a powerful invocation to Indra, seeking his strength, protection, and blessings for the community. It emphasizes the importance of unity and courage in facing challenges. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in situations where individuals or groups face difficulties, such as during a crisis, competition, or any form of struggle. By invoking the qualities of strength and bravery, individuals can find the motivation to overcome obstacles and achieve their goals. The significance of this mantra lies in its ability to inspire collective action and resilience, reminding us that we can call upon greater forces for support in our endeavors.
