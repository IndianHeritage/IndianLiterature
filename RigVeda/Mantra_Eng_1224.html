याभिर महाम अतिथिग्वं कशोजुवं दिवोदासं शम्बरहत्य आवतम् |याभिः पूर्ब्हिद्ये त्रसदस्युम आवतम् ताभिर ऊषु ऊतिभिर अश्विनाऽ गतम् ||
yābhir mahām atithigvaṁ kaśojuvaṁ divodāsaṁ śambarahatya āvatam | yābhiḥ pūrbhidye trasadasyum āvataṁ tābhir ū ṣu ūtibhir aśvinā gatam ||
  • yābhir: This word translates to "by whom" or "through which." It indicates a connection or means of action.
  • mahām: Meaning "great" or "mighty," this term emphasizes the significance of the subject being discussed.
  • atithigvaṁ: Refers to "the guest" or "the one who is welcomed." In many cultures, hospitality is a revered value, showcasing the importance of treating guests with respect and honor.
  • kaśojuvaṁ: This can be interpreted as "who is worthy" or "who is deserving." It highlights the qualities that make someone admirable or respected.
  • divodāsaṁ: This term refers to "the one who serves the divine" or "the devotee of the gods." It signifies a person who is dedicated to spiritual practices and the worship of deities.
  • śambarahatya: This word can be understood as "the one who overcomes obstacles" or "the conqueror of challenges." It reflects the idea of resilience and strength in facing difficulties.
  • āvatam: Meaning "to protect" or "to shelter," this term emphasizes the importance of safety and security in one's life.
  • pūrbhidye: This translates to "in the past" or "from ancient times," indicating a connection to tradition and history.
  • trasadasyum: Refers to "the one who is fearful" or "the one who trembles." It acknowledges human vulnerability and the natural fears that arise in life.
  • tābhir: Meaning "by them" or "through those," it indicates a collective action or influence.
  • ū: This word can be interpreted as "to" or "towards," suggesting direction or purpose.
  • ṣu: Meaning "good" or "well," it emphasizes positive outcomes or favorable conditions.
  • ūtibhir: This term refers to "by the powers" or "through the strengths," indicating the influence of divine or spiritual forces.
  • aśvinā: Refers to "the Ashvins," who are twin deities associated with health, medicine, and well-being in Vedic literature. They symbolize the importance of healing and vitality.
  • gatam: Meaning "gone" or "departed," it signifies a transition or movement from one state to another.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the significance of hospitality, respect for guests, and the importance of overcoming challenges with divine support. It highlights the qualities of resilience, protection, and the pursuit of well-being. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as in the practice of welcoming guests with warmth, facing life's challenges with courage, and seeking divine guidance in times of need. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of human experiences and the divine, encouraging individuals to cultivate strength, compassion, and a sense of community.
