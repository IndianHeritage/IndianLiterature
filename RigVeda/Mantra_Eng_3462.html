अनश्वो जातो अनभिषुर उक्त्यो रथस त्रिचक्रः परि वर्तते राजाḥ |महत तद वो देवस्य प्रवाचनं dyām ऋभवः पृथिवीं यच च पुष्यथा ||
anaśvo jāto anabhīśur ukthyo3 rathas tricakraḥ pari vartate rajaḥ | mahat tad vo devyasya pravācanaṁ dyām ṛbhavaḥ pṛthivīṁ yac ca puṣyatha ||
  • anaśvo jāto anabhīśur ukthyo: This phrase can be broken down into three parts.
    • anaśvo: Refers to something that is not a horse, symbolizing a state of being that is beyond the ordinary.
    • jāto: Means 'born' or 'arisen', indicating the emergence of something new.
    • anabhīśur: Translates to 'without fear', suggesting a state of confidence and courage.
  • ukthyo rathas tricakraḥ pari vartate rajaḥ: This line describes a chariot with three wheels, which can be interpreted as a metaphor for the journey of life.
    • ukthyo: Refers to hymns or praises, indicating the importance of words and intentions in our actions.
    • rathas tricakraḥ: The 'three-wheeled chariot' symbolizes balance and stability in life, where each wheel represents different aspects such as mind, body, and spirit.
    • pari vartate rajaḥ: Means 'the chariot moves in the realm', suggesting that our actions and intentions propel us forward in life.
  • mahat tad vo devyasya pravācanaṁ: This part emphasizes the significance of divine guidance.
    • mahat: Means 'great' or 'important', highlighting the value of the teachings.
    • tad vo: Translates to 'that for you', indicating a personal connection to the divine message.
    • devyasya pravācanaṁ: Refers to the 'divine proclamation', suggesting that we should heed the wisdom imparted by higher powers.
  • dyām ṛbhavaḥ pṛthivīṁ yac ca puṣyatha: This line calls for the nurturing of both the heavens and the earth.
    • dyām: Refers to the sky or heavens, symbolizing higher aspirations and spiritual growth.
    • ṛbhavaḥ: Means 'to nourish' or 'to cultivate', indicating the importance of care and attention.
    • pṛthivīṁ: Refers to the earth, representing our physical existence and responsibilities.
    • yac ca puṣyatha: Translates to 'and that which you cultivate', emphasizing the need for active participation in nurturing both spiritual and material aspects of life.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the essence of a balanced life, where one must cultivate both spiritual and material aspects. It teaches us to be fearless in our pursuits, to honor the divine guidance we receive, and to actively nurture our surroundings. In real life, this can be applied in various scenarios such as personal development, community service, and environmental conservation. By embodying these principles, we can lead a fulfilling life that honors both our inner and outer worlds.
