अपां पेरुं जीवधन्यम् भरामहे देवाव्यं सुहवम् अध्वरश्रियम् |सुरश्मिं सोमं इन्द्रियं यमीमहि तद् देवानाम् अवो अद्यā वृणीमहे ||
apām peruṁ jīvadhanyam bharāmahe devāvyaṁ suhavam adhvaraśriyam | suraśmiṁ somam indriyaṁ yamīmahi tad devānām avo adyā vṛṇīmahe ||
  • apām: This word translates to "of the waters." In many cultures, water is seen as a source of life and sustenance. For example, rivers and lakes are often central to communities, providing not just drinking water but also supporting agriculture and fishing.
  • peruṁ: This means "great" or "abundant." It signifies the vastness and richness of the waters, akin to how a bountiful harvest can sustain a community.
  • jīvadhanyam: This translates to "life-giving wealth." It emphasizes the importance of water as a vital resource for life, similar to how access to clean water is crucial for health and well-being in modern society.
  • bharāmahe: This means "we invoke" or "we bear." It indicates a call to action, suggesting that we should actively seek and cherish the blessings of nature.
  • devāvyaṁ: This translates to "divine" or "of the gods." It highlights the sacredness of the elements, reminding us to respect and honor nature as a divine gift.
  • suhavam: This means "pleasant" or "auspicious." It suggests that the blessings we seek should bring joy and positivity into our lives, much like how a beautiful day can uplift our spirits.
  • adhvaraśriyam: This translates to "the glory of the sacrifice." It refers to the importance of rituals and offerings in connecting with the divine, similar to how people today may engage in community service or charitable acts as a form of giving back.
  • suraśmiṁ: This means "in the light of the gods." It signifies enlightenment and guidance, akin to how we seek wisdom from mentors or spiritual leaders in our lives.
  • somam: This translates to "the moon" or "soma," often associated with nourishment and immortality. In real life, it can symbolize the cycles of nature and the importance of balance in our lives.
  • indriyaṁ: This means "senses" or "organs." It emphasizes the connection between our physical existence and the divine, reminding us to be mindful of our actions and their impact on the world around us.
  • yamīmahi: This translates to "we meditate upon." It suggests a practice of reflection and contemplation, similar to how mindfulness and meditation are used today to enhance mental well-being.
  • tad: This means "that." It refers to the divine essence we are invoking.
  • devānām: This translates to "of the gods." It reinforces the connection to the divine and the importance of seeking their blessings.
  • avo: This means "may we receive." It is a plea for divine grace and support in our endeavors.
  • adyā: This translates to "today." It emphasizes the immediacy of our request, highlighting the importance of living in the present moment.
  • vṛṇīmahe: This means "we choose" or "we select." It signifies the act of making conscious choices in our lives, akin to how we make decisions that shape our future.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a deep reverence for nature, particularly water, as a source of life and sustenance. It calls for an active engagement with the divine, encouraging us to seek blessings that bring joy and prosperity. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as environmental conservation efforts, community service, and personal mindfulness practices. By recognizing the sacredness of nature and our interconnectedness with it, we can foster a more harmonious existence, both individually and collectively. This mantra serves as a reminder to honor the resources we have and to make conscious choices that benefit not just ourselves but the world around us.
