तिग्मं चिद एमा महि वर्पो अस्य भसद अश्वो न यमसाना आसा |विजेहमानः परशुर न जिह्वां द्रविर न द्रावयति दारु धक्षत ||
tigmaṁ cid ema mahi varpo asya bhasad aśvo na yamasāna āsā | vijehamānaḥ paraśur na jihvāṁ dravir na drāvayati dāru dhakṣat ||
  • tigmaṁ: This word translates to "sharp" or "piercing." It signifies something that has a cutting edge or is intense in nature.
  • cid: This means "indeed" or "certainly," emphasizing the truth of the statement being made.
  • ema: This can be interpreted as "this" or "here," indicating a focus on the subject at hand.
  • mahi: This word means "great" or "earth," often referring to the vastness of the universe or the earth itself.
  • varpo: This translates to "form" or "body," suggesting a physical manifestation or presence.
  • asya: Meaning "of this," it connects the previous words to the subject being discussed.
  • bhasad: This word means "shining" or "radiant," indicating a quality of brightness or enlightenment.
  • aśvo: This translates to "horse," which in Vedic literature often symbolizes strength, speed, and vitality.
  • na: This means "not," negating the following term.
  • yamasāna: This can be interpreted as "the one who is restrained" or "the one who controls," referring to discipline or self-control.
  • āsā: This means "hope" or "expectation," indicating a sense of aspiration or desire.
  • vijehamānaḥ: This translates to "one who is victorious" or "one who overcomes," suggesting triumph over challenges.
  • paraśur: This means "axe," symbolizing power and the ability to cut through obstacles.
  • na: Again, this means "not," negating the following term.
  • jihvāṁ: This translates to "tongue," which can symbolize speech or communication.
  • dravir: This means "substance" or "wealth," indicating material or spiritual resources.
  • na: Once more, this means "not," negating the following term.
  • drāvayati: This translates to "to flow" or "to dissolve," suggesting a sense of movement or change.
  • dāru: This means "wood" or "tree," often symbolizing stability and growth.
  • dhakṣat: This translates to "burns" or "consumes," indicating transformation through fire.

The mantra can be interpreted as a powerful invocation of strength, resilience, and transformation. It speaks to the sharpness of the mind and spirit, the importance of self-control, and the ability to overcome obstacles. The imagery of the horse suggests vitality and energy, while the axe symbolizes the power to cut through challenges. The negations present in the mantra emphasize the importance of discipline and the avoidance of distractions that can lead one away from their goals.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • Personal Development: The mantra encourages individuals to cultivate sharpness in their thoughts and actions, promoting self-discipline and focus in achieving personal goals.
  • Overcoming Challenges: It serves as a reminder that one can overcome obstacles with determination and the right mindset, much like using an axe to clear a path through a dense forest.
  • Communication: The reference to the tongue highlights the importance of mindful communication, urging individuals to speak with intention and clarity.

In conclusion, this mantra emphasizes the significance of inner strength, discipline, and the transformative power of resilience. It serves as a guiding principle for individuals seeking to navigate the complexities of life with clarity and purpose, reminding them that they possess the tools necessary to overcome any challenge they may face.
