पवस्व देवमादनो विचर्षणिर अप्सां इन्द्राय वरुणाय वायवे |कृधī नो अद्य वरिवः स्वस्तिमद उरुक्षितौ गृणीहि दैव्यम् जनम् ||
pavasva devamādano vicarṣaṇir apsā indrāya varuṇāya vāyave | kṛdhī no adya varivaḥ svastimad urukṣitau gṛṇīhi daivyaṁ janam ||
  • pavasva: This word translates to "may you purify" or "may you cleanse." It signifies the act of purification, which is essential in many cultures and religions for spiritual and physical well-being.
  • devamādano: This can be interpreted as "the divine being" or "the godly essence." It emphasizes the connection to the divine and the importance of seeking blessings from higher powers.
  • vicarṣaṇir: This term refers to "the one who moves swiftly" or "the one who travels." It can symbolize the dynamic nature of life and the need for adaptability in various situations.
  • apsā: This word means "from the waters" or "of the waters." Water is often associated with life, nourishment, and purification, highlighting its significance in our existence.
  • indrāya: This refers to "to Indra," the king of gods in Vedic tradition, representing strength, power, and leadership. Indra is often invoked for protection and guidance.
  • varuṇāya: This means "to Varuna," the god of cosmic order and water. Varuna symbolizes the moral and ethical aspects of life, reminding us of the importance of righteousness.
  • vāyave: This translates to "to Vayu," the god of wind. Vayu represents movement, breath, and life force, emphasizing the importance of vitality and energy in our lives.
  • kṛdhī: This word means "may you create" or "may you bring forth." It signifies the act of manifestation and the power of intention in shaping our reality.
  • no: This translates to "for us," indicating a collective aspect, emphasizing community and shared experiences.
  • adya: This means "today," highlighting the immediacy and relevance of the mantra in the present moment.
  • varivaḥ: This can be interpreted as "the best" or "the most excellent," suggesting a pursuit of excellence in all endeavors.
  • svastimad: This word means "auspicious" or "well-being." It reflects the desire for health, prosperity, and positive outcomes in life.
  • urukṣitau: This translates to "in the vastness" or "in the great expanse," symbolizing the infinite possibilities and opportunities available to us.
  • gṛṇīhi: This means "may you sing" or "may you praise," indicating the importance of expressing gratitude and reverence.
  • daivyaṁ: This translates to "divine" or "heavenly," emphasizing the connection to the sacred and the importance of spirituality in our lives.
  • janam: This means "people" or "community," highlighting the significance of social bonds and collective well-being.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a profound invocation for purification, divine blessings, and the pursuit of well-being in the community. It emphasizes the interconnectedness of various elements of life—water, wind, and divine beings—while encouraging individuals to seek excellence and express gratitude. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in rituals, personal reflections, or community gatherings, serving as a reminder of the importance of spiritual connection, collective harmony, and the pursuit of a fulfilling life. By embodying the principles of this mantra, individuals can foster a sense of purpose and positivity in their daily lives.
