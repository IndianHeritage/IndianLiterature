मन्त्र: उता नो देव्य अदितिर उरुष्यतां नासत्याः |उरुष्यन्तु मरुतो वृद्धशवसः ||
uta no devy aditir uruṣyatāṁ nāsatyā | uruṣyantu maruto vṛddhaśavasaḥ ||
  • Mantra: "uta no devy aditir uruṣyatāṁ nāsatyā | uruṣyantu maruto vṛddhaśavasaḥ ||"
  • Translation: "May the divine Aditi, the mother of the gods, grant us strength; may the Maruts, the storm deities, come to us with their powerful energy."
  • Word-by-Word Explanation:
    • uta: a word of invocation, often translated as "may" or "let us."
    • no: "our," indicating possession or connection.
    • devy: "divine," referring to the celestial or godly nature.
    • aditir: "Aditi," the mother of the gods, symbolizing boundless space and the nurturing aspect of the universe.
    • uruṣyatāṁ: "grant us strength," a plea for empowerment and support.
    • nāsatyā: "Nāsatyas," referring to the twin deities associated with truth and healing.
    • uruṣyantu: "may they come," a call for the presence of divine forces.
    • maruto: "Maruts," the storm gods, representing power, energy, and the forces of nature.
    • vṛddhaśavasaḥ: "with their powerful energy," emphasizing the strength and vitality of the Maruts.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In times of personal struggle, individuals often seek strength and support from higher powers, similar to how the mantra invokes Aditi for empowerment.
    • During natural disasters, communities may come together, invoking the Maruts for protection and strength, reflecting the collective human desire for safety and resilience.
    • In sports, athletes often seek motivation and strength, akin to calling upon divine forces to enhance their performance.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra highlights the significance of seeking divine support and strength in our lives.
    • It emphasizes the interconnectedness of humanity with the divine and the natural forces around us.
    • In real-life scenarios, it serves as a reminder to seek empowerment from both within and from the universe, fostering a sense of community and resilience.
