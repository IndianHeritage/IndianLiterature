प्रवाच्यं शश्वधां वीर्यं तद इन्द्रस्य कर्म यद अहिं विवृश्चत् |विज्ञ वज्रेण परिसदो जग्हानायन्न आपो 'यनम इच्छमानाः ||
pravācyaṁ śaśvadhā vīrya1ṁ tad indrasya karma yad ahiṁ vivṛścat | vi vajreṇa pariṣado jaghānāyann āpo 'yanam icchamānāḥ ||
  • pravācyaṁ: This term can be translated as "proclaiming" or "speaking forth." It signifies the act of expressing or declaring something important.
  • śaśvadhā: This word means "everlasting" or "eternal." It emphasizes the idea of permanence and continuity.
  • vīryaṁ: This refers to "strength" or "valor." It embodies the qualities of courage, power, and heroism.
  • tad: This means "that," indicating a reference to something previously mentioned or understood.
  • indrasya: This term relates to "Indra," the king of the gods in Vedic literature, often associated with rain, storms, and strength.
  • karma: This translates to "action" or "deed." It signifies the importance of actions and their consequences.
  • yad: This means "which" or "that which," linking the previous concepts to what follows.
  • ahiṁ: This word means "non-violence" or "harmlessness," highlighting the value of peace and compassion.
  • vivṛścat: This can be interpreted as "revealing" or "unfolding," suggesting the act of bringing something to light.
  • vi vajreṇa: This phrase translates to "with the thunderbolt," symbolizing power and the ability to overcome obstacles.
  • pariṣado: This means "the assembly" or "the gathering," indicating a collective effort or community.
  • jaghānāyann: This term can be understood as "striking down" or "defeating," suggesting a decisive action against negativity or challenges.
  • āpo: This word means "waters," often symbolizing life, nourishment, and purification.
  • 'yanam: This refers to "movement" or "flow," indicating the dynamic nature of existence.
  • icchamānāḥ: This translates to "desiring" or "wishing," reflecting the aspirations and intentions of individuals or the community.

The mantra speaks to the themes of strength, action, and the importance of community in overcoming challenges. It emphasizes the need for a collective effort to achieve goals while maintaining a commitment to non-violence and compassion. The reference to Indra suggests that divine support and strength are available to those who act with righteousness.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • In leadership, where a leader must proclaim a vision (pravācyaṁ) that is everlasting (śaśvadhā) and rooted in strength (vīryaṁ) to inspire their team.
  • In community initiatives, where collective action (pariṣado) is necessary to address social issues, emphasizing the importance of working together to achieve common goals.
  • In personal development, where individuals strive to act (karma) with integrity and compassion (ahiṁ), revealing their true potential (vivṛścat) while overcoming obstacles (vi vajreṇa).

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the power of collective action, the importance of strength and integrity in our deeds, and the need for compassion in our interactions. It encourages individuals and communities to strive for greatness while remaining grounded in values that promote peace and harmony.
