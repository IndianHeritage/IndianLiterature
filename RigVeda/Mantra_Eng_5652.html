अमूरा विश्वा वृषणाव इमा वां न यासु चित्रं ददृशे न यक्षम् |द्रुहः साचन्ते अनृताः जनानां न वां निण्याण्य अचिते अभूवन ||
amūrā viśvā vṛṣaṇāv imā vāṁ na yāsu citraṁ dadṛśe na yakṣam | druhaḥ sacante anṛtā janānāṁ na vāṁ niṇyāny acite abhūvan ||
  • amūrā: This word translates to "immortal" or "eternal." It signifies the everlasting nature of certain truths or principles in life.
  • viśvā: Meaning "all" or "universal," it emphasizes inclusivity and the interconnectedness of all beings and elements in the universe.
  • vṛṣaṇāv: This term can be interpreted as "the powerful" or "the strong," often referring to forces or entities that possess great strength or influence.
  • imā: Translates to "these," indicating a focus on the present or specific aspects of life.
  • vāṁ: Meaning "you," it addresses the audience directly, creating a personal connection.
  • na yāsu: This phrase means "not in which," suggesting a negation or absence of certain qualities or conditions.
  • citraṁ: Translates to "wonderful" or "extraordinary," highlighting the beauty and complexity of life.
  • dadṛśe: Meaning "to see" or "to perceive," it emphasizes the importance of awareness and observation in understanding life.
  • na yakṣam: This means "not to be deceived," indicating the need for discernment and wisdom in navigating life's challenges.
  • druhaḥ: Translates to "deceit" or "treachery," pointing to the negative aspects of human nature that can lead to conflict and misunderstanding.
  • sacante: Meaning "to be present" or "to exist," it emphasizes the importance of being grounded and aware in the moment.
  • anṛtā: This word means "falsehood" or "untruth," highlighting the significance of truthfulness in our interactions.
  • janānāṁ: Translates to "of people," indicating a focus on humanity and social relationships.
  • na vāṁ: Meaning "not you," it creates a distinction between the speaker and the audience, emphasizing individual responsibility.
  • niṇyāny: This term means "to be led" or "to be guided," suggesting the importance of guidance in life.
  • acite: Translates to "in the unworthy" or "in the unclean," indicating a caution against negative influences.
  • abhūvan: Meaning "they have not become," it suggests a potential for growth and transformation that has not yet been realized.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the importance of truth, awareness, and the interconnectedness of all beings. It serves as a reminder to remain vigilant against deceit and to seek wisdom in our perceptions of the world. In real life, this can be applied in various scenarios, such as in personal relationships where honesty and clear communication are vital, or in professional settings where integrity and ethical behavior are crucial for success. By embodying the principles outlined in this mantra, individuals can foster a more harmonious and truthful existence, contributing positively to their communities and the world at large.
