परिक्षिता पितरा पूर्वजावरि ऋतस्य योना क्षयतः समोकसा | dyावापृथिवी वरुणाय सवरते घृतवत् पयो महिषाया पिन्वतः ||
parikṣitā pitarā pūrvajāvarī ṛtasya yonā kṣayataḥ samokasā | dyāvāpṛthivī varuṇāya savrate ghṛtavat payo mahiṣāya pinvataḥ ||
  • Parikṣitā: This term refers to the concept of being examined or scrutinized. In real life, this can relate to how individuals or societies are often judged based on their actions and decisions.
  • Pitarā: This means 'ancestors' or 'forefathers.' It emphasizes the importance of lineage and heritage, reminding us of the values and teachings passed down through generations.
  • Pūrvajāvarī: This term can be interpreted as 'the one who comes before' or 'the ancient.' It signifies the wisdom of the past and how it shapes our present and future.
  • Ṛtasya: This word refers to 'truth' or 'cosmic order.' It highlights the significance of living in harmony with the natural laws and ethical principles that govern existence.
  • Yonā: Meaning 'source' or 'origin,' this term points to the fundamental essence from which all things arise, akin to the idea of a nurturing mother or the earth itself.
  • Kṣayataḥ: This translates to 'diminishing' or 'decreasing.' It can be related to the transient nature of life and the inevitability of change.
  • Samokasā: This term can be understood as 'equally' or 'in balance.' It suggests the importance of maintaining equilibrium in life, whether in relationships, work, or personal well-being.
  • Dyāvāpṛthivī: This phrase refers to 'heaven and earth,' symbolizing the duality of existence and the interconnectedness of all things.
  • Varuṇāya: Varuna is the deity associated with water and cosmic order. This term signifies the divine aspect of nature and the importance of respecting natural elements.
  • Savrate: This means 'with devotion' or 'with reverence.' It emphasizes the need for sincerity and dedication in our actions and relationships.
  • Ghṛtavat: This translates to 'like ghee' or 'nourishing.' It symbolizes the richness and sustenance that comes from pure intentions and actions.
  • Payo: Meaning 'milk,' this term represents nourishment and the nurturing aspects of life, akin to the care provided by a mother.
  • Mahiṣāya: This refers to 'the great one' or 'the powerful.' It can symbolize strength and resilience in the face of challenges.
  • Pinvataḥ: This means 'to be filled' or 'to be nourished.' It signifies the importance of abundance and fulfillment in life.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of life, emphasizing the importance of truth, balance, and reverence for nature and our ancestors. It serves as a reminder to live in harmony with the cosmic order and to honor the wisdom of the past while nurturing the present. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as family relationships, environmental stewardship, and personal growth. By embodying the principles outlined in this mantra, individuals can strive for a more meaningful and fulfilling existence, fostering a sense of connection with both their heritage and the world around them.
