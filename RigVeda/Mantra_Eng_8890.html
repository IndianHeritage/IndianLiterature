देवो देवान् परिभूर् ऋतेन वहा नो हव्यं प्रथमश् चिकीत्वान् |धूमकेतुः समिधा भार्जीको मन्द्रो होताऽ नित्यो वाचां यजीयान् ||
devo devān paribhūr ṛtena vahā no havyam prathamaś cikitvān | dhūmaketuḥ samidhā bhāṛjīko mandro hotā nityo vācā yajīyān ||
  • devo devān paribhūr ṛtena vahā no havyam prathamaś cikitvān
    • Devo devān: This phrase translates to "the gods of the gods." It signifies the supreme divinity or the ultimate source of all divine beings.
    • Paribhūr: This means "encompassing" or "pervading." It suggests that this supreme divinity is present everywhere and in everything.
    • Ṛtena: This word refers to "truth" or "cosmic order." It emphasizes the importance of living in harmony with the universal laws.
    • Vahā: This means "to carry" or "to bring forth." It indicates the act of delivering or manifesting something into reality.
    • No havyam: This translates to "our offerings." It signifies the importance of making offerings to the divine, which can be interpreted as acts of devotion or service.
    • Prathamaś cikitvān: This means "the first to understand." It suggests that the divine is the source of all knowledge and wisdom.
  • dhūmaketuḥ samidhā bhāṛjīko mandro hotā nityo vācā yajīyān
    • Dhūmaketuḥ: This translates to "the one with smoke as a banner." It can symbolize the presence of the divine in the form of offerings that create smoke, such as in fire rituals.
    • Samidhā: This means "kindled" or "ignited." It refers to the act of lighting a fire, which is often a central element in rituals and offerings.
    • Bhāṛjīko: This term can be interpreted as "the one who is radiant" or "brilliant." It signifies the divine's illuminating presence.
    • Mandro: This means "the one who is invoked." It refers to the divine being called upon during rituals.
    • Hotā: This translates to "the priest" or "the one who offers." It emphasizes the role of the priest in performing rituals and making offerings to the divine.
    • Nityo: This means "eternal." It signifies the everlasting nature of the divine and its presence in all aspects of life.
    • Vācā: This translates to "with speech" or "through words." It highlights the importance of verbal expressions in rituals and prayers.
    • Yajīyān: This means "the one who is worshipped." It signifies the divine as the object of devotion and reverence.

Conclusion:

The mantra emphasizes the significance of recognizing the supreme divine presence that pervades all aspects of life. It highlights the importance of truth, knowledge, and the act of making offerings as a means of connecting with the divine. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied through various forms of worship, rituals, and acts of service that honor the divine. By living in accordance with cosmic order and making conscious offerings, individuals can cultivate a deeper connection with the divine and foster a sense of harmony in their lives. This mantra serves as a reminder of the eternal nature of the divine and the importance of devotion in our daily lives.
