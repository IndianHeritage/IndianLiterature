ईजे यज्ञेभिः शशमे शमीभिर ऋधद्वारायाग्नये ददाशा |एव च न तं यशसाम अजुष्टिर नांहो मर्तं नशते न प्रदृप्तिः ||
īje yajñebhiḥ śaśame śamībhir ṛdhadvārāyāgnaye dadāśa | evā cana taṁ yaśasām ajuṣṭir nāṁho martaṁ naśate na pradṛptiḥ ||
  • īje yajñebhiḥ: This phrase can be translated as "I invoke with sacrifices." In the context of the RigVeda, yajña (sacrifice) is a central theme, representing the act of offering to the divine. In real life, this can be seen in various cultures where rituals and offerings are made to seek blessings or favor from higher powers.
  • śaśame: This word means "to bring peace" or "to calm." It signifies the importance of tranquility and harmony in life. For example, in modern life, practices like meditation and yoga are often employed to achieve inner peace and reduce stress.
  • śamībhir: This term refers to "with the peace-bringing trees." The mention of trees symbolizes nature's role in providing solace and stability. In real life, spending time in nature, such as parks or forests, can have a calming effect on individuals, promoting mental well-being.
  • ṛdhadvārāyāgnaye: This translates to "to the fire that brings prosperity." Fire in Vedic rituals is a symbol of transformation and purification. In contemporary terms, this can relate to the idea of hard work and dedication leading to success, akin to the saying "you reap what you sow."
  • dadāśa: This means "he gave." It emphasizes the act of giving, which is a fundamental aspect of many cultures. In real life, acts of kindness and generosity can foster community and strengthen relationships.
  • evā cana: This phrase can be interpreted as "thus indeed." It reinforces the certainty of the preceding statements, suggesting that the invocation and actions taken will yield results.
  • taṁ yaśasām: This means "that of the famous." It refers to the pursuit of fame or recognition. In today's world, this can relate to the desire for success and acknowledgment in various fields, whether in careers, arts, or social contributions.
  • ajuṣṭir: This translates to "not to be deprived." It signifies the importance of abundance and fulfillment in life. In practical terms, it can relate to ensuring that one's needs are met, whether materially or emotionally.
  • nāṁho martaṁ: This phrase means "the mortal being." It reflects on human existence and the transient nature of life. In real life, it serves as a reminder to cherish moments and relationships, as life is fleeting.
  • naśate: This means "does not perish." It suggests the idea of immortality or the enduring nature of one's actions and legacy. In modern contexts, this can relate to how one's contributions to society can have lasting impacts, even after one's physical presence is gone.
  • na pradṛptiḥ: This translates to "there is no attachment." It emphasizes the importance of detachment from material possessions and desires. In real life, this can be seen in philosophies that advocate for minimalism and the pursuit of spiritual over material wealth.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of invoking peace, prosperity, and recognition through acts of sacrifice and giving. It highlights the significance of maintaining harmony with nature and the importance of one's actions in shaping their legacy. In real life, this mantra serves as a guiding principle for individuals seeking fulfillment, encouraging them to engage in selfless acts, appreciate the transient nature of life, and strive for inner peace amidst the chaos of modern existence.
