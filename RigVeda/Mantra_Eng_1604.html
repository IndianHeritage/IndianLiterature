आयं स होतā यो द्विजन्मा विश्वा दधे वार्याणि श्रवस्यā |मार्तो यो अस्मै सुतुको ददाश ||
ayaṁ sa hotā yo dvijanmā viśvā dadhe vāryāṇi śravasyā | marto yo asmai sutuko dadāśa ||
  • ayaṁ - This word translates to "this" or "this one," indicating a specific focus or subject.
  • sa - Meaning "he" or "that one," it refers to a person or entity being discussed.
  • hotā - This term means "the priest" or "the one who offers," often associated with rituals and sacrifices in Vedic traditions.
  • yo - Translates to "who," introducing a relative clause that describes the subject.
  • dvijanmā - This word means "born twice," referring to a person who has undergone a spiritual rebirth, often associated with the initiation into Vedic studies.
  • viśvā - Meaning "all" or "universal," it signifies inclusiveness and the entirety of existence.
  • dadhe - This translates to "he establishes" or "he creates," indicating an action of bringing something into being.
  • vāryāṇi - Meaning "strengths" or "powers," it refers to the various capabilities or virtues that one can possess.
  • śravasyā - This word means "of fame" or "of glory," indicating the importance of reputation and honor.
  • marto - Translates to "the mortal" or "human," emphasizing the human condition and its limitations.
  • yo - Again, "who," continuing the relative clause.
  • asmai - Meaning "to him" or "to that one," indicating a direction of action or offering.
  • sutuko - This term means "the one who is wise" or "intelligent," referring to a person with knowledge and understanding.
  • dadāśa - Translates to "he gives" or "he bestows," indicating an act of generosity or sharing.

The mantra can be interpreted as a call to the divine or a higher power, recognizing the importance of wisdom, strength, and the establishment of fame through virtuous actions. It emphasizes the role of the priest or spiritual guide in facilitating this connection and the significance of being 'born twice'—once in the physical sense and again through spiritual awakening.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios:

  • Spiritual Growth: Individuals seeking personal development can reflect on the idea of being 'born twice' as a metaphor for transformation through education and self-awareness.
  • Community Leadership: Leaders can embody the qualities of the 'hotā' by guiding their communities with wisdom and establishing a legacy of strength and honor.
  • Personal Relationships: The mantra encourages individuals to give generously to others, fostering relationships built on mutual respect and understanding.

Conclusion: This mantra highlights the significance of wisdom, strength, and the importance of spiritual rebirth in achieving a meaningful life. It serves as a reminder that through knowledge and virtuous actions, one can establish a legacy that resonates with the values of honor and respect in both personal and communal contexts.
