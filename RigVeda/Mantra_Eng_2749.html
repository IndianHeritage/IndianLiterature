यज्ञो हि त इन्द्र वर्धनो भूदुत प्रियः सुतसोमो मियेदहः |यज्ञेन यज्ञम अव यज्ञियः सन् यज्ञास ते वज्रम अहिहत्य आवत ||
yajño hi ta indra vardhano bhūd uta priyaḥ sutasomo miyedhaḥ | yajñena yajñam ava yajñiyaḥ san yajñas te vajram ahihatya āvat ||
  • yajño hi ta indra vardhano bhūd uta priyaḥ sutasomo miyedhaḥ
    • yajño: This term refers to a sacrificial ritual or offering, which is central to Vedic traditions. It symbolizes the act of giving and sharing with the divine.
    • hi: This word means "indeed" or "certainly," emphasizing the importance of the preceding term.
    • ta: This means "that," referring to the offerings made in the yajña.
    • indra: Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, representing strength, power, and the forces of nature.
    • vardhano: This means "nourishing" or "increasing," indicating that the offerings enhance the divine connection.
    • bhūd: This translates to "may it be" or "let it be," expressing a wish or intention.
    • uta: This means "and" or "also," linking the ideas together.
    • priyaḥ: This means "beloved" or "dear," suggesting that the offerings are cherished by the divine.
    • sutasomo: This refers to the "soma," a sacred drink in Vedic rituals, symbolizing vitality and divine inspiration.
    • miyedhaḥ: This means "may it be attained" or "may it be realized," indicating a desire for the blessings of the offerings.
  • yajñena yajñam ava yajñiyaḥ san yajñas te vajram ahihatya āvat
    • yajñena: This means "by the sacrifice" or "through the offering," reinforcing the idea that the act of giving is crucial.
    • yajñam: This refers to the "sacrifice" itself, emphasizing its significance in the spiritual realm.
    • ava: This means "down" or "from," indicating a flow or transfer of energy or blessings.
    • yajñiyaḥ: This term refers to those who are worthy of the offerings, suggesting that the divine is receptive to sincere devotion.
    • san: This means "being" or "existing," indicating the presence of the divine in the act of sacrifice.
    • yajñas: This is the plural form of yajña, referring to multiple sacrifices or offerings.
    • te: This means "your," indicating a personal connection to the divine.
    • vajram: This translates to "thunderbolt" or "weapon," symbolizing strength and protection.
    • ahihatya: This means "without harm" or "unharmed," suggesting that the blessings are protective.
    • āvat: This means "may it come" or "may it arrive," expressing a desire for the blessings to manifest.

Conclusion:

The mantra emphasizes the significance of yajña (sacrifice) as a means to connect with the divine, particularly through offerings that are cherished by Indra, the king of the gods. It highlights the idea that through sincere acts of giving, one can attain blessings, nourishment, and protection. In real life, this can be applied in various ways, such as through charitable acts, community service, or any form of selfless giving that fosters a sense of connection and gratitude. The mantra serves as a reminder that our actions, when rooted in love and devotion, can create a positive impact not only in our lives but also in the lives of others, ultimately leading to a harmonious existence.
