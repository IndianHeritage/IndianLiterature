जनिताश्वानां जनित गवाम् असि पिबा सोमं मदाया कं शतक्रतो |यं ते भागम् अधारयन विश्वाः सेहानः पृतना उरु ज्रयः सम अप्सुजिन मरुत्वां इन्द्र सत्पते ||
janitāśvānāṁ janitā gavām asi pibā somam madāya kaṁ śatakrato | yaṁ te bhāgam adhārayan viśvāḥ sehānaḥ pṛtanā uru jrayaḥ sam apsujin marutvām̐ indra satpate ||
  • janitāśvānāṁ: This term refers to the "progenitor of horses." In ancient Vedic culture, horses were symbols of power and wealth. They were essential for transportation and warfare.
  • janitā gavām: This translates to "progenitor of cows." Cows were considered sacred and were a source of sustenance, wealth, and agricultural productivity.
  • asi pibā somam: "You are drinking soma." Soma was a ritual drink believed to confer immortality and divine inspiration. It symbolizes the pursuit of higher consciousness and spiritual awakening.
  • madāya: This means "for intoxication" or "for joy." It indicates the blissful state achieved through spiritual practices or divine experiences.
  • kaṁ śatakrato: "O hundred-fold powerful one." This refers to Indra, the king of gods, known for his strength and ability to overcome obstacles.
  • yaṁ te bhāgam adhārayan: "Whom the worlds have supported." This suggests that the divine or the powerful are supported by the collective energies of the universe.
  • viśvāḥ sehānaḥ: "All beings together." This emphasizes the interconnectedness of all life forms and the importance of unity.
  • pṛtanā uru jrayaḥ: "The great strength of the earth." This can be interpreted as the foundational power of nature and the earth's resources that sustain life.
  • sam apsujin: "With the waters." This refers to the life-giving properties of water, essential for survival and growth.
  • marutvām̐ indra satpate: "O Indra, lord of the Maruts." The Maruts are storm deities, representing the forces of nature. Indra's connection to them signifies his control over natural elements.

Conclusion: This mantra encapsulates the essence of life, prosperity, and the divine connection between nature and humanity. It highlights the importance of horses and cows in Vedic society, symbolizing strength and sustenance. The invocation of Indra emphasizes the need for divine support in overcoming challenges. In real life, this mantra can be applied to the pursuit of harmony with nature, the importance of community support, and the quest for spiritual enlightenment. It serves as a reminder to seek balance in our lives, respect the resources provided by the earth, and recognize the interconnectedness of all beings.
