कुत्साय शुष्णम अशुषं नि बरहीः प्रपित्वे अहनः कुयवं सहस्राः |सद्यो दस्यून प्र मृण कुत्स्येन प्र सूरश चक्रं वृहताद अभीक ||
kutsāya śuṣṇam aśuṣaṁ ni barhīḥ prapitve ahnaḥ kuyavaṁ sahasrā | sadyo dasyūn pra mṛṇa kutsyena pra sūraś cakraṁ vṛhatād abhīke ||
  • kutsāya: This term can be interpreted as "to the one who is cursed" or "to the one who is in distress." It signifies a state of suffering or hardship.
  • śuṣṇam: This word means "dry" or "thirsty," often symbolizing a lack of nourishment or spiritual fulfillment.
  • aśuṣaṁ: This can be understood as "not dry" or "full of life," indicating a contrast to the previous term, suggesting the presence of vitality and abundance.
  • ni barhīḥ: This phrase refers to "the sacred grass" or "the offerings," which are essential in Vedic rituals, symbolizing purity and connection to the divine.
  • prapitve: This term means "to the ancestors" or "to the forefathers," highlighting the importance of lineage and respect for those who came before us.
  • ahnaḥ: This word translates to "of the day" or "of the sun," representing light, knowledge, and the passage of time.
  • kuyavaṁ: This can be interpreted as "the deceitful" or "the wicked," indicating the presence of negativity or malevolence in the world.
  • sahasrā: This term means "thousands," often used to denote abundance or a multitude of blessings.
  • sadyo: This word translates to "immediately" or "suddenly," suggesting a prompt response or action.
  • dasyūn: This term refers to "the robbers" or "the enemies," symbolizing obstacles or challenges that one may face.
  • pra mṛṇa: This phrase means "to kill" or "to overcome," indicating the need to confront and eliminate negativity.
  • kutsyena: This can be understood as "by the curse" or "through the distress," suggesting that challenges may arise from negative influences.
  • pra sūraś: This term means "to the brave" or "to the valiant," emphasizing the importance of courage in overcoming difficulties.
  • cakraṁ: This word translates to "wheel" or "circle," often symbolizing the cycle of life, time, and the universe.
  • vṛhatād: This term means "great" or "vast," indicating the expansive nature of existence and the divine.
  • abhīke: This can be interpreted as "to the one who is invoked" or "to the one who is called upon," suggesting the importance of prayer and connection to the divine.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the struggle between light and darkness, abundance and scarcity, and the importance of courage and connection to the divine. In real life, it serves as a reminder to confront challenges with bravery and to seek spiritual nourishment amidst adversity. It emphasizes the significance of honoring our ancestors and the need to overcome negativity in our lives. By invoking the divine and recognizing the cycles of existence, we can find strength and resilience in our daily struggles.
