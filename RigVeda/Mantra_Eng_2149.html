अध्वर्यवो यः स्व अशनं जग्हान यः शुष्णम अशुषं यो व्यंसम् |यः पिप्रुं नमुचिं यो रुधिक्रां तस्मा इन्द्रायांधसो जुहोत ||
adhvaryavo yaḥ sv aśnaṁ jaghāna yaḥ śuṣṇam aśuṣaṁ yo vyaṁsam | yaḥ pipruṁ namuciṁ yo rudhikrāṁ tasmā indrāyāndhaso juhota ||
  • adhvaryavo: This term refers to the priests who perform the rituals, particularly the ones who are responsible for the offerings and sacrifices in Vedic ceremonies. In real life, this can be likened to individuals who take on leadership roles in community services or organizations, guiding others in their efforts.
  • yaḥ sv aśnaṁ jaghāna: This phrase translates to "who has slain the one who is the eater of the self." It symbolizes overcoming one's own desires or ego. In modern life, this can be seen in individuals who practice self-discipline, such as those who resist temptations for a greater goal, like athletes training for a competition.
  • yaḥ śuṣṇam aśuṣaṁ: This means "who has conquered the one who is dry and without life." It represents the triumph over lifelessness or stagnation. In a contemporary context, this can relate to people who strive to bring innovation and creativity into their work or personal lives, revitalizing stagnant situations.
  • yo vyaṁsam: This translates to "who has brought forth the one who is hidden." It signifies the revelation of hidden truths or potentials. In real life, this can be compared to educators or mentors who help individuals discover their hidden talents and capabilities.
  • yaḥ pipruṁ namuciṁ: This phrase means "who has subdued the one who is the source of desire." It reflects the idea of controlling one's cravings and impulses. In everyday life, this can be seen in individuals practicing mindfulness or meditation to manage their desires and maintain focus on their goals.
  • yo rudhikrāṁ: This translates to "who has bound the one who is fierce." It symbolizes the ability to control aggressive or destructive forces. In a real-world scenario, this can be likened to conflict resolution strategies where individuals or leaders manage disputes and foster peace.
  • tasmā indrāyāndhaso juhota: This means "therefore, let us offer to Indra, the one who is the lord of the blind." Indra represents strength and power, and this line emphasizes the importance of seeking guidance and strength from higher powers or principles. In practical terms, this can be seen in how people turn to mentors, spiritual leaders, or even philosophical teachings for guidance in challenging times.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of overcoming personal and external challenges through self-discipline, revelation of hidden truths, and seeking guidance from higher powers. Its significance lies in its applicability to various aspects of life, such as personal growth, leadership, conflict resolution, and spiritual guidance. By embodying the principles outlined in this mantra, individuals can navigate their lives with greater purpose and strength, ultimately leading to a more fulfilling existence.
