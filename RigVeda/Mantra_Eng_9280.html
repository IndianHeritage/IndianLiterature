दिवा यम् अग्निम पृथिवी जनिष्टाम आपस् तवष्टा भृगवो यं सहोभिः |ईळेन्यम प्रथमम् मातरिश्वा देवास तातक्षुर मनवे यजत्रम् ||
dyāvā yam agnim pṛthivī janiṣṭām āpas tvaṣṭā bhṛgavo yaṁ sahobhiḥ | īḻenyam prathamam mātariśvā devās tatakṣur manave yajatram ||
  • dyāvā yam agnim: This phrase translates to "the sky (dyāvā) that has produced fire (agnim)." In a real-life context, fire symbolizes energy, transformation, and purification. For example, in many cultures, fire is used in rituals to signify the transformation of offerings into spiritual energy.
  • pṛthivī janiṣṭām: Here, "pṛthivī" refers to the earth, and "janiṣṭām" means "that which has been born." This can be interpreted as the earth giving birth to life. In our daily lives, this can be seen in agriculture, where the earth nurtures plants and animals, providing sustenance for humanity.
  • āpas tvaṣṭā: "āpas" means "waters," and "tvaṣṭā" refers to the divine craftsman or creator. This suggests the importance of water in creation and sustenance. For instance, water is essential for life, agriculture, and industry, highlighting its role as a fundamental element in our existence.
  • bhṛgavo yaṁ sahobhiḥ: "bhṛgavo" refers to the sages or seers, and "sahobhiḥ" means "together with." This implies the collective wisdom of sages guiding humanity. In modern life, we often seek guidance from wise individuals or mentors who help us navigate challenges.
  • īḻenyam prathamam: This translates to "the first offering." It signifies the importance of prioritizing our offerings to the divine or to the greater good. In practice, this can be seen in charitable acts or community service, where individuals contribute to society as a primary duty.
  • mātariśvā devās: "mātariśvā" refers to the wind or the atmosphere, and "devās" means "gods." This suggests the divine presence in nature. In real life, we can observe how the wind influences weather patterns, which in turn affects agriculture and daily life.
  • tatakṣur manave yajatram: "tatakṣur" means "that which is seen," "manave" refers to humanity, and "yajatram" means "worship." This indicates that what we perceive in the world around us should inspire our worship and gratitude. In everyday life, this can manifest as appreciation for nature and the environment, leading to sustainable practices.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the interconnectedness of the elements—sky, earth, water, and wind—and their roles in creation and sustenance. It highlights the importance of wisdom, offerings, and gratitude in our lives. In a real-life scenario, this mantra can inspire individuals to respect nature, seek guidance from wise mentors, and contribute positively to society, fostering a sense of community and responsibility towards the environment.
