पाकः पृच्छामि मनसाविजानन देवानाम एनाऽनिहितापदानी |वत्से बष्कयेऽधि सप्त तन्तून् विटत्निरे कवयः ओतवाऽउ ||
pākaḥ pṛcchāmi manasāvijānan devānām enā nihitā padāni | vatse baṣkaye 'dhi sapta tantūn vi tatnire kavaya otavā u ||
  • pākaḥ: This word can be interpreted as "the one who cooks" or "the one who prepares." In a broader sense, it signifies the act of transformation or creation, much like how a chef transforms raw ingredients into a delicious meal.
  • pṛcchāmi: This means "I ask" or "I inquire." It reflects the human quest for knowledge and understanding, similar to how students seek answers from teachers or mentors in their pursuit of education.
  • manasāvijānan: This term can be broken down into "manasa" (mind) and "vijānan" (to know or understand). It emphasizes the importance of mental clarity and awareness, akin to how mindfulness practices help individuals gain insight into their thoughts and emotions.
  • devānām: This translates to "of the gods." It signifies a connection to the divine or higher powers, much like how people turn to spirituality or religion for guidance and support in challenging times.
  • enā: This word means "these" or "this." It indicates a focus on specific aspects or elements, similar to how one might concentrate on particular goals or objectives in life.
  • nihitā padāni: This phrase means "the established paths" or "the fixed ways." It suggests the importance of following a righteous path, much like how ethical principles guide individuals in making decisions in their personal and professional lives.
  • vatse: This word means "calf" or "young one." It symbolizes innocence and potential, similar to how nurturing a child can lead to their growth and development into a responsible adult.
  • baṣkaye: This can be interpreted as "to nourish" or "to support." It reflects the idea of providing sustenance, much like how communities come together to support one another during difficult times.
  • 'dhi: This word means "to hold" or "to possess." It signifies the importance of retaining knowledge and wisdom, akin to how individuals strive to learn from their experiences and apply that knowledge in the future.
  • sapta tantūn: This phrase translates to "seven threads." The number seven often symbolizes completeness or wholeness, much like how various aspects of life—such as health, relationships, and career—are interconnected and contribute to overall well-being.
  • vi tatnire: This means "they have spread" or "they have extended." It reflects the idea of growth and expansion, similar to how ideas and innovations can spread through society, leading to progress and development.
  • kavaya: This word means "poets" or "wise ones." It signifies the role of creativity and wisdom in shaping culture and society, much like how artists and thinkers inspire change through their work.
  • otavā: This can be interpreted as "to invoke" or "to call upon." It reflects the act of seeking assistance or guidance, similar to how individuals pray or meditate for support in their lives.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of seeking knowledge, understanding the divine, and following established paths in life. It highlights the interconnectedness of various aspects of existence, from nurturing the young to invoking wisdom and creativity. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can serve as a reminder to individuals to pursue their goals with clarity and purpose, to support one another in their journeys, and to remain open to the guidance of higher powers or wisdom. By embodying these principles, individuals can lead more fulfilling and meaningful lives.
