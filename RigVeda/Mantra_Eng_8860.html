शं नो देवीर अभिष्टय आपो भवन्तु पीतये |शं योर अभि श्रवन्तु नः ||
śaṁ no devīr abhiṣṭaya āpo bhavantu pītaye | śaṁ yor abhi sravantu naḥ ||
  • śaṁ no devīr abhiṣṭaya: This phrase can be translated as "May the divine energies (deities) bestow peace upon us." Here, "śaṁ" signifies peace or tranquility, while "devīr" refers to the divine or goddesses. "Abhiṣṭaya" implies the act of bestowing or granting.
  • āpo bhavantu pītaye: This part translates to "May the waters (āpo) be nourishing (pītaye) for us." In this context, "āpo" refers to water, which is essential for life, and "pītaye" suggests nourishment or sustenance.
  • śaṁ yor abhi sravantu naḥ: This can be interpreted as "May peace (śaṁ) flow (sravantu) towards us." "Yor" indicates a connection or relationship, while "naḥ" means "to us." This line emphasizes the desire for peace to continuously flow into our lives.

  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In a community setting, this mantra can be recited during gatherings or rituals to invoke peace and harmony among the participants, fostering a sense of unity.
    • In personal life, individuals may chant this mantra during meditation or yoga practices to cultivate inner peace and a sense of calmness, especially during stressful times.
    • In environmental contexts, the reference to water can remind us of the importance of preserving our natural resources, as clean water is vital for health and well-being.

  • Conclusion: The mantra "śaṁ no devīr abhiṣṭaya āpo bhavantu pītaye | śaṁ yor abhi sravantu naḥ" emphasizes the significance of peace and nourishment in our lives. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of divine energies, nature, and human existence. By invoking peace and the sustenance provided by water, we can apply this mantra in various aspects of life, from personal well-being to community harmony and environmental stewardship. Its significance lies in the universal desire for tranquility and the essential resources that support life, making it relevant in both spiritual and practical contexts.
