सद्यश्चिद्यस्य चर्कृतिः परिद्यां देवो नैति सूर्यः |त्वेषं शवो दधिरे नाम यज्ञियम् मरुतो वृत्रहं शवो ज्येष्ठं वृत्रहं शवः ||
sadyaś cid yasya carkṛtiḥ pari dyāṁ devo naiti sūryaḥ | tveṣaṁ śavo dadhire nāma yajñiyam maruto vṛtrahaṁ śavo jyeṣṭhaṁ vṛtrahaṁ śavaḥ ||
  • Mantra: "sadyaś cid yasya carkṛtiḥ pari dyāṁ devo naiti sūryaḥ | tveṣaṁ śavo dadhire nāma yajñiyam maruto vṛtrahaṁ śavo jyeṣṭhaṁ vṛtrahaṁ śavaḥ ||"
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • sadyaś: immediately, right away
    • cid: indeed, certainly
    • yasya: whose
    • carkṛtiḥ: creation, activity
    • pari: around, in all directions
    • dyāṁ: the sky, heavens
    • devo: the divine, god
    • naiti: does not go, does not leave
    • sūryaḥ: the sun
    • tveṣaṁ: in this, in this form
    • śavo: the dead, the lifeless
    • dadhire: they hold, they support
    • nāma: name, fame
    • yajñiyam: related to sacrifice, offering
    • maruto: the Maruts, storm deities
    • vṛtrahaṁ: slayer of Vritra, the obstructer
    • jyeṣṭhaṁ: the elder, the greatest
    • vṛtrahaṁ: slayer of Vritra (repeated for emphasis)
    • śavaḥ: lifeless, dead
  • Explanation:
    • This mantra speaks of the divine presence that is omnipresent and does not leave the heavens.
    • The reference to the sun indicates a source of light and life that is constant and unwavering.
    • The mention of the Maruts, who are storm deities, signifies the power of nature and the forces that can both create and destroy.
    • The term "vṛtrahaṁ" refers to the slayer of Vritra, symbolizing the overcoming of obstacles and challenges in life.
    • The phrase "nāma yajñiyam" suggests that through offerings and sacrifices, one can attain divine favor and support.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In daily life, when faced with challenges, one can invoke the spirit of this mantra by engaging in acts of kindness and service, akin to making offerings.
    • Just as the sun rises every day, bringing light and warmth, individuals can strive to be a source of positivity and support for others.
    • In moments of despair or feeling 'lifeless', one can remember the strength of the Maruts and seek to overcome their own 'Vritra'—the obstacles that hinder their progress.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of divine presence in our lives and the need for resilience in the face of challenges.
    • It teaches us that through our actions and offerings, we can connect with the divine and draw strength from it.
    • In a broader sense, it encourages us to recognize the interconnectedness of life, nature, and the divine, reminding us that we are never truly alone.
