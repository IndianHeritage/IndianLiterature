ऋतस्य वाऽकेशिनाऽयोग्याभिर् घृतस्नुवाऽ रोहिताधुरी धिष्वा | अथ वः देवान् देव विश्वान् स्वध्वराऽ कृणुहि जातवेदः ||
ṛtasya vā keśinā yogyābhir ghṛtasnuvā rohitā dhuri dhiṣva | athā vaha devān deva viśvān svadhvarā kṛṇuhi jātavedaḥ ||
  • ṛtasya vā: This phrase translates to "of the truth" or "of the cosmic order." In real life, this can be related to the importance of honesty and integrity in our actions and decisions. For example, a business that operates transparently and ethically can build trust with its customers and stakeholders.
  • keśinā: This word means "with hair" or "with the one who has hair." It can symbolize vitality and life force. In a practical sense, this can be seen in the way we nurture our physical and mental well-being, much like how we take care of our hair as a reflection of our health.
  • yogyābhir: This translates to "with those who are fit" or "with those who are capable." It emphasizes the importance of surrounding ourselves with capable and skilled individuals. In real life, this can be applied in team settings, where collaboration with skilled colleagues leads to better outcomes.
  • ghṛtasnuvā: This term refers to "the one who is rich in ghee" or "the one who is nourished." Ghee is often associated with nourishment and purity in Indian culture. This can be related to the idea of nurturing our relationships and communities, ensuring that we contribute positively to the well-being of others.
  • rohitā: This word means "red" or "the one who is red." In a broader sense, it can symbolize energy and passion. In real life, this can be seen in individuals who pursue their passions with vigor, inspiring others to do the same.
  • dhuri dhiṣva: This phrase means "be seated on the chariot" or "take your place." It signifies taking responsibility and being proactive in our lives. For instance, a leader who takes charge during a crisis exemplifies this principle by guiding their team through challenges.
  • athā vaha: This translates to "now bring" or "now carry." It indicates a call to action, urging us to take steps towards our goals. In real life, this can be seen in project management, where teams must take initiative to move forward with their plans.
  • devān: This word means "gods" or "divine beings." It can represent higher ideals or aspirations. In practical terms, this can be related to striving for excellence in our endeavors, much like how athletes aim for greatness in their sports.
  • deva viśvān: This translates to "all gods" or "universal divinity." It emphasizes the interconnectedness of all beings. In real life, this can be reflected in community service, where individuals come together to support one another, recognizing the shared humanity among all.
  • svadhvarā: This term means "with self-generated power" or "with one's own strength." It highlights the importance of self-reliance and personal empowerment. In real life, this can be seen in individuals who overcome adversity through their own determination and resilience.
  • kṛṇuhi: This means "make" or "create." It signifies the act of bringing something into existence. In practical terms, this can be related to innovation and creativity in various fields, such as technology or the arts.
  • jātavedaḥ: This term refers to "the one who knows all births" or "the all-knowing." It symbolizes wisdom and knowledge. In real life, this can be applied to the pursuit of education and lifelong learning, where individuals seek to expand their understanding of the world.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of truth, responsibility, and the pursuit of knowledge. It encourages us to surround ourselves with capable individuals, nurture our relationships, and take proactive steps towards our goals. In a world where integrity and collaboration are vital, this mantra serves as a reminder of the significance of these values in our daily lives. By embodying the principles outlined in this mantra, we can contribute positively to our communities and strive for personal and collective growth.
