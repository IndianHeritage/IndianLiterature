शुनःशेपो ह्य अह्वद गृभीतस् त्रिष्व आदित्यं द्रुपदेषु बद्धः |अवैनं राजा वरुणः ससृज्याद विद्वाम् अडब्धो वि मुमोकतु पाशान् ||
śunaḥśepo hy ahvad gṛbhītas triṣv ādityaṁ drupadeṣu baddhaḥ | avainaṁ rājā varuṇaḥ sasṛjyād vidvām̐ adabdho vi mumoktu pāśān ||
  • śunaḥśepo: This term refers to a character from the Rigveda, often associated with a dog or a dog-like figure. In a broader sense, it symbolizes loyalty and the bond between humans and animals.
  • hy: This is a particle that emphasizes the preceding word, often translated as "indeed" or "certainly."
  • ahvad: This word can be interpreted as "having been called" or "summoned," indicating a sense of invitation or recognition.
  • gṛbhītas: Meaning "having been taken" or "captured," it suggests a sense of possession or acceptance.
  • triṣv: This means "in three," which could refer to three realms or aspects of existence.
  • ādityaṁ: This term refers to the sun or solar deities, symbolizing light, knowledge, and divinity.
  • drupadeṣu: This word relates to the "Drupada," which can signify a place or a lineage, often associated with strength and valor.
  • baddhaḥ: Meaning "bound" or "tied," it indicates a connection or commitment.
  • avainaṁ: This translates to "he (the king) did not abandon him," suggesting protection and support from authority.
  • rājā: This means "king," representing leadership, governance, and responsibility.
  • varuṇaḥ: Referring to Varuna, the god of cosmic order and water, symbolizing moral authority and the upholder of truth.
  • sasṛjyād: This can be interpreted as "having created" or "having brought forth," indicating the act of creation or manifestation.
  • vidvām̐: Meaning "the wise one," it refers to knowledge and wisdom, emphasizing the importance of understanding.
  • adabdho: This term means "unharmed" or "unbound," suggesting freedom and liberation.
  • vi: A particle that can mean "in" or "with," indicating a connection or presence.
  • mumoktu: This translates to "to be liberated" or "to attain freedom," emphasizing the desire for liberation from constraints.
  • pāśān: Meaning "bonds" or "ties," it symbolizes the limitations or attachments that bind individuals.

Conclusion: This mantra reflects themes of loyalty, protection, and the quest for liberation. It emphasizes the importance of wisdom and the role of authority in guiding individuals towards freedom from their bonds. In real life, this can be seen in the relationships we build with our communities, the guidance we seek from leaders, and the pursuit of knowledge that helps us overcome personal challenges. The mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life, the significance of support systems, and the ultimate goal of achieving freedom from limitations, both physical and mental.
