तद् विविद्धि यत् त इन्द्रो जुजोषत् स्तुहि सुष्टुतिं नमसां विवास। उप भूष जरीतर मा रुवण्यः श्रावयाऽवाचं कुविद अङ्ग वेदत्॥
tad viviḍḍhi yat ta indro jujoṣat stuhi suṣṭutiṁ namasā vivāsa | upa bhūṣa jaritar mā ruvaṇyaḥ śrāvayā vācaṁ kuvid aṅga vedat ||
  • tad viviḍḍhi: This phrase can be translated as "that which is known" or "that which is understood." It emphasizes the importance of knowledge and understanding in our lives.
  • yat ta indro jujoṣat: Here, "Indra" refers to the king of the gods, symbolizing strength and power. This part suggests that the knowledge we seek is connected to divine strength and support.
  • stuhi suṣṭutiṁ: This translates to "praise the good praise." It highlights the significance of expressing gratitude and appreciation for the positive aspects of life.
  • namasā vivāsa: "Namasā" means "with reverence" or "with respect." This phrase encourages us to approach knowledge and understanding with humility and respect.
  • upa bhūṣa: This can be interpreted as "to adorn" or "to enhance." It suggests that knowledge and understanding can beautify our lives and surroundings.
  • jaritar mā ruvaṇyaḥ: This phrase can be understood as "do not let the old age or decay affect you." It serves as a reminder to remain youthful in spirit and to embrace life with vigor.
  • śrāvayā vācaṁ kuvid aṅga vedat: This translates to "let the words of wisdom be heard." It emphasizes the importance of sharing knowledge and wisdom with others, fostering a community of learning.

  • Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the essence of seeking knowledge, reverence, and the importance of sharing wisdom. In real life, it can be applied in various scenarios such as education, where students are encouraged to approach learning with respect and humility. It also highlights the significance of community and collaboration in sharing knowledge, which is vital in workplaces and social settings. By embodying the principles of this mantra, individuals can enhance their lives and contribute positively to society.
