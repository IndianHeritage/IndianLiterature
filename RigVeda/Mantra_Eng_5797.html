महेशुल्काय वरुणस्य नु त्विषा ओजो मिमाते ध्रुवम् अस्य यत स्वम् |अजामिम अन्यः श्नथयन्तम् आतिरद दभ्रेभिर अन्यः प्र वृणोति भूयसः ||
mahe śulkāya varuṇasya nu tviṣa ojo mimāte dhruvam asya yat svam | ajāmim anyaḥ śnathayantam ātirad dabhrebhir anyaḥ pra vṛṇoti bhūyasaḥ ||
  • mahe - This word translates to "great" or "mighty." It signifies the vastness and power of the subject being addressed.
  • śulkāya - This term refers to "the one who is worthy of offerings" or "the one who is to be honored." It indicates respect and reverence towards a divine entity.
  • varuṇasya - Varuna is a deity associated with water, cosmic order, and truth. This word emphasizes the connection to the divine and the natural order of the universe.
  • nu - This is an expression of inquiry or emphasis, often translated as "indeed" or "truly."
  • tviṣa - Meaning "light" or "radiance," this word highlights the illuminating qualities of the divine.
  • ojo - This refers to "energy" or "vitality," suggesting the life force that sustains all beings.
  • mimāte - This means "to be invoked" or "to be called upon," indicating a plea for divine assistance or presence.
  • dhruvam - Translated as "certain" or "steadfast," this word conveys a sense of assurance and reliability.
  • asya - This means "of this" or "of him," referring back to the divine entity being addressed.
  • yat - Meaning "that which," it introduces a clause that elaborates on the qualities or actions of the divine.
  • svam - This translates to "own" or "self," indicating possession or inherent qualities.
  • ajāmim - This term can be interpreted as "the one who is unborn" or "eternal," referring to the timeless nature of the divine.
  • anyaḥ - Meaning "another," it suggests the presence of multiple entities or forces at play.
  • śnathayantam - This word means "to be drawn or pulled," indicating a dynamic action or influence.
  • ātirad - This translates to "to surpass" or "to go beyond," suggesting a transcendence of limitations.
  • dabhrebhir - This term refers to "the powerful" or "the strong," emphasizing strength and capability.
  • anyaḥ - Again, this means "another," reinforcing the idea of multiple forces or influences.
  • pra - This prefix means "forth" or "forward," indicating movement or progression.
  • vṛṇoti - This means "to choose" or "to select," suggesting an active decision-making process.
  • bhūyasaḥ - This translates to "the greater" or "the more significant," indicating a hierarchy of importance.

The mantra can be interpreted as a call to the great Varuna, invoking his light and energy, and expressing a desire for his steadfast presence and guidance. It acknowledges the divine's eternal nature and the dynamic forces at play in the universe, suggesting that while there are many influences, the divine is the ultimate source of strength and choice.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • Seeking Guidance: When faced with difficult decisions, individuals can invoke the qualities of Varuna for clarity and strength.
  • Environmental Awareness: Given Varuna's association with water and cosmic order, this mantra can inspire respect for nature and the importance of maintaining ecological balance.
  • Personal Growth: The idea of surpassing limitations can motivate individuals to strive for personal excellence and transcend challenges.

Conclusion: This mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of all beings and the divine presence that guides and supports us. By invoking the qualities of Varuna, individuals can find strength, clarity, and a deeper understanding of their place in the universe.
