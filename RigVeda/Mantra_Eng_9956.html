इन्द्र उक्तेन शवसा परुर दधे बृहस्पते प्रतरितास्य आयुषः |यज्ञो मनुः प्रमतिर नः पिता हि कम आ सर्वतातिम अदितिं वृणीमहे ||
indra ukthena śavasā parur dadhe bṛhaspate pratarītāsy āyuṣaḥ | yajño manuḥ pramatir naḥ pitā hi kam ā sarvatātim aditiṁ vṛṇīmahe ||
  • Indra: Refers to the king of the gods in Vedic tradition, symbolizing strength, power, and the ability to overcome obstacles.
  • Ukthena: Means "by the utterance" or "through the invocation," indicating the importance of words and prayers in achieving one's desires.
  • Śavasā: Translates to "with the dead" or "with the lifeless," which can symbolize overcoming death or challenges.
  • Parur: Means "to bestow" or "to grant," suggesting the act of giving or providing something valuable.
  • Dadhe: Means "to establish" or "to set," indicating the act of creating or forming something solid.
  • Bṛhaspate: Refers to the deity of wisdom and eloquence, representing knowledge and guidance.
  • Pratarītāsy: Means "of the one who is to be protected," indicating the need for safeguarding and support.
  • Āyuṣaḥ: Translates to "of life" or "of longevity," emphasizing the importance of a long and fulfilling life.
  • Yajño: Refers to a sacrifice or offering, highlighting the significance of rituals and devotion in spiritual practices.
  • Manuḥ: Represents the archetypal man or the first human, symbolizing humanity and its responsibilities.
  • Pramatir: Means "intellect" or "understanding," indicating the importance of wisdom in decision-making.
  • Naḥ: Means "our," indicating a collective identity or community.
  • Pitā: Refers to "father," symbolizing guidance, protection, and authority.
  • Hi: Means "indeed," emphasizing certainty or affirmation.
  • Kam: Means "who," indicating a search for understanding or knowledge.
  • Ā: Means "to," indicating direction or purpose.
  • Sarvatātim: Means "all-pervading" or "universal," suggesting a connection to the divine or the cosmos.
  • Aditiṁ: Refers to the mother of the gods, symbolizing the infinite and the nurturing aspect of the universe.
  • Vṛṇīmahe: Means "we choose" or "we invoke," indicating an active participation in the spiritual process.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the importance of invoking divine powers through words and rituals to seek protection, wisdom, and longevity. It highlights the interconnectedness of humanity with the divine and the universe. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied in various contexts such as personal prayers, community rituals, and decision-making processes where wisdom and guidance are sought. By recognizing the significance of our words and actions, we can strive for a life filled with purpose, strength, and harmony.
