विष्वे देवासो अप्तुरः सुतं आगन्त तूर्णयः |उष्रा इव स्वसराणि ||
viśve devāso apturaḥ sutam ā ganta tūrṇayaḥ | usrā iva svasarāṇi ||
  • Translation: "All the gods, having been invoked, come quickly to the offerings, like the swift-moving waters."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • viśve: All, universal
    • devāso: Gods, divine beings
    • apturaḥ: Having been invoked, called upon
    • sutam: Offering, sacrifice
    • ā ganta: Come, approach
    • tūrṇayaḥ: Quickly, swiftly
    • usrā: Like the swift-moving waters
    • svasarāṇi: Relatives, kin, or those who are close
  • Contextual Meaning:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of invoking divine energies and the expectation that they will respond swiftly to our calls.
    • It draws a parallel between the gods and the rapid flow of water, suggesting that divine assistance is readily available if one sincerely seeks it.
    • In real life, this can be seen in various rituals and prayers where individuals seek guidance, support, or blessings from a higher power.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In many cultures, people perform rituals or prayers during significant life events such as weddings, births, or during times of crisis, invoking divine presence for blessings.
    • For instance, during a wedding ceremony, the couple may invoke blessings from deities to ensure a harmonious and prosperous life together, expecting a swift response in the form of love and support.
    • Similarly, in times of distress, individuals may turn to prayer, seeking comfort and guidance, believing that their calls will be answered quickly.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness between humans and the divine, highlighting the importance of faith and the act of invocation.
    • It signifies that when one approaches the divine with sincerity and intention, help and guidance will come swiftly, much like the flowing waters that nourish and sustain life.
    • In a broader sense, it encourages individuals to seek support from their community and loved ones, as they too can be seen as divine helpers in times of need.
