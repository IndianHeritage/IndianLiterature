pra śaṁtamā varuṇaṁ dīdhitī gīr mitram bhagam aditiṁ nūnam aśyāḥ | pṛṣadyoniḥ pañcahotā śṛṇotv atūrtapanthā asuro mayobhuḥ ||
  • pra śaṁtamā: This term can be translated as "the most peaceful" or "the one who brings peace." In real life, peace is essential for harmony in personal relationships, communities, and nations. For example, a mediator in a conflict resolution scenario embodies this quality by promoting understanding and tranquility.
  • varuṇaṁ: Varuna is often associated with the cosmic order and the moral law (Rta). In contemporary life, this can be likened to the rule of law in society, where justice and fairness are upheld. For instance, a judge who ensures that justice is served reflects the essence of Varuna.
  • dīdhitī: This word means "brightness" or "radiance." In a practical sense, it can represent knowledge and enlightenment. For example, educators and mentors who illuminate the minds of their students embody this quality, guiding them towards a brighter future.
  • gīr: This term refers to "speech" or "words." The power of words is evident in everyday life, such as in public speaking or persuasive communication. A politician who inspires change through eloquent speech exemplifies the significance of "gīr."
  • mitram: Meaning "friend" or "ally," this term emphasizes the importance of friendship and support in our lives. Real-life examples include the bonds formed in teamwork or the support systems we create in our personal lives, highlighting the value of companionship.
  • bhagam: This translates to "portion" or "share." In a societal context, it can refer to the equitable distribution of resources. For instance, community programs that ensure food security for all demonstrate the principle of sharing and caring for one another.
  • aditiṁ: Aditi represents the concept of boundlessness or infinity. In real life, this can be seen in the limitless potential of human creativity and innovation. For example, technological advancements that push the boundaries of what is possible reflect the spirit of Aditi.
  • nūnam: This word can be interpreted as "indeed" or "certainly," emphasizing certainty and assurance. In practical terms, it can relate to the confidence we have in our beliefs and decisions, such as a scientist's confidence in their research findings.
  • aśyāḥ: This term refers to "the good" or "the beneficial." In everyday life, this can be seen in acts of kindness and charity, where individuals strive to do good for others, such as volunteering at local shelters.
  • pṛṣadyoniḥ: This can be understood as "the source of all beings." In a real-life context, it can represent the interconnectedness of all life forms, as seen in environmental conservation efforts that aim to protect biodiversity.
  • pañcahotā: This term refers to the "five offerings" or "sacrifices." In modern life, this can be related to the sacrifices individuals make for their families or communities, such as parents working hard to provide for their children.
  • śṛṇotv: Meaning "to listen," this emphasizes the importance of active listening in communication. In real life, effective leaders often practice active listening to understand the needs and concerns of their team members.
  • atūrtapanthā: This term can be interpreted as "the path of the righteous." In contemporary society, this can relate to ethical decision-making and integrity in business practices, where companies prioritize ethical standards over profit.
  • asuro: This word means "the divine" or "the celestial." In real life, it can represent the pursuit of higher ideals and values, such as justice, equality, and compassion, which are often championed by activists and leaders.
  • mayobhuḥ: This term can be understood as "the one who is filled with joy." In everyday life, this can be seen in individuals who spread positivity and happiness, such as community leaders who organize events to uplift spirits and foster joy among people.
