त्रिपाजस्यो वृषभो विश्वरूप उता त्र्युधा पुरुध प्रजावान् |त्र्यनीकः पत्यते माहिनावान् स रेतोधा वृषभः शाश्वतीनाम् ||
tripājasyo vṛṣabho viśvarūpa uta tryudhā purudha prajāvān | tryanīkaḥ patyate māhināvān sa retodhā vṛṣabhaḥ śaśvatīnām ||
  • tripājasyo: This term can be broken down into "tri" meaning three and "pājasya" which refers to the one who is associated with or has three aspects. In a real-life context, this can symbolize the three fundamental aspects of existence: creation, preservation, and destruction, akin to the Hindu trinity of Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva.
  • vṛṣabho: This word translates to "the bull" or "the strong one." In many cultures, the bull represents strength, fertility, and virility. For example, in agriculture, a bull is often seen as a symbol of hard work and productivity, essential for farming communities.
  • viśvarūpa: Meaning "of universal form," this term signifies the all-encompassing nature of the divine. In real life, this can be related to the idea that every individual is part of a larger whole, emphasizing unity in diversity, much like how various cultures contribute to the global tapestry.
  • uta: This word means "and" or "also," indicating a connection or addition of ideas.
  • tryudhā: This translates to "in three ways" or "threefold." This can be seen in various aspects of life, such as the three stages of life (childhood, adulthood, old age) or the three dimensions of time (past, present, future).
  • purudha: This term means "the one who is well-versed" or "knowledgeable." In a practical sense, this can refer to individuals who are wise and knowledgeable, such as teachers or mentors who guide others in their learning journeys.
  • prajāvān: This translates to "the one who has progeny" or "the creator of life." This can be related to the importance of family and community in nurturing future generations, highlighting the role of parents and educators in shaping society.
  • tryanīkaḥ: This term means "the one who is threefold" or "having three aspects." This can be applied to various philosophies that emphasize balance, such as the balance of mind, body, and spirit in holistic health practices.
  • patyate: This translates to "is worshipped" or "is revered." In real life, this can refer to the respect and reverence given to leaders, elders, or deities in various cultures, emphasizing the importance of honoring those who guide and protect us.
  • māhināvān: This term means "the one who is powerful" or "the one who possesses strength." This can be seen in leaders or figures in society who inspire and empower others through their actions and decisions.
  • sa: This word means "he" or "that one," referring to the subject of the mantra.
  • retodhā: This translates to "the one who possesses vitality" or "the one who is full of energy." In real life, this can relate to individuals who are dynamic and full of life, inspiring others through their enthusiasm and passion.
  • vṛṣabhaḥ: Again referring to "the bull," symbolizing strength and fertility, reinforcing the earlier mention of this powerful symbol.
  • śaśvatīnām: This term means "of the eternal" or "of the everlasting." This can be connected to the idea of legacy and the impact one leaves behind, emphasizing the importance of actions that contribute to the greater good and endure through time.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of strength, knowledge, and the interconnectedness of life. It emphasizes the importance of recognizing the threefold nature of existence and the role of individuals in nurturing and guiding future generations. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire us to embrace our responsibilities as creators, protectors, and educators within our communities, fostering unity and strength in diversity. It serves as a reminder of the eternal values that guide us through the various stages of life, encouraging us to live with purpose and integrity.
