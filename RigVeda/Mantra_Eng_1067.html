एव नो अग्नि समिधा वृधानो रेवत पावक श्रवसे वि भाहि |तन नो मित्रो वरुणो मामहन्ताम् अदितिः सिन्धुः पृथिवी उता द्यौः ||
evā no agne samidhā vṛdhāno revat pāvaka śravase vi bhāhi | tan no mitro varuṇo māmahantām aditiḥ sindhuḥ pṛthivī uta dyauḥ ||
  • evā no agne: This phrase translates to "O Agni, may you be with us." Agni is the fire god in Vedic tradition, symbolizing energy, transformation, and purification. In real life, this can be likened to the importance of fire in our daily lives, from cooking to providing warmth and light.
  • samidhā vṛdhāno: This means "kindled and growing." It signifies the nurturing aspect of fire, which not only provides warmth but also fosters growth. For example, in agriculture, fire is used in controlled burns to enrich the soil, promoting healthy crop growth.
  • revat pāvaka: "O pure fire." Here, 'pāvaka' refers to the purifying nature of fire. In many cultures, fire is used in rituals to cleanse and purify spaces and individuals. For instance, in Hindu weddings, fire is a central element, symbolizing purity and the sanctity of the union.
  • śravase vi bhāhi: This translates to "may you shine forth with glory." It emphasizes the idea of radiance and the positive influence of fire. In a metaphorical sense, this can relate to individuals who inspire others through their actions, much like a leader who shines brightly in their community.
  • tan no mitro varuṇo: "May that (fire) be our friend, Varuna." Varuna is the god of cosmic order and water. This line suggests a harmonious relationship between fire and water, representing balance in nature. In real life, this can be seen in ecosystems where fire and water coexist, such as in wetlands that rely on periodic fires to maintain biodiversity.
  • māmahantām aditiḥ: "Aditi, the boundless." Aditi is the mother of gods and represents infinity and the universe. This line signifies the vastness of existence and the interconnectedness of all beings. In our lives, this can be reflected in the idea of community and the importance of nurturing relationships that transcend boundaries.
  • sindhuḥ pṛthivī uta dyauḥ: "the ocean, the earth, and the sky." This phrase encompasses the entirety of the natural world. It reminds us of our place within the universe and the need to respect and protect our environment. For example, the ongoing efforts to combat climate change highlight the significance of caring for our planet, which is represented by the earth, ocean, and sky.

  • Conclusion: This mantra encapsulates the essence of harmony between elements—fire, water, earth, and sky—while emphasizing the importance of purity, growth, and interconnectedness. In real life, it serves as a reminder to cultivate balance in our lives, respect nature, and foster relationships that promote collective well-being. The invocation of Agni and Varuna highlights the significance of seeking guidance from natural forces, encouraging us to live in alignment with the rhythms of the universe.
