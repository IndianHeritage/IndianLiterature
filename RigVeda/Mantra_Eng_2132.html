ṛtur janitrī tasyā apas pari makṣū jāta āviśad yāsu vardhate | tad āhanā abhavat pipyuṣī payo 'ṁśoḥ pīyūṣam prathamaṁ tad ukthyam ||

  • tasyā: This word translates to 'of her', referring to the nurturing aspect of nature or the Earth. It emphasizes the connection we have with our environment and how it sustains us.
  • apas: This means 'water'. Water is essential for life, and its significance can be seen in agriculture, drinking, and sanitation. For instance, rivers and lakes are vital for communities, providing resources for farming and daily living.
  • pari: This word means 'around' or 'surrounding'. It indicates the encompassing nature of the environment and how everything is interconnected. For example, the ecosystem shows how plants, animals, and humans rely on each other.
  • makṣū: This refers to 'bees'. Bees play a crucial role in pollination, which is essential for the growth of many plants. In real life, the decline of bee populations has significant implications for food production and biodiversity.
  • jāta: This means 'born' or 'produced'. It signifies the creation and birth of life, which can be seen in the cycles of nature, such as the birth of animals in spring.
  • āviśad: This translates to 'entered' or 'pervaded'. It suggests the idea of something spreading or becoming present in a space, much like how ideas or cultures spread through societies.
  • yāsu: This means 'in which'. It connects the previous ideas to the context of the mantra, emphasizing the environment and its nurturing qualities.
  • vardhate: This means 'grows' or 'increases'. It reflects the idea of growth in nature, such as plants growing towards the sun or communities flourishing through cooperation.
  • tad: This translates to 'that'. It refers back to the nurturing aspects of nature discussed earlier.
  • āhanā: This means 'sound' or 'call'. It can represent the communication within nature, such as the sounds of animals or the rustling of leaves, which are all part of the natural symphony.
  • abhavat: This means 'became' or 'was'. It indicates a transformation or realization, much like how individuals grow and change through experiences.
  • pipyuṣī: This refers to 'nectar'. Nectar symbolizes nourishment and sweetness in life, akin to the joys and pleasures we experience.
  • payo: This means 'milk'. Milk is often associated with nourishment and sustenance, representing the care and support we receive from our environment and loved ones.
  • 'ṁśoḥ: This translates to 'of the essence'. It signifies the core or fundamental nature of something, reminding us to seek the essence of life and our experiences.
  • pīyūṣam: This means 'the most excellent' or 'the best'. It emphasizes the idea of striving for the best in life, whether in personal growth, relationships, or contributions to society.
  • prathamaṁ: This means 'first' or 'foremost'. It suggests prioritizing what is essential in life, such as health, relationships, and personal values.
  • tad: Again, this refers back to the nurturing aspects of nature.
  • ukthyam: This means 'sacred utterance' or 'mantra'. It signifies the importance of words and intentions in shaping our reality and connecting with the divine.
