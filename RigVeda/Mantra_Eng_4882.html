आ युवानः कवयो यज्ञियासो मरुतो गन्त गृणतो वरस्याम् |अचित्रं चिद् हि जिन्वथाऽ वृद्धन्त इत्त्था नक्षन्तो नरो अंगिरस्वत् ||
ā yuvānaḥ kavayo yajñiyāso maruto ganta gṛṇato varasyām | acitraṁ cid dhi jinvathā vṛdhanta itthā nakṣanto naro aṅgirasvat ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • ā yuvānaḥ: "O youthful ones" - This refers to the young and energetic beings, possibly invoking the spirit of youth and vitality.
    • kavayo: "the wise" - This term signifies those who possess knowledge and wisdom, often referring to sages or poets.
    • yajñiyāso: "who are engaged in sacrifice" - This highlights the importance of rituals and sacrifices in Vedic culture, emphasizing the connection between the material and spiritual worlds.
    • maruto: "the Maruts" - These are storm deities, representing the forces of nature and the power of the wind.
    • ganta: "to go" - This indicates movement or progression, possibly towards a goal or destination.
    • gṛṇato: "singing" - This suggests the act of praising or invoking, often through hymns or chants.
    • varasyām: "in the realm of the divine" - This phrase points to the spiritual or celestial domain where the divine beings reside.
    • acitraṁ: "not diverse" - This implies unity or a singular focus, contrasting with chaos or confusion.
    • cid: "consciousness" - This refers to awareness or the essence of being, often associated with the ultimate reality.
    • hī: "indeed" - A particle that emphasizes the truth of the statement.
    • jinvathā: "you may thrive" - This encourages growth and prosperity, suggesting that one can flourish through wisdom and action.
    • vṛdhanta: "growing" - This indicates development or increase, whether in knowledge, strength, or resources.
    • itthā: "thus" - This word connects the previous ideas to the conclusion or result.
    • nakṣanto: "the shining ones" - This refers to celestial beings or stars, symbolizing guidance and illumination.
    • naro: "humans" - This term emphasizes the human experience and the role of individuals in the cosmic order.
    • aṅgirasvat: "of the Angiras" - This refers to a lineage of sages known for their wisdom and contributions to Vedic literature.
  • Significance and Real-Life Application:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of youth, wisdom, and the pursuit of knowledge through rituals and sacrifices.
    • In real life, it can be applied to the idea of community and collaboration, where individuals come together to achieve common goals, much like the Maruts who work together in nature.
    • The call for growth and thriving can be seen in personal development, where individuals are encouraged to seek knowledge and wisdom to improve their lives and the lives of those around them.
    • In a broader sense, this mantra can inspire collective efforts in society, such as environmental initiatives or community service, where the focus is on unity and progress.
