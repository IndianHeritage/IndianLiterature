ऋतेन ऋतं धरुणं धारण्त यज्ञस्य शाके परम व्योमन |दिवो धर्मन् धरुणे सेदुषो नृñ जातैर अजाताम् अभि ये ननक्षुः ||
ṛtena ṛtaṁ dharuṇaṁ dhārayanta yajñasya śāke parame vyoman | divo dharman dharuṇe seduṣo nṝñ jātair ajātām̐ abhi ye nanakṣuḥ ||
  • ṛtena: This word refers to the concept of "truth" or "cosmic order." In real life, it can be likened to the principles of honesty and integrity that govern human interactions.
  • ṛtaṁ: This is the noun form of "ṛtena," emphasizing the idea of the universal truth or the natural order of the universe. For example, the laws of nature that dictate the changing seasons can be seen as manifestations of ṛta.
  • dharuṇaṁ: This term means "that which supports" or "upholds." In a practical sense, it can refer to the foundational values that support a community or society, such as justice and compassion.
  • dhārayanta: This means "those who uphold" or "maintainers." In everyday life, this could refer to leaders or individuals who work to maintain social harmony and uphold ethical standards.
  • yajñasya: This word translates to "of the sacrifice" or "of the offering." In modern contexts, it can symbolize the acts of giving and selflessness, such as charitable donations or community service.
  • śāke: This term can mean "in the branches" or "in the aspects." It suggests the various dimensions of life and how they are interconnected, much like the branches of a tree that support its growth.
  • parame: This means "supreme" or "ultimate." It can refer to the highest ideals or goals in life, such as achieving enlightenment or self-realization.
  • vyoman: This word means "in the sky" or "in the heavens." It can symbolize aspirations and the pursuit of higher knowledge or spiritual growth.
  • dvo: This translates to "of the two" or "of the duality." It reflects the balance between opposing forces in life, such as joy and sorrow, success and failure.
  • dharman: This refers to "dharma," or the moral law that governs individual conduct. In real life, it can be seen in the ethical choices we make daily.
  • dharuṇe: Similar to "dharuṇaṁ," it emphasizes the supportive nature of dharma in maintaining order and balance in life.
  • seduṣo: This term means "of the good" or "of the virtuous." It highlights the importance of virtue in our actions and decisions.
  • nṝñ: This refers to "men" or "humankind." It signifies the collective responsibility of humanity to uphold truth and righteousness.
  • jātair: This means "by the born" or "by the created." It can refer to the various communities and societies that arise from human interactions.
  • ajātām̐: This translates to "unborn" or "eternal." It signifies the timeless nature of truth and righteousness that transcends generations.
  • abhi: This means "towards" or "in the direction of." It indicates the proactive approach we must take in pursuing truth and righteousness.
  • ye: This translates to "those who." It emphasizes the collective effort required to uphold these values.
  • nanakṣuḥ: This term can mean "to be nourished" or "to be sustained." It reflects the idea that living in accordance with truth and righteousness nourishes the soul and sustains life.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of truth, righteousness, and the interconnectedness of all beings. It calls upon individuals to uphold these values in their lives, suggesting that by doing so, they contribute to the greater cosmic order. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied in various contexts, such as in leadership, community service, and personal relationships. By embodying these principles, individuals can create a harmonious society that thrives on mutual respect and ethical conduct.
