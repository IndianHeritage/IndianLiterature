उत ब्रह्माणो मरुतो मे अस्येन्द्रः सोमस्य सुषुतस्य पेयाः |तद हि हव्यं मनुषे गा अविन्दद अहन्न अहिम पपिवां इन्द्रो अस्य ||
uta brahmāṇo maruto me asyendraḥ somasya suṣutasya peyāḥ | tad dhi havyam manuṣe gā avindad ahann ahim papivām̐ indro asya ||
  • uta - This word can be translated as "and" or "also," indicating a connection or addition to what has been previously mentioned.
  • brahmāṇo - Refers to the "Brahman," the ultimate reality or cosmic spirit in Hindu philosophy, representing the source of all creation.
  • maruto - This term refers to the "Maruts," the storm deities or winds, symbolizing strength, power, and the forces of nature.
  • me - Translates to "my," indicating a personal connection or possession.
  • asyendraḥ - "Indra" is the king of the gods and the god of rain and thunderstorms, while "asya" means "of this," referring to the context of the mantra.
  • somasya - Refers to "Soma," a sacred drink in Vedic rituals, symbolizing immortality and divine inspiration.
  • suṣutasya - Means "well-prepared" or "well-made," indicating the quality of the offerings or experiences.
  • peyāḥ - Translates to "drink" or "beverage," often referring to the offerings made to the deities.
  • tad - Means "that," referring to the offerings or actions being discussed.
  • havyam - Refers to "that which is offered," particularly in the context of Vedic rituals.
  • manuṣe - Means "to humans" or "in human affairs," indicating the relevance of the offerings to human life.
  • gā - Refers to "songs" or "praises," often associated with hymns or expressions of devotion.
  • avindad - Means "found" or "obtained," indicating the act of discovering or receiving something valuable.
  • ahann - Translates to "I have," indicating possession or realization.
  • ahim - Refers to "the serpent" or "the enemy," symbolizing challenges or obstacles.
  • papivām̐ - Means "I have drunk," indicating the act of consuming or accepting something beneficial.
  • indro - Again refers to "Indra," emphasizing his role in the context of the mantra.
  • asya - Means "of this," reinforcing the connection to the previous elements mentioned.

The mantra essentially speaks to the relationship between the divine, the offerings made by humans, and the blessings received in return. It highlights the importance of well-prepared offerings (like Soma) and the role of deities like Indra in bestowing strength and prosperity upon humanity.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as:

  • Rituals and Offerings: Just as the mantra emphasizes the importance of offerings to the gods, individuals can engage in rituals that express gratitude and seek blessings for their endeavors.
  • Personal Growth: The idea of overcoming challenges (symbolized by "ahim") resonates with personal struggles. By acknowledging obstacles and seeking divine support, individuals can find strength to persevere.
  • Community and Cooperation: The collective aspect of the mantra encourages communities to come together in shared rituals, fostering unity and collaboration for common goals.

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness between the divine and human actions. It emphasizes the significance of intention behind offerings and the blessings that can be received through devotion and perseverance. By applying these principles in daily life, individuals can cultivate a deeper sense of purpose and connection to the greater cosmos.
