मन्त्र: हिरण्यहस्तो असुरः सुनीथः सुमृळीकः स्ववाम् यातुर्वार्वाङ् |अपसेधन रक्षसो यातुधानान् अस्ताद् देवः प्रतिदोषं गृणानः ||
hiraṇyahasto asuraḥ sunīthaḥ sumṛḻīkaḥ svavām̐ yātv arvāṅ | apasedhan rakṣaso yātudhānān asthād devaḥ pratidoṣaṁ gṛṇānaḥ ||
  • hiraṇyahasto: This term translates to "one who has golden hands." In a metaphorical sense, it can refer to someone who is skilled, prosperous, and capable of creating wealth or beauty. For example, a talented artist or craftsman who creates beautiful works can be seen as having "golden hands."
  • asuraḥ: This word means "powerful" or "divine being." In the context of the mantra, it signifies a being of great strength and authority, often associated with the gods. In real life, this could relate to leaders or influential figures who have the power to effect change.
  • sunīthaḥ: This term means "well-guided" or "noble." It suggests a person who is wise and follows a righteous path. For instance, a mentor or teacher who guides students towards ethical and moral decisions embodies this quality.
  • sumṛḻīkaḥ: This translates to "one who is well-versed" or "knowledgeable." It refers to someone who possesses wisdom and understanding. A scientist or scholar who contributes to knowledge in their field exemplifies this trait.
  • svavām̐: This means "to go forth" or "to advance." It indicates a proactive approach to life, encouraging individuals to take initiative. For example, an entrepreneur who starts a new business venture is embodying this spirit of advancement.
  • yātv: This term can be interpreted as "may you go" or "may you proceed." It is a blessing or encouragement for someone to move forward on their path.
  • arvāṅ: This word refers to "all directions" or "everywhere." It signifies a universal presence or influence, suggesting that the qualities mentioned are applicable in all aspects of life.
  • apasedhan: This means "to protect" or "to guard." It emphasizes the importance of safeguarding oneself and others from harm. In real life, this could relate to community leaders who work to ensure the safety and well-being of their constituents.
  • rakṣaso: This term refers to "demons" or "evil forces." It highlights the challenges and adversities one may face in life. For example, societal issues such as crime or corruption can be seen as the "demons" that need to be addressed.
  • yātudhānān: This translates to "those who cause harm" or "malevolent beings." It signifies the presence of negativity or obstacles in life that one must confront.
  • asthād: This means "to stand firm" or "to establish." It encourages resilience and strength in the face of adversity. A person who stands up for their beliefs despite opposition exemplifies this quality.
  • devaḥ: This term means "god" or "divine being." It represents the higher powers or forces that guide and protect individuals. In real life, this could relate to the moral compass or spiritual beliefs that guide a person's actions.
  • pratidoṣaṁ: This translates to "in every challenge" or "in every obstacle." It emphasizes the idea that challenges are a part of life and must be faced with courage and determination.
  • gṛṇānaḥ: This means "to sing" or "to praise." It signifies the importance of expressing gratitude and acknowledging the positive aspects of life. For example, a community coming together to celebrate achievements reflects this spirit of praise.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of strength, wisdom, and resilience in the face of challenges. It encourages individuals to embrace their skills and knowledge, to protect themselves and others, and to confront adversities with courage. In real life, these principles can be applied in various scenarios, such as leadership, education, and community building. By embodying the qualities of being well-guided, knowledgeable, and proactive, individuals can navigate life's challenges effectively and contribute positively to society.
