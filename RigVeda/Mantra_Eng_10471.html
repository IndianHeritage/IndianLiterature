याः सरूपा विरूपा एकरूपा यासाम अग्निर इष्ट्या नामानि वेद |या अंगिरसस् तपसेह चक्रुस ताभ्यः परजण्य महि शर्मा यच्छ ||
yāḥ sarūpā virūpā ekarūpā yāsām agnir iṣṭyā nāmāni veda | yā aṅgirasas tapaseha cakrus tābhyaḥ parjanya mahi śarma yaccha ||
  • yāḥ sarūpā virūpā ekarūpā: This phrase refers to the diverse forms of existence.
    • yāḥ - those who
    • sarūpā - having a beautiful form
    • virūpā - having a different or varied form
    • ekarūpā - having a single form
    This can be understood as the various manifestations of life and nature, which can be seen in the diversity of species, cultures, and ideas in our world today. For example, consider the variety of flowers in a garden, each with its unique beauty yet all contributing to the overall harmony of the garden.
  • yāsām agnir iṣṭyā nāmāni veda: This part speaks about the knowledge and offerings associated with fire (Agni).
    • yāsām - of whom
    • agnir - fire
    • iṣṭyā - through offerings
    • nāmāni - names or identities
    • veda - knowledge or wisdom
    Fire is often seen as a purifying element and a source of energy. In real life, we can relate this to the importance of knowledge and education, which can illuminate our paths and help us grow. Just as fire transforms materials, knowledge transforms our understanding and capabilities.
  • yā aṅgirasas tapaseha cakrus: This line refers to the sages (Aṅgirasas) who have performed penance or meditation.
    • yā - who
    • aṅgirasas - the sages or wise ones
    • tapaseha - through their penance
    • cakrus - have created or established
    This can be seen in the context of modern-day thinkers and leaders who dedicate themselves to the pursuit of knowledge and truth, often through rigorous study and self-discipline. For instance, scientists and philosophers who spend years researching to contribute to human understanding exemplify this dedication.
  • tābhyaḥ parjanya mahi śarma yaccha: This final part is a prayer for blessings and protection.
    • tābhyaḥ - from them
    • parjanya - the rain or abundance
    • mahi - great or vast
    • śarma - comfort or protection
    • yaccha - grant or give
    This can be interpreted as a request for the blessings of abundance and safety in our lives. In practical terms, this reflects our desire for prosperity and security, whether it be in our personal lives, communities, or nations. For example, during times of crisis, people often seek the support of their communities and leaders for guidance and protection.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of diversity, knowledge, dedication, and the quest for blessings. It signifies the interconnectedness of all forms of existence and the importance of wisdom and community support in navigating life's challenges. In real life, this mantra can inspire individuals to embrace diversity, seek knowledge, practice self-discipline, and foster a sense of community, ultimately leading to a more harmonious and prosperous existence.
