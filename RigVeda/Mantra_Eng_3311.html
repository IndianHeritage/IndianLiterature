मन्त्र: अत्राह ते हरिवस ता उ देवीर अवोभिर इन्द्र स्तवन्त स्वसारः |यत सिम अनु प्र मुचो बद्बधाना दीर्घाम अनु प्रसितिं स्यन्दयध्यै ||
atrāha te harivas tā u devīr avobhir indra stavanta svasāraḥ | yat sīm anu pra muco badbadhānā dīrghām anu prasitiṁ syandayadhyai ||
  • atrāha te harivas tā u devīr avobhir indra stavanta svasāraḥ
    • atrāha: Here, "atra" means "here" or "in this place," and "aha" signifies "today" or "now." This indicates a present moment of invocation.
    • te: This translates to "to you," referring to a divine entity, in this case, Indra.
    • harivas: This term can be interpreted as "the one who brings joy" or "the one who is auspicious," often associated with the divine qualities of Indra.
    • tā u: "tā" means "those" and "u" can be understood as "also," suggesting a connection to other divine beings or forces.
    • devīr: This word means "goddesses" or "divine beings," indicating the presence of multiple divine forces in the invocation.
    • avobhir: This term means "with praises" or "through hymns," emphasizing the act of worship and reverence.
    • indra: Refers to Indra, the king of gods in Vedic literature, known for his strength and ability to overcome obstacles.
    • stavanta: This means "who are praising," indicating the act of worship directed towards Indra.
    • svasāraḥ: This translates to "the ones who are related" or "the kin," suggesting a communal aspect of worship.
  • yat sīm anu pra muco badbadhānā dīrghām anu prasitiṁ syandayadhyai
    • yat: This means "what" or "that which," introducing a clause that describes a condition or a request.
    • sīm: This can be interpreted as "boundary" or "limit," indicating a desire to transcend limitations.
    • anu: This means "following" or "after," suggesting continuity or progression.
    • pra: This prefix means "forth" or "forward," indicating a movement towards something greater.
    • muco: This translates to "release" or "liberate," indicating a desire for freedom from constraints.
    • badbadhānā: This term means "those who are bound" or "entangled," referring to beings caught in the cycle of life.
    • dīrghām: This means "long" or "extended," suggesting a desire for longevity or enduring presence.
    • anu prasitiṁ: This translates to "following the path" or "continuing the journey," indicating a quest for progress.
    • syandayadhyai: This means "to be fulfilled" or "to be realized," indicating the ultimate goal of the invocation.

Conclusion:

This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the importance of communal worship and the invocation of divine forces, particularly Indra, to transcend limitations and seek liberation. It highlights the significance of praise and reverence in connecting with the divine, fostering a sense of unity among worshippers. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as:

  • Community Gatherings: When people come together for festivals or rituals, invoking divine blessings can strengthen communal bonds and promote harmony.
  • Personal Challenges: Individuals facing obstacles can recite this mantra to seek strength and guidance from higher powers, encouraging resilience and perseverance.
  • Spiritual Practices: In meditation or prayer, this mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of all beings and the pursuit of liberation from worldly attachments.

Overall, the mantra serves as a powerful reminder of the divine presence in our lives and the importance of collective worship in overcoming challenges and seeking fulfillment.
