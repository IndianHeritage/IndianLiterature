तद् वीर्यं वो मरुतो महित्वनं दीर्घं ततान सूर्यो न योजanam |एता न यामे अगृभीतशोचिषो 'नश्वदां यन् नयातना गिरिम् ||
tad vīryaṁ vo maruto mahitvanaṁ dīrghaṁ tatāna sūryo na yojanam | etā na yāme agṛbhītaśociṣo 'naśvadāṁ yan ny ayātanā girim ||
  • tad vīryaṁ: This phrase translates to "that strength" or "that power." It signifies the immense energy and vitality that is sought after.
  • vo maruto: "O Maruts," referring to the Maruts, the storm deities in Vedic literature, known for their power and speed. They symbolize the forces of nature and the dynamic aspects of life.
  • mahitvanaṁ: This means "of great importance" or "greatness." It emphasizes the significance of the strength being referred to.
  • dīrghaṁ: Translated as "long" or "enduring," it suggests a lasting quality, indicating that the strength sought is not fleeting but persistent.
  • tatāna: This word means "to stretch" or "to extend," implying the expansion of this strength or power.
  • sūryo: Referring to the "Sun," which is often associated with life, energy, and illumination. The Sun is a vital source of energy in our lives.
  • na yojanam: This translates to "not to be joined" or "not to be connected," suggesting a separation or independence from something.
  • etā: Meaning "these," it refers to the qualities or aspects being discussed.
  • na yāme: This can be interpreted as "in this time" or "in this moment," indicating a specific context or situation.
  • agṛbhītaśociṣo: This phrase means "not to be grasped" or "ungraspable light," suggesting something that is beyond reach or understanding, yet illuminating.
  • 'naśvadāṁ: This translates to "not to be destroyed" or "imperishable," indicating a quality of permanence and resilience.
  • yan ny ayātanā: Meaning "that which does not move" or "that which is stable," it refers to a foundation or core strength that remains constant.
  • girim: This word means "mountain," symbolizing stability, strength, and endurance in the face of challenges.

Conclusion: The mantra speaks to the quest for enduring strength and power, invoking the Maruts and the Sun as symbols of vitality and illumination. In real life, this can be applied to personal development, where individuals seek to cultivate inner strength and resilience. Just as the Maruts represent the dynamic forces of nature, we too can harness our energies to overcome obstacles. The mantra encourages us to strive for a lasting impact in our lives, to remain grounded like a mountain, and to seek illumination in our endeavors. It serves as a reminder that true strength is not just about physical power but also about mental and spiritual endurance, guiding us through life's challenges with clarity and purpose.
