मन्त्र: इन्दुर इन्द्राय पवतः इति देवासो अब्रुवन् | वाचस् पतिर मखस्यते विश्वस्येशान ओजसा ||
indur indrāya pavata iti devāso abruvan | vācas patir makhasyate viśvasyeśāna ojasā ||
  • Indur: This word translates to "the moon" or "the luminous one." In many cultures, the moon symbolizes calmness, intuition, and the cyclical nature of life.
  • Indrāya: This refers to "Indra," the king of the gods in Vedic tradition, associated with rain, thunderstorms, and war. Indra represents strength and power.
  • Pavata: This means "to blow" or "to move." It can refer to the wind, which is often seen as a carrier of messages and a symbol of change.
  • Devāso: This translates to "the gods" or "divine beings." It signifies the higher powers that govern the universe.
  • Abruvan: This means "they spoke" or "they declared." It indicates communication or the sharing of wisdom among the divine.
  • Vācas: This word means "speech" or "word." It emphasizes the importance of communication and expression in human life.
  • Patir: This translates to "lord" or "master." It signifies authority and leadership.
  • Makhasyate: This refers to "sacrifice" or "offering." In Vedic culture, sacrifices were a way to connect with the divine and seek blessings.
  • Viśvasyeśāna: This means "the lord of all" or "the master of the universe." It signifies the omnipresence of the divine in all aspects of life.
  • Ojasā: This translates to "with energy" or "with strength." It represents vitality and the life force that drives existence.

The mantra can be interpreted as a divine communication where the gods, represented by Indra and the moon, convey their wisdom and strength through the medium of the wind. It emphasizes the significance of speech and sacrifice in connecting with the divine and acknowledges the omnipresence of the divine energy in the universe.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios:

  • Communication: Just as the gods communicate their wisdom, effective communication is essential in our daily lives, whether in personal relationships or professional settings.
  • Leadership: The reference to "Patir" highlights the importance of strong leadership. In any organization, a good leader inspires and guides their team, much like Indra leads the gods.
  • Connection with the Divine: The concept of sacrifice in "Makhasyate" can be seen in acts of kindness and service to others, fostering a sense of community and connection with something greater than oneself.
  • Embracing Change: The wind ("Pavata") symbolizes change. Embracing change and being adaptable is crucial in today's fast-paced world.

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life, the importance of communication, leadership, and the divine energy that permeates our existence. By embodying these principles, we can lead more fulfilling and harmonious lives.
