अत उ त्वा पितुभृतो जनित्रीर अन्नावृधम् प्रति चरन्त्य अन्नैः |तां ईं प्रत्य एषि पुनर अन्यरूपा असि त्वं विक्षु मानविषु होताः ||
ata u tvā pitubhṛto janitrīr annāvṛdham prati caranty annaiḥ | tā īm praty eṣi punar anyarūpā asi tvaṁ vikṣu mānuṣīṣu hotā ||
  • ata u tvā pitubhṛto janitrīr annāvṛdham prati caranty annaiḥ
    • ata u tvā: This phrase can be translated as "therefore, you" or "thus, you." It indicates a connection or a conclusion drawn from previous thoughts or actions.
    • pitubhṛto: This term refers to "the one who is nourished by the father" or "the one who is supported by the father." It signifies the importance of paternal support in one's life.
    • janitrīr: This word means "the mother" or "the one who gives birth." It emphasizes the nurturing aspect of motherhood.
    • annāvṛdham: This translates to "nourished by food" or "sustained by nourishment." It highlights the essential role of food in sustaining life.
    • prati caranty annaiḥ: This phrase means "they move towards food" or "they go in search of nourishment." It reflects the instinctual drive of living beings to seek sustenance.
  • tā īm praty eṣi punar anyarūpā asi tvaṁ vikṣu mānuṣīṣu hotā
    • tā īm: This can be interpreted as "those who" or "they." It refers to the subjects of the mantra, possibly the deities or forces of nature.
    • praty eṣi: This means "you return" or "you come back." It suggests a cyclical nature of existence and the return of energies or entities.
    • punar: This translates to "again" or "once more," reinforcing the idea of cycles and renewal.
    • anyarūpā: This term means "in different forms" or "in various manifestations." It indicates the diversity of existence and the many forms life can take.
    • asi tvaṁ: This means "you are" or "you exist." It affirms the presence and essence of the subject being addressed.
    • vikṣu: This word refers to "the one who is a seeker" or "the one who is searching." It emphasizes the quest for knowledge and sustenance.
    • mānuṣīṣu: This translates to "among humans" or "in human beings." It highlights the human experience and the connection to the divine or the universal.
    • hotā: This term means "the one who offers" or "the priest." It signifies the role of a mediator between the divine and the earthly realms.

Conclusion:

The mantra reflects the interconnectedness of life, emphasizing the roles of nourishment, parental support, and the cyclical nature of existence. It highlights how beings, both divine and human, seek sustenance and return in various forms. In real life, this can be seen in the way families support each other, the importance of food in our daily lives, and the continuous cycle of life and death. The mantra serves as a reminder of our dependence on one another and the natural world, encouraging gratitude for the nourishment we receive and the relationships we cultivate.
