मन्त्र: अचोदसो नो धन्वन्त्विन्दवः प्र सुवानासो बृहद्दिवेषु हरयः |विच नशन् न इषो अरातयो 'र्यो नशन्त सनीषन्त नो धियः ||
acodaso no dhanvantv indavaḥ pra suvānāso bṛhaddiveṣu harayaḥ | vi ca naśan na iṣo arātayo 'ryo naśanta saniṣanta no dhiyaḥ ||
  • acodaso no dhanvantv indavaḥ pra suvānāso bṛhaddiveṣu harayaḥ
    • acodaso: This term can be interpreted as "those who are not obstructed" or "unhindered." It signifies a state of freedom and openness.
    • no: This is a pronoun meaning "our" or "us," indicating a collective identity.
    • dhanvantv: Refers to "health" or "well-being." It is derived from the root word "Dhanvantari," who is considered the god of medicine in Hindu mythology.
    • indavaḥ: This word means "the celestial beings" or "gods," often associated with the divine forces that govern the universe.
    • pra: A prefix meaning "towards" or "in favor of," indicating a direction or intention.
    • suvānāso: This can be translated as "those who are well-versed" or "skilled," suggesting a sense of expertise or proficiency.
    • bṛhaddiveṣu: This term means "in the vast heavens," referring to the expansive nature of the universe and the divine realms.
    • harayaḥ: This word signifies "those who bring joy" or "delight," often associated with positive energies and uplifting forces.
  • vi ca naśan na iṣo arātayo 'ryo naśanta saniṣanta no dhiyaḥ
    • vi: This word means "and" or "also," indicating a connection between ideas.
    • ca: Another conjunction meaning "and," reinforcing the idea of addition or inclusion.
    • naśan: This term can be interpreted as "those who perish" or "those who are lost," suggesting a contrast to the previous ideas of health and well-being.
    • na: A negation meaning "not," emphasizing the avoidance of negative outcomes.
    • iṣo: This word means "of the powerful" or "of the strong," referring to those with authority or influence.
    • arātayo: This term can be translated as "enemies" or "opponents," indicating a sense of conflict or struggle.
    • 'ryo: This is a form of "noble" or "worthy," suggesting a distinction between the righteous and the unrighteous.
    • naśanta: This word means "those who are lost" or "those who have perished," reinforcing the theme of loss.
    • saniṣanta: This term can be interpreted as "those who are aware" or "those who understand," indicating a sense of consciousness and mindfulness.
    • no dhiyaḥ: This phrase means "our thoughts" or "our intellect," emphasizing the importance of mental clarity and wisdom.

Conclusion:

The mantra emphasizes the importance of health, well-being, and the pursuit of knowledge while acknowledging the presence of challenges and adversities. It encourages individuals to seek divine guidance and support from celestial beings to overcome obstacles and maintain a positive mindset. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as:

  • In healthcare, promoting holistic well-being and the importance of mental health alongside physical health.
  • In personal development, encouraging individuals to cultivate skills and knowledge to navigate life's challenges effectively.
  • In community building, fostering a sense of unity and collective strength to overcome adversities.

Ultimately, the mantra serves as a reminder to seek harmony, wisdom, and resilience in the face of life's complexities.
