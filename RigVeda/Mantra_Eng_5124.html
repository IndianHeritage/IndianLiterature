ब्राह्मणासः पितरः सोम्यासः शिवे नो द्यावापृथिवी अनहसः | पूषा नः पातु दुरिताद ऋतावृद्धो रक्षां माकिर नो अघशंस इषत ||
brāhmaṇāsaḥ pitaraḥ somyāsaḥ śive no dyāvāpṛthivī anehasā | pūṣā naḥ pātu duritād ṛtāvṛdho rakṣā mākir no aghaśaṁsa īśata ||
  • brāhmaṇāsaḥ: This term refers to the learned or wise individuals, often associated with the priestly class in Vedic society. They are seen as the custodians of knowledge and spiritual wisdom.
  • pitaraḥ: This word means "fathers" or "ancestors." It signifies the importance of lineage and the respect for those who came before us, emphasizing the connection between generations.
  • somyāsaḥ: This term can be interpreted as "peaceful" or "gentle." It reflects a state of calmness and tranquility, which is essential for spiritual growth and harmony in life.
  • śive: This word means "auspicious" or "beneficial." It denotes the positive aspects of life and the blessings that come from living in accordance with dharma (righteousness).
  • no: This is a pronoun meaning "our," indicating a collective identity and shared experiences among individuals.
  • dyāvāpṛthivī: This phrase refers to "heaven and earth," symbolizing the duality of existence and the interconnectedness of all things in the universe.
  • anehasā: This term means "without harm" or "free from obstacles." It suggests a desire for a life that is unencumbered by difficulties and challenges.
  • pūṣā: This is a reference to the deity Pūṣan, who is associated with nourishment and protection. It signifies the importance of sustenance, both physical and spiritual.
  • naḥ: This means "us" or "our," reinforcing the communal aspect of the mantra.
  • pātu: This verb means "may protect." It expresses a plea for safeguarding and guidance from higher powers.
  • duritād: This term means "from misfortunes" or "from difficulties." It highlights the human experience of facing challenges and the desire to overcome them.
  • ṛtāvṛdho: This phrase refers to "truth and righteousness." It emphasizes the importance of living a life aligned with moral values and ethical principles.
  • rakṣā: This means "protection" or "safeguarding." It is a call for divine intervention to shield individuals from harm.
  • mākir: This term means "may we not be harmed." It is a direct appeal for safety and well-being.
  • no: Again, this pronoun emphasizes the collective aspect of the plea.
  • aghaśaṁsa: This term refers to "sin" or "evil." It signifies the desire to be free from negative influences and actions that lead to suffering.
  • īśata: This means "may we be blessed" or "may we receive grace." It is a hopeful expression for divine favor and support.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates a profound plea for protection, guidance, and nourishment from the divine. It emphasizes the importance of wisdom, respect for ancestors, and the pursuit of a peaceful and auspicious life. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during family gatherings, rituals, or personal reflections, where individuals seek blessings for themselves and their loved ones. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life, the significance of moral values, and the collective journey towards overcoming challenges and achieving harmony.
