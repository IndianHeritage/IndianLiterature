पिषङ्गरूपः सुभरो वयोधाः श्रुष्टि वीरो जायते देवकामः | प्रजां त्वष्टा विश्यतु नाभिम अस्मे अथ देवानाम अप्य एतु पाठः ||
piśaṅgarūpaḥ subharo vayodhāḥ śruṣṭī vīro jāyate devakāmaḥ | prajāṁ tvaṣṭā vi ṣyatu nābhim asme athā devānām apy etu pāthaḥ ||
  • piśaṅgarūpaḥ: This term refers to a being or entity that has a yellowish or golden hue. In a real-life context, this can symbolize prosperity and abundance, akin to the golden color of ripe crops or the sun, which is essential for life.
  • subharo: This word means 'well-formed' or 'handsome'. It can be related to the idea of beauty and harmony in nature, such as a well-maintained garden or a balanced ecosystem that supports diverse life forms.
  • vayodhāḥ: This translates to 'the strong' or 'the powerful'. In everyday life, this can be seen in individuals who exhibit strength and resilience, such as athletes or leaders who inspire others through their actions.
  • śruṣṭī: Meaning 'creation' or 'the act of creating', this word can be connected to the creative processes in art, science, and innovation. For example, artists creating masterpieces or scientists developing new technologies that benefit society.
  • vīro: This term means 'hero' or 'brave one'. In contemporary society, heroes can be found in various forms, such as first responders, activists fighting for justice, or everyday individuals who stand up for what is right.
  • jāyate: This means 'is born' or 'comes into being'. It signifies the emergence of new ideas, movements, or even life itself, such as the birth of a child or the launch of a new initiative aimed at social change.
  • devakāmaḥ: This term refers to 'the desire of the gods' or 'divine wish'. It can be interpreted as the aspirations that align with higher purposes, such as humanitarian efforts that seek to improve the lives of others.
  • prajāṁ: This means 'creatures' or 'beings'. It encompasses all forms of life, highlighting the interconnectedness of all living beings, from humans to animals and plants.
  • tvaṣṭā: This term refers to 'the creator' or 'the artisan'. In real life, this can be likened to skilled craftsmen, engineers, or anyone who shapes the world through their creativity and expertise.
  • viṣyatu: This means 'may it be established' or 'may it come to fruition'. It reflects the hope and intention behind actions, such as community projects aimed at improving local infrastructure or education.
  • nābhim: This translates to 'the navel' or 'the center'. It symbolizes the core or essence of something, much like the central role of family or community in an individual's life.
  • asme: This means 'to us' or 'for us'. It emphasizes the collective aspect of existence, reminding us that our actions impact not just ourselves but also those around us.
  • athā: This translates to 'and then' or 'therefore', indicating a continuation or consequence of previous thoughts or actions.
  • devānām: This means 'of the gods'. It signifies the divine influence in our lives, akin to the guidance we seek from mentors or spiritual leaders.
  • apy: This term means 'even' or 'also', suggesting inclusivity and the idea that all beings are part of a larger whole.
  • etu: This translates to 'may it come' or 'let it arrive', expressing a desire for positive outcomes and blessings.
  • pāthaḥ: This means 'path' or 'way'. It symbolizes the journey of life, the choices we make, and the direction we take in pursuit of our goals.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of creation, strength, and divine aspiration. It emphasizes the interconnectedness of all beings and the importance of striving for beauty, harmony, and purpose in life. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire individuals to embrace their creative potential, act heroically in their communities, and work towards collective well-being. It serves as a reminder that our actions, guided by higher aspirations, can lead to the establishment of a more harmonious and prosperous world.
