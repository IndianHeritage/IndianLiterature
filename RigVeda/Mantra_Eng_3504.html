इन्द्रा युवं वरुणा भूतम अस्याः धियः प्रेतारा वृषभेव दhenोः |सां नो दुधीयद यवसेव गत्वी सहस्रधारा पयसा मही गौः ||
indrā yuvaṁ varuṇā bhūtam asyā dhiyaḥ pretārā vṛṣabheva dhenoḥ | sā no duhīyad yavaseva gatvī sahasradhārā payasā mahī gauḥ ||
  • Indrā: Refers to Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, symbolizing strength, power, and leadership.
  • Yuvaṁ: Means 'young' or 'youthful', indicating vitality and energy.
  • Varuṇā: Refers to Varuna, the god of cosmic order and water, representing the moral and ethical aspects of life.
  • Bhūtam: Translates to 'that which exists' or 'the essence', emphasizing the importance of existence and reality.
  • Asyā: Means 'of this', referring to the context or the subject matter at hand.
  • Dhiyaḥ: Refers to 'intellect' or 'understanding', highlighting the importance of wisdom and knowledge.
  • Pretārā: Means 'those who have passed away', indicating a connection to ancestors or the past.
  • Vṛṣabheva: Translates to 'like a bull', symbolizing strength and fertility.
  • Dhenoḥ: Refers to 'the cow', which is a symbol of nourishment and abundance in Vedic culture.
  • Sā: Means 'that', referring to the subject of the mantra.
  • No: Means 'us', indicating a collective or community aspect.
  • Duhīyad: Translates to 'may it nourish', emphasizing the desire for sustenance and support.
  • Yavaseva: Refers to 'like the grass', symbolizing growth and fertility.
  • Gatvī: Means 'having gone', indicating movement or progress.
  • Sahasradhārā: Translates to 'with a thousand streams', symbolizing abundance and richness.
  • Payasā: Refers to 'milk', a symbol of nourishment and purity.
  • Mahī: Means 'great' or 'earth', representing stability and grounding.
  • Gauḥ: Refers to 'cow', again emphasizing nourishment and the importance of agriculture.

Conclusion: This mantra invokes the blessings of Indra and Varuna, seeking their guidance for strength, wisdom, and nourishment. It emphasizes the importance of community, growth, and the connection to our ancestors. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios such as during agricultural rituals, community gatherings, or personal reflections on growth and sustenance. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life, the importance of ethical living, and the need for support from both the divine and the community. By invoking these deities, individuals can seek strength and guidance in their endeavors, fostering a sense of unity and purpose in their lives.
