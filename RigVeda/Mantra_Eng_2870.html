इन्द्रो हर्यन्तम अर्जुनं वज्रं शुक्रैर अभिवृतम् |अपावृणोद धरिभिर अद्रिभिः सुतं उद गा हरिभिर आजत ||
indro haryantam arjunaṁ vajraṁ śukrair abhīvṛtam | apāvṛṇod dharibhir adribhiḥ sutam ud gā haribhir ājata ||
  • Indro: Refers to Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, symbolizing strength, power, and leadership.
  • Haryantam: This term can be interpreted as "one who is adorned" or "one who is decorated," indicating the importance of appearance and valor.
  • Arjunaṁ: Refers to Arjuna, a central character in the Mahabharata, known for his skills in archery and his moral dilemmas, representing the human struggle between duty and righteousness.
  • Vajraṁ: Translates to "thunderbolt" or "diamond," symbolizing indestructibility and immense power.
  • Śukrair: Means "with brightness" or "with purity," suggesting the importance of clarity and virtue in one's actions.
  • Abhīvṛtam: Means "surrounded" or "enveloped," indicating protection and support from divine forces.
  • Apāvṛṇod: Translates to "he uncovered" or "he revealed," suggesting the act of bringing forth knowledge or truth.
  • Dharibhir: Refers to "the bearers" or "the holders," which can symbolize those who carry responsibilities or burdens.
  • Adribhiḥ: Means "by the mountains," indicating strength and stability, as mountains are often seen as symbols of endurance.
  • Sutam: Refers to "the son" or "the offspring," emphasizing lineage and the importance of heritage.
  • Ud gā: Means "to sing forth" or "to proclaim," indicating the act of expressing or celebrating something significant.
  • Haribhir: Refers to "by the divine beings" or "by the gods," suggesting divine intervention or support.
  • Ājata: Means "born" or "arisen," indicating the emergence of new ideas, strength, or leadership.

This mantra can be interpreted as a call for divine strength and support, particularly in the context of leadership and moral dilemmas. It emphasizes the importance of purity, protection, and the strength derived from one's heritage and divine support. The imagery of Indra and Arjuna highlights the balance between power and righteousness, suggesting that true strength comes from virtuous actions and the support of the divine.


In real life, this mantra can be applicable in various scenarios, such as:

  • Leadership Roles: Leaders can draw inspiration from this mantra to embody strength and righteousness in their decision-making processes.
  • Personal Challenges: Individuals facing moral dilemmas can reflect on the qualities of Arjuna, seeking clarity and support in their choices.
  • Community Support: The idea of being surrounded by divine beings can encourage individuals to seek and offer support within their communities.

Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its call for strength, purity, and divine support in the face of challenges. It serves as a reminder that true power is not just about physical strength but also about moral integrity and the support we receive from our heritage and the divine. In today's world, this mantra can inspire individuals to lead with virtue, seek clarity in their actions, and foster a sense of community and support.
