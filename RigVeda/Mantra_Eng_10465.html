प्रसूतो भक्षं अकरं चराव अपि स्तोमं चेमं प्रथमः सूरीर उन्मृजे |सुते सातेन यद् आगमं वाम प्रति विश्वामित्रजमदग्नī दमे ||
prasūto bhakṣam akaraṁ carāv api stomaṁ cemam prathamaḥ sūrir un mṛje | sute sātena yady āgamaṁ vām prati viśvāmitrajamadagnī dame ||
  • Prasūto: This word translates to "born" or "produced." It signifies the origin or creation of something.
  • Bhakṣam: This means "food" or "nourishment." It represents the sustenance necessary for life.
  • Akaraṁ: This translates to "without form" or "formless." It can refer to the abstract or spiritual aspects of existence.
  • Carāv: This means "moving" or "mobile." It indicates the dynamic nature of life and the universe.
  • Api: This translates to "also" or "even." It is used to add emphasis or inclusivity.
  • Stomaṁ: This refers to "praise" or "hymn." It signifies the importance of expressing gratitude and reverence.
  • Cemam: This means "this" or "here." It points to the immediate context or situation.
  • Prathamaḥ: This translates to "first" or "foremost." It indicates priority or significance.
  • Sūrir: This means "wise" or "intelligent." It refers to those who possess knowledge and understanding.
  • Un: This is a particle that can mean "not" or "without." It negates the following term.
  • Mṛje: This translates to "to cleanse" or "to purify." It signifies the act of removing impurities.
  • Sute: This means "in the birth" or "in the production." It relates to the process of creation.
  • Sātena: This translates to "with a hundred." It signifies abundance or multiplicity.
  • Yady: This means "if." It introduces a conditional statement.
  • Āgamaṁ: This translates to "coming" or "arriving." It signifies the arrival of something significant.
  • Vām: This means "your" or "of you." It indicates possession or relation.
  • Prati: This translates to "towards" or "in relation to." It indicates direction or focus.
  • Viśvāmitra: This refers to a sage known for his wisdom and contributions to Vedic literature.
  • Jamadagnī: This is another sage, known for his knowledge and spiritual insight.
  • Dame: This means "in the house" or "in the dwelling." It signifies a place of safety and comfort.

The mantra speaks to the interconnectedness of creation, nourishment, and the pursuit of wisdom. It emphasizes the importance of recognizing the formless essence of life while also acknowledging the material aspects that sustain us. The invocation of sages like Viśvāmitra and Jamadagnī highlights the value of wisdom and knowledge in navigating life's complexities.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as:

  • Personal Growth: Just as the mantra emphasizes the importance of wisdom, individuals can seek knowledge and understanding to navigate their own life journeys.
  • Community Building: The idea of nourishment can extend to fostering relationships and supporting one another within a community, ensuring that everyone is cared for.
  • Environmental Awareness: Recognizing the formless essence of life can lead to a deeper appreciation for nature and the need to protect our environment.

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the balance between the material and spiritual aspects of life. It encourages individuals to seek wisdom, nurture relationships, and appreciate the interconnectedness of all beings. By embodying these principles, one can lead a more fulfilling and harmonious life.
