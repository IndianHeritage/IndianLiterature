अतिथिम् मानवश्च पितुः न यस्यासयाः |अमī च विश्वे अमृतासः आ वयो हव्याः देवेषु आ वयः ||
atithim mānuṣāṇām pitur na yasyāsayā | amī ca viśve amṛtāsa ā vayo havyā deveṣv ā vayaḥ ||
  • atithim: This word translates to "guest" or "host." In many cultures, including Indian traditions, the concept of hospitality is highly valued. For example, when a guest arrives at a home, they are treated with utmost respect and care, often being offered food and comfort.
  • mānuṣāṇām: This means "of humans." It emphasizes the human aspect of the mantra, indicating that the teachings are relevant to human behavior and social interactions.
  • pitur: This translates to "father." In many societies, the father figure is seen as a protector and provider, symbolizing authority and guidance. For instance, a father often plays a crucial role in teaching values and ethics to children.
  • na yasyāsayā: This phrase means "who does not have." It suggests a lack of something essential, possibly referring to the absence of hospitality or kindness towards others.
  • amī ca viśve: This translates to "and all these." It indicates inclusivity, suggesting that the teachings apply to everyone, not just a select few.
  • amṛtāsa: This word means "immortal" or "eternal." It signifies the idea of lasting values and principles that transcend time and space, much like the enduring nature of kindness and hospitality.
  • ā vayo: This translates to "we are." It implies a collective identity, suggesting that the values discussed are shared among all people.
  • havyā deveṣv ā vayaḥ: This phrase can be interpreted as "we are offerings to the gods." It highlights the idea that our actions, especially those of kindness and hospitality, are offerings to the divine, reinforcing the connection between human behavior and spiritual beliefs.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of hospitality and kindness in human interactions. It teaches that treating guests with respect and generosity is not just a social obligation but a spiritual practice that connects us to the divine. In real life, this can be applied in various scenarios, such as welcoming friends and family into our homes, participating in community events, or even in professional settings where creating a welcoming environment fosters collaboration and goodwill. The significance of this mantra lies in its reminder that our actions towards others reflect our values and beliefs, ultimately contributing to a more harmonious society.
