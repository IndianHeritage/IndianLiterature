तत् सु वाम मित्रावरुणा महित्वम् ईर्मा तस्थुषीर अहभिर दुदुहरे |विश्वाः पिन्वथः स्वसरस्य धेनाऽनु वाम एकः पविर आ ववर्त ||
tat su vām mitrāvaruṇā mahitvam īrmā tasthuṣīr ahabhir duduhre | viśvāḥ pinvathaḥ svasarasya dhenā anu vām ekaḥ pavir ā vavarta ||
  • tat: This word translates to "that" or "that which." It signifies a connection to a higher reality or truth.
  • su: Meaning "good" or "well," it emphasizes positivity and auspiciousness.
  • vām: This means "your" or "of you," indicating a personal connection or relationship.
  • mitrāvaruṇā: This compound word refers to the deities Mitra and Varuna, who represent friendship, harmony, and the cosmic order. They symbolize the balance of nature and the importance of maintaining relationships.
  • mahitvam: This means "greatness" or "majesty," referring to the divine qualities of the deities mentioned.
  • īrmā: This word can be interpreted as "may we have" or "let us attain," indicating a desire for blessings or qualities.
  • tasthuṣīr: This term refers to "the one who is strong" or "the powerful one," suggesting a plea for strength and support.
  • ahabhir: Meaning "with the day," it signifies the importance of time and the cycles of life.
  • duduhre: This translates to "they have given" or "they have bestowed," indicating the act of receiving blessings or gifts.
  • viśvāḥ: This means "all" or "universal," suggesting inclusivity and the interconnectedness of all beings.
  • pinvathaḥ: This word means "may you nourish" or "may you provide sustenance," emphasizing the importance of nourishment in life.
  • svasarasya: This translates to "of the sister" or "of the female," which can symbolize nurturing and care.
  • dhenā: Meaning "cow" or "nourisher," it represents abundance, fertility, and the sustenance provided by nature.
  • anu: This means "following" or "after," indicating a sense of continuity and progression.
  • vām: Again, this refers to "your," reinforcing the personal connection.
  • ekaḥ: This means "one" or "single," suggesting unity and oneness.
  • pavir: This translates to "pure" or "sacred," indicating the importance of purity in thoughts and actions.
  • ā: Meaning "from" or "out of," it indicates a source or origin.
  • vavarta: This means "to turn" or "to revolve," suggesting the cyclical nature of life and the universe.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of divine blessings, harmony, and nourishment in our lives. It calls upon the deities Mitra and Varuna to bestow their greatness and support, highlighting the interconnectedness of all beings and the need for unity and purity. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during rituals for prosperity, seeking harmony in relationships, or invoking strength in challenging times. It serves as a reminder of the importance of nurturing our connections with others and the environment, fostering a sense of community and support.
