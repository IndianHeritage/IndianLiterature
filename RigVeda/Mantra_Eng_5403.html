श्रवच छृतकर्ण ईयते वसूनां नू चिन नो मर्दिषद गिरः |सद्यश चिद यः सहस्राणि शतां ददन नकीर दित्सन्तम आ मिनत ||
śravac chrutkarṇa īyate vasūnāṁ nū cin no mardhiṣad giraḥ | sadyaś cid yaḥ sahasrāṇi śatā dadan nakir ditsantam ā minat ||
  • śravac: This word refers to "hearing" or "listening." It emphasizes the importance of being attentive and receptive to knowledge and wisdom.
  • chrutkarṇa: This term combines "chrut" (to hear) and "karṇa" (ear), indicating the act of listening carefully. It suggests that one should not only hear but also understand and absorb what is being communicated.
  • īyate: This means "is invoked" or "is called upon." It implies that the act of listening is a way to invoke deeper understanding and connection with the divine or the universe.
  • vasūnāṁ: This word refers to "the good" or "the auspicious." It signifies the positive aspects of life and the blessings that come from listening to wisdom.
  • nū: This is an expression of emphasis, often translated as "indeed" or "certainly." It reinforces the importance of the following statement.
  • cin: This means "to know" or "to perceive." It highlights the necessity of gaining knowledge through attentive listening.
  • no: This is a pronoun meaning "us" or "our," indicating a collective experience or understanding.
  • mardhiṣad: This term can be interpreted as "to be harmed" or "to be affected." It suggests that neglecting to listen can lead to negative consequences.
  • giraḥ: This word means "words" or "speech." It emphasizes the power of spoken words and their impact on our lives.
  • sadyaś: This means "immediately" or "at once." It indicates the urgency of the message being conveyed.
  • cid: This term refers to "consciousness" or "awareness." It signifies the importance of being aware and present in the moment.
  • yaḥ: This is a relative pronoun meaning "who." It connects the previous ideas to the subject being discussed.
  • sahasrāṇi: This means "thousands." It suggests abundance and the vastness of knowledge available to those who listen.
  • śatā: This translates to "hundreds." It further emphasizes the multitude of insights that can be gained through attentive listening.
  • dadan: This means "giving" or "providing." It indicates the act of sharing knowledge and wisdom.
  • nakir: This term can be interpreted as "not" or "without." It suggests a negation, emphasizing what is absent when one does not listen.
  • ditsantam: This means "desiring" or "yearning." It reflects the human tendency to seek knowledge and understanding.
  • ā minat: This phrase can be translated as "to be mindful" or "to pay attention." It underscores the importance of being aware and engaged in the process of learning.

Conclusion:

The mantra emphasizes the significance of listening and being receptive to knowledge and wisdom. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied in various contexts, such as:

  • Education: Students who actively listen in class are more likely to understand and retain information, leading to better academic performance.
  • Relationships: In personal relationships, listening to one another fosters understanding and strengthens bonds, reducing conflicts and misunderstandings.
  • Professional Settings: In the workplace, effective communication and active listening can enhance teamwork and collaboration, leading to more successful outcomes.

Overall, the mantra serves as a reminder of the power of attentive listening and its role in personal growth, relationships, and professional success.
