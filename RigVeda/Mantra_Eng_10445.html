महनाद वनंकरणाल लोमभ्यस ते नखेभ्यः |यक्ष्मं सर्वस्माद आत्मनस तम इदं वि वृहामि ते ||
mehanād vanaṁkaraṇāl lomabhyas te nakhebhyaḥ | yakṣmaṁ sarvasmād ātmanas tam idaṁ vi vṛhāmi te ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • mehanād: From the great effort or strength
    • vanaṁkaraṇāl: By the creation of the forest
    • lomabhyas: From the hair or fur
    • te: To you
    • nakhebhyaḥ: From the nails
    • yakṣmaṁ: From disease or illness
    • sarvasmād: From all
    • ātmanas: Of the self or soul
    • tam: That
    • idaṁ: This
    • vi: Indeed
    • vṛhāmi: I make or I create
  • Interpretation:
    • This mantra speaks of invoking strength and protection from various sources of harm, including physical ailments and spiritual disturbances.
    • The reference to "great effort" suggests that overcoming challenges requires significant strength and determination.
    • The mention of "forest" symbolizes a place of refuge and healing, indicating that nature can be a source of strength and recovery.
    • The terms "hair" and "nails" may represent the physical body, emphasizing the need to protect oneself from illness and negativity.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In times of stress or illness, individuals often seek solace in nature, such as going for a walk in the woods, which can provide mental and physical rejuvenation.
    • People may engage in practices like yoga or meditation to strengthen their inner self and protect against negative energies, aligning with the mantra's call for self-protection.
    • Communities often come together to support one another during difficult times, reflecting the collective strength that can be drawn from unity, akin to the "great effort" mentioned in the mantra.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of inner strength, the healing power of nature, and the need for protection from various forms of harm.
    • Its significance lies in the reminder that we can draw upon our own resources and the support of our environment and community to overcome challenges.
    • In real life, this mantra can be applied in practices of self-care, community support, and seeking refuge in nature during times of difficulty.
