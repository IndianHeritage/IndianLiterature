दिवा चिदस्मै पृथिवी नमते शुष्माच्चिदस्य पर्वताभयन्ते |यः सोमपाः निचितो वज्रबाहुर्यो वज्रहस्तः स जनास इन्द्रः ||
dyāvā cid asmai pṛthivī namete śuṣmāc cid asya parvatā bhayante | yaḥ somapā nicito vajrabāhur yo vajrahastaḥ sa janāsa indraḥ ||
  • dyāvā cid asmai: This phrase translates to "the sky indeed bows down to him." It signifies the vastness and the reverence of the sky, which is often seen as a divine entity in many cultures. In real life, this can be related to the respect we give to nature and the universe, acknowledging its power and beauty.
  • pṛthivī namete: This means "the earth bows down to him." Here, the earth is personified, indicating that it yields to the greatness of a powerful being or force. In our lives, this can be interpreted as the respect we should have for our planet and the need to protect it, as it sustains all life.
  • śuṣmāc cid asya: This translates to "from the subtle, indeed, he is." It suggests that the essence of this being is found in the subtle aspects of existence. In practical terms, this can relate to the idea that true strength and wisdom often lie beneath the surface, in the intangible qualities of character and spirit.
  • parvatā bhayante: This means "the mountains are afraid." It implies that even the mightiest of natural formations recognize the power of this being. In real life, this can be seen in the way we often feel small in the presence of great natural wonders, reminding us of our place in the universe.
  • yaḥ somapā: This translates to "he who drinks the soma." Soma is often associated with immortality and divine knowledge in Vedic texts. This can be related to the pursuit of knowledge and enlightenment in our lives, emphasizing the importance of seeking wisdom.
  • nicito vajrabāhur: This means "with arms like thunder." It symbolizes strength and power. In real life, this can be seen in individuals who exhibit great resilience and determination, often overcoming challenges with their inner strength.
  • yo vajrahastaḥ: This translates to "he who has a hand like thunder." It reinforces the idea of immense power and capability. In practical terms, this can relate to leaders or individuals who have the ability to effect change and inspire others through their actions.
  • sa janāsa indraḥ: This means "he is the Indra of the people." Indra is a king of gods in Vedic mythology, representing authority and protection. This can be interpreted as the importance of leadership and the responsibility that comes with it, emphasizing the need for leaders to act for the welfare of their people.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the reverence for nature, the pursuit of knowledge, and the qualities of strength and leadership. It serves as a reminder of our connection to the universe and the responsibilities we hold towards our planet and society. In real life, it encourages us to respect nature, seek wisdom, and embody the qualities of strong and compassionate leadership, ultimately guiding us towards a harmonious existence with the world around us.
