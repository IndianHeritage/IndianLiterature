अग्निर ईशे बृहतो अध्वरस्याग्निर विश्वस्य हविषः कृतस्य |क्रतुं ह्य अस्य वसवो जुषन्ताथा देवाः दधिरे हव्यवाहम् ||
agnir īśe bṛhato adhvarasyāgnir viśvasya haviṣaḥ kṛtasya | kratuṁ hy asya vasavo juṣantāthā devā dadhire havyavāham ||
  • Agnir: This word refers to "Agni," the fire god in Vedic literature. Fire symbolizes purity, transformation, and the divine presence in rituals.
  • īśe: This means "I invoke" or "I worship." It indicates a call to the divine, expressing reverence and devotion.
  • bṛhato: This translates to "of the great" or "the vast." It signifies the greatness of the divine and the expansive nature of the universe.
  • adhvarasyāgnir: This phrase refers to "the fire of the sacrifice." It highlights the importance of fire in Vedic rituals, where it acts as a medium to connect the earthly realm with the divine.
  • viśvasya: Meaning "of all" or "universal," it emphasizes the inclusive nature of the divine and the interconnectedness of all beings.
  • haviṣaḥ: This word means "offerings" or "sacrifices." It represents the gifts and intentions we present to the divine.
  • kṛtasya: This translates to "that which is done" or "performed." It indicates the actions taken in the context of rituals and offerings.
  • kratuṁ: This means "intention" or "will." It signifies the mental and spiritual focus behind the offerings made to the divine.
  • hy: This is an emphatic particle, often translated as "indeed" or "certainly," reinforcing the statement that follows.
  • asya: Meaning "of this," it refers to the offerings and intentions being discussed.
  • vasavo: This refers to the "Vasus," a group of deities associated with various natural elements and prosperity.
  • juṣantā: This means "enjoying" or "delighting in." It indicates the pleasure derived from the offerings made to the divine.
  • athā: This translates to "then" or "therefore," linking the previous thoughts to the conclusion.
  • devā: This means "gods" or "deities," representing the divine forces that govern the universe.
  • dadhire: This translates to "they have established" or "they have upheld," indicating the active role of the deities in receiving and responding to offerings.
  • havyavāham: This means "the bearer of offerings." It signifies the divine's role in accepting and transforming our offerings into blessings.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the significance of fire (Agni) in rituals and the importance of intention behind offerings. It highlights the interconnectedness of all beings and the divine's role in receiving our sacrifices. In real life, this mantra can be applied to various situations where we seek to connect with the divine, whether through personal rituals, community gatherings, or even in moments of reflection and gratitude. By invoking the divine with sincerity and intention, we can foster a sense of connection, purpose, and harmony in our lives.
