ऋषे मन्त्रकृतां स्तोमैः कश्यपोद्वर्धयन गिरः |सोमं नमस्य राजानं यो जजñe वीरुधाम् पतिर इन्द्रायन्दो परि श्रव ||
ṛṣe mantrakṛtāṁ stomaiḥ kaśyapodvardhayan giraḥ | somaṁ namasya rājānaṁ yo jajñe vīrudhām patir indrāyendo pari srava ||
  • ṛṣe: This word refers to the sages or seers who have attained a deep understanding of the universe through meditation and spiritual practices. In real life, this can be likened to individuals who seek knowledge and wisdom through study and introspection.
  • mantrakṛtāṁ: This term means "those who create mantras" or "the makers of hymns." It emphasizes the importance of creativity and expression in spiritual practices. For example, poets and writers who craft meaningful verses can be seen as modern-day mantra creators.
  • stomaiḥ: This word refers to hymns or praises. In everyday life, this can be related to the act of expressing gratitude or appreciation, such as thanking someone for their help or celebrating achievements.
  • kaśyapodvardhayan: This phrase can be interpreted as "enhancing the glory of Kaśyapa," who is a revered sage in Hindu mythology. It signifies the importance of honoring and uplifting those who have contributed to knowledge and wisdom, similar to how we honor teachers and mentors in our lives.
  • giraḥ: This word means "words" or "speech." It highlights the power of communication and the impact of words in shaping our reality. For instance, motivational speakers use their words to inspire and uplift others.
  • somaṁ: Soma is a sacred drink in Vedic tradition, symbolizing immortality and divine inspiration. In contemporary terms, it can represent anything that nourishes the soul, such as art, music, or nature.
  • namasya: This term means "to bow down" or "to show respect." It reflects the importance of humility and reverence in our interactions with others, akin to how we show respect to elders or leaders.
  • rājānaṁ: This word means "kings" or "rulers." It signifies leadership and the responsibility that comes with it. In real life, this can be related to how leaders in various fields, such as politics or business, must act with integrity and wisdom.
  • yo jajñe: This phrase translates to "who was born" or "who has come into being." It emphasizes the idea of new beginnings and the potential for growth and transformation in life.
  • vīrudhām: This term means "of the plants" or "of the growth." It symbolizes fertility and abundance, akin to how we cultivate our gardens or nurture our relationships for growth.
  • patir: This word means "lord" or "master." It signifies authority and guidance, similar to how we look up to mentors or leaders for direction in our lives.
  • indrāyendo: This phrase refers to Indra, the king of gods, and "Indra" can symbolize strength and power. In real life, this can be related to the qualities we admire in leaders or role models.
  • pari srava: This term means "to flow all around" or "to spread." It signifies the idea of abundance and the sharing of resources, much like how communities come together to support one another.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the significance of wisdom, creativity, respect, and leadership in our lives. It teaches us to honor those who have contributed to our understanding and to express gratitude for the abundance we receive. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire us to cultivate knowledge, appreciate the beauty around us, and act with humility and integrity in our interactions. By embodying these principles, we can create a harmonious and prosperous environment for ourselves and others.
