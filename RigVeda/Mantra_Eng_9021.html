स्तोमं त इन्द्र विमदाऽजीजनन्न अपूर्व्यम् पुरुतमं सुदानवे |विद्मा ह्यस्य भोजनेनिस्य यदापशुं न गोपाः करामहे ||
stomaṁ ta indra vimadā ajījanann apūrvyam purutamaṁ sudānave | vidmā hy asya bhojanam inasya yad ā paśuṁ na gopāḥ karāmahe ||
  • stomaṁ: This word refers to a hymn or praise, often directed towards a deity. In real life, we can relate this to how people express gratitude or admiration through songs, poetry, or speeches.
  • ta: This is a pronoun meaning "that" or "those," indicating a reference to something significant, in this case, to Indra.
  • indra: Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic literature, representing strength, power, and the force of nature. In modern terms, we can think of leaders or influential figures who embody these qualities.
  • vimadā: This term can be interpreted as "delight" or "joy." In everyday life, this can be seen in moments of celebration or achievement, where joy is shared among people.
  • ajījanann: This means "having produced" or "having created." It reflects the idea of creation and innovation, akin to how new ideas or inventions come into being in our world.
  • apūrvyam: This word signifies something unprecedented or unique. In real life, we often encounter groundbreaking discoveries or experiences that change our perspective.
  • purutamaṁ: This translates to "the most ancient" or "the oldest." It can relate to traditions or wisdom passed down through generations, emphasizing the importance of heritage.
  • sudānave: This term refers to "the one who is well-fed" or "prosperous." In contemporary society, it can symbolize abundance and well-being, highlighting the importance of nourishment and care.
  • vidmā: This means "we know" or "we understand." It reflects the pursuit of knowledge and awareness, which is crucial in our quest for personal and collective growth.
  • hy: This is an emphatic particle, often used for emphasis, similar to saying "indeed" or "certainly." It underscores the importance of the statement that follows.
  • asya: This means "of this" or "of it," indicating possession or relation to something significant.
  • bhojanam: This translates to "food" or "nourishment." It highlights the essential role of sustenance in our lives, both physically and spiritually.
  • inasya: This term can mean "of the one who is nourished" or "of the one who is fed," emphasizing the relationship between nourishment and well-being.
  • yad: This means "that" or "which," introducing a clause that provides further information.
  • ā: This is a preposition meaning "from" or "out of," indicating a source or origin.
  • paśuṁ: This word refers to "cattle" or "animals," often symbolizing wealth and livelihood in agrarian societies.
  • na: This means "not," indicating negation or absence.
  • gopāḥ: This term refers to "herdsmen" or "protectors of cattle," symbolizing caretakers and guardians in society.
  • karāmahe: This means "we perform" or "we do," indicating action or intention.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of praise, creation, and nourishment in our lives. It reflects the interconnectedness of joy, knowledge, and prosperity, urging us to recognize and appreciate the sources of our sustenance and well-being. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied to our relationships with nature, our communities, and our pursuit of knowledge and innovation. By acknowledging the significance of these elements, we can foster a more harmonious and prosperous existence, much like the ancient wisdom encapsulated in the RigVeda.
