न यस्येन्द्रो वरुणो न मित्रो व्रतम् आर्यमाऽ न मिनन्ति रुद्रः |नारातयस् तं इदं स्वस्ति हुवे देवं सवितारं नमोभिः ||
na yasyendro varuṇo na mitro vratam aryamā na minanti rudraḥ | nārātayas tam idaṁ svasti huve devaṁ savitāraṁ namobhiḥ ||
  • Translation: "To whom Indra and Varuna do not belong, to whom Mitra and the vow of Aryama do not belong, to whom the Rudras do not harm, to that being I offer my salutations, the divine Savitar, with my prayers."
  • Key Deities:
    • Indra: The king of gods, associated with rain and thunderstorms, symbolizing strength and power.
    • Varuna: The god of cosmic order and water, representing moral authority and the upholder of truth.
    • Mitra: The god of friendship and contracts, embodying the principles of harmony and cooperation.
    • Aryama: A deity associated with the moral and ethical order, representing righteousness.
    • Rudra: A fierce god associated with storms and the hunt, often seen as a protector but also a destroyer.
    • Savitar: The sun god, representing illumination, life, and the source of energy.
  • Meaning and Context:
    • This mantra emphasizes the idea of seeking protection and blessings from a higher power, specifically Savitar, while acknowledging the roles of other deities.
    • The phrase "na yasyendro varuṇo" suggests a state of being that is beyond the influence of even the most powerful gods, indicating a quest for ultimate safety and peace.
    • In real life, this can be interpreted as a call for spiritual refuge, where one seeks to transcend worldly troubles and find solace in a higher truth.
    • For example, during challenging times such as personal loss or societal upheaval, individuals may recite this mantra to invoke a sense of divine protection and guidance.
  • Significance:
    • This mantra serves as a reminder of the importance of divine support in our lives, encouraging individuals to seek a connection with the divine.
    • It highlights the idea that true safety and peace come from a higher source, beyond the reach of earthly powers and conflicts.
    • In practical terms, this mantra can be used in daily prayers, meditation, or during rituals to invoke blessings and protection from the divine.
    • It can also be relevant in community gatherings, where collective prayers are offered for peace and harmony, reinforcing the bonds of friendship and cooperation.
