आग्ने वह वरुणमिष्टये न इन्द्रं दिवो मरुतो अन्तरिक्षात् |सीदन्तु बर्हिर विश्व आ यजत्राः स्वाहा देवाः अमृताः मादयन्ताम् ||
āgne vaha varuṇam iṣṭaye na indraṁ divo maruto antarikṣāt | sīdantu barhir viśva ā yajatrāḥ svāhā devā amṛtā mādayantām ||
  • Āgne: This word refers to Agni, the fire god, who is considered a vital force in Vedic rituals and symbolizes energy and transformation.
  • Vaha: This means "to carry" or "to bring forth," indicating a request for Agni to bring forth blessings.
  • Varuṇam: Varuna is the god of cosmic order and water, representing the moral and ethical aspects of the universe.
  • Iṣṭaye: This term means "for our worship" or "for our offerings," suggesting a plea for divine acceptance of the offerings made.
  • Na: This means "not," indicating a negation or a request for protection against negativity.
  • Indram: Indra is the king of the gods, associated with rain and thunderstorms, symbolizing strength and power.
  • Divo: This means "of the sky," referring to the celestial realm.
  • Maruto: The Maruts are storm deities, representing the forces of nature and the power of the wind.
  • Antarikṣāt: This means "from the atmosphere" or "from the space between heaven and earth," indicating a connection between the earthly and the divine.
  • Sīdantu: This term means "may they sit" or "may they be present," expressing a desire for the deities to be present during the ritual.
  • Barhir: This refers to the sacred grass or the altar, which is a central element in Vedic rituals.
  • Viśva: This means "all" or "universal," indicating inclusivity in the offerings and prayers.
  • Ā: This is a particle that can mean "to" or "for," indicating direction or purpose.
  • Yajatrāḥ: This refers to the participants in the ritual, the worshippers who are making offerings.
  • Svāhā: A sacred utterance used at the end of offerings, signifying the completion of the ritual.
  • Devā: This means "gods," referring to the divine beings being invoked.
  • Amṛtā: This means "immortal" or "eternal," indicating the divine nature of the gods.
  • Mādayantām: This means "may they be pleased" or "may they be joyful," expressing a wish for the deities to be satisfied with the offerings.

Conclusion: This mantra is a profound invocation that seeks the blessings of various deities, particularly Agni, Varuna, and Indra, during a ritual. It emphasizes the importance of divine presence and acceptance in the act of worship. In real life, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during personal prayers, community rituals, or significant life events like weddings and festivals. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of the natural and divine realms, encouraging individuals to seek harmony and blessings in their lives. The significance lies in the acknowledgment of divine forces that govern the universe and the importance of rituals in fostering a sense of community and spiritual connection.
