वृषाकपायि रेवति सुपुत्र आदु सुस्नुषे |घसत त इन्द्र उक्षणः प्रियं काचित्करं हविः विश्वस्माद इन्द्र उत्तरः ||
vṛṣākapāyi revati suputra ād u susnuṣe | ghasat ta indra ukṣaṇaḥ priyaṁ kācitkaraṁ havir viśvasmād indra uttaraḥ ||
  • vṛṣākapāyi: This term can be broken down into two parts: "vṛṣa" meaning "strength" or "bull" and "kapāyi" which can refer to "one who is capable" or "one who possesses." Together, it signifies a powerful entity or force, often associated with strength and vitality.
  • revati: This word translates to "prosperity" or "wealth." In a broader sense, it can also refer to auspiciousness and well-being, indicating a state of abundance and positive energy.
  • suputra: This term means "good son" or "virtuous offspring." It emphasizes the importance of righteous lineage and the values passed down through generations, highlighting the significance of nurturing good character in children.
  • ād u: This phrase can be interpreted as "and also" or "furthermore," indicating a continuation of thought or an addition to the previous ideas presented.
  • susnuṣe: This word means "to be praised" or "to be honored." It reflects the idea of recognition and respect, suggesting that the subject of the mantra is worthy of admiration.
  • ghasat: This term can be understood as "to consume" or "to partake." It implies an action of taking in or absorbing, which can relate to the idea of receiving blessings or nourishment.
  • ta indra: "Indra" is a significant deity in Vedic literature, often associated with rain, storms, and strength. The phrase "ta indra" can be interpreted as "that Indra," referring to the divine force that brings about change and transformation.
  • ukṣaṇaḥ: This word means "the one who brings forth" or "the one who causes to grow." It signifies a nurturing aspect, suggesting that the divine force is responsible for growth and prosperity.
  • priyaṁ: This term translates to "beloved" or "dear." It indicates affection and a close relationship, emphasizing the importance of love and connection in the context of the mantra.
  • kācitkaraṁ: This phrase can be interpreted as "something that is done" or "an action that is taken." It suggests the importance of deeds and actions in achieving desired outcomes.
  • havir: This word refers to offerings or sacrifices, often made in rituals. It signifies the act of giving and the importance of generosity in spiritual practices.
  • viśvasmād: This term means "from all" or "from the universe." It indicates a universal connection, suggesting that the blessings and energies come from a broader cosmic source.
  • indra uttaraḥ: This phrase can be understood as "Indra, the supreme" or "Indra, the highest." It emphasizes the exalted status of the divine force being invoked, highlighting its significance in the cosmic order.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of strength, prosperity, virtuous lineage, and divine blessings. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life and the importance of nurturing positive qualities in ourselves and our descendants. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as family gatherings, rituals for prosperity, or moments of reflection on personal growth. By invoking these qualities, individuals can strive for a life filled with strength, abundance, and meaningful connections, ultimately leading to a harmonious existence. The significance of this mantra lies in its ability to inspire individuals to cultivate virtues and seek blessings from the divine for a prosperous and fulfilling life.
