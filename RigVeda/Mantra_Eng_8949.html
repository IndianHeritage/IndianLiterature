पुषा त्वेतश्च्यावयतु प्र विद्वान् अनष्टपशुर भुवनस्य गोपाः |स त्वैतेभ्यः परि ददात पितृभ्योऽग्निर्देवभ्यः सुविदात्रियेब्यः ||
pūṣā tvetaś cyāvayatu pra vidvān anaṣṭapaśur bhuvanasya gopāḥ | sa tvaitebhyaḥ pari dadat pitṛbhyo 'gnir devebhyaḥ suvidatriyebhyaḥ ||
  • pūṣā: This word refers to the deity Pūṣan, who is associated with nourishment, prosperity, and the protection of cattle. In real life, this can be likened to the role of a guardian or protector in our communities, ensuring the well-being of those around us.
  • tvetaś: This term means "may you" or "you who." It signifies a direct address to the deity, invoking their presence and blessings.
  • cyāvayatu: This means "may he remove" or "may he drive away." It suggests the act of dispelling negativity or obstacles in life, akin to how we seek to eliminate challenges in our personal and professional lives.
  • pra vidvān: This translates to "the wise one" or "the knowledgeable one." It emphasizes the importance of wisdom and knowledge in guiding our actions and decisions, similar to how mentors or educators influence our paths.
  • anaṣṭapaśur: This term means "the one who has not lost his cattle." It symbolizes abundance and prosperity, reflecting the idea that one should strive to maintain their resources and wealth, much like how farmers work diligently to protect their livestock.
  • bhuvanasya: This means "of the world" or "of the universe." It indicates a connection to the larger cosmos, reminding us of our place within it and the interconnectedness of all beings.
  • gopāḥ: This word means "the protectors of cattle" or "herdsmen." It represents those who care for and nurture life, akin to parents or guardians in our lives who provide support and protection.
  • sa: This means "he" or "that one," referring back to the deity Pūṣan, reinforcing the focus on divine intervention.
  • tvaitebhyaḥ: This translates to "to these" or "to those," indicating a connection to the community or the collective.
  • pari dadat: This means "may he give" or "may he bestow." It signifies the act of sharing blessings and resources, much like how communities come together to support one another in times of need.
  • pitṛbhyo: This term means "to the ancestors." It highlights the importance of honoring our forebears and recognizing their contributions to our lives, similar to how we celebrate family traditions and legacies.
  • 'gnir: This refers to "fire," which symbolizes purification, transformation, and the divine presence. Fire is often used in rituals to invoke blessings and is a source of warmth and sustenance in our lives.
  • devebhyaḥ: This means "to the gods." It emphasizes the need for divine guidance and support in our endeavors, akin to how people seek spiritual guidance in their lives.
  • suvidatriyebhyaḥ: This translates to "to those who are well-disposed" or "to those who are benevolent." It signifies the importance of surrounding ourselves with positive influences and supportive individuals, much like how friendships and alliances shape our experiences.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of seeking divine protection, wisdom, and abundance in our lives. It emphasizes the interconnectedness of individuals within a community and the importance of honoring our ancestors and the divine. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during rituals for prosperity, community gatherings, or personal reflections on gratitude and support. By invoking the blessings of Pūṣan and recognizing the roles of protectors and wise individuals in our lives, we can foster a sense of unity, abundance, and well-being in our communities.
