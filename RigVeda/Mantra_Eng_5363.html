उक्त-उक्ते सोम इन्द्रं ममाद नीतhe-नीथे मगवाणं सुतासः |यद ईं सबाधः पितरं न पुत्राः समानदक्षाऽवसे हवन्ते ||
uktha-ukthe soma indram mamāda nīthe-nīthe maghavānaṁ sutāsaḥ | yad īṁ sabādhaḥ pitaraṁ na putrāḥ samānadakṣā avase havante ||
  • uktha-ukthe: This term refers to the act of chanting or reciting hymns. In a real-life context, this can be likened to the practice of prayer or meditation, where individuals recite mantras or affirmations to connect with a higher power or to focus their minds.
  • soma: Soma is a sacred drink mentioned in the Vedas, often associated with immortality and divine inspiration. In modern life, this can symbolize the pursuit of knowledge and enlightenment, akin to how people seek wisdom through education and personal growth.
  • indram: Indra is the king of the gods and represents strength and power. In everyday life, this can be seen in leaders or influential figures who guide and protect their communities, much like a mentor or a strong leader in a workplace.
  • mamāda: This term can be interpreted as "may I be blessed" or "may I receive." It reflects a desire for divine favor, similar to how individuals often seek blessings or good fortune in their endeavors, such as starting a new job or embarking on a significant life change.
  • nīthe-nīthe: This phrase suggests a call for guidance or direction. In practical terms, it can relate to seeking advice from trusted friends or mentors when faced with difficult decisions, emphasizing the importance of community and support.
  • maghavānaṁ: This refers to the powerful or mighty one, often associated with wealth and prosperity. In real life, this can be connected to the pursuit of success and abundance, whether in career, relationships, or personal achievements.
  • sutāsaḥ: This term means "sons" or "offspring." It highlights the importance of family and lineage, reflecting how individuals often strive to create a legacy or to nurture the next generation, whether through parenting or mentorship.
  • yad īṁ sabādhaḥ: This phrase can be interpreted as "whatever is beneficial." It emphasizes the pursuit of positive outcomes in life, akin to setting goals that align with one's values and aspirations.
  • pitaraṁ: This means "father" or "ancestor," symbolizing respect for one's heritage and the wisdom passed down through generations. In contemporary society, this can be seen in the importance of family traditions and the role of elders in guiding younger generations.
  • na putrāḥ: This translates to "not sons," which can imply a broader understanding of family beyond biological ties. It reflects the idea of community and chosen family, where support and love extend beyond blood relations.
  • samānadakṣā: This term suggests "those who are capable" or "those who are skilled." It highlights the value of competence and expertise in various fields, encouraging individuals to develop their skills and contribute meaningfully to society.
  • avase havante: This phrase can be interpreted as "they who are worthy" or "those who are deserving." It underscores the importance of merit and integrity in achieving success, resonating with the idea that hard work and dedication lead to rewards.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of seeking divine guidance, the importance of family and community, and the pursuit of knowledge and prosperity. In real-life scenarios, it serves as a reminder to value relationships, seek wisdom, and strive for personal growth. By embodying these principles, individuals can navigate life's challenges with strength and purpose, fostering a sense of connection and fulfillment in their journeys.
