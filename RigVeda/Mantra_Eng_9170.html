मन्त्र: दिविष्पृशं यज्ञं अस्माकं अश्विनाः जीराध्वरं कृतुं सुम्नमिष्टये | प्राचीनरश्मिं आहुतं घृतेन तद् देवानाम् अवो अद्य वृणीमहे ||
divispṛśaṁ yajñam asmākam aśvinā jīrādhvaraṁ kṛṇutaṁ sumnam iṣṭaye | prācīnaraśmim āhutaṁ ghṛtena tad devānām avo adyā vṛṇīmahe ||
  • Divispṛśaṁ: This term translates to "that which touches the heavens." It signifies the divine or celestial aspects of existence.
  • Yajñam: Refers to a sacrifice or offering, often associated with rituals that seek to please the gods and ensure harmony in the universe.
  • Asmākam: Means "our" or "belonging to us," indicating a collective identity and shared purpose among the practitioners.
  • Aśvinā: Refers to the Aśvins, the twin deities in Vedic mythology known for their healing and benevolent qualities.
  • Jīrādhvaraṁ: This can be interpreted as "the well-prepared sacrifice," emphasizing the importance of intention and preparation in rituals.
  • Kṛṇutaṁ: Means "may you make" or "may you create," invoking a sense of agency and action towards achieving a desired outcome.
  • Sumnam: Translates to "auspiciousness" or "well-being," highlighting the goal of the ritual to bring about positive results.
  • Iṣṭaye: Refers to "for the purpose of our desires," indicating that the offerings are made with specific intentions in mind.
  • Prācīnaraśmim: This term means "the ancient ray" or "the primordial light," symbolizing wisdom and enlightenment that has been passed down through generations.
  • Āhutaṁ: Means "that which has been invoked" or "offered," signifying the act of calling upon the divine through rituals.
  • Ghṛtena: Refers to "with ghee," which is a sacred substance in Vedic rituals, symbolizing purity and nourishment.
  • Tad: Means "that," referring to the offerings made to the deities.
  • Devānām: Translates to "of the gods," indicating the divine recipients of the offerings.
  • Avo: Means "may you come" or "may you approach," inviting the deities to accept the offerings.
  • Adyā: Refers to "today," emphasizing the immediacy and relevance of the ritual in the present moment.
  • Vṛṇīmahe: Means "we choose" or "we invoke," expressing the collective will of the practitioners to seek the blessings of the divine.

Conclusion: This mantra encapsulates the essence of Vedic rituals, emphasizing the importance of intention, preparation, and collective identity in seeking divine blessings. It serves as a reminder that our actions, when performed with sincerity and purpose, can invoke positive outcomes in our lives. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as during personal or communal ceremonies, where individuals come together to express gratitude, seek guidance, or celebrate significant life events. By invoking the divine through rituals, we align ourselves with higher principles and foster a sense of community and shared purpose.
