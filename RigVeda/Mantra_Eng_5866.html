ये वायव इन्द्रमादनास आदेवासो नितोषनासो आर्यः |घ्नन्तो वृत्राणि सूरिभिः श्याम सासह्वांसो युधा नृभिर अमित्रान् ||
ye vāyava indramādanāsa ādevāso nitośanāso aryaḥ | ghnanto vṛtrāṇi sūribhiḥ ṣyāma sāsahvāṁso yudhā nṛbhir amitrān ||
  • Translation: "Those who are like the wind, who are the Indra of the mind, the divine beings who are the protectors, who are noble; they strike down the Vritra (the forces of obstruction) with the strength of the heroes, and they engage in battle against the enemies of mankind."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • ye vāyava: "Those who are like the wind" - This refers to beings or forces that are dynamic, powerful, and capable of swift action.
    • indramādanāsa: "Indra of the mind" - Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic literature, symbolizing strength and leadership. Here, it suggests a powerful mental state or consciousness.
    • ādevāso: "divine beings" - Refers to celestial or divine entities that guide and protect.
    • nitośanāso: "protectors" - Those who safeguard and ensure the well-being of others.
    • aryaḥ: "noble" - Represents those who are virtuous and honorable.
    • ghnanto vṛtrāṇi: "strike down the Vritra" - Vritra symbolizes obstacles or challenges; this phrase indicates overcoming difficulties.
    • sūribhiḥ: "with the strength of heroes" - Emphasizes the valor and courage required to face challenges.
    • ṣyāma: "engage in battle" - The act of confronting and fighting against adversities.
    • sāsahvāṁso: "against the enemies" - Refers to the opposition faced in life.
    • yudhā nṛbhir amitrān: "battle against the enemies of mankind" - This highlights the collective struggle against forces that threaten humanity.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In personal development, individuals often face internal Vritras, such as self-doubt or fear. By cultivating a strong mindset (indramādanāsa), they can overcome these challenges.
    • In a community, leaders (ādevāso) work to protect and uplift their members, embodying the noble qualities (aryaḥ) that inspire others to act courageously.
    • In social justice movements, activists (sūribhiḥ) confront systemic issues (vṛtrāṇi) with bravery, striving to create a better society for all (yudhā nṛbhir amitrān).
  • Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of strength, nobility, and collective action in overcoming obstacles. It serves as a reminder that, like the wind, we must be dynamic and resilient in our pursuits. In real life, this mantra can inspire individuals and communities to confront challenges with courage and unity, fostering a spirit of protection and empowerment against adversities.
