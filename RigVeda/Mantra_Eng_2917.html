माध्यंदिनस्य सवनस्य धानाः पुरोळाशम् इन्द्र कृष्वेह चारुम् |प्र यत स्तोताऽ जरिता तूर्यर्थो वृषायमाण उप गीरभिर ईट्टे ||
mādhyaṁdinasya savanasya dhānāḥ puroḻāśam indra kṛṣveha cārum | pra yat stotā jaritā tūrṇyartho vṛṣāyamāṇa upa gīrbhir īṭṭe ||
  • mādhyaṁdinasya: This term refers to the "midday" or "noon" time, symbolizing the peak of energy and activity in the day.
  • savanasyā: This word relates to the "sacrifice" or "offering," indicating a ritualistic aspect where offerings are made to deities.
  • dhānāḥ: This translates to "wealth" or "prosperity," emphasizing the importance of abundance in life.
  • puroḻāśam: This term can be understood as "the one who is invoked," often referring to a deity or divine presence that is called upon during rituals.
  • indra: Indra is a prominent deity in Vedic literature, known as the god of rain and thunderstorms, symbolizing strength and power.
  • kṛṣveha: This can be interpreted as "to the one who is engaged in agriculture," highlighting the connection between divine blessings and agricultural prosperity.
  • cārum: Meaning "beautiful" or "pleasant," this word suggests the aesthetic and harmonious aspects of life.
  • pra yat: This phrase means "wherever" or "in whatever way," indicating a sense of universality in the invocation.
  • stotā: This refers to the "praise" or "hymn," emphasizing the act of worship and reverence towards the divine.
  • jaritā: This term means "the one who is invoked" or "the one who is called upon," reinforcing the idea of seeking divine assistance.
  • tūrṇyartho: This translates to "swiftly" or "quickly," suggesting the urgency and immediacy of the requests made in the mantra.
  • vṛṣāyamāṇa: This can be understood as "the one who brings forth," indicating the role of the divine in providing sustenance and support.
  • upa gīrbhir: This phrase means "with the utterances" or "with the words," highlighting the power of speech and prayer in invoking divine blessings.
  • īṭṭe: This translates to "I invoke" or "I call upon," emphasizing the personal connection and intention behind the prayer.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the essence of invoking divine blessings for prosperity, particularly in the context of agriculture and daily life. It highlights the importance of rituals and offerings in seeking abundance and support from the divine. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applicable during agricultural festivals, rituals for prosperity, or any situation where individuals seek divine intervention for success and well-being. The significance lies in the recognition of the interconnectedness between human efforts, divine blessings, and the natural world, encouraging a harmonious relationship with both the spiritual and material aspects of life.
