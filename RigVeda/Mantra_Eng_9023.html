इन्द्र सोमं इमं पिब मधुमन्तं चमू सुतम् |अस्मे रयिं न धारणय वि वो मदे सहस्रिणं पुरूवसो विवक्षसे ||
indra somam imam piba madhumantaṁ camū sutam | asme rayiṁ ni dhāraya vi vo made sahasriṇam purūvaso vivakṣase ||
  • Indra: Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, often associated with rain, thunderstorms, and war. He represents strength and power.
  • Somam: Soma refers to a sacred drink, often associated with immortality and divine inspiration. It symbolizes the essence of life and vitality.
  • Piba: This means "drink." It signifies the act of consuming or absorbing something beneficial.
  • Madhumantaṁ: This term translates to "sweet" or "pleasant." It indicates the enjoyable and nourishing qualities of the drink.
  • Camū: This refers to a "host" or "army." It symbolizes collective strength and unity.
  • Sutam: This means "born" or "produced." It signifies the outcome or result of a process.
  • Asme: This translates to "for us." It indicates a personal or collective need.
  • Rayiṁ: This means "wealth" or "prosperity." It symbolizes abundance and success.
  • Ni dhāraya: This means "hold" or "sustain." It signifies the act of maintaining or nurturing something valuable.
  • Vi vo: This translates to "for you." It indicates a sense of sharing or offering.
  • Made: This means "joy" or "delight." It signifies the emotional and spiritual upliftment that comes from abundance.
  • Sahasriṇam: This translates to "thousands" or "many." It indicates a vast quantity or multitude.
  • Purūvaso: This refers to a "noble" or "generous" person. It symbolizes the ideal qualities of leadership and benevolence.
  • Vivakṣase: This means "you desire" or "you wish." It signifies intention and aspiration.

The mantra essentially invokes Indra to partake in the sweet Soma, which is produced by the collective strength of the community. It emphasizes the importance of unity and shared prosperity, asking for the sustenance of wealth and joy for all. The desire for abundance is not just for oneself but for the collective, highlighting the interconnectedness of individuals within a society.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • Community Building: Just as the mantra calls for collective strength, communities can thrive when individuals come together to support one another, sharing resources and joy.
  • Leadership: Leaders can embody the qualities of Purūvaso by being generous and noble, ensuring that their actions benefit the larger group rather than just themselves.
  • Celebration of Abundance: The act of sharing food and drink during festivals can be seen as a modern-day interpretation of the Soma ritual, where joy and prosperity are celebrated collectively.

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the importance of unity, shared prosperity, and the joy that comes from collective efforts. It encourages individuals to seek not just personal gain but to uplift their communities, fostering a spirit of generosity and collaboration.
