इन्द्राय सोम परि षिच्यसे नृभिर नृचक्षाऽ ऊर्मिः कविर अज्यसे वने |पूर्वीर हि ते श्रुतयः सन्ति यातवे सहस्रं अश्वा हरयश्चमूषदः ||
indrāya soma pari ṣicyase nṛbhir nṛcakṣā ūrmiḥ kavir ajyase vane | pūrvīr hi te srutayaḥ santi yātave sahasram aśvā harayaś camūṣadaḥ ||
  • Indrāya: This refers to Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, often associated with rain, thunder, and war.
  • Soma: Soma is a sacred drink in Vedic tradition, symbolizing immortality and divine inspiration.
  • Pari ṣicyase: This means "you are being poured" or "you are being anointed," indicating a ritualistic offering.
  • Nṛbhir: This translates to "by men" or "by humans," emphasizing the role of humanity in the worship of the divine.
  • Nṛcakṣā: This means "with human eyes," suggesting a connection between the divine and human perception.
  • Ūrmiḥ: This word means "wave" or "flow," often symbolizing the flow of energy or consciousness.
  • Kavir: This translates to "the wise one" or "the poet," indicating the importance of wisdom and creativity in understanding the divine.
  • Ajyase: This means "you are being offered," referring to the act of giving or sacrifice.
  • Vane: This translates to "in the forest," symbolizing nature and the wilderness, where divine experiences can occur.
  • Pūrvīr: This means "of the ancients" or "of the past," connecting the present with the wisdom of previous generations.
  • Hi: This is an emphatic particle, often used for emphasis in Vedic texts.
  • Te: This means "your," referring to the divine or the gods.
  • Srutayaḥ: This translates to "the heard" or "the revealed," indicating knowledge that has been passed down through tradition.
  • Yātave: This means "to the one who travels" or "to the one who moves," suggesting a dynamic aspect of the divine.
  • Sahasram: This translates to "thousands," indicating abundance or a multitude of offerings.
  • Aśvā: This means "horses," often symbolizing strength, speed, and vitality.
  • Harayaś: This translates to "the bringers" or "the carriers," indicating those who bring forth blessings.
  • Camūṣadaḥ: This means "of the armies," suggesting a collective effort or community in worship.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the importance of ritual, community, and the connection between the divine and humanity. It highlights how offerings made to Indra through the sacred Soma can invoke blessings and wisdom. In real life, this can be seen in various cultural practices where communities come together to celebrate, offer gratitude, and seek divine guidance, such as during festivals, communal prayers, or even in personal moments of reflection and gratitude. The significance lies in recognizing the interconnectedness of all beings and the importance of honoring the past while seeking wisdom for the future.
