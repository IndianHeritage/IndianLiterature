एते धावन्तीन्दवः सोमाऽ इन्द्राय घृष्वयः |मत्सरासः स्वरविदः ||
ete dhāvantīndavaḥ somā indrāya ghṛṣvayaḥ | matsarāsaḥ svarvidaḥ ||
  • Translation: "These flowing (or rushing) ones, the Somas, are for Indra, the one who is strong. They are the ones who are full of desire and know the essence of the heavens."

  • Word Breakdown:
    • ete: These
    • dhāvantīndavaḥ: Flowing or rushing ones
    • somā: Somas (a sacred drink in Vedic tradition)
    • indrāya: For Indra (the king of gods, associated with strength and rain)
    • ghṛṣvayaḥ: The one who is strong or powerful
    • matsarāsaḥ: Full of desire or ambition
    • svarvidaḥ: Those who know the essence of the heavens

  • Contextual Explanation:
    • The mantra speaks of the Somas, which are not just a drink but symbolize the essence of life and vitality. In real life, this can be compared to the pursuit of knowledge and experiences that enrich our lives.
    • Indra represents strength and leadership. In modern contexts, this can relate to leaders in various fields who harness their strength to guide others, much like a CEO leading a company or a coach guiding a sports team.
    • The phrase "full of desire" can be seen in individuals who are ambitious and strive for success, such as entrepreneurs who work tirelessly to achieve their dreams.
    • "Those who know the essence of the heavens" can be likened to scientists and thinkers who seek to understand the universe, contributing to advancements in technology and philosophy.

  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of ambition, strength, and the pursuit of knowledge. It encourages individuals to strive for their goals while being aware of the greater universe around them.
    • In real life, it serves as a reminder that our desires and ambitions should be aligned with a deeper understanding of our purpose and the world we inhabit.
    • Ultimately, it signifies the interconnectedness of our aspirations with the cosmic order, urging us to act with intention and awareness in our pursuits.
