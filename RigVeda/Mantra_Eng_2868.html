द्याम इन्द्रो हरिधायसम पृथिवीं हरिवार्पसम् |अधारयद् धरितोर भूरे भोजनेम् ययोः अन्तर हरिष्चरत् ||
dyām indro haridhāyasam pṛthivīṁ harivarpasam | adhārayad dharitor bhūri bhojanaṁ yayor antar hariś carat ||
  • dyām - This word refers to the sky or the celestial realm. It symbolizes the vastness and the infinite possibilities that exist beyond our immediate perception.
  • indro - Indra is the king of the gods in Vedic literature, often associated with strength, power, and the ability to overcome obstacles. He represents leadership and authority.
  • haridhāyasam - This term can be interpreted as the one who possesses a golden or radiant body, symbolizing purity, brilliance, and divine qualities.
  • pṛthivīṁ - This word means the earth, representing the physical realm, stability, and the foundation of life.
  • harivarpasam - This can be understood as the one who has a beautiful or auspicious form, indicating attractiveness and positive qualities.
  • adhārayad - This term means to support or uphold, suggesting the importance of stability and strength in life.
  • dhara - This refers to the earth or the ground, emphasizing the connection to our physical existence.
  • bhūri - This word means abundant or plentiful, indicating richness and prosperity.
  • bhojanaṁ - This translates to food or nourishment, highlighting the necessity of sustenance for life.
  • yayor - This term means "of which" or "of whom," linking the previous concepts together.
  • antar - This means "within" or "inside," suggesting introspection and the inner journey.
  • hariś - This refers to the divine or the sacred, indicating a connection to spirituality.
  • carat - This means "moving" or "acting," emphasizing the dynamic nature of existence.

The mantra speaks to the relationship between the celestial and the earthly realms, highlighting the importance of divine support in our lives. It suggests that just as Indra, the king of gods, upholds the earth and provides nourishment, we too must seek strength and support from higher powers while remaining grounded in our physical existence.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios:

  • In leadership roles, one can draw inspiration from Indra's qualities, striving to be a strong and supportive figure for others.
  • In times of difficulty, individuals can look to the vastness of the sky (dyām) for hope and possibilities, reminding themselves that challenges can be overcome.
  • In nurturing relationships, the emphasis on nourishment (bhojanaṁ) can serve as a reminder to provide emotional and physical support to loved ones.

In conclusion, this mantra encapsulates the significance of divine support, the importance of grounding oneself in reality, and the need for nourishment in all aspects of life. It encourages individuals to seek strength from higher powers while remaining connected to the earth and the people around them, fostering a balanced and fulfilling existence.
