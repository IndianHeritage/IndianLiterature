एतं तृतस्य योषणो हरिं हिन्वन्त्य अद्रिभिः |इन्दुम इन्द्राय पीतये ||
etaṁ tritasya yoṣaṇo hariṁ hinvanty adribhiḥ | indum indrāya pītaye ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • etaṁ: this
    • tritasya: of the third
    • yoṣaṇo: of the woman
    • hariṁ: the one who is yellow or golden, often referring to the divine or the sun
    • hinvanty: they who bring or carry
    • adribhiḥ: with stones or mountains
    • indum: the moon
    • indrāya: for Indra, the king of gods
    • pītaye: for drinking or to drink
  • Interpretation:
    • This mantra speaks of a divine connection, where the third woman (possibly representing a divine feminine aspect) is bringing forth the golden essence (often associated with prosperity or divinity) with the help of mountains or stones.
    • The mention of the moon and Indra suggests a relationship between the celestial and the earthly, where the moon is offered to Indra, symbolizing nourishment and sustenance.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In agriculture, the idea of bringing forth prosperity can be likened to farmers who rely on the mountains (land) to cultivate crops that are nourished by the moon's cycles, which affect planting and harvesting times.
    • In a spiritual context, this mantra can be seen as a call for divine blessings, where individuals seek to connect with higher powers (like Indra) for guidance and support in their endeavors.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra emphasizes the importance of divine connection and the interplay between nature and spirituality.
    • It serves as a reminder of the need to honor the forces of nature and the divine in our lives, encouraging us to seek balance and harmony.
    • In modern life, this can be applied in practices such as mindfulness, meditation, and rituals that acknowledge the interconnectedness of all beings and the environment.
