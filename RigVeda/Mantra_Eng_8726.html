अपो वसानाḥ परि कोशम् अर्षतीन्दुर हियानाḥ सोत्रिभिः |जनयञ ज्योतिर मन्दना अवीवशद गाः कृत्वानो न निर्णिजम् ||
apo vasānaḥ pari kośam arṣatīndur hiyānaḥ sotṛbhiḥ | janayañ jyotir mandanā avīvaśad gāḥ kṛṇvāno na nirṇijam ||
  • apo vasānaḥ: This phrase can be translated as "the waters that flow." In a real-life context, this can refer to the importance of water in sustaining life, agriculture, and the environment. For example, rivers and lakes are vital for drinking water, irrigation, and supporting ecosystems.
  • pari kośam: This means "surrounding the treasure." In life, this can symbolize the protection of valuable resources, whether they are material wealth, knowledge, or relationships. For instance, a family may work together to safeguard their home and values, creating a nurturing environment.
  • arṣatīndur: This translates to "the moon that shines." The moon is often associated with calmness and guidance. In real life, it can represent the guidance we receive from mentors or the inspiration we find in art and nature. For example, a teacher can illuminate a student's path, much like the moonlight in darkness.
  • hiyānaḥ sotṛbhiḥ: This phrase means "with the help of the wise." It emphasizes the importance of wisdom and knowledge in achieving goals. In practical terms, this can be seen in how people seek advice from experts or elders when making significant life decisions, such as career choices or personal relationships.
  • janayañ jyotir: This translates to "creating light." Light symbolizes knowledge, enlightenment, and positivity. In everyday life, this can be reflected in the pursuit of education and self-improvement. For instance, a community may come together to support a local library, fostering a culture of learning.
  • mandanā avīvaśad: This means "the beautiful and the auspicious." It signifies the importance of beauty and positivity in our lives. In real life, this can be seen in how people decorate their homes or celebrate festivals, creating an atmosphere of joy and harmony.
  • gāḥ kṛṇvāno na nirṇijam: This translates to "the cows that are not lost." Cows are often seen as symbols of wealth and nourishment in many cultures. This phrase can represent the idea of maintaining what is valuable and ensuring that resources are not wasted. For example, sustainable farming practices help preserve the land and livestock for future generations.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the interconnectedness of nature, wisdom, and the pursuit of beauty and knowledge. It teaches us to value our resources, seek guidance, and create a nurturing environment for ourselves and others. In real life, these principles can be applied in various scenarios, such as environmental conservation, community building, and personal development. By embodying the essence of this mantra, we can lead more fulfilling and harmonious lives.
