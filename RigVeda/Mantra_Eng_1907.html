प्र वां शरद्वान वृषभो न निष्शाट पूर्वीर इषश्चरति मध्वा इष्णन | एवैर अन्यस्य पीपयन्त वाजैर वेषन्तीर ऊर्ध्वा नद्यो न आगुः ||
pra vāṁ śaradvān vṛṣabho na niṣṣāṭ pūrvīr iṣaś carati madhva iṣṇan | evair anyasya pīpayanta vājair veṣantīr ūrdhvā nadyo na āguḥ ||
  • pra vāṁ: This phrase can be interpreted as "to you" or "for you," indicating a direction of intention or offering.
  • śaradvān: This word refers to the "autumn" or "the season of plenty," symbolizing abundance and prosperity.
  • vṛṣabho: Meaning "the bull," which is often associated with strength, power, and fertility in various cultures.
  • na niṣṣāṭ: This translates to "not to be restrained" or "not to be held back," suggesting freedom and the ability to move forward without obstacles.
  • pūrvīr: This term means "the ancients" or "the elders," referring to wisdom passed down through generations.
  • iṣaś: This word can mean "desires" or "wishes," indicating the aspirations or goals one may have.
  • carati: Translated as "moves" or "travels," it signifies progress or the journey of life.
  • madhva: This word means "sweetness" or "honey," symbolizing the rewards of life and the sweetness of experiences.
  • iṣṇan: This can be interpreted as "to be desired" or "to be sought after," emphasizing the importance of pursuing one's goals.
  • evair: Meaning "with these," it suggests a collective or communal aspect to the actions being described.
  • anyasya: This translates to "of another" or "of others," indicating the interconnectedness of individuals in a community.
  • pīpayanta: This word means "nourishing" or "sustaining," highlighting the importance of support and care within relationships.
  • vājair: Referring to "the steeds" or "the horses," which symbolize speed, power, and the ability to overcome challenges.
  • veṣantīr: This can be interpreted as "those who are adorned" or "those who are decorated," suggesting beauty and the importance of presentation.
  • ūrdhvā: Meaning "upward" or "higher," it signifies aspirations and the pursuit of higher goals.
  • nadyo: This word means "rivers," symbolizing the flow of life and the journey of experiences.
  • na āguḥ: Translated as "do not come," indicating a sense of permanence or stability in the context of the mantra.

Conclusion: This mantra from the RigVeda encapsulates themes of abundance, strength, freedom, wisdom, and the interconnectedness of life. It emphasizes the importance of pursuing one's desires while being supported by a community. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied to personal growth, where individuals strive for their goals while also nurturing relationships with others. The imagery of rivers and the upward journey reflects the continuous flow of life and the aspiration to reach greater heights. This mantra serves as a reminder of the balance between individual aspirations and communal support, highlighting the significance of both in achieving a fulfilling life.
