प्र ये जाताः महिना ये च नु स्वयम् प्र विद्मना ब्रुवता एवयामरुत् |क्रत्वा तद् वो मरुतो नाधृषे शवो दानां मह्ना तद् एषां अधृष्टासो नाद्रयः ||
pra ye jātā mahinā ye ca nu svayam pra vidmanā bruvata evayāmarut | kratvā tad vo maruto nādhṛṣe śavo dānā mahnā tad eṣām adhṛṣṭāso nādrayaḥ ||
  • Translation: "Those who are born from the great (or powerful) ones, and those who speak with knowledge, we know them. O Maruts, with your strength, do not let the dead (or the lifeless) be without offerings; may we not be deprived of the great gifts of those who are unseen."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • pra ye jātā: "Those who are born" - Refers to beings or entities that have emerged from a powerful source.
    • mahinā: "from the great" - Indicates a connection to greatness or power, possibly referring to divine or natural forces.
    • ye ca nu svayam: "and those who speak with knowledge" - Suggests the importance of wisdom and understanding in communication.
    • pra vidmanā: "we know them" - Acknowledges the recognition of these knowledgeable beings.
    • bruvata: "who speak" - Emphasizes the act of speaking or conveying knowledge.
    • evayāmarut: "O Maruts" - Invokes the Maruts, the storm deities associated with strength and vitality.
    • kratvā: "with your strength" - A call for the Maruts to use their power.
    • tad vo maruto: "do not let the dead" - A plea to the Maruts regarding the offerings to the deceased.
    • nādhṛṣe: "be without offerings" - Highlights the importance of offerings in maintaining a connection with the divine and the ancestors.
    • śavo dānā: "the dead" - Refers to those who have passed away, emphasizing the need for remembrance and respect.
    • mahnā: "great gifts" - Represents the blessings and benefits that come from honoring the deceased.
    • tad eṣām adhṛṣṭāso: "of those who are unseen" - Refers to the ancestors or spirits that are not visible but are still present.
    • nādrayaḥ: "may we not be deprived" - A wish to not lose out on the blessings from the unseen forces.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In many cultures, rituals are performed to honor ancestors, ensuring that their spirits are remembered and that they continue to bless the living. For instance, during the Day of the Dead in Mexico, families create altars to honor their deceased loved ones, offering food and flowers.
    • In Hindu traditions, Shradh ceremonies are conducted to pay homage to ancestors, reflecting the belief that the dead require offerings for their peace and to ensure their blessings upon the family.
    • In modern contexts, the mantra can be seen as a reminder of the importance of knowledge and wisdom in our interactions, encouraging us to seek understanding and communicate effectively.
  • Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the significance of honoring the powerful forces of nature and the wisdom of those who have come before us. It serves as a reminder of our connection to the past and the importance of maintaining relationships with both the seen and unseen. In real life, this can manifest through rituals, respect for ancestors, and the pursuit of knowledge, ultimately guiding us to live with awareness and gratitude for the gifts we receive from those who have shaped our existence.
