शतानीकाः हेतयोऽस्य दुष्टरा इन्द्रस्य समिषो महीः |गिरिर्न भुज्मा मगवत्सु पिन्वते यद् ईं सुताः अमन्दिषुः ||
śatānīkā hetayo asya duṣṭarā indrasya samiṣo mahīḥ | girir na bhujmā maghavatsu pinvate yad īṁ sutā amandiṣuḥ ||
  • śatānīkā: This term refers to "hundreds" or "many," indicating a multitude or a large number of something.
  • hetayo: This means "causes" or "reasons," suggesting that there are underlying factors or motivations behind actions or events.
  • asya: Translated as "of this," it points to something specific that is being discussed or referred to.
  • duṣṭarā: This word means "difficult" or "hard to overcome," indicating challenges or obstacles that may be present.
  • indrasya: Referring to "Indra," the king of the gods in Vedic literature, symbolizing power, strength, and authority.
  • samiṣo: This term can be interpreted as "of the mighty" or "of the strong," reinforcing the idea of strength and capability.
  • mahīḥ: Meaning "earth" or "greatness," it signifies the vastness of the world or the importance of the earth in the context of life.
  • girir: This translates to "mountain," symbolizing stability, permanence, and the challenges that come with great heights.
  • na: A negation, meaning "not," indicating the absence of something.
  • bhujmā: This term means "to be consumed" or "to be devoured," suggesting a sense of loss or sacrifice.
  • maghavatsu: Referring to "the powerful" or "the mighty," often associated with Indra, emphasizing strength and capability.
  • pinvate: This means "to enjoy" or "to take pleasure in," indicating a sense of fulfillment or satisfaction.
  • yad: Translated as "that" or "which," it refers to something specific that is being highlighted.
  • īṁ: This can be interpreted as "the essence" or "the core," suggesting a fundamental quality or characteristic.
  • sutā: Meaning "sons" or "offspring," indicating lineage or the continuation of legacy.
  • amandiṣuḥ: This term means "in the joyous" or "in happiness," suggesting a state of bliss or contentment.

The mantra speaks to the complexities of life, the challenges we face, and the strength required to overcome them. It highlights the importance of understanding the causes behind our struggles and the role of powerful forces, like Indra, in navigating these challenges. The imagery of mountains and the earth signifies stability and the foundational aspects of life that we must engage with.


In real life, this mantra can be applied to various situations where individuals face difficulties, such as in personal relationships, career challenges, or societal issues. For instance, when someone is striving to achieve a goal, they may encounter numerous obstacles (duṣṭarā) that require them to seek the strength (indrasya) to persevere. Understanding the reasons (hetayo) behind these challenges can lead to better strategies for overcoming them.


In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the resilience required in the face of adversity. It encourages individuals to recognize the multitude of factors at play in their lives and to draw upon their inner strength and the support of powerful forces around them. By doing so, one can navigate life's challenges with a sense of purpose and joy, ultimately leading to personal growth and fulfillment.
