अप्तूर्ये मरुत आपीर एषो 'मन्दन्न इन्द्रं अनु दातिवाराः |तेभिः साकं पिबतु वृत्रखादः सुतं सोमं दाशुषः स्वे सधस्थे ||
aptūrye maruta āpir eṣo 'mandann indram anu dātivārāḥ | tebhiḥ sākam pibatu vṛtrakhādaḥ sutaṁ somaṁ dāśuṣaḥ sve sadhasthe ||
  • aptūrye: This term can be understood as "in abundance" or "fullness." It signifies the idea of having enough or more than enough, which is a common aspiration in human life.
  • maruta: Refers to the Maruts, the storm deities in Vedic literature. They symbolize strength, power, and the forces of nature. In real life, this can be likened to the unpredictable yet powerful forces of nature that can bring both destruction and renewal.
  • āpir: This word means "to flow" or "to pour." It suggests the idea of abundance flowing into life, much like water from a river that nourishes the land.
  • eṣo: Translates to "this" or "he," indicating a specific focus or subject in the mantra.
  • 'mandann: This term can be interpreted as "with food" or "nourishing." It emphasizes the importance of sustenance, both physical and spiritual, in our lives.
  • indram: Refers to Indra, the king of the gods and the god of rain and thunderstorms. Indra represents leadership, courage, and the ability to overcome obstacles, akin to a leader in a community or organization.
  • anu: Means "after" or "following," indicating a sequence or a cause-and-effect relationship.
  • dātivārāḥ: This can be understood as "those who give" or "the givers." It highlights the importance of generosity and sharing in human relationships.
  • tebhiḥ: Translates to "with them," referring to the Maruts and their qualities.
  • sākam: Means "together" or "in unity," emphasizing the importance of collaboration and community.
  • pibatu: This term means "may they drink," suggesting the act of receiving or partaking in something beneficial.
  • vṛtrakhādaḥ: Refers to "the slayer of Vritra," a metaphor for overcoming obstacles and challenges in life. Vritra represents ignorance or obstacles that hinder progress.
  • sutaṁ: Means "son" or "offspring," symbolizing the fruits of one's labor or the results of one's actions.
  • somaṁ: Refers to Soma, a sacred drink in Vedic tradition that symbolizes immortality and divine inspiration.
  • dāśuṣaḥ: This term can be interpreted as "of the giver" or "of the generous," reinforcing the theme of generosity.
  • sve: Means "in one's own" or "in one's own place," indicating a sense of belonging and identity.
  • sadhasthe: Translates to "in the place of well-being," suggesting a state of health, prosperity, and happiness.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the importance of abundance, generosity, and overcoming obstacles through unity and collaboration. It highlights the role of divine forces in our lives, encouraging us to seek nourishment—both physical and spiritual. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied in community building, leadership, and personal growth. By embodying the qualities of the Maruts and Indra, we can strive for a life filled with abundance, support, and the ability to overcome challenges, ultimately leading to a state of well-being and fulfillment.
