मह उग्राय तवसे सुवृक्तिम प्रेयर शिवतमाय पश्वः |गिर्वाहसे गिर इन्द्राय पूर्वीर धेहि तन्वे कुविद अङ्ग वेदत ||
maha ugrāya tavase suvṛktim preraya śivatamāya paśvaḥ | girvāhase gira indrāya pūrvīr dhehi tanve kuvid aṅga vedat ||
  • maha ugrāya: This phrase translates to "O great and powerful one." It signifies the immense strength and might of the divine. In real life, this can be related to individuals or forces that exhibit great power, such as leaders or natural phenomena like storms.
  • tavase: This means "to your strength" or "to your power." It emphasizes the importance of harnessing and recognizing one's own strength. For example, athletes train to enhance their physical power, showcasing the value of personal strength in achieving goals.
  • su-vṛktim: This translates to "good speech" or "excellent utterance." It highlights the significance of wise and positive communication. In everyday life, effective communication can lead to better relationships and understanding among people.
  • preraya: This means "inspire" or "motivate." It calls for divine inspiration to guide actions. In a real-world context, mentors and leaders inspire others to achieve their potential, much like how teachers motivate students to excel.
  • śivatamāya: This translates to "to the most auspicious" or "to the best." It signifies the pursuit of the highest good. In life, this can be seen in the efforts of individuals striving for ethical and moral excellence in their actions.
  • paśvaḥ: This means "to the animals" or "to the creatures." It reflects a connection to all living beings. In modern society, this can relate to animal rights and the importance of treating all creatures with respect and compassion.
  • girvāhase: This translates to "to the one who is praised" or "to the one who is celebrated." It emphasizes the importance of recognition and honor. In real life, this can be seen in how we celebrate achievements, whether in sports, academics, or community service.
  • gira indrāya: This means "to Indra, the lord of speech." Indra represents power and authority. In practical terms, this can relate to leaders or influential figures who shape public discourse and opinion.
  • pūrvīr: This translates to "from the ancients" or "from the predecessors." It signifies the wisdom passed down through generations. In real life, this can be seen in how traditions and cultural practices are preserved and respected.
  • dhehi: This means "bestow" or "grant." It is a call for blessings and support. In everyday life, this can relate to seeking help from others, whether in personal or professional contexts.
  • tanve: This translates to "to the body" or "to the physical form." It emphasizes the importance of physical well-being. In modern life, this can be related to health and fitness, highlighting the need for a healthy lifestyle.
  • kuvid: This means "to the unwise" or "to the ignorant." It reflects the need for guidance and wisdom. In real life, this can be seen in educational efforts aimed at enlightening those who lack knowledge.
  • aṅga vedat: This translates to "the limbs of knowledge." It signifies the various aspects of understanding and wisdom. In practical terms, this can relate to the interdisciplinary approach in education, where different fields of knowledge are interconnected.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of strength, wisdom, and communication in achieving the highest good. It calls for inspiration and guidance from the divine, recognizing the interconnectedness of all beings. In real life, these principles can be applied in various contexts, such as personal development, leadership, and community engagement. By embodying these values, individuals can contribute positively to society and foster a harmonious existence.
