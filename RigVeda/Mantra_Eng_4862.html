मन्त्र: भरद्वाजायाव धुक्षत द्विता | धेनुं च विश्वदोहसम इषं च विश्वभोजसम ||
bharadvājāyāva dhukṣata dvitā | dhenuṁ ca viśvadohasam iṣaṁ ca viśvabhojasam ||
  • Bharadvājāyāva: This term refers to the sage Bharadvāja, who is known for his wisdom and knowledge in Vedic literature. In a real-life context, this can symbolize the pursuit of knowledge and the importance of learning from wise individuals.
  • Dhukṣata: This word can be interpreted as "to be nourished" or "to be cultivated." In practical terms, it signifies the importance of nurturing relationships, ideas, and personal growth. For example, just as a farmer cultivates his crops, individuals must cultivate their skills and relationships for success.
  • Dvitā: This term means "twice" or "twofold." It can represent the duality in life, such as the balance between material and spiritual pursuits. In real life, this can be seen in the need to balance work and personal life, ensuring that neither is neglected.
  • Dhenuṁ: This word translates to "cow," which is a symbol of wealth and nourishment in Vedic culture. In modern terms, it can represent the importance of sustainable resources and the need to care for our environment, as cows provide milk and other resources essential for life.
  • Viśvadohasam: This term can be understood as "universal nourishment." It emphasizes the interconnectedness of all beings and the importance of providing for one another. In real life, this can be reflected in community support systems, where individuals come together to help those in need.
  • Iṣaṁ: This word means "desire" or "wish." It highlights the significance of having aspirations and goals in life. For instance, setting personal or professional goals can drive individuals to achieve greater heights and contribute positively to society.
  • Viśvabhojasam: This term translates to "universal sustenance." It signifies the idea that sustenance should be available to all, promoting equality and shared resources. In a real-life context, this can relate to social justice movements that advocate for equal access to food, education, and healthcare.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of knowledge, nourishment, balance, and universal support. It teaches us that by cultivating our skills and relationships, aspiring for our goals, and ensuring that resources are shared equitably, we can create a harmonious and prosperous society. In today's world, these principles can guide us in our personal lives, community interactions, and broader societal contributions, fostering a sense of unity and collective well-being.
