मन्त्र: अन्तर दूतो रोधसी दसम ईयते होता निषत्तो मनुष्यः पुरोहितः |क्षयम् बृहन्तम् परि भूषति द्युभिर देवैभिर अग्निर इषितो धियावसुः ||
antar dūto rodasī dasma īyate hotā niṣatto manuṣaḥ purohitaḥ | kṣayam bṛhantam pari bhūṣati dyubhir devebhir agnir iṣito dhiyāvasuḥ ||
  • antar dūto rodasī dasma īyate: This phrase can be translated as "the inner messenger moves between the two worlds." Here, "antar" means inner, "dūto" refers to a messenger, "rodasī" signifies the two realms or worlds, and "dasma īyate" implies movement or transition. In real life, this can be likened to the role of communication in bridging gaps between different perspectives or cultures, much like how a diplomat or mediator operates between nations.
  • hotā niṣatto manuṣaḥ purohitaḥ: This translates to "the priest, the human, who is unimpeded, calls upon the divine." "Hotā" refers to the priest or one who invokes, "niṣatto" means unimpeded or free, "manuṣaḥ" signifies human, and "purohitaḥ" indicates a spiritual guide or priest. This can be related to the role of leaders or spiritual guides in society who facilitate communication with higher ideals or values, much like community leaders who inspire and guide their followers.
  • kṣayam bṛhantam pari bhūṣati: This means "the great one adorns the destruction." "Kṣayam" refers to destruction or decline, "bṛhantam" means great or vast, and "pari bhūṣati" implies adorning or embellishing. This can be interpreted in real life as the idea that from destruction or chaos, new opportunities and growth can arise, similar to how natural disasters can lead to community rebuilding and resilience.
  • dyubhir devebhir agnir iṣito dhiyāvasuḥ: This translates to "the fire, controlled by the gods, is the source of wisdom." "Dyubhir" refers to the heavens or divine, "devebhir" means by the gods, "agnir" signifies fire, "iṣito" implies controlled or directed, and "dhiyāvasuḥ" means the source of wisdom or knowledge. In practical terms, this can be seen in how knowledge and enlightenment often arise from intense experiences or challenges, much like how fire is used to forge metal into tools and weapons, symbolizing transformation and growth.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the interconnectedness of communication, spirituality, destruction, and wisdom. It signifies the importance of bridging gaps between different realms of existence, whether they be cultural, spiritual, or personal. In real life, it teaches us that effective communication and guidance can lead to enlightenment, and that from destruction, new beginnings can emerge. This mantra is applicable in various scenarios, such as conflict resolution, community rebuilding after disasters, and personal growth through challenges. It serves as a reminder of the cyclical nature of life and the potential for transformation through understanding and wisdom.
