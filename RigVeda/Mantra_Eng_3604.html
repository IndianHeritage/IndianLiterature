इन्द्रज्येष्ठान बृहद्भ्यः पर्वतेभ्यः क्षयाम् एभ्यः सुवसि पस्त्यावतः |यथा-यथा पतयन्तो वियेमिर एविव तस्थुः सविताḥ सवाय ते ||
indrajyeṣṭhān bṛhadbhyaḥ parvatebhyaḥ kṣayām̐ ebhyaḥ suvasi pastyāvataḥ | yathā-yathā patayanto viyemira evaiva tasthuḥ savitaḥ savāya te ||
  • Indrajyeṣṭhān: This term refers to the highest or most powerful aspects of Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic literature. Indra is often associated with strength, rain, and thunder.
  • Bṛhadbhyaḥ: This word means "from the great ones" or "from the vast." It signifies the importance of drawing strength and wisdom from those who are greater or more knowledgeable.
  • Parvatebhyaḥ: This translates to "from the mountains." Mountains are often seen as symbols of stability, permanence, and strength in many cultures.
  • Kṣayām̐: This term means "to destroy" or "to remove." It indicates a desire to eliminate obstacles or negativity.
  • Ebhaḥ: This word can be interpreted as "these" or "those," referring to the entities or forces being addressed in the mantra.
  • Suvasi: This means "well-being" or "prosperity." It emphasizes the importance of health and abundance in life.
  • Pastyāvataḥ: This term refers to "the one who is nourished" or "the one who provides sustenance." It highlights the role of nurturing and support in achieving well-being.
  • Yathā-yathā: This phrase means "as" or "in the manner that." It suggests a conditional or situational aspect to the following actions.
  • Patayanto: This translates to "flying" or "moving swiftly." It can symbolize progress, movement, or the pursuit of goals.
  • Viyemira: This term can be interpreted as "to be established" or "to be firm." It indicates a sense of stability and grounding.
  • Tasthuḥ: This means "may they stand" or "may they remain." It expresses a wish for permanence and endurance.
  • Savitaḥ: This refers to "the sun" or "the solar deity." The sun is often associated with life, energy, and illumination.
  • Savāya: This term means "to you" or "for you," indicating a personal connection or offering.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the importance of drawing strength from powerful sources, such as the divine and nature, to overcome obstacles and achieve well-being. It highlights the interconnectedness of life, where the support of greater forces leads to personal growth and stability. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied in various contexts, such as seeking guidance from mentors (bṛhadbhyaḥ), finding strength in nature (parvatebhyaḥ), and fostering a sense of community and support (pastyāvataḥ) to navigate challenges. The mantra serves as a reminder to remain grounded and connected to the sources of strength and nourishment in our lives, ultimately leading to a more prosperous and fulfilling existence.
