दश राजानः समिता अयज्यवः सुदासम् इन्द्रावरुणा न युयुधुः |सत्याः नृणाम अद्मसदाम उपस्तुतिर देवाः एषाम अभवन देवहूतिषु ||
daśa rājānaḥ samitā ayajyavaḥ sudāsam indrāvaruṇā na yuyudhuḥ | satyā nṛṇām admasadām upastutir devā eṣām abhavan devahūtiṣu ||
  • daśa rājānaḥ - "ten kings": This phrase refers to a coalition of ten kings who came together for a common purpose. In real life, this can be likened to various alliances formed in politics or business where multiple leaders unite to achieve a shared goal.
  • samitā - "assembled": This indicates that these kings have gathered together, emphasizing the importance of unity and collaboration. For example, in modern times, international summits or conferences where leaders come together to discuss global issues reflect this concept.
  • ayajyavaḥ - "who performed sacrifices": This highlights the significance of rituals and offerings in ancient times. In contemporary society, this can be compared to acts of charity or community service, where individuals or groups contribute to the welfare of others.
  • sudāsam - "Sudasa": This refers to a specific king, Sudasa, who is often seen as a symbol of righteousness and virtue. In real life, leaders who prioritize ethical governance and social justice can be seen as modern-day Sudasas.
  • indrāvaruṇā - "Indra and Varuna": These are two significant deities in Vedic literature, representing power and cosmic order. Their mention signifies the divine support for the righteous. In today's context, this can be related to the idea of moral authority and the belief that just actions are supported by higher principles.
  • na yuyudhuḥ - "did not fight": This phrase suggests that despite their power, these kings chose not to engage in conflict. This can be paralleled with modern conflict resolution strategies where dialogue and negotiation are preferred over violence.
  • satyā nṛṇām - "truth among men": This emphasizes the importance of truthfulness and integrity in human interactions. In everyday life, this can be seen in the value placed on honesty in personal relationships and professional environments.
  • admasadām - "of those who dwell in homes": This refers to the common people, highlighting that the actions of the kings impact the lives of ordinary citizens. In real life, this can be related to how government policies affect the daily lives of individuals and families.
  • upastutir - "praise": This indicates the act of honoring or acknowledging the contributions of others. In modern society, this can be seen in awards, recognitions, and appreciation events that celebrate achievements in various fields.
  • devā eṣām - "the gods of these": This phrase suggests that the divine is involved in the affairs of these kings and their people. In contemporary terms, this can be interpreted as the belief that higher values and ethics guide human actions.
  • abhavan devahūtiṣu - "became divine offerings": This signifies that the actions and sacrifices of these kings were seen as offerings to the divine. In real life, this can be related to the idea that selfless acts contribute to the greater good and are recognized as virtuous.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of leadership, unity, and righteousness. It highlights the importance of collaboration among leaders, the value of truth and integrity, and the impact of their actions on society. In today's world, this mantra serves as a reminder of the significance of ethical governance, conflict resolution, and the need for leaders to prioritize the welfare of their people. By embodying these principles, individuals and leaders alike can contribute to a more harmonious and just society.
