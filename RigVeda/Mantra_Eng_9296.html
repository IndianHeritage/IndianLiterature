अभीदं एकं एकोऽस्मि निष्शाल अभिद्वा किमु त्रयः करोति |खले न पर्षाण प्रति हन्मि भूरी किम् मा निन्दन्ति शत्रवोऽनिन्द्राः ||
abhī3dam ekam eko asmi niṣṣāḻ abhī dvā kim u trayaḥ karanti | khale na parṣān prati hanmi bhūri kim mā nindanti śatravo 'nindrāḥ ||
  • Translation: "I am this one, I am the only one; I do not fear the duality of existence. What do the three do? I do not harm the stones; what do the enemies, who are not to be criticized, do?"
  • Word-by-Word Explanation:
    • abhī3dam: This refers to "this" or "this one," indicating a sense of singularity and unity.
    • ekam: Meaning "one," it emphasizes the idea of oneness in existence.
    • eko asmi: Translates to "I am the only one," reinforcing the concept of individuality and uniqueness.
    • niṣṣāḻ: This term suggests a state of fearlessness or lack of concern.
    • dvā: Meaning "two," it introduces the idea of duality in existence.
    • kim u: Translates to "what do," posing a question about the actions of others.
    • trayaḥ: Meaning "three," it may refer to the three aspects of existence or consciousness.
    • karanti: This means "do" or "act," indicating the actions taken by others.
    • khale: Refers to "stones," symbolizing something that is unyielding or unchangeable.
    • na: Meaning "not," it negates the action of harming.
    • parṣān: This term also refers to "stones," emphasizing their steadfastness.
    • prati: Meaning "against," indicating a direction of action.
    • hanmi: Translates to "I harm," suggesting an action that is being negated.
    • bhūri: Meaning "abundant" or "many," it refers to the multitude of enemies or challenges.
    • kim: Again, meaning "what," posing another question.
    • mā: Meaning "do not," it serves as a negation.
    • nindanti: This means "criticize" or "speak ill of," referring to the actions of enemies.
    • śatravo: Refers to "enemies," indicating those who oppose or challenge.
    • 'nindrāḥ: Meaning "who are not to be criticized," suggesting a deeper understanding of the nature of opposition.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In a workplace setting, an individual may feel unique and confident in their abilities (ekam) despite the presence of competition (dvā). They choose not to engage in harmful actions against their colleagues (na parṣān prati hanmi), focusing instead on their own growth.
    • In personal relationships, one may encounter conflicts (bhūri) but choose to rise above criticism (mā nindanti) and maintain a sense of self-worth and integrity.
    • In spiritual practices, the mantra can be a reminder of the unity of existence, encouraging individuals to embrace their individuality while recognizing the interconnectedness of all beings.
  • Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of recognizing one's individuality and the fearlessness that comes with it. It teaches that while there may be dualities and challenges in life, one should not engage in harmful actions against others. Instead, it encourages a focus on self-awareness and personal growth. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts, such as personal development, conflict resolution, and fostering a sense of community, reminding us to rise above negativity and embrace our unique paths.
