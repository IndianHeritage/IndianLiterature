पप्राथ क्षाम महि दंसो व्य उर्वीम् उप द्यां ऋष्वो बृहद इन्द्र स्तभायः |अधारयो रोदसी देवपुत्रे प्रात्ने मातरा यह्वी ऋतस्य ||
paprātha kṣām mahi daṁso vy u1rvīm upa dyām ṛṣvo bṛhad indra stabhāyaḥ | adhārayo rodasī devaputre pratne mātarā yahvī ṛtasya ||
  • paprātha: This term can be understood as "to spread" or "to extend." In a real-life context, it can refer to the act of sharing knowledge or resources, much like how communities come together to support one another in times of need.
  • kṣām: This word signifies "patience" or "forbearance." In everyday life, patience is crucial in various situations, such as waiting for a job opportunity or dealing with personal challenges.
  • mahi: Meaning "great" or "earth," this term can symbolize the vastness of the universe and our place within it. For example, when we look at the night sky, we are reminded of our smallness in comparison to the cosmos.
  • daṁso: This can be interpreted as "the one who is fierce" or "the one who protects." In real life, this could relate to individuals who stand up for justice or protect their communities, much like activists or leaders.
  • vy u1rvīm: This phrase can be translated as "to the vastness" or "to the expanse." It reflects the idea of reaching out beyond oneself, akin to how people strive for personal growth and exploration.
  • upa dyām: Meaning "towards the sky" or "to the heavens," this can symbolize aspirations and goals. For instance, pursuing higher education or personal dreams can be seen as reaching for the sky.
  • ṛṣvo: This term refers to "the wise" or "the seers." In real life, it can represent mentors or guides who provide wisdom and insight, helping others navigate their paths.
  • bṛhad: Meaning "great" or "vast," this word emphasizes the importance of grand ideas and visions. For example, movements for social change often start with a big idea that inspires many.
  • indra: This refers to the deity Indra, often associated with strength and leadership. In a modern context, it can symbolize strong leaders who inspire and protect their people.
  • stabhāyaḥ: This term means "to stabilize" or "to support." In real life, this can relate to the support systems we build, such as family, friends, or community organizations that help us through tough times.
  • adhārayo: This word signifies "to uphold" or "to support." It can be seen in the context of individuals or institutions that uphold values and ethics in society.
  • rodasī: Meaning "the two worlds" or "the heavens and the earth," this can symbolize the connection between the spiritual and the material realms, much like how we balance our spiritual beliefs with everyday life.
  • devaputre: This translates to "son of the gods," representing divine qualities or aspirations. In real life, it can refer to individuals who embody noble traits and inspire others.
  • pratne: This term means "to invoke" or "to call upon." It reflects the act of seeking help or guidance, similar to how people pray or meditate for clarity and support.
  • mātarā: Meaning "mother," this word symbolizes nurturing and care. In real life, it can represent the unconditional love and support that mothers provide to their children.
  • yahvī: This term can be interpreted as "the one who is strong" or "the one who is powerful." It can relate to individuals who demonstrate resilience in the face of adversity.
  • ṛtasya: Meaning "of truth" or "of order," this word emphasizes the importance of living in harmony with the truth. In real life, it can relate to ethical living and integrity in our actions.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of strength, support, wisdom, and the pursuit of truth. It encourages individuals to spread knowledge, be patient, and strive for greatness while upholding values and nurturing relationships. In real life, these principles can guide us in our personal and communal endeavors, reminding us of the interconnectedness of our actions and the importance of supporting one another in our journeys.
