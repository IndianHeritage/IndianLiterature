ततृदानाḥ सिन्धवः क्षोदसाऽ रजः प्र सस्रुर् धेनवो यथा |स्यन्ना अश्वा इवाध्वनो विमोचने विह यद् वर्तन्त एण्यः ||
tatṛdānāḥ sindhavaḥ kṣodasā rajaḥ pra sasrur dhenavo yathā | syannā aśvā ivādhvano vimocane vi yad vartanta enyaḥ ||
  • tatṛdānāḥ: This term can be interpreted as "those who give" or "the givers." It emphasizes the importance of generosity and sharing in society.
  • sindhavaḥ: Refers to "the rivers" or "the waters." In a broader sense, it symbolizes the flow of life, abundance, and nourishment that rivers provide.
  • kṣodasā: This word can mean "with strength" or "powerful." It suggests the vitality and energy that comes from the natural world.
  • rajaḥ: Translated as "dust" or "particles," it signifies the material aspects of life and the earth's resources.
  • pra sasrur: This phrase means "they have flowed forth" or "they have emerged." It indicates the movement and dynamism of life and nature.
  • dhenavo: Refers to "the cows" or "nourishing beings." Cows are often seen as symbols of wealth and sustenance in Vedic culture.
  • yathā: This word means "as" or "like," drawing a comparison to illustrate a point.
  • syannā: This can be interpreted as "may there be" or "let there be," expressing a desire or intention.
  • aśvā: Refers to "horses," which symbolize strength, speed, and vitality in Vedic literature.
  • ivādhvano: This means "in the path" or "on the way," indicating a journey or a process of movement.
  • vimocane: Translated as "in liberation" or "in release," it signifies the act of freeing oneself from constraints.
  • vi: This word can mean "in" or "with," often used to indicate a relationship or connection.
  • yad: Meaning "that" or "which," it refers to something specific that is being discussed.
  • vartanta: This means "they exist" or "they are present," indicating the ongoing nature of existence.
  • enyaḥ: This can be interpreted as "the others" or "those who are different," suggesting a sense of community or collective existence.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the interconnectedness of life, the importance of generosity, and the dynamic flow of nature. It highlights how the givers (like rivers and cows) nourish and sustain life, while also acknowledging the strength and vitality that comes from these natural elements. In real life, this mantra can be applied to the principles of sustainability, community support, and the importance of sharing resources. It serves as a reminder that our actions, like the flowing rivers, have the power to impact others positively, encouraging a harmonious existence with nature and each other.
