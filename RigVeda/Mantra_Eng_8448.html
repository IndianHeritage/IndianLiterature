मनीषिभिः पवते पूर्व्यः कविर नृभिर यतः परि कोशाम् अचिक्रदत् |तृतस्य नाम जनयन मधु क्षरद इन्द्रस्य वायोः सख्याय कर्तवे ||
manīṣibhiḥ pavate pūrvyaḥ kavir nṛbhir yataḥ pari kośām̐ acikradat | tritasya nāma janayan madhu kṣarad indrasya vāyoḥ sakhyāya kartave ||
  • manīṣibhiḥ: This term refers to the wise or learned individuals, those who possess deep understanding and insight.
  • pavate: This means "to blow" or "to move," often associated with the wind or air, symbolizing the force of nature.
  • pūrvyaḥ: This translates to "from the east," indicating a direction that is often associated with new beginnings or the rising sun.
  • kavir: This word means "the poet" or "the seer," someone who has the ability to perceive deeper truths and express them through words.
  • nṛbhir: This refers to "men" or "humans," emphasizing the connection between divine forces and human beings.
  • yataḥ: This means "from where," indicating a source or origin.
  • pari kośām̐: This phrase can be interpreted as "the surrounding space" or "the enveloping atmosphere," suggesting a holistic view of existence.
  • acikradat: This means "to create" or "to manifest," indicating the act of bringing something into being.
  • tritasya: This refers to "of the third," which can symbolize a triad or a significant third element in a process.
  • nāma: This means "name," indicating the importance of identity and recognition.
  • janayan: This translates to "to generate" or "to produce," emphasizing the act of creation.
  • madhu: This means "honey," often symbolizing sweetness, nourishment, and the rewards of life.
  • kṣarad: This means "flowing" or "spilling," indicating abundance and the natural flow of resources.
  • indrasya: This refers to "of Indra," the king of gods in Vedic tradition, symbolizing power and authority.
  • vāyoḥ: This means "of the wind," connecting the divine with the natural elements.
  • sakhyāya: This translates to "for friendship" or "for companionship," emphasizing the importance of relationships.
  • kartave: This means "to be done" or "to be accomplished," indicating action and purpose.

In summary, this mantra speaks to the interconnectedness of wisdom, nature, and human experience. It highlights the role of learned individuals in harnessing the forces of nature and the divine to create and nurture life. The reference to Indra and the wind suggests that these forces are not only powerful but also essential for fostering relationships and community.


Significance and Real-Life Application:

This mantra can be applied in various real-life scenarios, such as:

  • Leadership: Just as wise individuals guide others, effective leaders in any field must harness their knowledge and insights to inspire and create positive change.
  • Environmental Awareness: The connection to nature reminds us of the importance of respecting and nurturing our environment, much like the wind that sustains life.
  • Community Building: The emphasis on friendship and companionship highlights the need for collaboration and support within communities to achieve common goals.

Ultimately, this mantra serves as a reminder of the power of wisdom, the importance of nature, and the value of human connections in creating a harmonious and fulfilling life.
