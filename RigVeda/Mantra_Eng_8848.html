प्र केतुनā बृहता यात्य अग्निर आ रोडसी वृषभो रोरवीति |दिवश चिद अन्ताम् उपमाम् उद आनल् अपाम् उपस्थे महिषो ववर्ध ||
pra ketunā bṛhatā yāty agnir ā rodasī vṛṣabho roravīti | divaś cid antām̐ upamām̐ ud ānaḻ apām upasthe mahiṣo vavardha ||
  • pra ketunā: This phrase can be interpreted as "with the banner" or "with the standard." It signifies leadership and guidance, much like a flag bearer in a battle who leads the troops.
  • bṛhatā: Meaning "great" or "vast," this word emphasizes the importance of magnitude and significance in the context of the universe or existence.
  • yāty: Translated as "goes" or "moves," it indicates a journey or progression, suggesting that the subject is in motion towards a goal.
  • agnir: This word means "fire," which in Vedic literature often symbolizes energy, transformation, and purity. Fire is a crucial element in rituals and represents the divine presence.
  • ā rodasī: This can be understood as "to the sky" or "to the heavens," indicating a connection between the earthly realm and the divine or celestial.
  • vṛṣabho: Meaning "the bull," this term symbolizes strength, power, and virility. In many cultures, the bull is a revered animal representing fertility and abundance.
  • roravīti: This word can be interpreted as "roars" or "cries," suggesting a powerful expression of energy or emotion, akin to the roar of a lion or the sound of thunder.
  • divaś: Meaning "of the sky" or "heavenly," this word reinforces the connection to the celestial and the divine.
  • cid: This translates to "consciousness" or "awareness," indicating a state of being that is aware and enlightened.
  • antām̐: Meaning "the end" or "the ultimate," this word suggests a culmination or final destination.
  • upamām̐: This can be interpreted as "comparison" or "analogy," indicating a relationship or similarity between different entities or concepts.
  • ud ānaḻ: Meaning "rising" or "ascending," this phrase suggests growth, elevation, and progress towards a higher state of being.
  • apām: This word means "of the waters," indicating a connection to the element of water, which symbolizes life, nourishment, and purification.
  • upasthe: Translated as "in the lap" or "in the embrace," this suggests a nurturing or protective aspect, akin to a mother holding her child.
  • mahiṣo: Meaning "the great one" or "the powerful," this term emphasizes strength and authority.
  • vavardha: This translates to "grew" or "increased," indicating growth, development, and the flourishing of life.

  • Significance: This mantra encapsulates the essence of transformation, strength, and the connection between the earthly and the divine. It highlights the journey of life, the importance of consciousness, and the nurturing aspects of nature.
  • Real-life Application: In our daily lives, we can draw parallels to this mantra by recognizing the importance of leadership (pra ketunā) in our personal and professional journeys. Just as fire (agnir) transforms and purifies, we can embrace change and growth in our lives. The nurturing aspect (upasthe) reminds us of the importance of community and support systems that help us flourish (vavardha).

  • Conclusion: The mantra serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of all elements in life. It encourages us to embrace our journeys, recognize our strengths, and seek growth while remaining aware of the divine presence in our lives. By applying these principles, we can lead more fulfilling and purposeful lives.
