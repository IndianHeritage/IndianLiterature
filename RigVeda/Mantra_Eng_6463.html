अच्युताऽचिद्वोऽजन्मन्नानन्दति पर्वतासो वनस्पतिः |भूमिर्यामेषु रेजते ||
acyutā cid vo ajmann ā nānadati parvatāso vanaspatiḥ | bhūmir yāmeṣu rejate ||
  • Translation: The mantra can be translated as: "The imperishable (Acyuta) is the essence of your being; it rejoices in the mountains and the trees. The earth shines in the seasons."
  • Word Breakdown:
    • Acyutā: Refers to the imperishable or eternal aspect of existence, often associated with the divine or the unchanging reality.
    • Cid: Means consciousness or awareness, indicating the presence of a higher understanding.
    • Vo: Translates to "your," indicating a personal connection to the divine essence.
    • Ajmann: Refers to the essence or core of being, suggesting a deeper understanding of existence.
    • Ānānāditi: Means to rejoice or celebrate, indicating a sense of joy in existence.
    • Parvatāso: Refers to mountains, symbolizing stability and strength in nature.
    • Vanaspatiḥ: Refers to trees or plant life, representing growth and sustenance.
    • Bhūmir: Means earth, symbolizing grounding and stability.
    • Yāmeṣu: Refers to seasons, indicating the cyclical nature of life.
    • Rejate: Means to shine or radiate, suggesting vitality and energy.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • The concept of Acyuta can be seen in the way people find strength in their beliefs during challenging times, reflecting the eternal aspect of hope and resilience.
    • Mountains and trees are often places where individuals seek solace and connection with nature, illustrating the joy and peace that can be found in the natural world.
    • The earth's cycles, such as the changing seasons, remind us of the impermanence of life and the beauty that comes with each phase, encouraging us to appreciate the present moment.
  • Conclusion: The significance of this mantra lies in its reminder of the eternal essence within us and the joy that can be found in nature and the cycles of life. It encourages us to connect with our inner selves and the world around us, fostering a sense of peace and appreciation for the present. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in practices such as meditation, mindfulness, and environmental conservation, promoting a harmonious relationship with ourselves and the earth.
