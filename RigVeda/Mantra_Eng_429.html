घ्नन्तो वृत्रम अतरण rodasī अप उरु क्षयाय चक्रिरे |भुवत कण्वे वृषा dyumny आहुतः क्रन्दद अश्वो गविष्टिषु ||
ghnanto vṛtram ataran rodasī apa uru kṣayāya cakrire | bhuvat kaṇve vṛṣā dyumny āhutaḥ krandad aśvo gaviṣṭiṣu ||
  • Word-by-Word Meaning:
    • ghnanto: slaying or defeating
    • vṛtram: Vritra, a serpent or dragon representing chaos and obstruction
    • ataran: they crossed or overcame
    • rodasī: the two worlds or realms (often referring to the sky and the earth)
    • apa: away or apart
    • uru: wide or vast
    • kṣayāya: for destruction or annihilation
    • cakrire: they created or established
    • bhuvat: existing or being
    • kaṇve: in the context of the Kanva family or lineage
    • vṛṣā: the strong or powerful
    • dyumny: in glory or fame
    • āhutaḥ: invoked or called upon
    • krandad: cried out or shouted
    • aśvo: the horse, symbolizing strength and speed
    • gaviṣṭiṣu: in the pastures or fields
  • Interpretation:
    • This mantra speaks of the victory over chaos and obstacles, represented by Vritra, who is often seen as a force of drought and obstruction.
    • The act of slaying Vritra symbolizes overcoming challenges in life, whether they be personal, societal, or spiritual.
    • The reference to the two worlds (rodasī) indicates the connection between the physical and spiritual realms, suggesting that overcoming obstacles has implications in both areas.
    • The invocation of strength (vṛṣā) and glory (dyumny) highlights the importance of courage and honor in facing life's challenges.
    • The imagery of the horse (aśvo) in the fields (gaviṣṭiṣu) can be seen as a metaphor for freedom and the pursuit of goals, emphasizing the need for strength and speed in achieving one's aspirations.
  • Real-Life Examples:
    • In personal development, individuals often face internal Vritras, such as fear and self-doubt. Overcoming these can lead to personal growth and achievement.
    • In a professional context, businesses may encounter market challenges (Vritra) that require innovative solutions (slaying Vritra) to thrive and expand.
    • In social movements, activists often confront systemic obstacles (Vritra) and must rally strength and unity (vṛṣā) to bring about change.
  • Conclusion:
    • This mantra serves as a powerful reminder of the importance of overcoming obstacles in various aspects of life.
    • Its significance lies in the encouragement to confront and defeat the challenges that hinder progress, whether they are internal fears or external societal issues.
    • By invoking the strength and glory associated with overcoming Vritra, individuals and communities can find inspiration to pursue their goals and aspirations with courage and determination.
