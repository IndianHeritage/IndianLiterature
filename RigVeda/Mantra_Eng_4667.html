इन्द्रो यज्वाने पृणते च शिक्षति उपेद ददाति न स्वं मुषायति |भूयो-भूयो रयिमिदस्य वर्धयन्न अभिन्ने खिल्ये न दधाति देवयुम् ||
indro yajvane pṛṇate ca śikṣaty uped dadāti na svam muṣāyati | bhūyo-bhūyo rayim id asya vardhayann abhinne khilye ni dadhāti devayum ||
  • Indro: Refers to Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, symbolizing strength, power, and leadership.
  • Yajvane: Pertains to the act of sacrifice or offering, which is a central theme in Vedic rituals.
  • Pṛṇate: Means to nourish or fulfill, indicating the act of providing sustenance or support.
  • Śikṣaty: Translates to teaching or instructing, emphasizing the importance of knowledge and guidance.
  • Uped: Means to give or bestow, highlighting the act of sharing resources or blessings.
  • Dadāti: Also means to give, reinforcing the theme of generosity.
  • Na svam muṣāyati: Implies that one does not hoard or keep for oneself, promoting the idea of selflessness.
  • Bhūyo-bhūyo: Means repeatedly or continuously, suggesting the ongoing nature of blessings and prosperity.
  • Rayim: Refers to wealth or resources, indicating material and spiritual abundance.
  • Id asya: Means this (wealth) belongs to him, indicating ownership and responsibility.
  • Vardhayann: Means to increase or grow, emphasizing the idea of expansion and prosperity.
  • Abhinne: Refers to undivided or whole, suggesting unity and integrity.
  • Khilye: Means in the midst of, indicating a presence or involvement in the community.
  • Ni dadhāti: Means to establish or set down, indicating a foundation or grounding.
  • Devayum: Refers to the divine or godly aspect, suggesting a connection to higher powers.

The mantra emphasizes the importance of leadership, generosity, and the continuous sharing of resources. It highlights the role of Indra as a provider and protector, who nourishes and teaches, while also encouraging selflessness and community involvement. The repeated blessings and wealth are not just for personal gain but are meant to be shared for the greater good.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • In leadership roles, where a leader is expected to guide, support, and uplift their team or community.
  • In charitable actions, where individuals or organizations share their resources with those in need, embodying the spirit of selflessness.
  • In educational settings, where knowledge is imparted generously, fostering a culture of learning and growth.

In conclusion, this mantra serves as a reminder of the virtues of leadership, generosity, and community. It encourages individuals to act selflessly, share their blessings, and contribute to the welfare of others, thereby creating a harmonious and prosperous society.
