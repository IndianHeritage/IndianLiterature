रूपं-रूपम् मगवाऽबोभवीति मायाः कृत्वानस् तन्मं परि स्वाम् |त्रिर्यद् दिवः परि मुहूर्तम् आगात् स्वैर मन्त्रैर अनृतुपा ऋतावा ||
rūpaṁ-rūpam maghavā bobhavīti māyāḥ kṛṇvānas tanva1m pari svām | trir yad divaḥ pari muhūrtam āgāt svair mantrair anṛtupā ṛtāvā ||
  • rūpaṁ-rūpam: This phrase can be translated as "form upon form" or "appearance upon appearance." It suggests the idea of multiple manifestations or layers of reality. In real life, this can be seen in how people present themselves differently in various situations, such as being formal at work but casual with friends.
  • maghavā: This term refers to the powerful or mighty one, often associated with deities or forces of nature. In a contemporary context, it can symbolize influential figures in society, such as leaders or innovators who shape our world.
  • bobhavīti: This can be interpreted as "to become" or "to manifest." It reflects the process of transformation and growth. For example, a student becoming a professional in their field illustrates this concept of evolution and change.
  • māyāḥ: This word means "illusion" or "deception." It highlights the transient nature of reality and how perceptions can be misleading. In everyday life, social media often creates a façade that can mislead individuals about others' lives, emphasizing the importance of looking beyond appearances.
  • kṛṇvānas: This term means "creating" or "making." It signifies the act of bringing something into existence. In real life, this can relate to artists, entrepreneurs, or anyone who brings their ideas to fruition, showcasing creativity and innovation.
  • tanva1m: This can be interpreted as "body" or "form." It emphasizes the physical aspect of existence. In a practical sense, it reminds us of the importance of taking care of our physical health and well-being.
  • pari svām: This phrase means "around oneself" or "surrounding." It suggests the idea of being enveloped by one's environment or circumstances. In real life, this can relate to how our surroundings influence our thoughts and behaviors, such as the impact of a supportive community on personal growth.
  • trir yad divaḥ: This translates to "three times in the sky." It can symbolize the cyclical nature of life and the universe. For instance, the changing seasons reflect this cyclical pattern, reminding us of the constant flow of time and change.
  • pari muhūrtam: This means "around a moment" or "in a moment." It emphasizes the significance of time and the fleeting nature of experiences. In daily life, it can remind us to cherish moments, as they are often temporary and precious.
  • āgāt: This term means "came" or "arrived." It signifies the arrival of something significant. In real life, this can relate to milestones, such as achieving a goal or the arrival of a new opportunity.
  • svair mantrair: This translates to "with free mantras." It suggests the use of one's own words or expressions. In practice, this can relate to the importance of self-expression and communication in building relationships and understanding others.
  • anṛtupā: This term means "not false" or "truthful." It emphasizes the value of honesty and integrity. In real life, this can be seen in the importance of trust in personal and professional relationships.
  • ṛtāvā: This can be interpreted as "truth" or "order." It signifies the underlying principles that govern existence. In a practical sense, it can relate to the importance of ethics and moral values in guiding our actions and decisions.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates profound philosophical concepts about the nature of reality, transformation, and the importance of truth. It serves as a reminder to look beyond appearances, embrace change, and uphold integrity in our lives. In a world filled with distractions and illusions, this mantra encourages us to seek deeper understanding and authenticity in our interactions and experiences. Its significance is applicable in various real-life scenarios, from personal growth to ethical decision-making, guiding us toward a more meaningful existence.
