अग्निश्च यन् मरुतो विश्ववेदसो दिवो वहध्व उttarād अधि ष्णुभिः |ते मन्दसाना धुनयो ऋशादसो वामं धत्त यजमानाय सुन्वते ||
agniś ca yan maruto viśvavedaso divo vahadhva uttarād adhi ṣṇubhiḥ | te mandasānā dhunayo riśādaso vāmaṁ dhatta yajamānāya sunvate ||
  • Agniś ca: This phrase refers to "Agni," the fire god, who is a central figure in Vedic rituals. Fire symbolizes purity, transformation, and the divine presence in rituals.
  • yan maruto: "Maruto" refers to the storm gods or the winds. This highlights the connection between fire and the natural elements, emphasizing the importance of balance in nature.
  • viśvavedaso: This term means "all-knowing" or "omniscient." It suggests that the divine forces, represented by Agni and the Maruts, possess complete knowledge of the universe.
  • dīvo vahadhva: "Dīvo" means "of the heavens," and "vahadhva" implies "to carry" or "to bring forth." This indicates a connection between the earthly and the celestial, suggesting that the offerings made to Agni and the Maruts are carried to the heavens.
  • uttarād adhi ṣṇubhiḥ: This phrase can be interpreted as "from above, with the support of the divine." It emphasizes the idea that the blessings and energies from the divine realm are essential for the success of rituals.
  • te mandasānā: "Te" means "they," and "mandasānā" refers to those who are well-disposed or benevolent. This suggests that the divine forces are favorable and supportive of the worshippers.
  • dhunayo riśādaso: "Dhunayo" means "to purify" or "to cleanse," while "riśādaso" refers to the "sacred offerings." This indicates that the rituals performed are meant to purify the worshippers and their surroundings.
  • vāmaṁ dhatta: "Vāmaṁ" means "the good" or "the auspicious," and "dhatta" means "to bestow." This suggests that the divine forces are bestowing blessings upon the worshippers.
  • yajamānāya sunvate: "Yajamānāya" refers to the worshipper or the one performing the sacrifice, and "sunvate" means "to be pleased." This indicates that the ultimate goal of the mantra is to please the divine forces through the rituals performed.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda emphasizes the interconnectedness of fire, wind, and divine knowledge in the context of Vedic rituals. It highlights the importance of purifying oneself and one's surroundings through offerings to the divine. In real-life scenarios, this can be applied to various rituals and ceremonies where individuals seek blessings, guidance, and purification. The significance lies in recognizing the balance of natural elements and the divine, fostering a sense of respect and gratitude towards the forces that govern our lives. By performing rituals with sincerity and devotion, individuals can cultivate a harmonious relationship with the universe and its energies.
