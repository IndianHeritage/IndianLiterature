गोषा इन्दो नृषा अस्य अश्वसा वाजसा उता |आत्मा यज्ञस्य पूर्व्यः ||
goṣā indo nṛṣā asy aśvasā vājasā uta | ātmā yajñasya pūrvyaḥ ||
  • goṣā: This word refers to the "herd" or "group," often associated with cattle, which are considered sacred in Vedic culture. Cattle symbolize wealth and prosperity.
  • indo: This term means "of Indra," who is a principal deity in the Rigveda, representing strength, rain, and the heavens. Indra is often invoked for protection and blessings.
  • nṛṣā: This word translates to "man" or "human," emphasizing the connection between humanity and the divine.
  • asy: This means "of this" or "of these," indicating possession or association.
  • aśvasā: This term refers to "horses," which are symbols of speed, power, and nobility in Vedic literature. Horses were also essential for transportation and warfare.
  • vājasā: This word means "of strength" or "of vigor," often associated with the energy and vitality needed for life and rituals.
  • uta: This is a conjunction meaning "and" or "also," linking the various elements of the mantra.
  • ātmā: This term translates to "soul" or "self," representing the essence of an individual and their connection to the universe.
  • yajñasya: This word means "of the sacrifice" or "of the ritual," highlighting the importance of rituals in Vedic culture as a means to connect with the divine.
  • pūrvyaḥ: This term means "of the past" or "ancient," indicating the timeless nature of the rituals and the wisdom passed down through generations.

The mantra can be interpreted as a call to the divine, invoking the blessings of Indra and recognizing the interconnectedness of humanity, nature, and the divine through the symbols of cattle, horses, and the essence of the self. It emphasizes the importance of rituals and sacrifices in maintaining harmony and prosperity in life.


In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applied in various contexts:

  • Rituals and Celebrations: During festivals or significant life events, such as weddings or housewarming ceremonies, invoking blessings through rituals can bring a sense of community and divine protection.
  • Environmental Awareness: The reference to cattle and horses can remind us of the importance of respecting and nurturing our environment, as these animals are integral to agriculture and sustainability.
  • Personal Growth: The concept of the "self" or "ātmā" encourages individuals to reflect on their inner essence and strive for personal development, aligning their actions with their values.

In conclusion, this mantra encapsulates the essence of Vedic philosophy, emphasizing the significance of rituals, the interconnectedness of all beings, and the pursuit of harmony and prosperity in life. It serves as a reminder of our responsibilities towards ourselves, our community, and the environment.
