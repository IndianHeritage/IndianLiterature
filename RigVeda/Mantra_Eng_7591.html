स वृत्रहेन्द्र ऋभुक्षाः सद्योजजñानो हव्यो बभूव |कृत्वान्न अपांसी नार्यां पुरूणि सोमो न पीतो हव्यः सकिभ्यः ||
sa vṛtrahendra ṛbhukṣāḥ sadyo jajñāno havyo babhūva | kṛṇvann apāṁsi naryā purūṇi somo na pīto havyaḥ sakhibhyaḥ ||
  • sa vṛtrahendra: This phrase refers to "He who is the Indra of Vritra." In Vedic mythology, Indra is the king of the gods and the god of rain and thunderstorms, while Vritra is a serpent or dragon that represents drought and obstacles. This part of the mantra signifies the overcoming of challenges and the triumph of good over evil.
  • ṛbhukṣāḥ: This term translates to "the one who is nourished." It indicates the importance of sustenance and nourishment, both physical and spiritual. In real life, this can be related to the need for food, education, and emotional support that individuals require to thrive.
  • sadyo jajñāno: This means "born immediately" or "born at once." It suggests the idea of new beginnings and the emergence of new life or ideas. In practical terms, this can be seen in the birth of a child or the launch of a new project that brings hope and potential.
  • havyo babhūva: This translates to "has become worthy of offerings." It emphasizes the importance of being deserving of respect and offerings, which can be interpreted as the need for individuals to earn their place in society through good deeds and contributions.
  • kṛṇvann apāṁsi: This phrase means "creating waters." It symbolizes the act of generating life-sustaining resources, such as water, which is essential for survival. In real life, this can relate to environmental conservation efforts and the importance of clean water access.
  • naryā purūṇi: This translates to "the many of the people." It signifies community and the collective strength of individuals working together. This can be seen in social movements or community service initiatives where people unite for a common cause.
  • somo na pīto: This means "the soma is not drunk." Soma is a sacred drink in Vedic tradition, often associated with immortality and divine inspiration. This part of the mantra can imply the importance of moderation and the need to seek spiritual fulfillment without excess.
  • havyaḥ sakhibhyaḥ: This translates to "worthy of offerings to friends." It highlights the value of friendship and community support. In real life, this can be reflected in the importance of building strong relationships and networks that provide mutual aid and encouragement.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of overcoming obstacles, the importance of nourishment, new beginnings, deserving respect, creating essential resources, community strength, moderation in indulgence, and the value of friendship. Its significance lies in its applicability to various aspects of life, from personal growth and community building to environmental stewardship and spiritual fulfillment. By embodying these principles, individuals can contribute positively to their communities and lead fulfilling lives.
