अञ्जन्ति यं प्रथयन्तो न विप्रā वपावन्तं नाग्निना तपन्तः |पितुर न पुत्र उपसि प्रेष्ठ आगर्मो अग्निम ऋतयन्न असादि ||
añjanti yam prathayanto na viprā vapāvantaṁ nāgninā tapantaḥ | pitur na putra upasi preṣṭha ā gharmo agnim ṛtayann asādi ||
  • añjanti: This word translates to "they who are devoted" or "they who worship." It signifies the act of devotion and reverence towards a higher power or principle.
  • yam: This means "whom" or "that which," referring to the object of devotion or worship.
  • prathayanto: This translates to "they who spread" or "they who proclaim," indicating the act of sharing knowledge or wisdom.
  • na viprā: This means "not the learned" or "not the wise," suggesting that the focus is not solely on intellectual understanding but on deeper spiritual connection.
  • vapāvantaṁ: This can be interpreted as "the one who is nourished" or "the one who is sustained," referring to the divine or spiritual essence that sustains life.
  • nāgninā: This means "by fire," which symbolizes purification, transformation, and the divine presence in many cultures.
  • tapantaḥ: This translates to "those who are burning" or "those who are undergoing transformation," indicating the process of spiritual awakening or enlightenment.
  • pitur: This means "of the father," symbolizing lineage, heritage, and the connection to one's roots.
  • na putra: This translates to "not the son," suggesting a distinction between the physical and spiritual realms.
  • upasi: This means "to worship" or "to approach," indicating the act of seeking connection with the divine.
  • preṣṭha: This translates to "the beloved" or "the dearest," referring to the cherished aspects of life or the divine.
  • ā: This is a preposition meaning "from" or "out of," indicating a source or origin.
  • gharmo: This means "heat" or "warmth," symbolizing energy, passion, and the life force.
  • agnim: This translates to "fire," representing transformation, purification, and the divine spark within.
  • ṛtayann: This means "establishing" or "making true," indicating the act of bringing forth truth or reality.
  • asādi: This translates to "not attained" or "not achieved," suggesting the ongoing journey of spiritual growth and understanding.

Conclusion: This mantra emphasizes the importance of devotion, spiritual connection, and the transformative power of fire as a symbol of purification and enlightenment. It suggests that true understanding and connection to the divine come not just from intellectual knowledge but from a deeper, heartfelt devotion. In real life, this can be applied in various scenarios such as personal growth, community service, and spiritual practices where individuals seek to connect with their roots and the divine essence within themselves. The mantra encourages individuals to engage in practices that foster spiritual awakening and transformation, ultimately leading to a more profound understanding of life and existence.
