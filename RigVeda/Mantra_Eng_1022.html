पशून न चित्रा सुभगा प्रथाना सिंधुर न क्षोदा उर्विया व्य अश्वैत |अमिनती दैव्यानी व्रतानी सूर्यस्य चिति रश्मिभिर दृष्टाना ||
paśūn na citrā subhagā prathānā sindhur na kṣoda urviyā vy aśvait | aminatī daivyāni vratāni sūryasya ceti raśmibhir dṛśānā ||
  • paśūn: This word translates to "animals" or "creatures." In a broader sense, it can refer to all living beings. In real life, this can be seen in the way we interact with nature and the importance of biodiversity.
  • na citrā: Meaning "not varied" or "not different," this phrase suggests a sense of uniformity or harmony. For example, in a community, when people work together towards a common goal, they create a harmonious environment.
  • subhagā: This means "auspicious" or "fortunate." In life, we often seek auspicious beginnings, such as starting a new job or a new venture with positive energy and intentions.
  • prathānā: This translates to "the foremost" or "the best." It emphasizes the importance of prioritizing what is essential in our lives, such as family, health, and personal growth.
  • sindhur: Referring to "the ocean" or "river," it symbolizes vastness and depth. In real life, this can be likened to the vast opportunities and experiences life offers, much like the ocean's expanse.
  • na kṣoda: Meaning "not diminished" or "not reduced," this phrase can relate to the idea of resilience. For instance, despite challenges, one can maintain their spirit and strength.
  • urviyā: This word means "by the earth" or "of the earth," highlighting our connection to nature. It reminds us of the importance of environmental conservation and sustainable living.
  • vy aśvait: This can be interpreted as "with horses" or "with speed." Horses symbolize strength and progress, akin to how we strive for advancement in our personal and professional lives.
  • aminatī: This means "to be devoted" or "to be committed." In real life, commitment is crucial in relationships, careers, and personal goals.
  • daivyāni: This translates to "divine" or "heavenly," suggesting a connection to higher powers or spiritual beliefs. Many people find solace and guidance in their spiritual practices.
  • vratāni: Meaning "vows" or "resolutions," this emphasizes the importance of setting intentions and goals. For example, New Year’s resolutions are a common practice where individuals commit to self-improvement.
  • sūryasya: This means "of the sun," symbolizing light, energy, and life. The sun is essential for growth and sustenance, much like how positivity and motivation fuel our endeavors.
  • ceti: This translates to "with" or "by," indicating a connection or means to achieve something.
  • raśmibhir: Meaning "by the rays," it refers to the illuminating power of the sun's rays, which can be seen as a metaphor for knowledge and enlightenment.
  • dṛśānā: This means "of the seers" or "of the wise," emphasizing the value of wisdom and insight in navigating life’s challenges.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of harmony, auspiciousness, commitment, and the interconnectedness of life. It serves as a reminder to prioritize our relationships with nature, each other, and ourselves. In real-life scenarios, these principles can guide us in making thoughtful decisions, fostering community, and striving for personal growth. By embodying the essence of this mantra, we can create a more harmonious and fulfilling existence.
