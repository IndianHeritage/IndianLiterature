घृतवन्तः पावक ते स्तोकाः श्चोतन्ति मेदसः |स्वधर्मन् देववीतये श्रेष्ठं नो धेहि वार्यम् ||
ghṛtavantaḥ pāvaka te stokāḥ ścotanti medasaḥ | svadharman devavītaye śreṣṭhaṁ no dhehi vāryam ||
  • ghṛtavantaḥ: This term refers to those who possess ghee or clarified butter, symbolizing purity, nourishment, and richness. In a real-life context, ghee is often used in Indian cooking and rituals, representing abundance and the essence of life.
  • pāvaka: This word means 'pure' or 'sacred', often associated with fire. Fire is a vital element in many cultures, representing transformation and purification. For example, in Hindu rituals, fire is used in ceremonies to purify offerings and invoke divine blessings.
  • te: This simply means 'you' or 'your', addressing the divine or the audience of the mantra.
  • stokāḥ: This term means 'small' or 'little', indicating humility or the idea that even small offerings can be significant. In everyday life, this can be seen in acts of kindness, where even a small gesture can have a profound impact on someone's day.
  • ścotanti: This means 'they shine' or 'they illuminate', suggesting the idea of enlightenment or knowledge. In real life, this can relate to individuals who share wisdom or insights that help others grow and learn.
  • medasaḥ: This word refers to 'intellect' or 'wisdom'. In practical terms, it can be associated with education and the pursuit of knowledge, emphasizing the importance of learning in our lives.
  • svadharman: This term means 'one's own duty' or 'righteousness'. It highlights the importance of following one's path and responsibilities. In real life, this can be seen in professionals who adhere to ethical standards in their work, contributing positively to society.
  • devavītaye: This means 'for the sake of the divine' or 'to please the gods'. It emphasizes the connection between human actions and divine favor. In practice, this can be reflected in charitable acts or community service, where individuals seek to contribute to the greater good.
  • śreṣṭhaṁ: This term means 'the best' or 'the highest'. It signifies the aspiration to achieve excellence in one's endeavors. In real life, this can be seen in athletes striving for gold medals or students aiming for top grades.
  • no: This means 'us' or 'our', indicating a collective aspect of the mantra, suggesting that the blessings are sought for the community or group.
  • dhehi: This means 'grant' or 'bestow', a plea for divine blessings. In everyday life, this can be likened to seeking guidance or support from mentors or leaders.
  • vāryam: This term means 'protection' or 'safeguard', indicating a desire for safety and security. In real life, this can relate to the need for social support systems that protect individuals and communities from harm.

Conclusion: The mantra "ghṛtavantaḥ pāvaka te stokāḥ ścotanti medasaḥ | svadharman devavītaye śreṣṭhaṁ no dhehi vāryam ||" encapsulates the essence of purity, wisdom, and the pursuit of righteousness. It emphasizes the importance of small yet significant contributions to the community and the divine. In real life, this mantra serves as a reminder to strive for excellence in our duties, to seek knowledge, and to act with integrity. It encourages individuals to recognize the value of their actions, no matter how small, in the larger context of life and spirituality. By embodying these principles, we can create a more harmonious and enlightened society.
