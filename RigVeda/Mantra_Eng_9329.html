अहं होताऽन्यसीदं यजीयान् विश्वे देवाः मरुतो मा जुन्थः |अहर-अहर अश्विनाध्वर्यवः वाम ब्रह्मा समिद् भवति साहितिः वाम ||
ahaṁ hotā ny asīdaṁ yajīyān viśve devā maruto mā junanti | ahar-ahar aśvinādhvaryavaṁ vām brahmā samid bhavati sāhutir vām ||
  • ahaṁ hotā: This phrase translates to "I am the priest" or "I am the one who offers." In a real-life context, this can be seen as a call to take responsibility for one's actions and decisions, much like a leader or a guide in a community.
  • ny asīdaṁ: This means "I did not exist" or "I was not there." It reflects the idea of humility and the recognition of one's place in the universe, akin to acknowledging that we are part of a larger whole.
  • yajīyān: This term refers to "the one who performs sacrifices" or "the one who worships." In modern life, this can be interpreted as someone who engages in acts of service or devotion, whether through community service, environmental efforts, or personal sacrifices for the greater good.
  • viśve devā: This translates to "all the gods" or "the universal deities." It signifies the interconnectedness of all spiritual paths and the idea that there are many ways to reach a higher truth.
  • maruto mā junanti: This phrase means "the Maruts do not harm me." The Maruts are storm deities, representing the forces of nature. This can be seen as a metaphor for facing challenges and adversities in life without fear, trusting in the protective forces around us.
  • ahar-ahar: This means "day by day" or "every day." It emphasizes the importance of consistency and daily practice in our lives, whether in spiritual pursuits, personal growth, or professional endeavors.
  • aśvinādhvaryavaṁ: This refers to "the two Ashvins," who are divine horsemen and symbolize health and well-being. In real life, this can be related to the pursuit of physical and mental health, and the importance of balance in our lives.
  • vām brahmā: This translates to "your Brahman" or "your ultimate reality." It signifies the pursuit of truth and understanding of the universe, encouraging individuals to seek knowledge and wisdom.
  • samid bhavati: This means "becomes the fuel" or "is transformed." It suggests that through our actions and intentions, we can transform ourselves and our surroundings, much like how a fire is fueled to grow and spread warmth.
  • sāhutir vām: This translates to "your offering" or "your sacrifice." It highlights the importance of giving and sharing, whether it be time, resources, or love, in building a harmonious community.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates the essence of responsibility, humility, devotion, and the interconnectedness of life. It serves as a reminder to engage in daily practices that foster personal growth and community well-being. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire individuals to take on leadership roles, serve their communities, and seek a deeper understanding of themselves and the universe. By embodying the principles outlined in this mantra, one can contribute to a more harmonious and balanced existence, both personally and collectively.
