अबोधि होतā यजथाय देवान ऊर्ध्वो अग्निः सुमनाः प्रातर अस्तात् |समिद्धस्य रुशद अदर्शि पाजो महान देवस तमसो निर अमोचि ||
abodhi hotā yajathāya devān ūrdhvo agniḥ sumanāḥ prātar asthāt | samiddhasya ruśad adarśi pājo mahān devas tamaso nir amoci ||
  • abodhi: This term can be interpreted as "awakened" or "enlightened." It signifies a state of consciousness where one is aware and alert to the divine and the surrounding world.
  • hotā: This refers to the "priest" or "one who invokes." In the context of rituals, it denotes the person who performs the yajna (sacred fire ritual) and calls upon the deities.
  • yajathāya: This word means "for the purpose of sacrifice" or "for the ritual." It emphasizes the importance of performing rituals with intention and devotion.
  • devān: This translates to "the gods." It acknowledges the divine beings that are invoked during the rituals.
  • ūrdhvo: Meaning "upward" or "higher," this word suggests a connection to the higher realms or spiritual elevation.
  • agniḥ: This refers to "fire," which is a central element in Vedic rituals. Fire symbolizes purity, transformation, and the divine presence.
  • sumanāḥ: This means "well-disposed" or "with good thoughts." It indicates a positive mindset and intention behind the actions performed.
  • prātar: This translates to "in the morning," signifying the time of day when rituals are often performed, symbolizing new beginnings and fresh energy.
  • asthāt: This means "stood" or "established," indicating the presence and stability of the divine during the ritual.
  • samiddhasya: This refers to "kindled" or "lit," emphasizing the importance of the fire being properly prepared and ignited for the ritual.
  • ruśad: This can be interpreted as "shining" or "radiant," suggesting the brilliance of the divine presence during the ritual.
  • adarśi: Meaning "to see" or "to perceive," it indicates the act of witnessing the divine or the results of the ritual.
  • pājo: This translates to "the great one" or "the revered," referring to the divine being that is being worshipped.
  • mahān: This means "great" or "mighty," emphasizing the power and significance of the divine presence.
  • devas: This is another term for "gods," reiterating the focus on the divine beings in the context of the mantra.
  • tamaso: This translates to "darkness," symbolizing ignorance or the absence of light and knowledge.
  • nir: Meaning "without" or "free from," it indicates liberation from darkness or ignorance.
  • amoci: This means "to release" or "to set free," suggesting the act of overcoming obstacles and achieving enlightenment.

  • Significance: This mantra emphasizes the importance of rituals and the invocation of divine energies to achieve enlightenment and clarity. It highlights the role of the priest in connecting the material and spiritual worlds through the sacred fire.
  • Real-life Application: In modern life, this mantra can be applied to personal growth and spiritual practices. For instance, individuals can create a morning routine that includes meditation or prayer, symbolizing the "prātar" aspect, to set positive intentions for the day. The act of "kindling" one's inner fire through motivation and inspiration can lead to personal transformation.
