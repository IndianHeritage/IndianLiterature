अहं राजा वरुणो मह्यं तान्य असूर्याणि प्रथमā धारण्ति |क्रतुं सचन्ते वरुणस्य देवā राजाामि कृष्टर उपमस्य वव्रेḥ ||
ahaṁ rājā varuṇo mahyaṁ tāny asuryāṇi prathamā dhārayanta | kratuṁ sacante varuṇasya devā rājāmi kṛṣṭer upamasya vavreḥ ||
  • ahaṁ rājā: This phrase translates to "I am the king." It signifies a sense of authority and leadership. In real life, this can be related to individuals who take charge in various situations, such as a project manager leading a team or a community leader guiding their members.
  • varuṇo: Varuna is the Vedic deity associated with water, cosmic order, and moral authority. He represents the natural laws and the ethical framework within which society operates. In modern terms, this can be likened to the role of law and order in maintaining societal harmony.
  • mahyaṁ: This means "to me" or "for me," indicating a personal connection or appeal. It reflects the idea that the speaker is seeking guidance or favor from a higher power, similar to how individuals may seek mentorship or support in their personal or professional lives.
  • tāny asuryāṇi: This translates to "those that are not of the sun" or "the dark forces." It suggests a recognition of negative influences or challenges that one may face. In real life, this can be compared to obstacles such as negativity, doubt, or unethical behavior that individuals encounter in their journeys.
  • prathamā dhārayanta: This means "first they hold" or "they support." It implies the importance of foundational principles or values that guide one's actions. In everyday life, this can be seen in how core values shape decisions, such as integrity in business practices or compassion in personal relationships.
  • kratuṁ sacante: This translates to "the gods who are capable of sacrifice." It emphasizes the idea of selflessness and the willingness to give for the greater good. In contemporary society, this can be reflected in acts of charity, community service, or any endeavor where individuals prioritize collective well-being over personal gain.
  • varuṇasya devā: This means "the gods of Varuna." It signifies the divine forces that uphold cosmic order and morality. In real life, this can be related to the principles of justice and fairness that govern human interactions, akin to the role of judges or mediators in resolving conflicts.
  • rājāmi: This translates to "I rule" or "I govern." It reflects a sense of responsibility and stewardship. In practical terms, this can be seen in leadership roles where individuals are tasked with making decisions that affect others, such as in politics or organizational management.
  • kṛṣṭer upamasya: This means "of the excellence of cultivation." It suggests the importance of nurturing and developing one's skills and environment. In real life, this can be compared to personal development efforts, such as education, training, and self-improvement initiatives.
  • vavreḥ: This translates to "I have chosen." It indicates a conscious decision-making process. In everyday life, this can be related to the choices individuals make regarding their careers, relationships, and values, emphasizing the power of agency in shaping one's destiny.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of leadership, moral authority, personal responsibility, and the recognition of challenges. It serves as a reminder of the importance of ethical conduct and the need to cultivate one's abilities while navigating life's obstacles. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can inspire individuals to take charge of their lives, make conscious choices, and contribute positively to their communities, embodying the principles of justice, integrity, and selflessness. Its significance lies in its timeless relevance, encouraging individuals to strive for excellence while remaining grounded in their values and responsibilities.
