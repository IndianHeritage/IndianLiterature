शिवः कपोत इषितो नो अस्त्व अनागा देवाः शकुनो गृहेषु |अग्निर हि विप्रो जुषतां हविर नः परि हेतिः पक्षिणी नो वृणक्तु ||
śivaḥ kapota iṣito no astv anāgā devāḥ śakuno gṛheṣu | agnir hi vipro juṣatāṁ havir naḥ pari hetiḥ pakṣiṇī no vṛṇaktu ||
  • śivaḥ kapota iṣito no astv anāgā devāḥ śakuno gṛheṣu
    • śivaḥ: This word refers to the auspicious or benevolent aspect of divinity, often associated with Lord Shiva in Hinduism.
    • kapota: This translates to "dove" or "pigeon," symbolizing peace and purity.
    • iṣito: This means "may be desired" or "may be invoked," indicating a wish or prayer for something beneficial.
    • no astv: This phrase translates to "may it be for us," expressing a desire for blessings or good fortune.
    • anāgā: This means "free from sin" or "innocent," suggesting a state of purity and righteousness.
    • devāḥ: This refers to "gods" or divine beings, indicating the presence of higher powers in the invocation.
    • śakuno: This translates to "birds," which can symbolize freedom, transcendence, and the connection between the earthly and the divine.
    • gṛheṣu: This means "in our homes," indicating the context of domestic life and the importance of divine blessings in everyday living.
  • agnir hi vipro juṣatāṁ havir naḥ pari hetiḥ pakṣiṇī no vṛṇaktu
    • agnir: This word means "fire," which is a sacred element in Vedic rituals, symbolizing purity and transformation.
    • hi: This is a particle that emphasizes the preceding word, often translated as "indeed" or "for."
    • vipro: This refers to a "sage" or "wise person," indicating the importance of wisdom in invoking divine blessings.
    • juṣatāṁ: This means "may enjoy" or "may partake," suggesting a desire for the blessings of fire and wisdom.
    • havir: This translates to "offering," particularly in the context of Vedic rituals, where offerings are made to the gods.
    • naḥ: This means "for us," reinforcing the personal nature of the prayer.
    • pari hetiḥ: This phrase means "may it protect us," indicating a plea for safety and well-being.
    • pakṣiṇī: This translates to "birds," similar to the earlier mention, symbolizing freedom and the divine connection.
    • no vṛṇaktu: This means "may it choose for us," expressing a desire for divine selection and guidance.

Conclusion:

The mantra emphasizes the importance of invoking divine blessings for peace, purity, and protection in our homes and lives. It highlights the connection between the divine and the mundane, suggesting that by seeking the favor of the gods and wise sages, we can attain a state of innocence and safety. In real-life scenarios, this mantra can be applicable during rituals, family gatherings, or personal prayers, where individuals seek harmony and divine guidance in their daily lives. The significance lies in recognizing the need for spiritual support in achieving a balanced and fulfilling life, fostering a sense of community and connection with the divine.
