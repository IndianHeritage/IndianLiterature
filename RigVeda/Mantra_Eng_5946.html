ब्राह्मणासो अतिरात्रे न सोमे सरो न पूर्णम् अभितो वदन्तः |संवत्सरस्य तद अहः परि ष्ठ यन् मण्डूकाः प्रावृषीणम् बभूव ||
brāhmaṇāso atirātre na some saro na pūrṇam abhito vadantaḥ | saṁvatsarasya tad ahaḥ pari ṣṭha yan maṇḍūkāḥ prāvṛṣīṇam babhūva ||
  • brāhmaṇāso: This term refers to the learned or wise individuals, often associated with the priestly class in Vedic society. They are seen as the custodians of knowledge and spiritual wisdom.
  • atirātre: This word means "during the night" or "in the darkness." It signifies a time of introspection and contemplation, often when the mind is most active.
  • na some saro: This phrase can be interpreted as "not in the waters of the moon." The moon is often associated with calmness and tranquility, suggesting that the wisdom of the brāhmaṇas is not found in superficial or fleeting experiences.
  • na pūrṇam abhito vadantaḥ: This means "they do not speak of completeness all around." It implies that true wisdom transcends mere words and cannot be fully encapsulated in language.
  • saṁvatsarasya: This term refers to a year or a cycle of time, indicating the cyclical nature of existence and the importance of time in understanding life.
  • tad ahaḥ pariṣṭha: This translates to "that day is the culmination." It suggests that there is a specific moment or realization that brings together the insights gained over time.
  • yan maṇḍūkāḥ: This phrase means "that which the frogs." Frogs are often associated with rain and fertility, symbolizing renewal and the cyclical nature of life.
  • prāvṛṣīṇam: This term refers to the rainy season, a time of growth and abundance, highlighting the importance of nature in the cycle of life.
  • babhūva: This means "has become" or "has manifested," indicating the realization or manifestation of knowledge and wisdom.

Conclusion: The mantra emphasizes the importance of deep, introspective wisdom that transcends superficial understanding. It highlights the cyclical nature of time and existence, suggesting that true knowledge is revealed through experience and contemplation, particularly during times of darkness or uncertainty. In real life, this can be applied to moments of personal reflection, learning from challenges, and recognizing the growth that comes from difficult experiences. Just as the rainy season brings renewal and fertility, our struggles can lead to profound insights and personal development. This mantra serves as a reminder to seek deeper understanding and wisdom in our lives, especially during challenging times.
