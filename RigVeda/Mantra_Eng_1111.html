यो अश्वानां यो गवां गोपतिर्वशी य आरीतः कर्मणि-कर्मणि स्थिरः |वीळोश्चिदिन्द्रो यो असुन्वतो वधो मरुत्वन्तं सख्याय हवामहे ||
yo aśvānāṁ yo gavāṁ gopatir vaśī ya āritaḥ karmaṇi-karmaṇi sthiraḥ | vīḻoś cid indro yo asunvato vadho marutvantaṁ sakhyāya havāmahe ||
  • yo aśvānāṁ: This phrase translates to "who is the lord of the horses." In ancient times, horses were symbols of power and wealth. For example, in modern life, owning a horse can represent status and connection to nature.
  • yo gavāṁ: This means "who is the lord of the cows." Cows are revered in many cultures, especially in India, where they symbolize nourishment and abundance. In contemporary society, this can relate to sustainable farming and the importance of livestock in agriculture.
  • gopatir: This term refers to "the protector" or "the guardian." In real life, this can be likened to a leader or a mentor who guides and protects their community or family.
  • vaśī: This means "one who has control" or "master." In a modern context, this can refer to someone who has mastery over their emotions or circumstances, such as a successful entrepreneur who navigates challenges effectively.
  • ya āritaḥ: This translates to "who is invoked." This can relate to the practice of calling upon higher powers or energies in times of need, similar to how people pray or meditate for guidance.
  • karmaṇi-karmaṇi sthiraḥ: This phrase means "steadfast in actions." It emphasizes the importance of being consistent and reliable in one's duties, akin to a dedicated employee who consistently performs well at work.
  • vīḻoś cid indro: This can be interpreted as "the powerful Indra." Indra is often seen as a deity of strength and rain, representing the forces of nature. In real life, this can symbolize the importance of respecting and understanding natural forces, such as climate change and environmental conservation.
  • yo asunvato vadho: This means "who is the slayer of enemies." In a contemporary sense, this can refer to overcoming obstacles or challenges in life, such as a student who perseveres through academic difficulties.
  • marutvantaṁ: This term refers to "the stormy winds" or "the forces of nature." It signifies the unpredictable aspects of life, reminding us to be adaptable and resilient in the face of change.
  • sakhyāya: This translates to "for friendship." It highlights the importance of relationships and community support, similar to how friends come together to help each other in times of need.
  • havāmahe: This means "we invoke" or "we offer." It signifies the act of seeking blessings or support from higher powers, akin to how people gather for rituals or ceremonies to express gratitude and seek guidance.

Conclusion: The mantra encapsulates themes of leadership, protection, mastery, and the importance of community. It serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness of life, the respect for nature, and the significance of steadfastness in our actions. In real-life scenarios, these principles can be applied in various contexts, such as leadership roles in organizations, the importance of sustainable practices in agriculture, and the value of strong relationships in personal and professional life. By invoking these qualities, individuals can strive for a balanced and harmonious existence, fostering a sense of responsibility towards themselves and their communities.
