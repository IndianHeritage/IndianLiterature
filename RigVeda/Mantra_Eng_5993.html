इन्द्राय सुमदन्तमं सोमं सोतावरेण्यं |शक्र एनं पीपयद विश्वया धियाऽहिन्वानं न वाजयुम् ||
indrāya su madintamaṁ somaṁ sotā vareṇyam | śakra eṇam pīpayad viśvayā dhiyā hinvānaṁ na vājayum ||
  • Indrāya: This word refers to Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, symbolizing strength, power, and leadership.
  • Su: Meaning 'good' or 'well', it emphasizes the positive attributes associated with the subject.
  • Madintamaṁ: This translates to 'the most intoxicating', often referring to the divine nectar or soma, which is considered sacred and energizing.
  • Somaṁ: Soma is a ritual drink of importance in Vedic traditions, believed to confer immortality and divine inspiration.
  • Sotā: This means 'to pour' or 'to flow', indicating the act of offering or sharing the soma.
  • Vareṇyam: This translates to 'worthy of praise' or 'to be desired', highlighting the value of the soma and the act of offering it.
  • Śakra: Another name for Indra, emphasizing his might and ability to bestow blessings.
  • Eṇam: This means 'this' or 'him', referring to the soma being offered to Indra.
  • Pīpayad: This translates to 'to drink' or 'to consume', indicating the act of Indra receiving the soma.
  • Viśvayā: Meaning 'with all', it suggests a universal or all-encompassing aspect.
  • Dhiyā: This means 'intellect' or 'understanding', indicating the wisdom that comes from the divine experience.
  • Hinvānaṁ: This translates to 'to inspire' or 'to lead', suggesting the transformative power of the soma.
  • Na: Meaning 'not', it negates the following term.
  • Vājayum: This means 'to be defeated' or 'to be overcome', indicating strength and resilience against challenges.

The mantra essentially invokes Indra, asking him to partake of the soma, which is described as the most intoxicating and worthy of praise. It emphasizes the importance of sharing this divine nectar, which symbolizes knowledge, strength, and inspiration. The act of offering soma to Indra is a metaphor for seeking divine guidance and empowerment in our lives.


In real life, this mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as:

  • Leadership: Just as Indra is a leader among the gods, individuals in positions of authority can draw inspiration from this mantra to lead with wisdom and strength.
  • Personal Growth: The soma represents knowledge and enlightenment; thus, seeking knowledge and understanding can help individuals overcome challenges and achieve their goals.
  • Community and Sharing: The act of offering soma can be seen as a call to share resources and knowledge within a community, fostering unity and collective growth.

In conclusion, this mantra signifies the importance of divine inspiration, the pursuit of knowledge, and the strength to overcome obstacles. It serves as a reminder to seek wisdom and share it with others, ultimately leading to personal and communal empowerment.
