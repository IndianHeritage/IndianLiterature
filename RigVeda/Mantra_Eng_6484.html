विश्वं पश्यन्तो बिभृताः तनूष्वा तेन नो अधि वाचत |क्षमा रपो मरुत आतुरस्य न इष्कर्ता विहृतं पुनः ||
viśvam paśyanto bibhṛthā tanūṣv ā tenā no adhi vocata | kṣamā rapo maruta āturasya na iṣkartā vihrutam punaḥ ||
  • viśvam paśyanto: This phrase translates to "seeing the whole" or "perceiving the universe." It emphasizes the importance of having a holistic view of life and the interconnectedness of all beings.
  • bibhṛthā tanūṣv: This means "holding together in bodies." It suggests the idea of unity and strength in diversity, where different entities coexist and support one another.
  • ā tenā no adhi vocata: This can be interpreted as "may that speak to us." It is a call for guidance and wisdom from a higher power or the universe itself.
  • kṣamā rapo: This phrase refers to "the forgiveness of water." Water is often seen as a symbol of purity and renewal, and this part of the mantra highlights the importance of forgiveness and cleansing in our lives.
  • maruta āturasya: This translates to "the winds of distress." It acknowledges the challenges and struggles we face in life, represented by the winds that can be both gentle and fierce.
  • na iṣkartā vihrutam punaḥ: This means "not to be disturbed again." It conveys a desire for stability and peace after overcoming difficulties.

Conclusion: This mantra from the Rigveda encapsulates the essence of unity, guidance, forgiveness, and resilience. In real life, it serves as a reminder to maintain a holistic perspective, recognizing the interconnectedness of all beings. It encourages us to seek wisdom and guidance in times of distress, to practice forgiveness, and to strive for stability after facing challenges. This mantra can be applied in various scenarios, such as in conflict resolution, community building, and personal growth, where understanding and compassion are essential for harmony and progress.
