अध्वर्यवो 'रुणं दुग्धम् अंशुं जुहोतना वृषभाय क्षितीनाम् |गौराद वेदीयाम् अवपानम् इन्द्रो विश्वहेद याती सुतसोमम् इच्छन् ||
adhvaryavo 'ruṇaṁ dugdham aṁśuṁ juhotana vṛṣabhāya kṣitīnām | gaurād vedīyām̐ avapānam indro viśvāhed yāti sutasomam icchan ||
  • adhvaryavo: This term refers to the priests who perform the rituals, particularly the Adhvaryu, who is responsible for the physical aspects of the sacrifice.
  • 'ruṇaṁ: This word means 'debt' or 'obligation'. In the context of the mantra, it signifies the responsibilities that come with performing rituals.
  • dugdham: Translated as 'milk', it symbolizes purity and nourishment, often associated with the divine and the sustenance of life.
  • aṁśuṁ: This means 'light' or 'ray', representing knowledge, enlightenment, and the divine presence.
  • juhotana: This term means 'to offer' or 'to sacrifice', indicating the act of giving something valuable to the divine.
  • vṛṣabhāya: This refers to 'the bull', which is often a symbol of strength, fertility, and prosperity in Vedic literature.
  • kṣitīnām: This means 'of the earth', indicating the connection to the physical world and the importance of earthly sustenance.
  • gaurād: This term means 'from the cow', emphasizing the significance of cows in Vedic culture as symbols of wealth and nourishment.
  • vedīyām̐: This refers to the 'sacrificial altar', a sacred space where offerings are made to the deities.
  • avapānam: This means 'the offering' or 'the libation', which is a crucial part of the ritual where substances are poured out as a tribute to the gods.
  • indro: This refers to Indra, the king of the gods in Vedic mythology, representing power, strength, and the protector of the people.
  • viśvāhed: This term means 'to all beings', indicating the universal nature of the offerings and the inclusivity of the ritual.
  • yāti: This means 'to go' or 'to reach', suggesting the act of the offerings reaching the divine.
  • sutasomam: This refers to 'the soma', a sacred drink in Vedic rituals believed to confer immortality and divine inspiration.
  • icchan: This means 'desiring' or 'wishing', indicating the intentions behind the offerings made in the ritual.

Conclusion: This mantra encapsulates the essence of Vedic rituals, emphasizing the importance of offerings made to the divine for the sustenance of life and prosperity. It highlights the interconnectedness of the physical and spiritual realms, where the act of giving is seen as a way to fulfill obligations and seek blessings. In real life, this can be applied in various contexts, such as community gatherings, where sharing resources and intentions for the well-being of all is paramount. The significance of this mantra lies in its reminder of our responsibilities towards each other and the divine, fostering a sense of unity and purpose in our actions.
